# Distilled Animal Findings from Barrio-Amorós & Brewer-Carías (2008)

**Paper analyzed:** César L. Barrio-Amorós & Charles Brewer-Carías, *Herpetological results of the 2002 expedition to Sarisariñama, a tepui in Venezuelan Guayana, with the description of five new species*, Zootaxa 1942, 2008.

> Scope note: scientific names and common names follow the 2008 paper. Several names and family placements may have changed in later taxonomic literature. The paper is primarily about herpetofauna, so this file focuses on amphibians and reptiles, with a short section on other animals mentioned incidentally.

## High-level findings

- The 2002 expedition sampled **66 specimens representing 32 species** from six sites on and around Sarisariñama-tepui, Venezuela. The study also incorporates select reptiles from an earlier RCTV-USB expedition.
- The study described **five new taxa**: four explicitly new species from the 2002 material (*Anomaloglossus moffetti*, *Hyalinobatrachium mesai*, *Pristimantis sarisarinama*, *Gonatodes superciliaris*) and one newly named tree frog, *Hypsiboas tepuianus*, separated from eastern populations formerly treated as *H. benitezi*.
- Sarisariñama is treated as an isolated tepui or as part of a Jaua-Sarisariñama-Guanacoco complex connected at low elevations; the authors conservatively treat Sarisariñama as a unique zoogeographic unit pending more sampling.
- The paper identifies five apparently Sarisariñama-endemic species among the recognized herpetofauna: *Hyalinobatrachium mesai*, *Anomaloglossus moffetti*, *Stefania riae*, *Pristimantis sarisarinama*, and *Gonatodes superciliaris*.
- The strongest natural-diet observations are sparse and opportunistic: *Gonatodes superciliaris* ate a cricket; *Stefania riae* had a Homoptera insect in its stomach; *Bothrops atrox* contained a rodent; *Chironius* sp. contained a recently metamorphosed hylid frog; *Liophis torrenicola* apparently contained fish scales; *Lithobates palmipes* tadpoles fed on algae.
- Several species are noted as human food sources for Ye'kwana people, especially *Hypsiboas boans*, *Lithobates palmipes*, *Osteocephalus taurinus*, *Rhaebo guttatus*, *Chelonoidis denticulata*, and *Paleosuchus* sp.

## Sarisariñama habitat context

Sarisariñama-tepui is a pink sandstone plateau in Estado Bolívar, Venezuela, rising from about 400 m at its base to 2100 m at its western cliffs. The paper's collecting sites span lowland Canaracuni village/river habitats, foothill creeks, upper montane forest, bromeliad-rich summit scrub, and the giant Sima Mayor sinkhole. The authors emphasize that such tepuis function as biogeographic islands, with high endemicity despite generally species-poor summit herpetofaunas compared with surrounding lowlands.

## Site abbreviations used below

- **Camp I:** Edge of Sima Mayor, 1375 m; humid upper montane dwarf forest with epiphytes, no nearby streams.
- **Camp II:** Inside Sima Mayor, 1100 m, about 275 m below the rim; no permanent stream, water mainly in bromeliads/wall runoff.
- **Camp III:** Summit/open area near *Brocchinia* bromeliads, 1328 m.
- **Camp IV:** Southern slope, 1108 m, by a 20 m waterfall in upper montane forest.
- **Camp V:** Canaracuni village/airstrip, 406 m, savanna surrounded by evergreen rainforest and blackwater streams.
- **Locality VI:** Southern slope/foothills, 450-600 m; mountain creeks descending from massif.

## Taxonomic and ecological interpretation

### 1. New and apparently endemic species

The paper's most important contribution is taxonomic. Four species are newly described from the 2002 expedition: *Anomaloglossus moffetti*, *Hyalinobatrachium mesai*, *Pristimantis sarisarinama*, and *Gonatodes superciliaris*. The authors also name *Hypsiboas tepuianus* for eastern Guiana Shield populations previously included under *H. benitezi*. Of these, the paper treats five as apparently endemic to Sarisariñama: *Hyalinobatrachium mesai*, *Anomaloglossus moffetti*, *Stefania riae*, *Pristimantis sarisarinama*, and *Gonatodes superciliaris*. The caveat is important: some may later prove to occur on neighboring Jaua or Guanacoco, which are incompletely surveyed.

### 2. Strong lowland-versus-upland pattern

Lowland Camp V and Locality VI host many widespread Amazonian/Guianan species, including *Rhaebo guttatus*, *Chaunus marinus*, *Dendrobates leucomelas*, *Hypsiboas boans*, *Osteocephalus taurinus*, *Ameiva ameiva*, *Helicops angulatus*, and *Bothrops atrox*. In contrast, summit/sima/upper-slope habitats produced the most distinctive tepui species, especially *Stefania riae*, *Pristimantis sarisarinama*, *Gonatodes superciliaris*, and the highland stream frog *Anomaloglossus moffetti*.

### 3. Water and spray zones are biodiversity hotspots

Streams, waterfalls, creek margins, spray zones, and wet mossy walls are recurring collection sites. Examples include *Anomaloglossus moffetti* in waterfall spray and cascading streams, *Hypsiboas tepuianus* above streams and in waterfall spray, *Stefania riae* on wet mossy walls inside Sima Mayor, *Neusticurus tatei* in crevices within a cascade spray zone, and several lowland frogs and snakes in Canaracuni Creek.

### 4. Sima Mayor is not simply a barren sinkhole

Sima Mayor supports specialized or unexpected herpetofauna: the new gecko *Gonatodes superciliaris* was described from the sinkhole floor; *Stefania riae* was abundant on humid walls and vegetation; *Pristimantis sarisarinama* occurred in sima forests; and *Norops ortonii* and *Osteocephalus taurinus* appeared unexpectedly. The authors suggest some lowland species may enter unusual sima habitats through crevices or subterranean water routes.

### 5. Cultural importance to Ye'kwana people

The paper records Ye'kwana names for many animals and notes cultural/ecological roles. *Dendrobates leucomelas* (Wanadi Jiñamohidi) appears in mythology and basket iconography. *Pseustes poecilonotus polylepis* is respected as a protector against venomous snakes. *Bothrops atrox* (Kinoto) is associated with evil in Ye'kwana lore. Several frogs, tortoises, and caimans are noted as food resources.

## Food-source summary

### Natural diet directly observed or inferred in the paper

- *Gonatodes superciliaris*: cricket consumed by a male gecko.
- *Stefania riae*: Homoptera insect found in a male's stomach.
- *Lithobates palmipes*: tadpoles observed feeding on algae.
- *Liophis typhlus*: adult female appeared to be searching for frogs in a creek backwater.
- *Bothrops atrox*: rodent found in stomach.
- *Chironius* sp.: recently metamorphosed hylid frog in digestive tract, possibly *Hypsiboas tepuianus*.
- *Liophis torrenicola*: digestive tract mass apparently included fish scales.

### Species noted as human food sources

- *Rhaebo guttatus*: eaten by Ye'kwana.
- *Hypsiboas boans*: important dry-season protein source for Ye'kwana.
- *Osteocephalus taurinus*: eaten by Ye'kwana.
- *Lithobates palmipes*: highly appreciated food by Ye'kwana.
- *Chelonoidis denticulata*: important food source for some Venezuelan Amazonian tribes.
- *Paleosuchus* sp.: hunted and eaten by Ye'kwana; cooked skull found.

## Limitations of the evidence

- The authors intentionally collected representative samples rather than large series, so many habitat and diet statements are observational, not exhaustive.
- Many species had no diet data beyond a single stomach-content or feeding observation.
- Several identifications are tentative or unresolved: *Rhinella margaritifer* complex, *Hypsiboas* cf. *crepitans*, *Leptodactylus* cf. *andreae*, *Pristimantis* cf. *cavernibardus*, *Neusticurus* cf. *racenisi*, *Mabuya* sp., Tropiduridae sp., and *Chironius* sp.
- The high summit of Sarisariñama remained poorly explored; the paper explicitly says the results likely only scratch the surface of tepui diversity.
