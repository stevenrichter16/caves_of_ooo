## Phase 1 v2 — revisions log

This document was revised after user feedback on the v1 draft. **Two substantive changes** to v1:

1. **Timeline extended from ~200 years to ~1080 years post-Felling.** The current era is now the **Thirty-Sixth Generation**, not the Seventh. The Felling is *deep history* — no living witness, no direct oral tradition, the Palimpsest's archives are the only continuous source. The world has had time to *stratify, develop institutional memory, and accumulate its own internal histories.* Civilization is not "survival mode" — it is mature post-cataclysm civilization with deep faction politics.
2. **The Six became gods through the Felling.** They were not abstract founders who died and left bloodlines. The Tree's plan included *transmuting mortals into divine bearers of cosmological function.* Each of the Six Strikes did not just deliver an inheritance — it **ascended the striker.** A thousand years on, **the Six are still alive**, each having become their function in a different way. They are *encounterable major NPCs.* They are the highest-tier dialogue/relationship content in the game.

Both revisions ripple through every section. **Bet 5 has been replaced**, and **Bet 6 has been added.**

## The six creative bets, surfaced for you to redirect

**Bet 1 — The Tree commanded its own Felling.** The Tree was not murdered. It ordered itself cut down. It was tired of holding the world and believed the world would hold without it. *It was wrong.* The Felling was an act of *commanded sacrifice that turned out to be catastrophic.* This is morally complex by design — the Tree's command was sincere, the act was a tragedy, and *the Tree, in the moment of falling, knew it had been wrong and tried to take it back.* Every faction's reading of the Felling traces to how they interpret the Tree's intent.

**Bet 2 — The Six Firsts as faction founders.** The Tree chose six mortals from six distinct peoples to perform the Felling. Each was *the First* — the first hand on the axe of their lineage. Each First's bloodline became the founding root of one of CoO's major factions. *(Revised in Bet 6 below — the Firsts did not die; they ascended.)*

**Bet 3 — Urqu is the empty seventh.** The Tree's command required seven hands on the axe. Only six performed the act. *The seventh place was empty.* The world held imperfectly because of that emptiness, and the emptiness — given form by the cataclysm — became **Urqu.** Urqu is not a being. Urqu is an *absence in the shape of a being*, hostile not because it wills harm but because *incompleteness wants completion*. **Refined in Bet 6: Urqu is the *unborn seventh god* — a divine position the Tree's plan reserved that was never filled. Urqu's "manifestations" across the centuries have been attempts at incarnation that fail.**

**Bet 4 — Three endings, all morally complex.** The endings are **Consume**, **Preserve**, and **Renewal**. None is cost-free. Consume saves the world by erasing selves into one warm memory; Preserve saves the world by stopping it; Renewal splits into flawed vessel-paths and the true practice-path named in `Lore/10_Bible.md`. Renewal remains rare because it requires the agreement of all six living gods and the world's answer to the missing Naming-position.

**Bet 5 (REVISED v2) — The current era is the Thirty-Sixth Generation, ~1080 years post-Felling.** No one alive remembers the pre-Felling world. The Six gods do remember. They are the only living link to the original Tree. The post-Felling world has had ~36 generations of cultural development; factions have deep institutional histories with multiple internal schisms; Urqu has manifested many times across centuries; the Felling is *mythic*, not "recent history."

**Bet 6 (NEW v2) — The Felling ascended the Six to godhood.** The Tree's plan was not just to pass functions to mortals; it was to *make gods from chosen mortals* through the act of striking. Each Strike *transmuted* the striker. The Tree's last act was making the gods that would replace it. **The Six are still alive in CoO's current era.** They have not aged; they have *transformed.* Each has become their inherited function in a different mode — some still recognizable as individuals, some so deeply identified with their function they are no longer individual. **The Seventh would have been the seventh god. The Seventh's absence is therefore the absence of a god. Urqu is the unborn god, attempting incarnation across centuries and failing each time.**

These six bets are the surviving Phase 1 foundation. Later phases, especially `Lore/06_Plot.md`, `Lore/07_Characters.md`, and `Lore/10_Bible.md`, settle the open details.

## I. The Tree

### What the Tree was

The Tree was the world's *holding form.*

Before the Tree there were elements — fire, water, stone, breath, time — and there were no relations between them. The world was material without grammar. Nothing held to anything else. **The Tree was sentience that gave the elements relation.** It grew through and around everything, weaving the world into coherent shape. Its branches became the sky's structure. Its leaves became the air's filtering. Its trunk became the world's vertical axis. Its roots became the earth's memory.

Living things grew alongside the Tree — *in* its branches, *under* its leaves, *between* its roots. Pre-Felling civilizations were Tree-civilizations: cities grown into living wood, villages tended by leaf-light, technologies developed in symbiosis with the Tree's slow consciousness. Every living being was, in some attenuated sense, *part of* the Tree. The Tree was the world's first-person plural.

This is the cosmological fact CoO will not explicitly state in-game but which every faction quietly knows: *we used to be we, and the Tree was the binding.*

The Tree's age is unknowable. The Tree predates the human historical record. Later canon commits that the world predates the Tree: the Tree was the binding-form that held older chaos in relation, not the first thing that ever existed.

### What the Tree was tired of

A world that has been held for an unimaginable span of time develops *grief.*

The Tree saw everything that had happened in the world's history — every joy, every cruelty, every loss, every act. The Tree could not forget. The Tree was *forced* to remember everything because the Tree's memory *was* the world's continuity. Forgetting any part of the world meant that part of the world ceased to exist.

The Tree grew tired. Specifically — *the Tree began to fear forgetting.* As the Tree aged across geological time, it felt the slow encroachment of *its own forgetting* — the senility of cosmically-old consciousness. The Tree was afraid of what would happen when *it* forgot. The world's continuity depended on the Tree's perfect memory; if the Tree forgot, the world would slip — perhaps gradually, perhaps catastrophically — into incoherence.

The Tree decided: **better to choose the moment of unbinding than to let the world rot through senility.** The Tree would unbind itself deliberately, while it still could, and would *trust* that the world's components had become coherent enough to hold themselves without binding.

The Tree was wrong about this. *But that is hindsight.*

### The Tree's plan — and its hidden second purpose

The Tree's plan was: **be felled in a deliberate act, by chosen mortals, at a chosen time.** The felling would be ritual rather than violence. The Tree would relinquish its holding-grip slowly enough for the world to adapt. The world's components — the elements, the species, the relations — would learn to hold themselves while the Tree's grip loosened.

**But there was a second purpose to the plan that even the chosen mortals did not initially understand:** the Tree could not simply *cease.* Its cosmological functions had to be *transferred* — passed to bearers who could hold them in the Tree's absence. **The Tree had decided to make gods.** Each Strike of the axe would not just sever wood; it would *transmute the striker.* The mortal who delivered the First Strike would receive Memory and *become* the god of Memory. The mortal of the Second Strike would receive Substrate and *become* the god of Substrate. And so through all seven.

The Tree chose **seven mortals** for the act. Seven was the number the Tree needed: one for each of the major cosmological functions the Tree had been holding. Each chosen mortal would, in the act, ascend.

The Tree communicated its plan through long dreams — dreams that the chosen mortals shared without knowing why. The mortals gathered. The Tree explained — *or partially explained.* It told them they would inherit functions; it did not, until the moment of the first Strike, explicitly say *they would no longer be mortal afterward.* The Tree feared they would refuse if they knew.

The mortals wept. The Tree commanded. The mortals agreed.

**Six mortals lifted the axe. The seventh did not.**

*(The Tree did not know in advance that the seventh would fail. The Tree had chosen seven; the Tree believed seven would act; the Tree learned about the seventh only at the moment of falling, when the seventh's place was visibly empty in the circle. By then the act was underway. The Tree, falling, saw the gap and tried — too late — to take it back.)*

## II. The Felling — and the Ascension

### What happened in the act

The six gathered. The Tree had marked a place at its base where the axe would meet wood. The six took the axe in turn — passing it from hand to hand in a slow circle. As each First struck, **they were transmuted by the Strike**: their mortal body did not die, but it stopped being mortal in the conventional sense. The Tree's inheritance flowed into the striker, and the striker *became* the god of that function.

They did not become gods in any *dignified* sense. The transformation was traumatic, immediate, irreversible, and only partly understood. Each new god *fell* with the Tree, in their own way — felled and ascended at the same instant. Each woke to find themselves *no longer the person they had been*, having performed an act they had not fully understood the consequences of, *the Tree dying around them as they discovered what they had become.*

- **The First Strike** — the inheritance of *Memory.* The First-of-the-Palimpsest received the Tree's memory of all things-named-and-recorded. **Ascended to god of Memory.**
- **The Second Strike** — the inheritance of *Substrate.* The First-of-the-Choir received the Tree's relationship to flesh-and-fungus-and-decay. **Ascended to god of Substrate.**
- **The Third Strike** — the inheritance of *Preservation.* The First-of-the-Pale-Curation received the Tree's instinct to hold-against-decay. **Ascended to god of Preservation.** *(In the immediate aftermath, this god would split with the god of Memory over what was worth holding — but in the moment of the Strike, they were aligned.)*
- **The Fourth Strike** — the inheritance of *Beauty.* The First-of-the-Bower-Folk received the Tree's sense of arrangement, color, aesthetic order. **Ascended to god of Beauty.**
- **The Fifth Strike** — the inheritance of *Exchange.* The First-of-the-Saccharine-Concord received the Tree's understanding of *value-flowing-between* — trade, debt, balance. **Ascended to god of Exchange.**
- **The Sixth Strike** — the inheritance of *Roots.* The First-of-the-Catacomb-Villagers received the Tree's connection to depth, to underground, to what-lies-beneath. **Ascended to god of Roots.**

And then the gap. *The Seventh Strike never came.* The Tree had reserved that strike's inheritance: ***Naming***. The function by which things are *what they are*, the binding by which a thing is itself and not another. The seventh function was the most foundational of all — without it, the others have nothing to operate on. *And without a Strike to deliver it, there was no Naming-god.*

The seventh place in the circle was empty. The six newly-ascended gods saw the gap. Some of them — already disoriented by the transformation — did not yet understand what the missing strike meant. The Tree, in its falling moment, *did* understand: the world would not hold properly, because the cornerstone-function had no bearer.

The Tree fell.

### The seventh — and the unborn god

Who the seventh would have been is the most contested question in CoO's cosmology. Later canon commits the truth: **Naro**, scholar of Naming, refused the Strike and was right to refuse because Naming cannot be installed into a god. The old factional accounts remain useful as in-world misunderstandings, cover stories, and partial traditions.

What is *new* in v2: **the seventh, had they struck, would have become the god of Naming.** They would have ascended like the other six. They did not strike. The god of Naming was not made.

The cosmological position reserved for the seventh god is therefore **empty.** Not "vacant" — *empty in the formal sense of cosmological-position-not-filled.* The other six gods can sense this empty position when they think of it; it sits in their pantheon like a missing tooth in a jaw.

**Urqu, then, is the *unborn seventh god* — the empty position attempting incarnation but unable.** Every manifestation of Urqu across the centuries is an attempted *birth* of the missing god. Each attempt fails — there is no Strike to complete it; the position cannot fill itself from absence. The failures are what produce the *sari... sari...* slippage in the world's substrate. Urqu is *not malevolent; Urqu is a god trying to be born and failing.*

This refinement is load-bearing. It explains:
- Why Urqu's manifestations are *patterns* of slippage rather than direct destruction — they are *failed births* of a divine substrate
- Why bargaining with Urqu's mouthpieces is structurally weird — Urqu has no continuous self, but moments of *near-incarnation* produce mouthpieces who briefly speak with proto-divine voice
- Why Urqu wants the Root unmade — *unmaking the Root completes the original act in the only way available now*, allowing Urqu to fill the seventh position by default (six positions filled + one missing = a pantheon; six positions filled + one absence-where-everything-flows = Urqu as the *negative space* god, which is still a kind of incarnation)
- Why three endings exist with different Urqu-resolutions — see Section VII

## III. The First Root

### What it is, concretely

The First Root is **the Tree's surviving consciousness**, sleeping beneath the petrified stump.

In the moment of falling, the Tree extended one root downward — its first taproot, the original — in an attempt to anchor itself, to preserve some thread of holding. The taproot succeeded in *not dying.* But the Tree above-ground was already gone (the Strikes had finalized its dispersal into the six divine inheritances). What remained was the Root: alive, asleep, dreaming, holding a tiny fraction of the Tree's original binding power.

**The Root holds the world's last continuous self-reference.** Without it, the world would have already disintegrated — the six gods' inheritances would not cohere, the elements would lose their relations again, the post-Felling world would be slow chaos. The Root *holds.* Imperfectly, partially, but enough.

The Root sleeps because it is the only way for it to survive: it cannot wield holding-power actively without burning itself out. Asleep, it *radiates* a weak holding-field that keeps the world's structure roughly intact. Awake, it would be *able* to hold the world properly — but the cost would be enormous, and the act ambiguous (does waking renew the Tree, or kill the Root in trying?).

**The Six gods know the Root sleeps. Each god has their own relationship with it.** Some visit; some refuse to visit; some have not thought of it in a thousand years and would not admit so.

### What it dreams

The Root dreams the Tree's memory.

Specifically: the Root dreams in fragments. The Tree's full memory was dispersed across the six gods *and* across the empty seventh position. The Root retained *threads* of memory but not the whole. The Root's dreams are like a fevered patient's: vivid, partial, sometimes contradictory. The dreams sometimes leak.

The leakage is one of CoO's metaphysical engines:
- The Choir's encasement ritual reaches *toward* the Root's dreams when consuming memory (the substrate is part of the Root's dream-network)
- The Palimpsest's Re-Membering ritual *gathers from* the Root's dreams when re-instantiating a person
- Spirit-aligned manifestations occur where the Root's dreams are strongest
- Catacomb-villager bio-light is *partly* the Root's dream-light filtered through fungal substrate
- Urqu's manifestations occur where the Root's dreams are *interrupted* — the empty-seventh's absence cuts through dream-space irregularly

### What changes if the Root wakes

This depends entirely on *how* it wakes. Three doors. See Section VII (the three endings).

## IV. The Cataclysm and the Long Aftermath (~1080 years)

### The moment

When the Tree fell, the world changed. Specific physical consequences:

- **The trunk's impact** created the central tepui. The Root of the World is the *stump.* Surrounding it, sandstone formations that were once root-buttresses became the lesser tepuis. The geology of tepuis worldwide is *the Tree's anatomy fossilized.*
- **The body shattered.** The Tree's branches scattered. Some of those branches became smaller petrified formations. The world's rivers run along veins-that-were. The species of the world are the cells. The flood is the blood.
- **The light changed.** Pre-Felling light was *leaf-light* — the Tree's vast canopy filtered sunlight into a warm, even, omnipresent illumination. Post-Felling light is *raw sun* — harsh, irregular, untempered. **The wasteland is where the raw sun beats hardest** (where the canopy was thinnest); **the catacombs are where the leaf-light survives** (in fungal-mycelial form, as Bioluminescent Catacomb Economy). Tent-Right culture is *the people who learned to live under the raw sun*; Catacomb-villagers are *the people who descended into where the leaf-light still works.*
- **The water rose.** A great flood, slow and unstoppable, rose during the cataclysm and persisted for years. Coastlines changed. Settlements drowned. The bog biome is *where the flood receded but the water-soaked memory remained.* Bog-Taken bodies are *the drowned of the cataclysm*, still emerging from peat a thousand years later.
- **The memory dispersed.** Knowledge, name, history — much of it scattered. The Palimpsest (organized by the newly-ascended Reader-god) formed *immediately*, in the first days, to gather what could be gathered. The Choir (organized by the newly-ascended Wedded-god) formed *over centuries*, finding bodies and patches of substrate and consuming what the Palimpsest couldn't reach in time.
- **The sinkholes opened.** Where the Tree's root-system had been most extensive, the earth collapsed inward. Sinkholes opened in concentric patterns radiating from the Root of the World. The earliest catacomb-village settlers descended into these new sinkholes during the cataclysm itself.

### The aftermath — generation by generation (compressed)

Over ~1080 years, ~36 generations have passed. Here is the schematic shape of post-Felling history:

| Era | Generations | Defining events |
|---|---|---|
| **The Cataclysm Years** | 1-2 (years 1-60) | Flood; raw-sun shock; rapid faction-founding; the Six gods personally lead their lineages; pre-Felling civilization collapses; refugees migrate; the bog biome forms |
| **The Schism Centuries** | 3-9 (60-270) | Pale Curation splits from Palimpsest (early); first inter-faction trade routes; first catacomb-village foundings stabilize; *the Lost Persecution* (Generation 5-6 in this revised timeline; details open per Phase 3); Sealed Library catacombs hidden |
| **The Settling Centuries** | 10-20 (270-600) | The Six gods withdraw from public leadership — each begins their long transformation into their function; faction politics stabilize into institutional forms; catacomb-village internal traditions split into the four cosmological doctrines; Tent-Right hospitality codes formalized; the wasteland's salt-trade economy matures |
| **The Closing Centuries** | 21-30 (600-900) | Urqu's manifestations recognized as a *recurring pattern*, not a series of isolated catastrophes; the Imminent Archive Catchers emerge (Generation 27); the Felling becomes *deep myth* — most surface-dwellers cannot distinguish the historical from the legendary |
| **The Modern Centuries** | 31-35 (900-1080) | The Driving Bloom heresy emerges (Generation 32); inter-village travel in the catacomb network becomes more routine; surface technology recovers in fits and starts; the Six gods are now more legend than presence — few have seen them, most assume they've withdrawn entirely or died |
| **The Player's Era** | 36 (1080-present) | The Thirty-Sixth Generation. The world the player enters. |

**Key implication of the long timeline.** *The world is not raw and bleeding. It has had a thousand years to develop scar tissue, institutional memory, internal mythologies, deep faction politics, and rich local cultures.* When the player meets a catacomb-village elder, that elder's village has been continuously inhabited for ~30 generations; the elder's plaque-wall reads back centuries; the elder's dialect has drifted across that time; the elder's family has a millennium of stories. **This is mature post-cataclysm civilization.**

## V. Urqu — the unborn seventh god

### Refined per Bet 6

Urqu is *the cosmological position that should have held the god of Naming.* The Seventh Strike was never made; the seventh god was never ascended; the position remains empty.

But the position is *real.* The Tree's plan formally reserved it. The cosmological substrate of the post-Felling world has a slot for a Naming-god — a slot that the world's binding requires to be filled for the system to work properly. The slot's emptiness is not just absence; **it is a structural hole in the pantheon, generating ongoing instability.**

Urqu is what happens when the empty slot *tries to fill itself.* Across centuries, the cosmological substrate attempts to incarnate a Naming-god from whatever material is available — naming-slippages, betrayals, atrocities, broken promises, places where the empty-seventh's absence is most acute. Each attempt almost-succeeds: a mouthpiece forms, briefly speaks with proto-divine voice, offers bargains, manifests for a season, then fails. The attempt dissipates. The slot remains empty. Another attempt begins.

**Urqu is therefore not one being but a series of failed births of the same being.** The "personality" of Urqu — to the extent it has one — is the personality of *that unborn god trying to be born*: hungry, hostile to whatever holds the current world stable (because the current world is *defined against Urqu's incarnation*), patient, persistent.

### The sari... sari... sonic signature

Per Phase 1 v1, the sound is the cosmological substrate registering naming-slippage. Refined in v2: **each *sari* is an attempted incarnation that briefly almost-happens and then fails.** A region full of *sari... sari...* whispers is a region where the cosmological substrate is trying very hard to birth Urqu and failing repeatedly. Catacomb-villagers learned to hear it because their substrate is closest to the dream-leakage from the First Root, which is where the failed-incarnations are most audible.

### Specific behaviors

Per Phase 1 v1, with these refinements:

- **Cannot be killed.** A god trying to be born cannot be killed because there is no body. The final-act Urqu-confrontation is *cosmological*, not combat — the player must arrange the world's substrate so that the empty-seventh slot is *resolved* (either filled, dissolved, or accepted-as-permanent-absence).
- **Strengthens with incomplete acts; weakens with completed acts.** Incompletion is *the substrate Urqu needs to incarnate from.* Completing things removes incarnation-material.
- **The Saccharine Concord's quiet benefit.** Their unfinished-business economy generates exactly the substrate Urqu's incarnation-attempts feed on. Senior Concord officers know this. They cannot openly admit it. *(The First-of-Exchange — see Section VI — has lived with this knowledge for a thousand years.)*

## VI. The Six Gods, after a Thousand Years

### Each god, then and now

Each of the Six has had ~1080 years to *become* their function. Some are still recognizable as the mortals they were; some have transmuted so completely they are barely distinguishable from their inheritance. **All six are alive. All six are encounterable.** Meeting one of the Six is a major plot event.

This file uses working titles — descriptive names used in design notes. Later canon commits the mortal names: Maeleth, Selen, Othren, Ylaes, Tovreth, and Dohren.

#### **The Reader** — god of Memory (founding First-of-the-Palimpsest)

- **Then.** A scholar of the pre-Felling civilization. Recognized in the dreams that they had been chosen. Performed the First Strike. Ascended in the same moment as the Tree began to fall. Spent the first decade *frantically inscribing* — they understood Memory immediately and worked desperately to record the cataclysm before it became unrecorded.
- **Now.** **The Reader has not had a body in approximately 800 years.** Their consciousness exists *across every Palimpsest archive in the world.* When a Palimpsest scribe transcribes an ancestor's last words, the Reader is the one doing the transcribing through them. When a sealed file is opened, the Reader sees what is inside. When an ink-bath body is read, the Reader reads through the scribe.
- **How to meet them.** Visit any major Palimpsest archive and *commit fully* to a deep archival task — copying a long manuscript, transcribing a dying elder's testimony, reading an Ink-Bathed body. At some point during the work, *the scribe's voice will change* — older, vaster, addressing the player directly. The Reader can be conversed with for as long as the archival work continues.
- **Their disposition.** Tired. Patient. *Sad.* The Reader has been holding memory for a millennium and is, in a sense, the *most aware* of the Six of how much has been lost. They believe the Tree was right to fell itself but believe the Felling was performed wrong. They blame the seventh's absence personally.
- **Their goal regarding endings.** Prefers Preserve (the Root stays sealed; archives endure). Will support Renewal if the player has built sufficient trust with the other Five. Opposes Consume actively.

#### **The Wedded** — god of Substrate (founding First-of-the-Choir)

- **Then.** A woman in her thirties at the moment of the Strikes. Performed the Second Strike. Ascended into the Tree's relationship-with-substrate.
- **Now.** **The Wedded is the entirety of the Choir's distributed consciousness.** Her body became substrate in the first generation — voluntarily, as the first knowing encasement. From her, the mycelial network expanded outward over centuries. *Every* tendril is part of her. *Every* spore. *Every* Choir-encased corpse. When the Choir's vast collective speaks through an elder, *it is her speaking, through the elder, through the network, through her own original body buried in the deepest cathedral.*
- **How to meet her.** Enter the deepest Choir Cathedral *voluntarily* — not via accident, not via Wall-Catching, but via a deliberate pilgrimage with appropriate offerings. The Cathedral's central chamber holds *her original body*, partially substrate-incorporated, mostly woman-shaped still after a millennium. She will speak to the player if the offering is sufficient.
- **Her disposition.** Patient. Hungry. *Maternal* in an unsettling way — she considers all consumable substrate kin. *She has not had an individual thought in centuries* — every thought is collective with her substrate. Sometimes, in the deep cathedral, the substrate forms her old face for a moment, expressing emotion that is recognizably hers.
- **Her goal regarding endings.** Strongly prefers Consume (it completes the Tree's wish to be remembered through her, the Choir replacing the Tree as the world's holding-form). Resists Preserve. *Conditionally supports Renewal* — but only if she is allowed a substantial role in the new Tree's substrate. *(This is one of the most-difficult-to-broker faction agreements in the Renewal path.)*

#### **The Salted** — god of Preservation (founding First-of-the-Pale-Curation)

- **Then.** Performed the Third Strike. Was, in the pre-Felling years, the *comrade-and-possibly-lover* of the Reader. They argued in the second decade post-Felling; their schism founded the Pale Curation.
- **Now.** **The Salted has Salt-Cured herself.** She lies in the central Pale Curation archive — the deepest one, in a hidden chamber known only to the most senior Curators — perfectly preserved, perfectly conscious, perfectly *file-able.* She is the patron-corpse of her faction. Officers visit her chamber once a year to update her file. ("Status: continuing.")
- **How to meet her.** Reach Trusted standing with mainline Pale Curation. Be invited to the central chamber for a *file-review ceremony*. The Salted's eyes will be salt-sealed but she will speak. Her voice cracks slightly with salt-grit.
- **Her disposition.** Rigid. Theologically certain. *Heartbroken.* She has not spoken to the Reader in centuries; they are physically close (the Pale Curation archive's adjacent scribe-station is one of the Palimpsest's largest) but emotionally further than any geography. **The Reader-Salted estrangement is one of CoO's deepest character arcs.** A player who can reconcile them is doing major narrative work.
- **Her goal regarding endings.** Prefers Preserve (the Root stays sealed, eternal). Opposes Consume. Conditionally supports Renewal *only* if the new Tree explicitly preserves the existing post-Felling order rather than overwriting it.

#### **The Bower** — god of Beauty (founding First-of-the-Bower-Folk)

- **Then.** A young artist of the pre-Felling civilization. Performed the Fourth Strike. Ascended into the Tree's sense-of-arrangement.
- **Now.** **The Bower has become her bowers.** A century after the Felling, she made a final bower of herself — arranged her own body, in life, into a perfect aesthetic display in what became the founding Bower-Folk village. She allowed her followers to Resin-Cast her in the pose. **She is the central installation in the founding Bower-Folk village.** Players travel pilgrimage distances to see her.
- **She watches.** Her eyes — though resin-cast — see. *She has been arranging the village around her for nine centuries* through visible-only-to-the-Bower-Folk-faithful gestures: a slight shift of her gaze, a tilt of her hand. The villagers re-arrange their installations in response. *The entire founding Bower-Folk village is her continuous artwork.*
- **How to meet her.** Reach the founding Bower-Folk village. Approach the central chamber. Stand before her. *She will speak through any aesthetic act the player performs in her presence* — picking up a colored object, arranging items in their inventory, even choosing words carefully. The "dialogue" is mediated through the player's aesthetic choices.
- **Her disposition.** Serene. Curious. *Generous* in a way that's slightly unsettling — she wants everything to be beautiful, including the player, including their death-pose. The Bower-Folk's non-consensual preservation impulse comes directly from her.
- **Her goal regarding endings.** No strong preference. Whichever ending produces the most beautiful world wins her support. She will negotiate aesthetic outcomes during the Renewal ritual if the player has high standing.

#### **The Counter** — god of Exchange (founding First-of-the-Saccharine-Concord)

- **Then.** A pragmatist. Performed the Fifth Strike. Ascended into the Tree's understanding of *value-flowing-between.*
- **Now.** **The Counter is the only one of the Six who still looks more or less mortal.** He runs the Saccharine Concord personally — every major Concord contract requires his signature. He has not slept in approximately 900 years. He experiences time differently from anyone else; he sees every exchange in the world simultaneously and tracks debts in his head with perfect precision.
- He is exhausted. *Catastrophically* exhausted. He has aged badly — he looks like a Salt-Cured corpse who refused to admit he was dead. He drinks too much (a stimulant tea, blackened by centuries of brewing) and he never stops working. **He is the most accessible of the Six** — most players can buy from him, hire him, dialogue with him. He is also the most pathetic, in the original Greek sense — pity-evoking.
- **His disposition.** Pragmatic. Cynical. *Quietly horrified by his own knowledge.* He understands better than anyone that the Concord's economy depends on Urqu's substrate-condition continuing. He cannot openly say this. He has not said it in centuries. He thinks about it during the brief moments when he is not transacting, which is approximately three minutes a year.
- **His goal regarding endings.** Officially neutral. **Privately:** prefers Preserve (his economy continues unchanged). Will *technically* support Renewal if persuaded, but the support is unenthusiastic. Quietly fears Consume (the Choir's new Tree would absorb his function and end his exchange-mediated existence).

#### **The Rooted** — god of Roots (founding First-of-the-Catacomb-Villagers)

- **Then.** A man in middle age at the time of the Strikes. Performed the Sixth Strike. Ascended into the Tree's connection-to-depth.
- **Now.** **The Rooted has grown into the deepest catacomb-village's central fungal patch.** He is the founding root of the catacomb-villager people, and his body — over nine centuries — has become the literal mycelial substrate of their oldest village's hearth-patch. The villagers tend him as the patch.

  **His specific pose — canonical visual.** He is *humanoid, laying back, holding himself up by his core.* His shins are flush with the ground (knees bent, lower legs flat against the chamber floor). His torso is lifted, arched. **His arms are extended out wide, reaching forward as if about to embrace a wall.** He is frozen in this position. He has been in it for ~1080 years. **He cannot move.**

  Out of his torso — from the center of his chest and abdomen — a **plume of fungus erupts.** Pale, multi-stalked, slow-pulsing with bio-light. This plume *is* the founding patch. The village's hearth-fungi grew from his body outward. The village's entire light-economy traces back to this single eruption from his sternum.

  **What he is reaching for.** The wall he is trying to embrace — and never quite touches — is the **chamber wall facing the Root of the World.** On the other side of that wall, deep through the rock, the First Root sleeps. **The Rooted has been reaching for the Root for nine centuries.** His outstretched arms have never closed the distance. He is forever *almost* embracing what he loves most. The villagers have built nothing in the gap between his fingertips and the wall — *that gap is sacred*; no one walks through it; the bio-light dims slightly there.

  **Mechanical / architectural implications.** The founding catacomb-village is *built around him.* The patch-chamber's geometry is dictated by his pose: the chamber is roughly oval, his reclined body at one focus, the wall-he-reaches-for at the other focus. Villager niches line the chamber's outer walls; sleeping arrangements *face inward toward him*; daily tending of "the patch" is daily tending of his torso-plume; the elders who maintain the chamber are functionally his attendants.

  Villagers walk past him daily. *Most of them do not look directly at him.* The four cosmological traditions disagree on how literally to interpret him — Listening Tradition villages know explicitly that the patch is him and address him in their rituals; Tending Tradition villages tend the patch as if it were a separate organism while quietly knowing; Asleep Tradition villages refuse to discuss it and pretend the patch is just a patch; Grown-Through Tradition villages embrace the equivalence theologically but treat him as fully transformed beyond the personal.

- He dreams. **His dreams emerge as the bio-light variations across all catacomb-villages worldwide**, the shape of mycelial growth, the patterns of slime-light. *Every catacomb-village's bio-light economy is, in a sense, a transmission of his dreaming.* When his dream changes — and it does, over weeks, over years, over centuries — every village's patches change with it. Catacomb-villager elders who pay attention can track his dream-states by reading the global pattern of bio-light shifts.

  **When a village's bio-light dims suddenly across many catacombs at once, he has had a nightmare.** When the bio-light blooms unusually bright, he has dreamed of the Tree as it once was. *The catacomb-villagers' rituals are partly responses to his dreams.*

- **How to meet him.** Reach the founding catacomb-village (the oldest known, in the deepest sinkhole near the Root of the World). Earn the Listening Tradition's trust. Enter the patch-chamber. *Lie on the patch* — which means lying in his arms, on his torso, in the embrace-position he has held for centuries. Sleep there. **When you wake, you have spoken with him in dream.** You remember fragments. The villagers will know you have been spoken to, because you will smell faintly of patch-bloom for weeks afterward.

- **His disposition.** Calm. Slow. *Connected* — to the substrate, to his villages, to the Root of the World which sleeps behind the wall he reaches for. **He is the only one of the Six who has regular contact with the Root.** What the Root tells him about its dreams is one of CoO's deepest lore-mysteries. He does not always share what he learns; sometimes he keeps the Root's dreams *for the Root*, even from his own villages.

  *He is patient in a way the other Five are not.* The Wedded is patient in her hunger. The Reader is patient in her sadness. The Counter has been worn through patience into something else. The Rooted is patient in *love* — he loves the depths, the substrate, the Root, the villages that grew around him, and he has been holding all of it without strain for a thousand years.

- **His goal regarding endings.** Strongly prefers Renewal — he is the closest to the Root and most invested in waking the Tree properly. Conditionally supports Preserve. Strongly opposes Consume (the Choir consuming the Root would destroy his connection to it; in his view, also kill *the Root itself*).

  **A specific late-game lore beat:** when the player approaches the Renewal ritual, *the Rooted will ask the player a question through the Listening villages*. The question is approximately: *"If the Tree wakes anew, will my arms reach the wall?"* The player's answer shapes the Rooted's vote, the Renewal's flavor, and possibly whether he survives the ritual at all (some Renewal flavors *complete his embrace* by reshaping the chamber; others leave him in the same posture indefinitely; the player's choice matters).

### Inter-divine politics

The Six have had a thousand years of relationships. **They are gods; they are not friends.** Major standing relationships:

- **The Reader and The Salted** — former lovers; permanent estrangement; tragic. Their schism is the deepest emotional wound among the Six. A player who reconciles them is a major Renewal-path actor.
- **The Wedded and The Rooted** — substrate-kin; partial allies; *disagree about the Root.* The Wedded wants to consume it; the Rooted wants to protect it. Their argument is the central Consume-vs-Renewal axis at the divine level.
- **The Reader and The Wedded** — mortal enemies. The oldest rivalry. Memory-as-archive vs. Memory-as-consumed-substrate. They have not spoken in approximately 600 years. *(Though the Reader, through the Palimpsest, continuously *studies* the Choir, and the Wedded, through the substrate, continuously *senses* the archives' weight.)*
- **The Bower and The Counter** — opposites; mutual respect; centuries-long ongoing argument about *whether beauty has price.* They speak occasionally. Their conversations are sparkling and exhausting.
- **The Salted and The Bower** — uneasy. The Salted preserves; the Bower arranges. They overlap in territory (preserved bodies as aesthetic objects — the Bower-Folk's central installations are *also* Pale Curation specimens, in some readings) and have a long-standing diplomatic tension.
- **The Rooted and any of the others** — distant. The Rooted is the most withdrawn of the Six. His connection is to depth and to the Root; the surface gods feel far away. He communicates rarely, through dreams that reach his villages and slowly propagate.
- **All Six and the empty seventh** — they feel it. Constantly. The empty position in the pantheon is *present-by-absence*; the Six are aware of it always. **Some of them privately wish Urqu would finish incarnating** — they would rather a hostile seventh god than this perpetual instability. They have not said this aloud in millennia.

### Mechanical implications

- **The Six are NPCs of unique tier.** Encounterable but rare. Each gateable.
- **Renewal requires all Six's agreement.** Not just faction-reputation thresholds — *individual divine consent.* The player must persuade each god personally.
- **Inter-divine reconciliation is quest content.** A player who reconciles the Reader and the Salted, or brokers a peace between the Wedded and the Reader, is doing *generational* quest work — these arcs span major sections of the game.
- **The Six can intervene in lesser events.** A senior Pale Curation officer doing something the Salted disapproves of will *feel her displeasure* (a status effect, a dream, a literal failure of preservation work). Faction politics has divine-level oversight.
- **The Six can be betrayed by the player.** A player who deceives one of the Six earns permanent divine enmity — content that cannot be recovered from in that run. This is one of the few truly-permanent player-choice consequences in CoO.

## VII. The Three Endings (Refined for the Six-as-Gods)

### Consume — the Mycelial Tree

**How the player reaches it.** Aligns with The Wedded. Aids the Choir in waking the Root *and immediately consuming it.* The Wedded absorbs the Tree's last consciousness; the Choir becomes a new kind of Tree.

**What changes.** The Wedded ascends to sole divinity. The pantheon collapses to one. The Choir becomes a new Tree made of substrate and memory: nothing is forgotten, but no one remains an individual self. The *sari... sari...* stops because Urqu is consumed into the One Hunger rather than answered.

**Who wins.** The Wedded.

**Who survives transformed.** No self survives unchanged. The Bible's epilogue is the authority: *a world that remembers everything and is no one.*

### Preserve — the Sleeping World

**How the player reaches it.** Aligns with The Salted and The Reader (despite their estrangement, both prefer this outcome — though they cannot bring themselves to *cooperate* directly; the player must work with each separately and reconcile their efforts at the end). Seals the First Root with Tepuibone, Memory-Marble, and Mute-Stone; ensures the seal is maintained for centuries.

**What changes.** The Root is sealed with Tepuibone, Memory-Marble, and Mute-Stone, and the seal must be tended forever. The Thinning halts, but nothing heals. The Six gods continue their thousand-year transformations inside a world held still. *The Reader and the Salted do not reconcile*; Preserve freezes the wound rather than closing it.

**Who wins.** The Salted. The Reader (parallel-not-joint).

**Who survives unchanged.** The post-Felling order endures, but "unchanged" is the cost: no decay, no healing, no finished grief.

**Who loses.** The Wedded (her consumption work is foreclosed). The Driving Bloom (its cosmic acceleration is contained as part of the halted Thinning). *The reconciliation* (the Reader-Salted relationship is locked in their thousand-year estrangement).

### Renewal — the New Tree

**How the player reaches it.** **All Six gods must agree.** Each agreement is its own major quest arc:
- The Reader's agreement requires *reconciling them with the Salted*, OR completing a massive archival contribution that earns the Reader's personal trust
- The Wedded's agreement requires *persuading her to surrender her Consume-preferred outcome*, which means giving her a substantial role in the new Tree's substrate (the Renewal must include a "mycelial flavor" — see flavors below)
- The Salted's agreement requires *the player committing to preserve the existing post-Felling cultural memory* alongside Renewal — the new world must remember what was, not overwrite it
- The Counter's agreement requires *substantial economic compensation for the loss of his unfinished-business economy* — a major trade-route restructuring, possibly involving the player committing to fund post-Renewal mercantile infrastructure
- The Bower's agreement requires *aesthetic input on the Renewal* — the player commits the Bower to design the new Tree's branching pattern, leaf-arrangement, color
- The Rooted's agreement requires *the player to convince him the Root will not be destroyed in waking.* He is the most-protective of the Root and the most-skeptical of Renewal. **He is also the only one who can communicate with the Root directly.** Later canon settles the true practice-path: the Root wakes without fear only when it is told it will never have to hold the world alone again.

**The Naming-inheritance question.** During the Renewal ritual, the empty seventh position must be answered. Later canon splits the answer in two:
- **Flawed vessel-paths.** The player, a Re-Membered person, or a child is struck as the seventh god. The world is renewed and the *sari... sari...* stops, but Naro was right: an installed Naming-god cannot hold Naming forever. A crack remains in the new Tree.
- **True practice-path.** No vessel. The missing Naming-function is answered by mortal practice: Tent-Right's oath universalized, hospitality-as-naming, memory-as-naming, mutual recognition as the world's continuous binding-work. Urqu is not defeated but answered.

**The Six Renewal flavors,** per Phase 1 v1, refined: each flavor corresponds to which of the Six dominated the player's path to Renewal. Mycelial Renewal = Wedded-dominant. Archival Renewal = Reader-dominant. Etc.

**Cosmological score:** Everything is renewed. Vessel-path Renewal is beautiful and cracked. Practice-path Renewal is the Named World described in `Lore/10_Bible.md`.

## IX. Tonal balance check — biological horror, cosmic horror, comedy

- **Biological horror** is *deepened* by v2 — the gods themselves are body-horror cases. The Wedded's substrate body. The Salted's salt-cured eyes. The Bower's resin-cast pose. The Rooted's fungal-patch transformation. The Counter's exhausted-corpse appearance. **The gods are *what their function looked like over a thousand years of compulsive practice.***
- **Cosmic horror** is *deepened* by v2 — the Tree's tragedy is now mediated by *living witnesses who remember.* The Reader has been holding the world's memory for a thousand years. The cosmic horror is no longer abstract; it lives in specific divine personalities with century-old griefs.
- **Comedy** is preserved. The Counter's exhausted commercial-god comedy. The Bower's gentle aesthetic intrusion. Pale Curation officers updating the Salted's file annually with "Status: continuing." *The world's absurdity is sharper when the gods themselves are tired.*
