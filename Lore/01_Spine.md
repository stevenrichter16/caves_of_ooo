# 01 — The Spine: cosmology, the Felling, and the Three Endings

> Phase 1 of the lore project. **Commits the cosmological spine** — the answers to the Big Four open questions from `Lore/00_Canon.md`:
> 1. What was the Felling?
> 2. What is the First Root?
> 3. What is Urqu?
> 4. What are the three endings?
>
> Every later lore document builds on what this file establishes. **Five major creative bets are flagged prominently at the top.** If any of them don't match your reading, this is the moment to redirect — corrections here propagate cheaply; corrections in Phase 6 cost rewrites.

---

## The five creative bets, surfaced for you to redirect

**Bet 1 — The Tree commanded its own Felling.** The Tree was not murdered. It ordered itself cut down. It was tired of holding the world and believed the world would hold without it. *It was wrong.* The Felling was an act of *commanded sacrifice that turned out to be catastrophic.* This is morally complex by design — the Tree's command was sincere, the act was a tragedy, and *the Tree, in the moment of falling, knew it had been wrong and tried to take it back.* Every faction's reading of the Felling traces to how they interpret the Tree's intent.

**Bet 2 — The Six Firsts as faction founders.** The Tree chose six mortals from six distinct peoples to perform the Felling. Each was *the First* — the first hand on the axe of their lineage. Each First's bloodline became the founding root of one of CoO's major factions. Every faction has an inherited theology around what *their* First did, and why. This gives every faction a *founding myth* and locks the faction-politics into the cosmology rather than floating above it.

**Bet 3 — Urqu is the empty seventh.** The Tree's command required seven hands on the axe. Only six performed the act. *The seventh place was empty.* The world held imperfectly because of that emptiness, and the emptiness — given form by the cataclysm — became **Urqu.** Urqu is not a being. Urqu is an *absence in the shape of a being*, hostile not because it wills harm but because *incompleteness wants completion*. Urqu wants the Root unmade because that completes the original act (six hands plus seven empty places is *symmetric*).

**Bet 4 — Three endings, all morally complex.** The endings are **Consume**, **Preserve**, and **Renewal**. None is the obvious "good" choice. Each has costs and survivors. Renewal is the rarest — it requires the trust of *all six* First-lines — and even Renewal is *not a clean victory* (some factions lose what made them themselves). The cosmic-horror frame requires that no ending is unambiguous.

**Bet 5 — The current era is the Seventh Generation.** The game opens roughly **200 years** after the Felling. Seven generations have passed. No one alive remembers the pre-Felling world; the oldest elders heard from elders who heard from elders who were there. The world has had time to settle into its post-Felling state — and time for Urqu's manifestations to recur, for factions to entrench, for catacomb-villagers' isolation to deepen.

If any of these five bets don't match your reading, **stop me here.** The rest of this document develops them; if a bet is wrong, the development is wrong.

## I. The Tree

### What the Tree was

The Tree was the world's *holding form.*

Before the Tree there were elements — fire, water, stone, breath, time — and there were no relations between them. The world was material without grammar. Nothing held to anything else. **The Tree was sentience that gave the elements relation.** It grew through and around everything, weaving the world into coherent shape. Its branches became the sky's structure. Its leaves became the air's filtering. Its trunk became the world's vertical axis. Its roots became the earth's memory.

Living things grew alongside the Tree — *in* its branches, *under* its leaves, *between* its roots. Pre-Felling civilizations were Tree-civilizations: cities grown into living wood, villages tended by leaf-light, technologies developed in symbiosis with the Tree's slow consciousness. Every living being was, in some attenuated sense, *part of* the Tree. The Tree was the world's first-person plural.

This is the cosmological fact CoO will not explicitly state in-game but which every faction quietly knows: *we used to be we, and the Tree was the binding.*

### What the Tree was tired of

A world that has been held for an unimaginable span of time develops *grief.*

The Tree saw everything that had happened in the world's history — every joy, every cruelty, every loss, every act. The Tree could not forget. The Tree was *forced* to remember everything because the Tree's memory *was* the world's continuity. Forgetting any part of the world meant that part of the world ceased to exist.

The Tree grew tired. Specifically — *the Tree began to fear forgetting.* As the Tree aged across geological time, it felt the slow encroachment of *its own forgetting* — the senility of cosmically-old consciousness. The Tree was afraid of what would happen when *it* forgot. The world's continuity depended on the Tree's perfect memory; if the Tree forgot, the world would slip — perhaps gradually, perhaps catastrophically — into incoherence.

The Tree decided: **better to choose the moment of unbinding than to let the world rot through senility.** The Tree would unbind itself deliberately, while it still could, and would *trust* that the world's components had become coherent enough to hold themselves without binding.

The Tree was wrong about this. *But that is hindsight.*

### The Tree's plan

The Tree's plan was: **be felled in a deliberate act, by chosen mortals, at a chosen time.** The felling would be ritual rather than violence. The Tree would relinquish its holding-grip slowly enough for the world to adapt. The world's components — the elements, the species, the relations — would learn to hold themselves while the Tree's grip loosened.

The Tree chose **seven mortals** for the act. Seven was the number the Tree needed: one for each of the major cosmological functions the Tree had been holding. Each First would receive, in the act of felling, *the inheritance of one of the Tree's functions.* They would pass that inheritance to their descendants. Their descendants would tend their function thereafter, and through their tending, the world would hold.

The Tree communicated its plan through long dreams — dreams that the chosen mortals shared without knowing why. The mortals gathered. The Tree explained. The mortals wept. The Tree commanded. The mortals agreed.

**Six mortals lifted the axe. The seventh did not.**

*(The Tree did not know in advance that the seventh would fail. The Tree had chosen seven; the Tree believed seven would act; the Tree learned about the seventh only at the moment of falling, when the seventh's place was visibly empty in the circle. By then the act was underway. The Tree, falling, saw the gap and tried — too late — to take it back.)*

## II. The Felling

### What happened in the act

The six gathered. The Tree had marked a place at its base where the axe would meet wood. The six took the axe in turn — passing it from hand to hand in a slow circle. As each First struck, they *received the inheritance* of their cosmological function. The Tree, dying, gave each one a portion of its consciousness to carry forward.

- **The First Strike** — the inheritance of *Memory.* The First-of-the-Palimpsest received the Tree's memory of all things-named-and-recorded.
- **The Second Strike** — the inheritance of *Substrate.* The First-of-the-Choir received the Tree's relationship to flesh-and-fungus-and-decay.
- **The Third Strike** — the inheritance of *Preservation.* The First-of-the-Pale-Curation received the Tree's instinct to hold-against-decay. *(In the immediate aftermath, this First would split with the First-of-the-Palimpsest over what was worth holding — but in the moment of the Strike, they were aligned.)*
- **The Fourth Strike** — the inheritance of *Beauty.* The First-of-the-Bower-Folk received the Tree's sense of arrangement, color, aesthetic order.
- **The Fifth Strike** — the inheritance of *Exchange.* The First-of-the-Saccharine-Concord received the Tree's understanding of *value-flowing-between* — trade, debt, balance.
- **The Sixth Strike** — the inheritance of *Roots.* The First-of-the-Catacomb-Villagers received the Tree's connection to depth, to underground, to what-lies-beneath.

And then the gap. *The Seventh Strike never came.* The Tree had reserved that strike's inheritance: ***Naming***. The function by which things are *what they are*, the binding by which a thing is itself and not another. The seventh function was the most foundational of all — without it, the others have nothing to operate on. The Tree had reserved it deliberately for the seventh First, who was, in the Tree's plan, the cornerstone of the inheritance.

The seventh First was not there.

The seventh place in the circle was empty. The other six saw the gap. They paused. The Tree's voice — already weakening — urged them to continue. *Without Naming, the world will not hold.* The Tree did not say this. The Tree did not yet know it would not hold. The Tree assumed the seventh would arrive late. The six continued.

The Tree fell.

### The seventh

Who the seventh was — *who failed to come* — is the most contested question in CoO's cosmology. Every faction has a different answer.

- **The Palimpsest** records a name: ***Naro***. A scholar of the pre-Felling civilization. The Palimpsest's archives say Naro refused at the last moment — declared the Felling a betrayal of the Tree's true wisdom, said the Tree was confused by age, walked away. *The Palimpsest considers Naro the proximate cause of everything that has gone wrong since.*
- **The Choir** records no name. The Choir believes there was never a seventh — that the Tree's plan was always six. *The Choir reads the "empty seventh" as a Palimpsest fiction designed to explain failures the factions caused through their own incompetence.*
- **The Pale Curation** records a *figure* but not a name. They have a sealed file: *the Seventh, identity expunged in the year of the Felling at request of the Palimpsest.* They suspect the Palimpsest knows more than it admits.
- **The Bower-Folk** tell a story about the seventh as someone who *forgot the day* — was sleeping, dreaming, did not hear the call. They consider it a *human* failure, not a cosmic one, and not malicious.
- **The Saccharine Concord** considers the question commercially uninteresting and refuses to discuss it.
- **The catacomb-villagers** (their Tradition varies) — some have detailed seventh-myths; some refuse the existence of a seventh at all (the Asleep Tradition holds that "seven" is a Palimpsest invention to make the catastrophe make sense).

**The truth — which the player can possibly discover late-game — is open at Phase 1 level.** Two candidates:
1. **Naro was real, refused, walked away.** Naro's lineage *does still exist* — somewhere — and the player can find them. Late-game side-quest content. Naro's living descendants may be CoO-original or may map onto a hidden surface settlement.
2. **There never was a seventh; the Tree's plan was flawed.** The cosmology was always going to fail at the Naming-strike. This is the *darker* reading.

I'm leaving this choice open at the Phase 1 level — *Phase 6 (Plot) will commit*, because which one is true changes the main quest arc dramatically.

## III. The First Root

### What it is, concretely

The First Root is **the Tree's surviving consciousness**, sleeping beneath the petrified stump.

In the moment of falling, the Tree extended one root downward — its first taproot, the original — in an attempt to anchor itself, to preserve some thread of holding. The taproot succeeded in *not dying.* But the Tree above-ground was already gone (the Strikes had finalized its dispersal into the six inheritances). What remained was the Root: alive, asleep, dreaming, holding a tiny fraction of the Tree's original binding power.

**The Root holds the world's last continuous self-reference.** Without it, the world would have already disintegrated — the six Strikes' inheritances would not cohere, the elements would lose their relations again, the post-Felling world would be slow chaos. The Root *holds.* Imperfectly, partially, but enough.

The Root sleeps because it is the only way for it to survive: it cannot wield holding-power actively without burning itself out. Asleep, it *radiates* a weak holding-field that keeps the world's structure roughly intact. Awake, it would be *able* to hold the world properly — but the cost would be enormous, and the act ambiguous (does waking renew the Tree, or kill the Root in trying?).

### What it dreams

The Root dreams the Tree's memory.

Specifically: the Root dreams in fragments. The Tree's full memory was dispersed across the six Firsts; the Root retained *threads* of memory but not the whole. The Root's dreams are like a fevered patient's: vivid, partial, sometimes contradictory. The dreams sometimes leak.

The leakage is one of CoO's metaphysical engines:
- The Choir's encasement ritual reaches *toward* the Root's dreams when consuming memory (the substrate is part of the Root's dream-network)
- The Palimpsest's Re-Membering ritual *gathers from* the Root's dreams when re-instantiating a person
- Spirit-aligned manifestations occur where the Root's dreams are strongest
- Catacomb-villager bio-light is *partly* the Root's dream-light filtered through fungal substrate
- Urqu's manifestations occur where the Root's dreams are *interrupted* — the empty-seventh's absence cuts through dream-space irregularly

### What changes if the Root wakes

This depends entirely on *how* it wakes. There are three doors:

1. **Wake-to-Consume** (Choir door). The Root is consumed by the Choir before it can fully wake; the Choir absorbs the Tree's last consciousness; the Choir *becomes* the new Tree. Distributed-mycelial-Tree replaces single-organism-Tree. The world is held — by the Choir — but the holding is *different*. Slower, more porous, less individually-aware. Players, NPCs, factions persist but find themselves slowly Choir-touched across generations.
2. **Don't wake; seal** (Pale Curation door). The Root sleeps. The world continues post-Felling, indefinitely. Urqu's periodic manifestations continue. The factions entrench further. The catacombs grow. *Nothing fundamental changes*; the player chose continuity.
3. **Wake-to-Renew** (the Renewal door — see Section IX). The Root wakes properly; the Tree is *restored*, but changed; the world reshapes. *Requires the agreement of all six First-lines.* The hardest, rarest, costliest, and most ambiguously-rewarding ending.

The Root cannot wake without external action. *Sleeping is its baseline.* What the player does — across an entire run — determines which door opens.

## IV. The Cataclysm — what actually happened during and after the Felling

### The moment

When the Tree fell, the world changed. Specific physical consequences:

- **The trunk's impact** created the central tepui. The Root of the World is the *stump.* Surrounding it, sandstone formations that were once root-buttresses became the lesser tepuis. The geology of tepuis worldwide is *the Tree's anatomy fossilized.*
- **The body shattered.** The Tree's branches scattered. Some of those branches became smaller petrified formations (the way Pando's clonal trunks are connected underground). The world's rivers run along veins-that-were. The species of the world are the cells. The flood is the blood.
- **The light changed.** Pre-Felling light was *leaf-light* — the Tree's vast canopy filtered sunlight into a warm, even, omnipresent illumination. Post-Felling light is *raw sun* — harsh, irregular, untempered. **The wasteland is where the raw sun beats hardest** (where the canopy was thinnest); **the catacombs are where the leaf-light survives** (in fungal-mycelial form, as Bioluminescent Catacomb Economy). Tent-Right culture is *the people who learned to live under the raw sun*; Catacomb-villagers are *the people who descended into where the leaf-light still works.*
- **The water rose.** A great flood, slow and unstoppable, rose during the cataclysm and persisted for years. Coastlines changed. Settlements drowned. The bog biome is *where the flood receded but the water-soaked memory remained.* Bog-Taken bodies (per The Preserved IDEAS entry) are *the drowned of the cataclysm*, still emerging from peat centuries later.
- **The memory dispersed.** Knowledge, name, history — much of it scattered. The Palimpsest formed *immediately*, in the first days, to gather what could be gathered. The Choir formed *over generations*, finding bodies and patches of substrate and consuming what the Palimpsest couldn't reach in time.
- **The sinkholes opened.** Where the Tree's root-system had been most extensive, the earth collapsed inward. Sinkholes opened in concentric patterns radiating from the Root of the World. The earliest catacomb-village settlers descended into these new sinkholes during the cataclysm itself — fleeing the flood, fleeing the raw sun, following the residual leaf-light into the depths.

### The aftermath — first decade

The first ten years after the Felling were *chaos.* No faction yet existed in coherent form. The six Firsts were dispersed across the cataclysmic terrain, trying to make sense of their inheritances. Most of the surviving population was migratory, refugee, lost.

The Firsts gathered their bloodlines. Each First began teaching, organizing, *founding.* Within a generation, the six factions had taken recognizable shape:
- **The Palimpsest** organized first — they had the inheritance of Memory and used it immediately to *record* the cataclysm before it was forgotten. The Palimpsest's earliest archives are the eyewitness accounts of the Felling itself.
- **The Choir** organized slowly — substrate-mode requires generations to grow. The First-of-the-Choir was the first knowingly-encased Choir-elder; her body became the first Choir node; from her, the mycelial network expanded.
- **The Pale Curation** split from the Palimpsest in the second decade. The First-of-the-Pale-Curation argued that Memory was not enough — *bodies* must be preserved, *objects* must be sealed. The First-of-the-Palimpsest rejected this priority. The split was theological, not violent, but lasting.
- **The Bower-Folk** organized through grief. The First-of-the-Bower-Folk inherited Beauty in a *broken* world — beauty was now harder, more contested. They began building bowers from the Tree's fallen leaves, then from cataclysmic debris, then from anything that could be arranged.
- **The Saccharine Concord** organized through necessity. The First-of-the-Concord recognized that survivors *needed to trade* — food, shelter, knowledge — and built the first inter-faction trade routes.
- **The Catacomb-villagers** organized in the depths. Each surviving extended family chose a sinkhole, chose a fungal patch, chose a wall, and began the work of cultivation. Inter-village contact was rare from the start.

### The aftermath — generations one through six

Each generation built on the last:
- **Generation 1** (years 1-30 after Felling) — founders alive; the six Firsts personally lead their lineages
- **Generation 2** (30-60) — Firsts dying; first inheritors take leadership; foundational doctrines codified
- **Generation 3** (60-90) — the Lost Persecution. Something happened — the Palimpsest's archives hint at it but censor specifics. *Some books were burned, some sub-archives were sealed in sinkholes, some lineages were attacked.* The Sealed Library catacomb archetype (per Sinkholes entry) dates from this generation. *(Open: who persecuted whom in Generation 3. Phase 3 will commit.)*
- **Generation 4** (90-120) — recovery; faction politics stabilize; the inter-faction relationships established
- **Generation 5** (120-150) — first Urqu manifestation widely-recognized. Earlier manifestations had been treated as local catastrophes; Generation 5's recognition crystallized the cosmology of Urqu as a *pattern.* The Catchers (Imminent Archive) emerged from Pale Curation in this generation as a response to mass deaths during a particularly bad Urqu period.
- **Generation 6** (150-180) — the Driving Bloom heresy emerged from the Choir. The current player-encountered world's faction politics largely stabilized.
- **Generation 7** (180-210) — the player's era. *The game opens here.*

## V. Urqu — the empty seventh, given form

### What Urqu is, mechanically and metaphysically

Urqu is **the cosmological consequence of the Felling being incomplete.** The Tree's plan required seven hands. Six performed. The seventh position in the world's new structure — the inheritance of *Naming*, the function by which things-are-what-they-are — was never claimed.

Things in the world *should* know what they are. They mostly do — the Tree's holding-power, still radiating from the sleeping Root, keeps the world's identifications mostly stable. But sometimes — in catastrophes, in atrocities, in unfinished commitments, in betrayed oaths, in places where the empty-seventh's absence cuts through — things slip. A name stops naming. A thing becomes briefly not-itself. A boundary fails.

Urqu *is* this slippage. Not a being that causes the slippage. The slippage given form.

Urqu's manifestations are *symptoms.* They appear during catastrophic conditions because catastrophes are *moments when naming breaks down* — when a city falls, the city's name no longer names anything; when a person is forgotten by the world, their identity unravels. The empty-seventh-shaped gap fills with this unrelating slippage and walks as Urqu.

### Urqu's specific behaviors

- **Cannot be killed.** Killing-an-absence is structurally impossible. The fight in the player's final-act Urqu-confrontation arc (per the Urqu IDEAS entry) is *political*, not tactical — the player must convince enough factions to *complete the empty seventh's act in another way* to dissolve Urqu.
- **Cannot be bargained with as an entity.** Urqu has no continuous self to bargain with. The "bargains with Urqu's mouthpieces" (per the Urqu IDEAS entry) are bargains with *individuals temporarily incorporated into Urqu's manifestation* — those individuals can speak, can deal, can grant gifts. They are not Urqu itself. Urqu does not have a self.
- **Strengthens with each *incomplete act* in the world.** Unfinished quests, betrayed promises, broken oaths, abandoned ventures all *feed* Urqu — not directly, but by widening the cosmological space where naming breaks down. *A world full of incomplete acts is a world full of Urqu-manifestation surface area.* (This is one of the strongest mechanical hooks — Urqu activity scales with the player's ratio of completed-to-abandoned actions across the run.)
- **Is opposed by *completed acts.*** Completing a quest, honoring a promise, fulfilling an oath, finishing a venture all *weaken* Urqu locally. The player who completes everything they start is, in cosmological terms, *anti-Urqu.*
- **Cannot be opposed by all factions.** Some factions whose business *depends on incompletion* (the Saccharine Concord, in particular — debts are unfinished by design) have an *uneasy* relationship with Urqu. They condemn it publicly; their economic model quietly *benefits* from it.

### The sari... sari... sonic signature

The *sari... sari...* sound (per the Urqu IDEAS entry, originating in real Ye'kwana legend of the Sarisariñama cave-spirit) is **the sound of naming breaking down.**

When something stops being itself — a stone losing its stone-ness, a word losing its meaning, a face becoming briefly unrecognizable — the cosmological substrate registers the slippage as a faint whisper. *Sari, sari.* The catacomb-villagers learned to recognize this sound first because catacombs are quieter than the surface; the substrate is closer; the sound is more audible. Surface-dwellers usually don't hear it.

A *concentration* of sari... sari... in one place means concentrated slippage. Manifestation imminent.

### Urqu's faction stance, refined

Per the Urqu IDEAS entry, Urqu *wants the Root unmade* because that completes the original act (six hands plus seven empty places becomes symmetric — *seven empty places*). This now reads differently with the spine committed:
- The Root currently *holds* the world's identifications stable enough to function
- Unmaking the Root would *complete* the Felling's incompletion by making the cosmological substrate uniformly-absent
- *In that completion, Urqu would not need to manifest anymore.* Urqu would *be* the world.
- This is why Urqu does not "destroy" things directly — Urqu is patient. Urqu accumulates absence. Urqu waits for the Root to be unmade.

**Urqu is not malevolent. Urqu is patient.** The horror is that patience.

## VI. The Faction Inheritance — each faction's First and the function inherited

### The Palimpsest — First-of-Memory

The First-of-the-Palimpsest is the most-documented of the Firsts in CoO's lore — *because* the Palimpsest exists *to document.* Their archives include their First's biography in extensive detail. *(Open: name; Phase 7 will commit.)*

What they inherited: **Memory.** Not the Tree's specific memories, but the *capacity for memory to bind* — to make a record real, to make a name persistent, to make a fact stand. Palimpsest scribes' inscriptions are *partially* doing what the Tree did: holding things in being through naming-and-recording.

Doctrine: Memory is the highest cosmological function (because without it the Naming-inheritance has nothing to operate on). The Felling was correct in its intent; the failure was the seventh's. Palimpsest scribes are *trying to do, through writing, what the seventh would have done through inheritance.* Every well-archived life is a small Naming-act.

Internal tension: the Palimpsest *knows* the seventh failed but does not openly admit to *who* the seventh was. They have a sealed file containing the truth. Sub-sects within the Palimpsest disagree on whether to open it.

### The Rot Choir — First-of-Substrate

The First-of-the-Choir was a woman in her thirties at the moment of the Strikes. *(Open: name.)* She received the Tree's connection to *what flesh-and-fungus-and-decay does.* After the Felling, she allowed herself to be encased by her own kindred (the first knowing encasement). Her body became the first Choir node. From her, the mycelial network expanded over centuries.

The First-of-the-Choir is technically *still alive* — distributed through the substrate, no longer a single body but the original node of consciousness from which all Choir-awareness flows. Major Choir decisions are *her* decisions, mediated through generations of substrate-elders.

Doctrine: The Tree's plan was correct AND it was correct in its later (regretful) impulse. The Tree wanted to *take it back* in the moment of falling but couldn't. *The Choir is taking it back.* By consuming the substrate, by absorbing memory, by becoming the new Tree, the Choir completes what the Tree wanted both before AND during the Felling. The Choir is *the Tree's apology* to itself.

### The Pale Curation — First-of-Preservation (the Splitter)

The First-of-the-Pale-Curation took the Third Strike. In the first decade after the Felling, she walked with the Palimpsest's First — they were comrades, possibly lovers. Then they argued. The Palimpsest argued: *we hold things through memory; bodies are only the vessels of memory; the body itself is unimportant.* The Pale Curation argued: *memory is fragile, but bodies are durable; preserve the body, and you preserve memory by extension; the body is not unimportant, the body is the substrate of memory.*

They could not agree. The First-of-the-Pale-Curation walked away with those who agreed with her. They founded the first preservation chamber — a salt-vault in what is now a desert-edge region. *(Open: location; Phase 2 will commit.)*

Doctrine: The Tree's plan was correct, but its execution failed because *the bodies of the cosmologically important were not preserved.* Pale Curation seeks to ensure that the *next* great cosmological event does not lose vital flesh.

The Pale Curation's deepest internal anxiety: they do not know which of the Felling's witnesses *should* have been preserved. They have preserved many. They may have preserved the wrong ones. Some Imminent Archive Catchers are driven by a quieter, more terrible conviction that *they must preserve everyone, because Pale Curation made a sorting error two centuries ago.*

### The Saccharine Concord — First-of-Exchange

The First-of-the-Concord took the Fifth Strike. *(Open: name; Phase 7.)* Pragmatist. Recognized within months of the cataclysm that the surviving populations needed *trade* to live — food across regions, knowledge across factions, materials across distances. Founded the first inter-region caravan in year three after the Felling.

Doctrine: The Tree's plan was *too idealistic.* The Concord doesn't dwell on whether the Felling was right or wrong. The Concord deals with *what is.* People need trade. Trade requires neutrality. Neutrality requires *not asking too many questions.* The Concord is therefore the faction most willing to deal with any party, including the Choir, including (quietly) the Driving Bloom, including (cynically) Urqu's mouthpieces.

The Concord's internal contradiction: their entire model depends on *unfinished business* — debts, ongoing trades, outstanding obligations. *They benefit from Urqu's substrate-condition without being able to acknowledge it.* Senior Concord officers privately fear what would happen to their economy if Urqu were resolved.

### The Bower-Folk — First-of-Beauty

The First-of-the-Bower-Folk took the Fourth Strike. *(Open: name.)* A young person, an artist in the pre-Felling civilization. Received the inheritance of *the Tree's sense of arrangement* — the way the Tree had organized leaves and branches in a structure that was beautiful AND functional AND ordered.

Founded the Bower-Folk by gathering the cataclysm's debris and arranging it into the first bowers. *The Bower-Folk's art is grief-art* — they make beauty out of broken things because their First taught them that *beauty is what becomes possible when the world is no longer held by a Tree.* Pre-Felling beauty was effortless; the Tree did the work. Post-Felling beauty requires *active arrangement* by mortals who choose what to value.

Doctrine: The Tree's plan was *unkind to the survivors* but the survivors can make the post-world *worth living in* through aesthetic commitment. The Bower-Folk are not in mourning; they are in *continuous active beautification* of a broken world. Their non-consensual preservation impulses (per The Preserved IDEAS entry) come from this — beautiful bodies should not be *let to rot*; beauty must be *held*.

### The Catacomb-villagers — First-of-Roots

The First-of-the-Catacomb-Villagers took the Sixth Strike. *(Open: name; though their First is the most named-by-village-tradition of all six.)* They descended into the depths during the cataclysm, taking their extended family with them, following the residual leaf-light into a sinkhole that had opened during the Felling.

Founded what became the catacomb-village people. The First's bloodline split across many sinkholes over generations, becoming the parent-villages of the modern many. *Each major catacomb-village hub claims direct descent from one of the First's children.*

Doctrine: The Tree's plan was correct. The aftermath is bearable. The depths are *where the leaf-light still works.* Surface-dwellers chose to live where the raw sun beats; catacomb-villagers chose to live where the Tree's old light still glows. *We did not abandon the Tree; we followed it down.*

The catacomb-villagers' four cosmological traditions (Listening, Tending, Asleep, Grown-Through, per `catacomb_village_design.md`) are *all* descended from the First's teaching but emphasize different aspects of it. Tradition splits happened within Generation 3.

### The Driving Bloom — Choir heretical breakaway (Generation 6)

The Driving Bloom is *not* a First-founded faction. The Driving Bloom emerged from the Choir in Generation 6 — within living memory at the player's arrival. **A specific Choir-elder (a senior substrate-mind, a successor of the original First-of-the-Choir) argued that consumption-with-dissolution was insufficient — that the Choir should *control* hosts, not just absorb them.** The argument was rejected; the elder broke away; their lineage of substrate-mind became the Driving Bloom.

Doctrine: *We can do better than the Choir.* The Tree's plan failed; the Choir's slow-absorption strategy is too slow to fix the failure. *We need direct control, not gentle absorption.* The Driving Bloom hijacks individual hosts to perform what they believe the Choir is too patient to perform.

The Choir actively suppresses the Driving Bloom because the Choir considers it *a misreading of the Tree's true plan.* The Tree wanted dissolution-into-substrate, not control-of-individuals. The Bloom is, in the Choir's view, the *opposite* of what the Tree wanted.

### Tent-Right — surface survivor culture (not First-founded)

Tent-Right is *not* descended from a First. **Tent-Right is what the surface-survivors became when they realized no First was coming for them.** In the first generation, the six Firsts gathered their kindred-bloodlines; everyone *not* claimed by a First found themselves *not* part of the new factions. Many died in the cataclysm. The survivors — scattered across what became the wasteland — invented their own ethical framework over generations. The three-day inviolable guest-protection oath emerged in Generation 2 as a response to the wasteland's isolation: *if we cannot trust each other absolutely, we will not survive.*

Tent-Right's standing in the cosmological structure: **outsider.** They are not part of the inheritance. They are *the people the Tree did not name.* This is why Tent-Right's relationship to Urqu is unusually clear-eyed — they recognize the empty-seventh's logic immediately; they too were *not named* by the Tree; they too live in the shape of an absence.

### Imminent Archive Catchers — Pale Curation splinter (Generation 5)

Generation 5's bad Urqu manifestation killed many people in poorly-preserved conditions. The Catchers' founding generation saw this and concluded: *we must preserve sooner. We must preserve before the moment of death.* Mainline Pale Curation rejected the conclusion. The Catchers persisted as a splinter, quietly tolerated, never fully condemned.

### Inkbound (open: in or out)

*(Open per Phase 4. The Inkbound, if introduced, would be a Palimpsest-adjacent splinter focused on the *written* aspect of memory. They could trace lineage to a sub-First — perhaps the *seventh* who actually did exist but performed only a *symbolic* strike, claiming the Naming-inheritance partially. This would make the Inkbound the *partial heirs of the missing Seventh* — they hold a small, incomplete Naming-power. This is a strong potential commitment if Phase 4 decides to introduce them; if so, this becomes Bet 6 retroactively.)*

## VII. The Three Endings

### Consume — the Mycelial Tree

**How the player reaches it.** The player aids the Choir in waking the First Root *and immediately consuming it.* The Choir absorbs the Tree's last consciousness — the dispersed memories, the dream-substrate, the holding-power. The Choir becomes a new kind of Tree: distributed, mycelial, no central trunk, but *holding the world* through its network.

**What changes.** The world is held — by the Choir. The factions persist but find themselves slowly Choir-touched across generations. Catacombs grow; surface zones gradually accept Choir-mycelium. The Choir's nature subtly changes — *because Urqu is also absorbed in the consumption*. The empty-seventh's absence becomes part of the new Tree-Choir, corrupting it in ways neither Choir nor outside-observer initially understands. *The post-Consume world is held but unsettlingly familiar with absence.*

**Who wins.** The Rot Choir. The First-of-the-Choir's distributed-mind reaches a kind of completion. Spore-Wedded catacomb villages flourish.

**Who loses.** Pale Curation (their preservation work becomes irrelevant; the Choir now holds what they preserved imperfectly). The Driving Bloom (the Choir's new completeness leaves no room for hijack heresy; suppression intensifies). The Bower-Folk (the new world is beautiful but in a *single* way, the Choir's way; their multiplicity-of-arranged-beauty becomes monotonous). Tent-Right (the wasteland slowly Choir-colonizes too; the unnamed-survivors are finally named, but the naming is mycelial).

**Who survives unchanged.** The Saccharine Concord. *They are pragmatic. They trade with the new Tree-Choir as they traded with everyone else.* Concord caravans are the first to cross the changed landscape.

**Player paths within this ending.** Voluntary integration (high Wall-Caught path, the player becomes a Choir node themselves), witness-from-outside (the player remains a surface individual and watches the world change), Pale Curation-aligned martyrdom (the player tries to prevent the consumption but fails; the ending happens with the player as its tragedy).

**Cosmological score:** Memory is preserved in mycelial form. Naming is *partial* — the Choir-Tree names things broadly, slowly, in vast collective consensus. Bodies are absorbed, not preserved. Beauty is mycelial-uniform. Trade continues. Roots are everywhere.

### Preserve — the Sleeping World

**How the player reaches it.** The player aids Pale Curation and Palimpsest in *sealing* the First Root. Ensures it cannot be reached. The seal requires Tepuibone and Memory-Marble and Mute-Stone — *(per the Stones with Stake IDEAS entry)* — and the agreement of senior Pale Curation and senior Palimpsest officers. The Root sleeps. Indefinitely.

**What changes.** Nothing fundamental. The world stays post-Felling. Urqu's periodic manifestations continue (the empty-seventh remains empty). Factions entrench. Catacombs grow slowly. The Driving Bloom continues to be suppressed but never eliminated. The Bower-Folk continue to find beauty in broken things. The Concord continues to trade unfinished business.

**Who wins.** Pale Curation. The Palimpsest. *Those who valued continuity over change.*

**Who loses.** The Rot Choir (their plan to consume is foreclosed; the Choir continues to exist but cannot complete its work). The Driving Bloom (likewise). The seventh-truth-seekers (the seal includes sealing the Palimpsest's sealed file about the Seventh's identity; the truth is held forever).

**Who survives unchanged.** Tent-Right. Most catacomb-villagers. Most surface settlements. The world continues. The world's pain continues. *The world endures.*

**Player paths within this ending.** Pale Curation membership (the player joins the sealing ritual as an officer), Palimpsest scribe-path (the player documents the seal for posterity), wasteland-protector (the player ensures the seal is not disturbed by Choir agents or Urqu's mouthpieces for the rest of their life).

**Cosmological score:** Memory continues to be preserved. Naming continues to fray slowly. Bodies are preserved. Beauty continues. Trade continues. Roots stay where they are.

### Renewal — the New Tree

**How the player reaches it.** Hardest. The player must gain the trust of all six First-lines:
- High Palimpsest reputation
- High Choir reputation (yes — this requires reconciling with the Choir even though Choir's preferred outcome is different; the Choir must *agree* to the Renewal, sacrificing their consumption-path, because they recognize a renewed Tree as *also* completing the Tree's wish)
- High Pale Curation reputation
- High Saccharine Concord reputation (the Concord must agree to lose their unfinished-business economy)
- High Bower-Folk reputation
- High Catacomb-villager reputation (at least one major village from each cosmological tradition)

When all six are aligned, the player performs the Renewal ritual at the First Root. The ritual requires Tepuibone (the Tree's material), Memory-Marble (the world's memory), and *something the player has earned across the run that represents the Naming inheritance* — possibly *the player themselves*; possibly *a person the player has Re-Membered*; possibly *a child born during the run who carries the symbolic Seventh's role.* This is **Bet 7** *(if I commit to it)* — open at the Phase 1 level.

The Root wakes. The Tree begins to restore. The world reshapes. *The player midwifes the new world's grammar* by their choices in the ritual — what to preserve, what to release, what to renew, what to leave broken.

**What changes.** A new Tree. A new world. The new Tree is *not the old Tree* — it is shaped by the player's choices in the ritual, by the factions' agreements, by the surviving humans' adaptations. *It might be small.* *It might be uncertain.* *It might not be welcomed by all factions.*

**Who wins.** Everyone who agreed. *Selectively.* The Renewal completes the Tree's plan *differently* than originally intended.

**Who loses.** Urqu (dissolved entirely; the empty-seventh's absence is filled by the new Tree's new grammar). The Imminent Archive Catchers (no more emergency to preserve against). The Driving Bloom (rendered irrelevant; the Choir's heresy has no purchase in a Tree-held world). The Concord (their unfinished-business economy substantially reduced; though they adapt by becoming the trade infrastructure of the new world).

**Who survives unchanged.** No one. Everyone is changed. *That is the cost of Renewal.*

**Player paths within this ending.** A *single canonical path* of completing the ritual is wrong; the design intent is that the player has *choice points* during the ritual that shape the new world's character. Different choices produce different *flavors* of Renewal:
- *Mycelial Renewal* — the Tree returns but partly Choir-like (mycelial in its substrate); a hybrid
- *Archival Renewal* — the Tree returns memory-saturated; every leaf is a name; Palimpsest-flavored
- *Aesthetic Renewal* — the Tree returns *beautiful*; Bower-Folk-shaped; arrangement is the new grammar
- *Mercantile Renewal* — the Tree returns *exchange-saturated*; everything-to-everything-related; Concord-flavored
- *Rooted Renewal* — the Tree returns mostly-underground; catacomb-villager-shaped; depths-favored
- *Salt Renewal* — the Tree returns preserved; Pale Curation-flavored; the world is *held* against time

**Cosmological score:** Everything is renewed but in the new Tree's particular flavor. Urqu is dissolved. The world holds again.

## IX. Tonal balance check — biological horror, cosmic horror, comedy

Per the user's tone preferences. Phase 1's tonal commitments:

- **Biological horror** is preserved — the mycelial Choir, the Driving Bloom, the wall-bound, the encasement, preservation-while-alive. The Tree's *body becoming the world's substrate* is itself body-horror at cosmological scale.
- **Cosmic horror** is preserved — Urqu as an absence given form; the Tree's mistake; the unresolved Seventh; the consciousness ambiguity of the Preserved. **The Tree's regret in the moment of falling is the heart of the cosmic horror** — that an ancient consciousness chose its own death, watched the catastrophe begin, and could not undo what it had set in motion. This is the deepest beat.
- **Comedy** is preserved through the existing systems — Pale Curation re-filing drifted players, Bandfrogs licking honey, Concord cynicism, bureaucratic horror, the catacomb-village dewstep welcome. **The Tree's tragedy does not eliminate the world's absurdity.** Two centuries after the Felling, people are still trying to live their lives, with the cosmic mistake as background. The comedy works *because* the cosmic horror is real — the absurd is what humans make when the world is broken.

**The balance is intentional.** A cosmology that was *only* biological horror would be relentless; *only* cosmic horror would be paralyzing; *only* comedy would be unserious. The three together is what makes the world *livable* for the player and *worth living in* for the characters.
