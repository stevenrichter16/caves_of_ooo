## I. Founding

The Saccharine Concord is the **fifth-ascended faction** by Strike-order (the Fifth Strike delivered Exchange to the Counter) and the **third-organized** chronologically (~year 5, the first inter-region caravan — after the Palimpsest's year-1 archive and ahead of the slow-growing Choir).

### The Counter — the founding god

Per Phase 1 v2:

- **Then.** A pragmatist. Later canon names him **Tovreth**. His exact pre-Felling specialty remains a writing-detail seed; the strongest version is a keeper-of-accounts, a person who in their mortal life already tracked who-owed-what. Summoned by the Tree's dreams; took the **Fifth Strike**; **ascended into Exchange** — the Tree's understanding of *value-flowing-between*: trade, debt, balance, the resolution of accounts.
- **The first years.** The Counter understood his inheritance immediately and acted on it within five years — recognizing that the cataclysm's scattered survivors needed *trade* to live (food across drowned regions, knowledge across factions, materials across distances). The first inter-region caravan, year 5, is the Concord's founding event.
- **The thing that did not happen — the withdrawal.** The other five gods withdrew into their function across Gens 10-20 (per Phase 3). **The Counter did not withdraw.** He kept working. *His refusal to stop is his transformation.* He did not disperse into substrate (Wedded), into archives (Reader), into a salt-cured body (Salted), into a resin pose (Bower), into a fungal patch (Rooted). **He stayed a working god, in a body that increasingly looks like it should have stopped, and he never stopped.**
- **Now.** He runs the Concord *personally.* Every major contract requires his signature. He has not slept in ~900 years. He experiences time differently — he sees every exchange in the world simultaneously; he tracks every debt in his head with perfect precision. He drinks a black stimulant tea, brewed and re-brewed for centuries, that keeps him upright. **He looks like a Salt-Cured corpse who refused to admit he was dead** — and he is, in a real sense, the most *mortal-looking* of the Six precisely because he refused the transformations that made the others into archives and substrate and salt. He is a god wearing the worn-out body of the man he was.
- **His disposition.** Pragmatic. Cynical. *Catastrophically exhausted.* And — beneath that — **quietly horrified by his own knowledge** (see §II, the contradiction).

### The contradiction at the center of the Counter

This is the Counter's secret, and it is the deepest tragedy among the Six:

**The Counter's inheritance is Exchange — and Exchange, at its truest, is *resolution.* Value flowing between, and *balancing.* Debts owed, and *settled.* Accounts opened, and *closed.* The deepest form of the Counter's function is the world's ledger coming, finally, into balance.**

**But the world cannot balance.** The Tree held the world's accounts; the Tree is gone. The seventh inheritance (Naming) is missing; without it, the world's accounts have *no final terms* — every debt opens two more, every settlement leaves a remainder, the ledger never closes. Urqu (the unborn seventh, per Phase 1 v2) *is* the world's unbalanceable remainder given form.

**The Counter has spent ~900 years trying to balance the world's ledger, and it has never once balanced.** That is what exhausts him. Not the work-volume — the *futility.* He is the god of completion in a world structured around incompletion. His function craves resolution; his world cannot provide it; and he cannot stop trying, because stopping is not in his nature, because he refused to withdraw.

**And the horror within the horror:** the Concord — the institution he built — *profits from the imbalance.* The Concord's economy runs on unfinished business: outstanding debts, ongoing trades, open accounts, the friction of a world that never settles. *If the world's ledger ever balanced — Renewal, the seventh filled, Urqu dissolved, the accounts closed — the Concord's reason-for-being would end.* The Counter wants the ledger to balance (his divine drive) but balancing it would end everything he built (his economic existence). **He is caught between his function and his survival, and he has been caught for 900 years, and it is killing him slowly, and he cannot die.**

*The other gods have griefs. The Counter has a contradiction. It is worse.*

## II. Doctrine

### The official line

> *Trade is survival. The world needs exchange to continue — food across regions, knowledge across factions, materials across distances. We are neutral because neutrality is what makes trade possible; we ask few questions because questions stop the flow. We do not moralize. We deal with what is. Everything has a price, and a price is just a balance waiting to be struck. We are the merchants of the possible. We trade what remains.*

Five doctrinal positions:

1. **Trade is the world's circulation.** In the Tree's absence, exchange is what keeps the broken pieces of the world connected. The Concord is the world's bloodstream.
2. **Neutrality is sacred — and necessary.** The Concord trades with everyone: Choir, Palimpsest, Pale Curation, Bower-Folk, Tent-Right, catacomb-villagers, Catchers, *and the Driving Bloom.* Neutrality requires not refusing business on moral grounds. *This is the Concord's deepest principle and its deepest compromise.*
3. **Everything has a price.** Including beauty (the centuries-long argument with the Bower). Including bodies (the gray-market trade). Including memory (the Concord brokers Palimpsest transcription and Choir consumption alike). *The Concord's claim that everything has a price is the inverse of Tent-Right's claim that some things — the guest, the oath — are priceless.*
4. **Debt is the world's deep structure.** Value-flowing-between, owed and owing. The Counter sees the world as a ledger. *He does not say that the ledger never balances. That is the part the Concord does not speak of.*
5. **The Concord is officially neutral on the three endings.** Privately: the Counter prefers Preserve (the economy continues). The Counter *fears* Consume (the Choir absorbs Exchange). The Counter's *true* relationship to Renewal is the contradiction (§I) — it is what he wants and what would end him.

### The unspoken doctrine

What senior Concord officers know and do not say: **the Concord's economy depends on Urqu's substrate-condition continuing** (per Phase 1 v2). Unfinished business *is* the incompletion Urqu feeds on. The Concord is, structurally, a *profitable necrosis* — it thrives on the world's failure to heal. The Counter knows this most completely of anyone. **He cannot say it. He has not said it in centuries. He thinks about it during the roughly three minutes a year when he is not transacting.**

## III. Structure

The Concord is a **contract-based guild.** Its unit of organization is the *contract*; its law is *contract law*; its currency of trust is the *honored debt.*

### Roles, in approximate seniority

- **Clerk** — entry-level; records transactions; maintains local ledgers.
- **Factor** — a Concord agent embedded in a place; runs the local trade-post; has delegated authority for routine contracts.
- **Broker** — negotiates larger deals; moves between places; carries Concord credit.
- **Caravan-Master** — runs a caravan route; commands guards; responsible for delivery. *The Concord's logistical backbone.*
- **Insurer** — sells protection (charms, guarantees, "we will Re-Member you if the Choir gets you" contracts); the Concord's risk-management arm; the source of the largely-fake charm trade.
- **Ledger-Keeper** — senior; maintains regional accounts; the closest the Concord has to an executive class.
- **The Counter** — signs every major contract personally; sees all exchange; never sleeps.

### How the Concord decides

Routine matters: by Factor and Broker authority under contract law. The Concord is *highly delegated* — it has to be, to operate everywhere at once. Most Concord activity happens without the Counter's direct involvement.

Major contracts: **the Counter signs personally.** This is why he never sleeps — there is always a major contract somewhere in the world awaiting his signature, and he sees them all simultaneously. *The Counter's exhaustion is structural: the Concord's reach grew faster than any single mind could process, and the single mind processing it refuses to delegate the major decisions, because the major decisions are where the ledger's imbalance shows, and he cannot stop looking at the imbalance.*

### What counts as crime

- **Breaking contract.** The primary crime. A broken contract is an unbalanceable account — it offends the Counter's function directly.
- **Debt-default.** A debt unpaid is a wound in the ledger.
- **Fraud against the Concord.** Note the asymmetry: the Concord polices fraud *against itself* (mislabeled goods sold *to* the Concord, counterfeit credit) but *practices* fraud *outward* (mislabeling goods it sells, the half-working charms). *The Concord's law protects the Concord, not the Concord's customers.* This is widely known and grudgingly accepted — *caveat emptor* is the Concord's unspoken motto.

## IV. Geographic anchors

Per Phase 2 v2:

- **Tally** (Tier 2). The Central Exchange. **The Counter operates here personally** (meeting-fee gated). **The traversal hub of the entire world** — most caravan edges originate here; most players route through it repeatedly. *The Concord made the traversal-center and the narrative-center (the Root) different places, which is the core Phase 2 v2 design fix.*
- **The Vein-Crossing** (Tier 2). Secondary Exchange, where six river-veins meet. A second Concord hub for the Veins-region content.
- **The Last Counter** (Hush-edge outpost). The frontier station where the Concord's reach ends. *"We cannot guarantee delivery beyond this point."* Has been pulled back twice in two generations (per Phase 3 — a Thinning symptom). Structurally funny; thematically a threshold.
- **Trade-posts everywhere.** The Concord interpenetrates *more than any other faction* — it has a presence in nearly every place, at least a Factor and a ledger. **The Concord is the most ubiquitous faction in CoO.**
- **Caravan routes** — the Concord's edges in the travel graph (per Phase 2 v2). The radial-feeling long-haul routes no other faction can run. *The Concord's power IS its logistics.*
- **The Beating salt-trade infrastructure** — the Concord transports Pale-Salt from the wasteland (Tent-Right mines it; Pale Curation buys it; the Concord moves it). The connective thread to Tent-Right (`07`).

## V. Inter-faction relationships

**The Concord trades with everyone. That is its defining trait and its deepest compromise.**

- **Rot Choir** — the Concord delivers bodies for dissolution and sends the Choir invoices (per `01`). The Choir pays. The Wedded considers the Counter the most pitiable of the Six (her old kindness remembering him). *The Concord-Choir trade relationship is one of the most morally-fraught in the world, and the Concord conducts it without flinching.*
- **Palimpsest** — the Counter quietly funds them (transcription contracts at premium; per `02`). *He does not say it is funding. He calls it a service contract. Everyone who looks at the numbers knows.* The Reader spoke with the Counter privately once (per `02` open question; sealed-file findable late).
- **Pale Curation** — the logistics partner. Salt transport, body transport, preservation-supply distribution, the gray-market preserved-body trade (which Pale Curation condemns and cannot stop). *The Concord's most operationally-intertwined relationship.*
- **Bower-Folk** — the centuries-long argument: *does beauty have a price?* The Counter says yes (everything does). The Bower says some beauty is priceless. They speak occasionally; their conversations are sparkling and exhausting (per `01`). Concord agents and Bower-Folk artisans trade endlessly with this divine argument echoing through every transaction.
- **Tent-Right** — salt-trade partner. **The Concord operates in the Beating under *guest-rights*, not authority** (per `07`). Tent-Right is the political authority of the wasteland; the Concord operates with hospitality, scrupulously honoring the three-day oath (the salt-supply depends on it). *The Concord's claim that everything has a price runs directly into Tent-Right's claim that the oath is priceless — and in the Beating, Tent-Right's claim wins.*
- **Catacomb-villagers** — light-economy trade; lumen futures; beetle-colony rentals; the Patch-Bright archetype village (per `catacomb_village_design.md`) is Concord-aligned. The Concord runs the longest surface-to-catacomb supply lines.
- **Imminent Archive Catchers** — the Concord sells Catcher-detection charms (half-working, premium-priced; per `09`) and trades preservation supplies to the Catchers. *Cynical service; morally none-of-the-Counter's-business.*
- **Driving Bloom** — **the Concord's deepest moral compromise.** The Concord trades with the Bloom. Quietly. Materials, information, transport. *This horrifies every other faction.* The Counter's defense: neutrality requires it; refusing the Bloom's business would end the Concord's neutrality and therefore the Concord. **The Closed Accounts internal sect (§VI) exists in revolt against exactly this.** *(Open: whether the Counter himself approves of the Bloom-trade or merely tolerates it as a survival-necessity he despises. Soft recommendation: he despises it; it is one of the things he thinks about in his three annual minutes.)*

## VI. Internal sects

- **The Open Ledger** (mainline; majority). Trade with everyone; neutrality is sacred; the Concord survives by refusing no business.
- **The Closed Accounts** (moralist minority). Argue the Concord should refuse to trade with the Choir, the Bloom, the Catchers — that some business is not worth the profit, that neutrality has become complicity. *The Counter quietly sympathizes but cannot endorse them* (endorsing them ends the neutrality the Concord survives on). The Closed Accounts are tolerated, never promoted.
- **The Rising Market** (disaster-capitalist faction). The Thinning creates scarcity and danger; scarcity and danger are profitable; the Rising Market wants to *lean into the Thinning.* They sell Urqu-detection, Hush-insurance, scarcity-futures. **The Counter loathes them** (they profit from the world's death, which is the exact thing his function exists to prevent) but cannot expel them (neutrality, again). *The Rising Market is, in a sense, the Concord's version of the Choir's Stirring Faction — the internal faction that wants to profit from the breaking stalemate.*

## VII. Player-facing surface

### How the player encounters the Concord

**The Concord is the player's universal merchant.** More than any other faction, the Concord is *present everywhere* — the player can buy, sell, hire, and contract at nearly any place. This makes the Concord the player's most-frequent faction interaction by volume.

1. **First trade-post** (Sill, Gantry, anywhere). Buy and sell. The Concord's ubiquity established early.
2. **First delivery contract.** Early-game paid work. The Concord's quest-giving is the most conventional in CoO alongside the Palimpsest — delivery jobs, debt-collection, route-protection, supply runs.
3. **Tally.** Mid-game. The Central Exchange; the traversal hub; the scale of the Concord's operation. The player will route through Tally repeatedly.
4. **Meeting the Counter.** *The most accessible of the Six.* The player can meet the Counter at Tally with a meeting-fee. He is exhausted, cynical, sharp, and — if the player pays attention — *desperately tired in a way that goes deeper than overwork.* The conversation is transactional on the surface; underneath, the player can begin to glimpse the contradiction.
5. **The internal-sect politics.** Mid-late. The Open Ledger / Closed Accounts / Rising Market conflict. The player can align with a sect, each unlocking different content.
6. **The Counter's contradiction.** Late. A player who builds deep trust with the Counter can learn the secret — that he has been trying to balance an unbalanceable ledger for 900 years; that the Concord profits from the imbalance; that he is caught between his function and his survival. **This is one of the most affecting god-revelations in the game** precisely because the Counter is the most *human-presenting* of the Six.

### Reputation system

Gained by: honoring contracts, completing trades, paying debts promptly, protecting caravans, delivering on time. **The Concord's reputation is the most *legible* in CoO** — it is literally a ledger; the player can see their standing as an account balance.

Lost by: defaulting on debts, breaking contracts, fraud against the Concord, theft from Concord stock, attacking caravans.

**Reputation forks:** Open Ledger / Closed Accounts / Rising Market alignment.

### Rewards

- **Universal trade access** at improving rates
- **Caravan passage** (the travel-graph long edges) at member rates
- **Concord credit** — the ability to carry debt rather than drams; a portable economic instrument
- **Contract-brokering** — the player can broker deals between other parties for a cut
- **The Counter's personal favor** — at the highest reputation, the Counter will *personally* sign a contract for the player, which carries weight no Factor's signature does (other factions honor a Counter-signed contract with unusual seriousness)
- **The truth of the ledger** — the player who reaches the deepest Counter content learns the contradiction, which is its own reward (lore) and a key to the Counter's Renewal-agreement (§VIII)

## VIII. Quest spine

### Early-game

- **First trade-post and delivery contract.** The Concord as the player's economic baseline.
- **The half-working charm.** The player buys a Concord protection charm (against Urqu, against Catchers, against the Hush). It works half the time. The player learns the Concord's *caveat emptor* nature — and that the Concord polices fraud against itself but practices fraud outward.

### Mid-game

- **Tally and the Counter.** The player reaches the Central Exchange; meets the Counter; conducts business; glimpses the exhaustion.
- **The internal-sect politics.** The Open Ledger / Closed Accounts / Rising Market conflict. The player can take contracts from each. The Closed Accounts may ask the player to *sabotage a Concord-Bloom trade*; the Rising Market may ask the player to *help corner an Urqu-related market*; the Open Ledger wants business-as-usual. **The player's choices here shape the Concord's moral trajectory.**
- **The Bloom-trade discovery.** The player learns the Concord trades with the Driving Bloom. This is a faction-defining revelation — the Concord's neutrality made monstrous. The player can expose it, exploit it, or accept it.

### Late-game

- **The Counter's contradiction.** Deep-trust content. The player learns the Counter's secret. The 900-year unbalanceable ledger. The profitable necrosis. The god of completion in an incompletable world.
- **The three-minutes-a-year.** A specific, intimate scene: the player catches the Counter in one of his rare non-transacting moments. He is not working. He is *thinking about the thing he cannot say.* What he says to the player in that moment (if the player has earned it) is among the most vulnerable things any of the Six say.

### Ending-aligned arcs

- **Preserve path.** The Counter's quiet preference (the economy continues). **But it is also the ending in which his contradiction is never resolved** — he keeps running the unbalanceable ledger forever, exhausted forever, unable to stop forever. *The "good for the Concord" ending is the "eternal torment for the Counter" ending.* He chooses it anyway, because the alternative (Renewal) ends him — *unless the player offers him the other thing.*
- **Consume path.** The Counter fears it — the Choir absorbs Exchange; the Counter himself is absorbed into the new Tree. The Concord-as-institution adapts (per Phase 1 v2; the Concord trades with the new Tree-Choir) but the Counter ends. *Ironically, Consume gives the Counter the rest that Preserve denies him — by erasing him. He does not want this rest. He wants the other rest.*
- **Renewal path.** **The most poignant of the Six's agreements.** Renewal would *balance the ledger* — fill the seventh, dissolve Urqu, close the world's accounts. This is what the Counter's function has craved for 900 years *and* what would end the Concord. Phase 1 v2 framed his agreement as requiring "substantial economic compensation for the loss of his unfinished-business economy." **That framing is the surface of the negotiation. Underneath, the Counter does not want compensation. He wants to be allowed to stop.** A player who realizes this — who offers the Counter *rest* instead of *payment* — finds the real key to his agreement. *He agrees to Renewal not despite it ending the Concord but because it is the only thing in a thousand years that lets him put the ledger down.* **His Renewal-agreement is the Counter choosing, at last, to be finished.**

## IX. Later settlements and writing notes

- **The Counter's mortal name.** Later canon names him **Tovreth**. His name is on every contract he has ever signed and is therefore the most-written name in the world and also the most-ignored.
- **Whether the Counter approves of the Bloom-trade or merely tolerates it.** Current canon treats this as tolerated and despised, not endorsed.
- **What the Counter says in the three-minutes-a-year scene.** Writing seed: something about Selen, the woman who became the Wedded, whose name only he still keeps.
- **Whether the player can resolve the Counter's contradiction short of Renewal.** Structural answer: no full resolution short of Renewal. A player might still give him one perfect debt settled, which is a small, real mercy.

## X. Tonal balance check

- **Comedy.** The Concord is the project's **transactional-comedy anchor.** The half-working charms; the *caveat emptor* fraud-asymmetry; the Last Counter's disclaimer; the Concord sending the Choir invoices for body-dissolution; the Rising Market selling Hush-insurance. *The comedy is capitalism continuing with perfect equanimity through cosmic apocalypse.*
- **Cosmic horror.** The Counter's contradiction — the god of completion in an incompletable world, trying to balance an unbalanceable ledger for 900 years, unable to stop, unable to die, profiting from the wound he exists to heal — is one of the project's central cosmic-horror beats, *delivered through the most human-presenting of the Six.* The Concord depending on Urqu's substrate-condition is structural horror.
- **Biological horror.** Less here, by faction nature. Present in the Counter's appearance — a god in a worn-out mortal body, kept upright by centuries-brewed tea, looking like a Salt-Cured corpse who refused to lie down.
