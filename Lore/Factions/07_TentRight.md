# 04.07 — Tent-Right (the wasteland hospitality culture)

>
> Phase 4.07 of the lore project. Per-faction doc, paired with the Saccharine Concord (`04`) — the two factions defined by *travel and the spaces between settlements*, connected by the Beating salt-trade.

> **Tent-Right is the only major faction that is not god-founded.** They are *the people the Tree did not name* (per Phase 1 v2). And out of that absence they built — without inheritance, without a god, from nothing but necessity and each other — **a mortal reinvention of the missing seventh inheritance.** This doc contains the strongest cosmological payoff in the faction set: Tent-Right and Urqu are the two unnamed, and they are cosmic mirror-images of each other.

## I. Founding — the people the Tree did not name

Tent-Right has **no founding god and no founding Strike.** This is its defining fact.

When the Tree fell, it had chosen seven mortals to ascend (six who struck, one who didn't — per Phase 1 v2). Everyone *else* — the rest of the pre-Felling civilization — was *not chosen.* They were not summoned by the Tree's dreams; they were not given an inheritance; they were not made into gods or god-led factions. **They were simply left** — left to the cataclysm, the flood, and the raw sun.

Most of them died in the Cataclysm Years. The survivors scattered. Some were gathered by the six gods into the founding factions (the gods' lineages drew in those nearby). But a portion of the unchosen ended up in the **Beating** — the wasteland, where the raw sun beats hardest because the Tree's canopy was thinnest there (per Phase 2 v2 / Phase 3). *They were the people no god came for, in the place the Tree had protected least.*

### The making of the oath (~Gen 12, ~year 360)

For ~12 generations the wasteland-survivors had no coherent culture — scattered clans, fighting over wells and salt, dying of thirst and of each other. **The wasteland kills everyone who cannot trust.** A traveler who cannot be sure of shelter dies between settlements; a host who cannot be sure of a guest's intentions kills first and starves later.

Out of this, in Generation 12, the survivors made a choice that no god gave them: **the three-day oath.** *Anyone who reaches your tent and claims hospitality is protected for three days, absolutely, regardless of who they are or what they have done. You will feed them, shelter them, and defend them with violence if necessary. The bond is inviolable. Breaking it is the only unforgivable crime.* (Per IDEAS.md; structural-Bedouin inspiration.)

The oath was sworn first at the place that became **the First Tent** (Phase 2 v2). It spread because *it worked* — wasteland clans that kept the oath survived; clans that broke it were destroyed (by reputation-collapse: an oathbreaking clan could no longer find hospitality anywhere, and in the wasteland that is a death sentence). Within a few generations the oath was wasteland law.

**Tent-Right is not a faith. It is not a god's instrument. It is a *covenant the unchosen made with each other* to survive a world that abandoned them.** This is the most important thing about them.

## II. The cosmological payoff — Tent-Right as mortal Naming; the Urqu mirror

This is the doc's central commitment, and it is the strongest single cosmological connection in the faction set.

### The two unnamed

The seventh inheritance — **Naming**, the function by which a thing is *what it is*, held coherent, made itself-and-not-another — was never delivered (per Phase 1 v2). The seventh god was never made. The empty position became **Urqu**: the unnamed *god-position*, hostile, hungry, an absence-in-the-shape-of-a-being trying and failing to incarnate.

**Tent-Right is the unnamed *people.*** They too were not-named by the Tree — not chosen, not inherited, left to the absence. They too are *an absence in the shape of a people.*

**And here is the mirror: Urqu became a hostile incompletion; Tent-Right became a generous freedom.** Same root condition (the unnamed); opposite expression. Urqu's absence *hungers* — it wants to fill itself by unmaking the world. Tent-Right's absence *gives* — the oath, hospitality, the three-day bond. **They are the positive image of Urqu's negative.** Two answers to the same question: *what does the unnamed do?* Urqu's answer is *consume.* Tent-Right's answer is *welcome.*

### The oath as mortal Naming

Here is what Tent-Right does not fully understand about itself (and what the player can discover): **the three-day oath is a mortal reinvention of the seventh inheritance.**

When a Tent-Right host takes a guest under the oath, they *name them guest.* They make the guest *coherent-with-them* — held, protected, real, defended. **This is, in miniature, exactly the function the seventh god was meant to deliver: Naming. Making a thing itself, holding it coherent against the world's slippage.** Tent-Right does it *manually*, *socially*, *willed* — without a god, without an inheritance, one guest at a time, by choosing it over and over.

**Tent-Right reinvented the missing seventh inheritance from scratch, as a human practice.** The consequences ripple through the world's metaphysics:

- **The oath is a tiny anti-Urqu field.** Where Tent-Right hospitality holds, naming holds; where naming holds, Urqu's slippage cannot take root. **Tent-Right settlements have measurably weaker Urqu-bleed than anywhere else** (per Phase 2 v2 — Urqu is a distributed infection of places; Tent-Right places resist it). The wasteland *should* be Urqu-saturated (it borders the Hush, it is far from the Root's holding-field) — and yet it is among the safest places in the world, because *the oath does naming where the Root cannot reach.*
- **This is why Catchers cannot operate in Tent-Right settlements** (per `09`). The Catcher's preservation-pot is an act of unmaking-the-person's-agency; the oath *names the guest protected*, and the naming holds against the Catcher. The hospitality oath supersedes the preservation because *the oath is a stronger naming.* **The same holds against the Bower-Folk Caster** (per `05`): non-consensual Resin-Cast is the identical act — unmaking a guest's agency to fix them — and the oath forbids it for the identical reason. *Any faction that would preserve a person against their will is barred where the oath holds; the principle is general, not Catcher-specific.*
- **This is why Tent-Right's relationship to Urqu is uncannily clear-eyed.** They recognize Urqu immediately — they too are the unnamed; they too live in the shape of an absence. *They are not afraid of Urqu the way other factions are.* They understand it as kin — the sibling who chose to consume instead of welcome.

### The Renewal-Naming candidate

This is the endgame payoff. The Renewal ending requires filling (or deliberately accepting-as-empty) the seventh Naming-inheritance (per Phase 1 v2 — the options were: the player becomes the seventh god / a Re-Membered NPC / a child born during the run / deliberate emptiness).

**Tent-Right offers a fifth option, and it is the most thematically resonant: the oath itself.**

A player who has gone deep with Tent-Right — taken the oath, defended guests, understood the cosmological significance — can offer, at the Renewal ritual, *the oath* as the seventh inheritance. **The new world adopts hospitality-as-naming. The seventh position is filled not by a god, not by a person, but by a universal mortal practice.** Naming becomes something the world's people *do*, willed, one guest at a time, forever — rather than something a god holds for them.

**This answers Urqu in the most complete way possible: not by filling its empty position with a being, but by answering its absence with the generous version of the same absence.** Urqu was the unnamed-that-consumes; the oath is the unnamed-that-welcomes; and if the world adopts the oath, *the question Urqu was the wrong answer to gets the right answer.* Urqu dissolves not because it was defeated but because *the world finally chose welcome over hunger* — and the unnamed-god-position is satisfied by a world that names *each other.*

**This is the most resonant Renewal flavor in the project.** It makes Tent-Right — the humble, god-less, abandoned wasteland people — *the secret key to the most complete possible ending.*

## III. Doctrine

Tent-Right does not have *doctrine* in the way the god-factions do (they have no god to interpret). They have **the oath, and the ethics that grew around it.**

1. **The guest is sacred.** Three days, inviolable, regardless of who they are or what they have done. *The oath does not ask questions.* A murderer who reaches your tent and claims hospitality is protected, for three days, even from their victims' kin. *After three days, the protection ends and the world resumes — but for three days, the guest is held.*
2. **The oath supersedes all other loyalties.** Faction, debt, enmity, even blood. A Tent-Right host who has sworn the oath to a guest will defend that guest against their own clan if necessary. *This is the oath's terrible cost and its whole power.*
3. **We serve each other, not a god.** Tent-Right knows the gods are real (they have seen Urqu's mirror; some have traded at the Salt-Vault and seen what the Salted is). They simply *do not serve one.* They are the world's only functionally-humanist faction — their ethics are *chosen*, not *inherited.* **This is a point of fierce pride.** *We were not named by a god, so we belong to ourselves, and we made our own law, and it is better than any inheritance because we chose it.*
4. **The Tree's absence is our freedom.** Where the god-factions experience the Felling as loss (their gods are diminished, their world broken), Tent-Right experiences it as *liberation.* They were never the Tree's instruments; they were never claimed; the Felling did not break their relationship to the Tree because *they never had one.* They are the only people who lost nothing in the Felling, because they were given nothing to lose.
5. **Some things are priceless.** The direct inverse of the Concord's "everything has a price." The guest is priceless. The oath is priceless. Water shared in the deep wasteland is priceless. *This is the doctrinal collision with the Concord that runs through every salt-trade negotiation in the Beating.*

## IV. Structure

Tent-Right is **clan-based and decentralized.** There is no central authority — *the oath is the authority*, more than any person.

### Roles

- **Clan-elders** — lead individual clans; adjudicate disputes; but do not command beyond their clan.
- **Oath-keepers** — those whose specific role is to enforce hospitality, witness oaths, and pursue oathbreakers. The closest thing Tent-Right has to law-enforcement, but their authority comes *from the oath*, not from rank.
- **Well-keepers** — control water sources. In the wasteland, water is survival; well-keepers hold enormous practical power, tempered by the oath (a well-keeper who refuses a guest water is an oathbreaker).
- **Salt-masters** — run the salt economy (the Pale-Salt mining that supplies Pale Curation via the Concord).
- **Caravan-guards** — protect travelers and trade across the wasteland.
- **The Namers** *(emerging; see §VI)* — a small group beginning to suspect the oath's cosmological significance.

### Authority

**The oath is supreme.** No elder, no well-keeper, no clan can override it. A dispute between clans is resolved by appeal to the oath's principles. *Tent-Right is the only major faction whose ultimate authority is a practice rather than a person or a god.* (Pale Curation's authority is the Salted, however silent; the Choir's is the substrate; the Palimpsest's is the Reader; Tent-Right's is *the oath itself* — an idea, held by everyone, owned by no one.)

### Crime

- **Breaking hospitality.** The supreme crime. The only unforgivable one. An oathbreaker is exiled — and in the wasteland, exile is death, because no clan will offer an oathbreaker hospitality, and no one survives the wasteland alone. *The punishment is not execution; it is the withdrawal of the oath, which is the same thing, slower.*
- **Harming a guest** (while under oath). A form of oathbreaking.
- **Failing to defend a guest.** A form of oathbreaking.
- **Poisoning a well.** The second-worst crime — an attack on everyone's survival.

*There is no concept of crime against property in the wasteland sense the Concord would recognize — the wasteland's economy is gift-and-oath-based as much as trade-based. Theft exists but is minor compared to oathbreaking.*

## V. Geographic anchors

Per Phase 2 v2:

- **Wellmeet** (Tier 2). The major Tent-Right hub; gateway to the harder wasteland. The three-day oath is law here, scrupulously kept. Salt-trade originates nearby.
- **The First Tent** (Beating). The sacred site where the oath was first sworn (~Gen 12). Pilgrimage destination. The cultural and political heart of Tent-Right. *Not a temple — there is no god — but a monument to a choice.*
- **The Great Salt Plain** (Beating). Largest wasteland salt-flat; Pale-Salt mining; the salt economy's center. Tent-Right is the political authority; the Concord operates under guest-rights; Pale Curation buys the salt.
- **Mobile encampments** across the Beating — Tent-Right is substantially nomadic; many clans move with water and season.
- **Trading towns at wells and crossroads** — semi-permanent settlements where the wasteland's sparse trade concentrates.

**The Beating as refuge.** Per `09`: Tent-Right settlements are the one place Catchers cannot operate, and (per §II) the place with the weakest Urqu-bleed in the world. **For a wounded, pursued, or Urqu-threatened player, claiming Tent-Right hospitality is a genuine mechanical sanctuary** — three days of absolute protection, enforced by the oath, reinforced by the cosmological anti-Urqu field. *The wasteland looks like the most dangerous place and is, for a guest, the safest.*

## VI. Internal sects

- **The Oath-Keepers** (mainline; majority). Keep the oath as it is; the three-day bond, the wasteland law.
- **The Wide-Tent** (universalists). Argue the oath should be *extended to the whole world* — that hospitality is too good to keep to the wasteland; that every settlement everywhere should adopt the three-day bond. *(They are, without knowing it, arguing for the Renewal-Naming option — the oath as universal practice. The Namers, §below, are beginning to give them cosmological language for it.)*
- **The Deep-Tent** (purists). Argue the oath is *for the wasteland only* — that outsiders who adopt it dilute it, that the oath's power comes from the wasteland's necessity, that bringing it to soft lands where survival is easy would hollow it out.
- **The Namers** *(new; the cosmological-discovery sect)*. A small, growing group who have *noticed* the strange facts: Urqu cannot touch them; Catchers cannot operate in their settlements; the Urqu-bleed is weaker where the oath holds. They are beginning to theorize that **the oath is doing something larger than survival — that it is doing the thing the gods do.** They do not yet have the full picture (that the oath is mortal Naming, the seventh inheritance reinvented), but they are reaching toward it. *The Namers are the player's door into the §II cosmological payoff.*

## VII. Player-facing surface

### How the player encounters Tent-Right

1. **First wasteland crossing.** The player enters the Beating; learns it is harsh, vast, and that survival depends on hospitality. *The first time the player claims the oath and is protected, the mechanic announces itself: three days, absolute, enforced.*
2. **Claiming hospitality as refuge.** The player learns the oath is a *mechanical sanctuary* — from Catchers (who cannot follow), from pursuit (which must wait outside), from Urqu-bleed (weakest here). **This is one of the most useful defensive mechanics in the game**, and it is *free* — it costs nothing but reaching a Tent-Right settlement and claiming the bond.
3. **Wellmeet and the salt economy.** Mid-game. The player engages with the wasteland's trade, the salt-mining, the Concord's guest-rights operation, the well-keepers' power.
4. **Taking the oath.** The player can *swear the oath themselves* — becoming oath-bound, gaining Tent-Right standing, and accepting the *obligation to host others.* A player who has taken the oath must, when someone claims their hospitality, protect them — *even when it is inconvenient, even when the guest is the player's enemy.* **This is one of the most interesting player-commitment mechanics in CoO** — the oath binds the player, not just the NPCs.
5. **The Namers' subplot.** Mid-late. The Namers approach the player (an outsider who has seen the wider world) with their strange observations. The player can help them investigate — and discover, with them, that the oath is mortal Naming, that Tent-Right is Urqu's mirror, that the wasteland people accidentally reinvented the missing seventh inheritance.
6. **The First Tent.** Late. The pilgrimage to the sacred site of the first oath. *Not a god-encounter (Tent-Right has no god) but a culture-encounter at its deepest* — the monument to the choice the unchosen made.
7. **The Renewal-Naming offer.** Endgame. A player who has gone deep with Tent-Right can carry the oath to the Renewal ritual as the seventh-inheritance answer (§II). **This is the secret most-complete Renewal flavor**, and Tent-Right — the god-less wasteland people — is its source.

### Reputation system

Gained by: honoring the oath (as guest *and* as host), defending guests, contributing to wells and salt-work, respecting the three-day bond, helping the Namers.

Lost by: **breaking hospitality (catastrophic — the single worst reputation loss in the game; an oathbreaking player is refused hospitality across all Tent-Right settlements, losing the sanctuary mechanic entirely)**, harming guests, poisoning wells, treating the oath as transactional.

**Reputation forks:** Oath-Keeper / Wide-Tent / Deep-Tent / Namer alignment.

### Rewards

- **Hospitality sanctuary** — the three-day protection mechanic; a refuge from Catchers, pursuit, and Urqu-bleed
- **Pale-Salt** at wasteland rates (cheaper than buying it after the Concord's markup)
- **Wasteland survival capability** — water-finding, heat-resistance, sandstorm-navigation
- **The oath as a player ability** — once oath-bound, the player can extend hospitality to NPCs, which has its own uses (pacifying hostiles who claim the bond; protecting allies; creating temporary safe-zones)
- **The Namers' cosmological knowledge** — understanding the oath-as-Naming, which is the key to the Renewal-Naming option
- **The most complete Renewal flavor** — the oath as the seventh inheritance

## VIII. Quest spine

### Early-game

- **First wasteland crossing and first oath claimed.** The mechanic announces itself; the player learns the wasteland's logic.
- **The pursued guest.** Early Tent-Right content: someone claims hospitality at a settlement the player is in, pursued by a hostile party that must wait outside. The player witnesses the oath enforced — the host clan defending a guest they have no other reason to protect. *The player's introduction to what the oath costs and what it means.*

### Mid-game

- **Wellmeet, the salt economy, the Concord collision.** The player engages with the Beating's trade and politics; sees Tent-Right's "some things are priceless" collide with the Concord's "everything has a price."
- **Taking the oath.** The player swears it; becomes bound; experiences the obligation when someone claims their hospitality at an inconvenient moment.
- **The Namers' approach.** The strange observations. The investigation begins.

### Late-game

- **The cosmological discovery.** The player and the Namers piece it together: the oath is mortal Naming; Tent-Right is Urqu's mirror; the unnamed people reinvented the missing seventh inheritance. **One of the most satisfying lore-revelations in the game** — and it recontextualizes the humble wasteland faction as cosmologically central.
- **The First Tent.** The pilgrimage; the monument to the choice; the deepest expression of Tent-Right's culture.
- **The Urqu-kinship encounter.** A player who has understood the mirror may approach Urqu differently — not as the other factions do (defense, dread, bargaining-with-mouthpieces) but as *kin.* Later canon makes this the true practice-path's deepest insight: Tent-Right does not defeat Urqu, it answers the unnamed sibling.

### Ending-aligned arcs

- **Renewal path (the great Tent-Right arc).** The player carries the oath to the Renewal ritual as the seventh-inheritance answer. The world adopts hospitality-as-naming. Urqu is answered, not defeated. **The most complete and most thematically-resonant ending in the game**, and Tent-Right is its source. *(Requires deep Tent-Right alignment + the Namers' knowledge + having taken the oath + having understood the mirror.)*
- **Preserve path.** Tent-Right endures, as it always has. **Of all factions, Tent-Right is least changed by Preserve** — they were never named, never inherited, never depended on the Root; they will outlast the eternal stalemate as they have outlasted everything. *In a sense, Preserve is the ending where Tent-Right's "the Tree's absence is our freedom" is permanently vindicated — the world stays broken, and the unnamed people keep welcoming each other, forever, in the one place the gods never reached.*
- **Consume path.** Tent-Right resists longest (the wasteland is too dry for the Choir; the oath's naming-field resists absorption). But in the Consume ending, the Choir eventually colonizes even the Beating (per Phase 1 v2); **the oath becomes one of the last holdouts** — the final places where people still name each other before the substrate names them all. *A poignant last-stand image.*

## IX. Later settlements and implementation notes

- **Tent-Right can "speak to Urqu as kin."** Settled as a true-Renewal key. It should not let the player command Urqu; it lets them understand and answer it.
- **Oath-as-Renewal-Naming.** Settled as symbolic at the ritual, then literalized after true Renewal when the post-Renewal world adopts the practice. The Wide-Tent universalists' dream becomes the new world's naming-function.
- **The Namers' full theory and how complete it gets.** Implementation/writing note: decide how much of the theory is directly explained versus inferred through rites, hospitality failures, and Urqu encounters.
- **Whether there are pre-Felling Tent-Right-equivalents.** Open. *(Soft recommendation: no — hospitality-as-survival is a post-Felling invention, born of the wasteland's necessity; the pre-Felling Tree-civilization, held coherent by the Tree, never needed it. The oath is genuinely new in the world — the one wholly post-Felling cosmological innovation, made by mortals, not gods.)* **This is a strong thematic point: the gods inherited their functions from the dying Tree; Tent-Right invented theirs. The newest cosmological force in the world is the one no god made.**

## X. Tonal balance check

- **Cosmic horror, inverted into cosmic hope.** Tent-Right is the project's counterweight — the place where the cosmic-horror frame *opens toward grace.* The Urqu-mirror (the two unnamed; one consumes, one welcomes) is the project's most hopeful cosmological idea, and it sits inside the same horror-structure as everything else. *The same condition that produced Urqu produced the oath. The horror and the hope are the same fact, chosen differently.*
- **Biological horror.** Minimal — the wasteland is too dry for the Choir, too harsh for the bog, too sun-blasted for substrate. Tent-Right is the *cleanest* faction, bodily. (Which is itself a relief in the project's palette — the player who reaches the Beating gets a respite from the mycelial and the preserved.)
- **Comedy.** Dry (literally). The Concord's guest-rights operation under Tent-Right authority is structurally funny — the world's most pragmatic merchants forced to respect a sacred bond they cannot price. Well-keeper bureaucracy. The Deep-Tent purists grumbling about soft-land outsiders diluting the oath. *The comedy is the friction between the priceless and the priced.*
