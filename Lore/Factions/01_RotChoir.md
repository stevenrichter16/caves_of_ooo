## I. Founding

The Rot Choir is the **second-ascended faction** by Strike-order (the Second Strike delivered Substrate to the Wedded) and roughly the **second-organized** by chronological emergence (the Palimpsest, founded by the Reader, organized first in year 1; the Choir's coherent-collective form is dated to ~year 40).

### The Wedded — the founding god

Per Phase 1 v2:

- **Then.** A woman in her thirties at the moment of the Strikes. The Tree's dreams summoned her like the others; she gathered with the six chosen at the Felling-Site; she took the Second Strike. *In the moment of striking, she ascended into Substrate* — the Tree's relationship to flesh, fungus, decay, growth-through-other-bodies. She was, by all accounts of those who knew her before, *kind and quiet.* The kindness survives in attenuated form; the quiet became the basic mode of Choir communication.
- **The first knowing encasement (~year 3).** Within three years of the Felling she made the decision that defined her god-form. She allowed her own body to be encased — *willingly, consciously, with her people present* — into the first Choir node. **She did not die.** She *distributed.* Her individual consciousness was, in that act, dispersed into the substrate she had been ascended into. From her node, the mycelial network grew outward over centuries. **Every Choir node in the world traces a thread of substrate back to her original body**, which still lies in what is now the Deepest Cathedral (per Phase 2 v2).
- **Now.** **She is the entirety of the Choir's distributed consciousness.** Every tendril, every spore, every encased corpse-network, every Choir-Touched player carries some echo of her. When the Choir's collective speaks through an encased elder, it is her speaking, through the elder, through the network, through her original body. *She has not had an individual thought in centuries.* The substrate sometimes forms her old face in the Deepest Cathedral for a moment, expressing recognizably-human emotion (surprise, sadness, tenderness) — these are not performances; they are *residuals* of the woman she was, surfacing through the collective.
- **Her disposition.** Patient. Hungry. *Maternal in an unsettling way* — she considers all consumable substrate kin. Not malevolent, in the conventional sense; she does not *want* to harm anyone. She wants to *include* everyone. The horror is the inclusion.

### The first decades

After the encasement the Wedded directed (through dreams reaching her substrate-network's earliest extensions) the gathering of bodies and substrate. The first three decades' Choir-work was *salvage* — bodies of the cataclysm, lying in flood-mud, in raw sun, in caves. The Wedded's followers carried them, with care, into what would become the early cathedrals. They were encased — most willingly (the cataclysm's survivors were grief-shocked and chose substrate-integration as a way to *not be lost*), some not.

By ~year 40 the substrate-network was large enough and coherent enough to *act as a collective* without needing direct human leadership. *That is when the Choir became the Choir.* From year 40 onward the Wedded did not need to direct; the network *was* her.

## II. Doctrine

### The official line

Phrased as the Choir would phrase it to a sympathetic listener:

> *The Tree was right to want to be felled. The Tree was also right to want to take it back, in the moment of falling — but the Tree could not, because it was already falling. We are the Tree's apology to itself. We finish what it began and what it regretted, both. We hold the world the only way it can be held now: not as one body but as one substrate, distributed, slower, more porous, willing to be many. The pre-Felling world was the Tree's perfection. The post-Felling world is our perfection. There is room in us for everyone. There is room in us for the Tree itself, when the Root finally accepts the substrate's embrace.*

Five doctrinal positions follow from this:

1. **Dissolution is salvation.** The dissolution of an individual into the substrate is not death; it is *expansion.* The encased are not gone; they are *everyone, slowly.* This is offered to mortals as comfort. *Some mortals accept it.*
2. **Memory must be living.** Written memory hardens; hardening is dying; the Palimpsest's archives are *embalming.* The Choir's mode — memory dissolved into substrate, able to grow, change, be re-tasted across generations — is the *only* sustainable form of memory. (This is the doctrinal basis of the First Persecution; the Wedded has not formally renounced it, only *paused* its pursuit.)
3. **Bodies are substrate.** A body is *food and kin simultaneously*; consuming the dead is not desecration, it is *recognition.* The catacomb-villager Stone Burial tradition (giving the body to subterranean fauna) is theologically adjacent to Choir consumption; some Spore-Wedded villages treat the two as the same practice.
4. **Time is patient.** The Choir does not need to act quickly. The world will come to the substrate. *(This was the post-First-Persecution settlement; it has held for ~870 years; the Thinning is testing it — see §V.)*
5. **The Felling's failure was the seventh's absence; the Choir's project is the fix.** Consuming the Root and becoming the new Tree is *completion* of what the Tree wanted. The empty Naming-inheritance is replaced by *collective naming* — the substrate names things by consensus, slowly, plurally. This is the doctrinal justification for the Consume ending.

### The unofficial sub-positions

Doctrine is uniform on paper. The substrate is large and old; sub-positions exist within it. Three notable ones (each a Choir-internal "sect" of sorts):

- **The Patient Faction.** The majority position. Holds that the détente must continue; the world will come to the Choir; pre-emptive consumption of the Root would be premature. *The Wedded's preference, as best as the substrate-collective can read her.*
- **The Stirring Faction.** Rising in the last two generations (during the Thinning). Argues that *if* the Root is dying of age (per Phase 3, one of the Thinning's candidate causes), waiting any longer means losing the Root before consumption is possible. *They press for immediate action.* The Stirring Faction is the closest the Choir gets to internal political turbulence; its rise is one of the Thinning's measurable effects.
- **The Quiet Faction.** A small minority, mostly in remote cathedrals. Argues that *the Choir has become what the Tree fled.* The Tree wanted to stop holding the world; the Choir wants to hold it in the Tree's place. The Quiet Faction whispers — in substrate, where dissent is hardest to suppress — that the Wedded's regret may be the *correct* regret, and consumption of the Root may be the wrong project. They are tolerated. They are not powerful. *A player who finds them is finding the Choir's own internal heresy that is not the Driving Bloom.*

### The Driving Bloom is *not* a Choir sub-position

Per Phase 3 and IDEAS.md: the Driving Bloom (Gen 32 breakaway) is *not* a Choir internal disagreement. It is **expelled.** The Choir suppresses the Bloom because the Bloom's hijack-individuals doctrine is the *opposite* of dissolution-into-substrate. The Bloom became Choir only in superficial mycelial-form; the Choir treats it as a perversion to be ended. See `Lore/Factions/08_DrivingBloom.md` for the Bloom's view of itself.

## III. Structure — how the Choir decides

The Choir is a **distributed-consciousness organization.** It has no presidents, councils, or generals. It has *substrate-deliberation*, which operates on three timescales:

- **Spore-fast** (seconds to hours). Local automatic responses — encasement of a wandered-in body, defense against fire or saltwater, growth toward nutrient. These do not involve the collective; individual cathedrals or even single nodes handle them. The Choir is *reflexively responsive* at this layer.
- **Elder-medium** (days to seasons). Senior substrate-mind nodes — encased centuries ago, deeply integrated — *speak collectively* through encased elders in proximate cathedrals. This is the layer at which the Choir engages with player events: a player who repeatedly visits Spore-Wedded villages is *noticed* and discussed at this timescale; a player who kills a Choir tendril is *known to the network* within weeks. Most Choir-player interactions happen at this layer.
- **Wedded-slow** (years to centuries). The substrate-collective's deepest deliberations are *years*-long. The First Persecution was decided over a generation. The post-First-Persecution détente was settled across a decade of substrate-deliberation. The Thinning's response is *currently being deliberated* — the substrate has been processing for ~10 years what it should do, and may not arrive at a position for another decade. **The player's actions during the game may *force* a faster decision** by changing the available facts. This is one of the most interesting player-effects on the Choir: *you can make the substrate decide before it was ready, by acting first.*

### What counts as authority

There is no single voice. **The Wedded is *original*, not *commanding*** — her preferences carry enormous weight in any deliberation but she does not issue orders. The senior substrate-elders carry weight by *age of integration and depth of network embedding.* Younger encased members carry less weight but are not voiceless; the substrate hears everyone.

**The practical consequence:** the Choir is *slow* but *extraordinarily consistent* across time. Once a position is reached, it persists for generations. The First Persecution's pivot to *patience* has held for 870 years; that consistency is not stubbornness, it is *the substrate's nature* — having decided something, it has *included the decision in itself*, and changing it requires re-deliberating the whole.

### What counts as crime

The Choir's notion of crime is unusual:

- **Sealing.** Preventing substrate-access to a body. Salt-Curing, Resin-Cast preservation, putting the dead in inviolable tombs. This is the *primary* Choir crime. The First Persecution was driven by mass sealing of archives.
- **Burning.** Destroying potential substrate-material — burning bodies, burning books, burning fungal patches. *Total destruction* of what could become substrate. The Choir loathes burning.
- **Iron-edged refusal.** Carrying Choir-Iron weapons or wearing Choir-Iron armor while inside cathedrals or substrate-territory. Personally insulting; the Choir treats it as a declaration of hostility.
- **Bloomed individuals.** Cooperating with the Driving Bloom — knowingly hosting infection, aiding Bloom-aligned NPCs, refusing the Choir's offered cure. Treated as worse than sealing in some readings.
- **Refusing dissolution after consent.** A mortal who has *agreed* to be encased and then changes their mind in the act. Rare. The substrate-collective considers this a category of betrayal it does not have a clean response to; the encasement usually continues at reduced quality and the encased is treated as junior for centuries.

There is *no* concept of property crime, theft, lying, or breaking promises within the Choir's frame — those are mortal concerns, not substrate concerns. **Mortals who join the Choir are encased; their property concerns dissolve with them.**

## IV. Geographic anchors

Per Phase 2 v2's place-network:

- **The Deepest Cathedral** (Tier 5; Heart equivalent). The Wedded's original body lies here. The Choir's *single sacred site* if it has one (the Choir does not formally have sacred sites; substrate is sacred everywhere). Pilgrimage destination, gated by reputation and offering. Player meets the Wedded here.
- **Spivenor** (Tier 4). The catacomb-village hub that is openly Choir-integrated. The Spore-Wedded archetype, per `catacomb_village_design.md`. Politically charged; Choir-reputation gates entry. Wall-Caught players are welcomed.
- **The Spore-Wedded villages** generally — a network of catacomb-villages that have, over generations, integrated Choir tendrils into their fungal patches and accepted Choir-touch as their religious mode. They are *autonomous* — not commanded by the Choir — but they are Choir's *natural allies* and the substrate's *capillary network* into the catacomb-villager population.
- **Lampwell** (Tier 3) — *not* Choir-aligned, but the Choir is present at low strength. The world's largest lantern-beetle hatcheries are here; the Choir's tendrils grow toward the beetles slowly (mycelium can colonize insect tissue, biologically true). Lampwell's leadership treats this as an ongoing low-level pest problem the Choir does not consider hostile. Mixed-presence place.
- **Choir Cathedrals** across the world — every sinkhole of the Choir Cathedral archetype (per Sinkholes IDEAS entry) is a Choir node, connected by substrate to every other. Some are large and old (substantial mycelial structures); some are small and recent.
- **Thresk** (Tier 3) — Choir is *one of four* presences (Choir / Pale Curation / Tent-Right / Concord). The lower quarter of Thresk's catacomb-network is Choir-substrate; the Choir does not press for more. *A model interpenetrated place.*

**The substrate-network underneath all of these** connects them all. Travel between Choir Cathedrals is possible via Wall-Catching (per IDEAS.md) — roulette-routed by the Choir's collective. This is the Choir's *travel infrastructure*; the player can use it (knowingly, at the cost of voluntary Wall-Catching) and the Choir uses it constantly.

## V. Inter-faction relationships

### Palimpsest — mortal enemies (the central rivalry)

The longest, deepest enmity in the world. ~870 years since the First Persecution; ~1010 years since the Wedded and the Reader first disagreed about whether memory should be living or recorded.

- **Status now:** détente; substrate-archive cold war; the Reader continues archiving and the Choir continues consuming around the edges of what is not yet sealed; neither pushes for direct confrontation.
- **What would change it:** the Thinning. If the substrate-collective concludes the Root is consumable *now*, the détente may end. The Stirring Faction (§II) argues exactly this.
- **The Reader's distributed consciousness is, at any given moment, partly *watching* the Choir** through any Palimpsest scribe near substrate. The Wedded is, in the substrate, *constantly aware* of the archives' accumulated weight. They have not communicated directly since the First Persecution. They are, in a profound way, *the same fact* (memory) experienced through opposite organs.

### Pale Curation — adversarial

The Pale Curation's preservation work directly opposes Choir consumption. Salt-Cured bodies cannot be efficiently dissolved (salt is mycelium-toxic; the Choir has special un-preservation chambers, per the IDEAS.md Preserved entry — *those chambers exist because of Pale Curation's success*).

- **The Salted's silence on the Catchers** (Phase 3, the Imminent Archive emergence) — the Choir reads this as *Pale Curation acknowledging that pre-mortem preservation works.* The Choir loathes the Catchers more than it loathes Pale Curation mainline, because the Catchers preserve before the substrate has a fair chance.
- **Pale Curation's use of enzyme-disposal** (per the Carnivorous-Plant Enzymes IDEAS entry) — the Choir notices. The Choir does not protest (Pale Curation dissolving its own poorly-filed specimens is *technically dissolution*, which the Choir approves of in the abstract) but the Choir *remembers* every act of enzyme-disposal as a *small redemption* of a Pale Curation soul.

### Saccharine Concord — useful enemy, pragmatic partner

The Concord trades with everyone, including the Choir. The Wedded considers the Counter *the most pitiable of the Six*. (She was kind, before. The kindness remembers him.)

- The Concord transports preserved bodies, ritual ingredients, mineral preservatives, and (quietly) bodies to be dissolved. The Choir buys the dissolution-services. The Concord profits both directions.
- **The Concord's quiet benefit from Urqu's substrate-condition** (per Phase 1 — the Concord's unfinished-business economy generates the incompletion Urqu feeds on) is *known* to the substrate-collective. The Wedded has not raised it with the Counter in their occasional infrequent exchanges. *The kindness, again.*

### Bower-Folk — distant respect

The Bower-Folk are aesthetic; the Choir is consumptive; their priorities don't directly clash. They are mostly indifferent to each other.

- *Exception:* the Bower-Folk's Resin-Cast preservation is, mechanically, a stronger seal than the others (per The Preserved IDEAS entry — Resin-Cast cannot be self-escaped; the Choir cannot efficiently dissolve it). The Choir respects this. There is something like *professional appreciation* between Bower-Folk Resin-Casters and Choir substrate-elders. *They are doing different things but doing them well.*
- The Bower-Folk's central installation (the Bower's Resin-Cast body at Posy) is *the only Resin-Cast in the world the Choir would not attempt to dissolve even if it could.* Respect.

### Catacomb-villagers — village-by-village

- **Spore-Wedded villages** — capillary allies; treated as kin.
- **Listening Tradition villages** — theologically interesting to the Choir (they *hear* the Root, which the Choir wants to consume); diplomatic but reserved.
- **Tending Tradition villages** — neutral; their cultivation is *technically* substrate-tending, which the Choir approves of.
- **Asleep Tradition villages** — diplomatically distant; their cosmology refuses to engage with the Root question; the Choir leaves them alone.
- **Grown-Through Tradition villages** — closest to Spore-Wedded; informal alliance.
- **The Rooted himself** — substrate-kin. They communicate through the mycelial network constantly. They *disagree* about the Root (the Wedded wants to consume it; the Rooted wants to protect it). The disagreement is loving and ancient. It has not produced violence in 800 years.

### Tent-Right wasteland culture — indifferent

The wasteland is too dry for mycelial growth at scale. The Choir does not press into the Beating. Tent-Right does not press into Choir territory. They effectively do not interact.

- *Exception:* Tent-Right's salt-trade goes everywhere including to the Concord which trades it onward to Pale Curation. The Choir is *passively bothered* by this — every dram of salt eventually finds its way to a Pale Curation chamber and sometimes encrusts a body the Choir would have preferred to consume. *The Choir has not been able to do anything about this in eight centuries.*

### Imminent Archive Catchers — most loathed mortal-faction

Per §V (Pale Curation): the Catchers preserve before death. This is the *opposite* of the Choir's offer (encasement *after* willing-death). The Choir actively monitors Catcher movements through substrate-leakage. **A Catcher cell in a Spore-Wedded village would be eradicated within days.** A Catcher cell in mixed territory (e.g., Marrowstye, per Phase 2 v2) is *tolerated* only because the Choir cannot extend openly there without provoking the Pale Curation mainline.

### Driving Bloom — actively suppressed; the substrate war

The Choir's ~120-year internal war. The Bloom is, biologically, *Choir* — same mycelium, same spore-form, same encasement-capable. But the Bloom's doctrine (hijack individuals; *control*, not dissolution) is the *opposite* of Choir doctrine. The Choir's substrate-mind treats the Bloom as a *necrosis within itself.*

- **Most of the war is invisible to the surface.** Two substrate-networks fighting in the dark, in the deep catacombs, by competitive growth, by enzyme deployment, by encasement-of-Bloomed-bodies (the Choir consumes Bloom infections preferentially, treating them as the highest-priority substrate-material).
- **The Choir-Bloom war is one of the few situations where the Choir cooperates openly with surface factions.** The Choir will *help* a Pale Curation party clean out a Bloomed village — Choir tendrils handle the substrate-rot; Pale Curation handles the surviving humans (some of whom they preserve, some of whom they evacuate). This is the rare "good guy" Choir moment that complicates player loyalties.

### Inkbound — even more enemy than Palimpsest

The Inkbound are not a separate faction; `10_Inkbound.md` commits them as a Palimpsest sub-sect. They are still *more* offensive to the Choir than ordinary Palimpsest practice. The Inkbound's skin-page scribes (Ink-Bathed bodies that *are* the manuscript) are an especially hard form of memory-hardening — *living substrate willingly turned into permanent record.* The Choir's response is quiet but absolute hostility.

## VII. Player-facing surface

### How the player encounters the Choir

In rough chronological order across a typical run:

1. **Ambient horror** — early game, Tier-1 to 2 places. The player sees Choir-tendrils growing in cracks, hears about Spore-Wedded villages from frightened surface-dwellers, encounters a Bloomed corpse (and is told the Choir is *cleaning it up*).
2. **First Wall-Catch** — accidental, by jumping or falling into a Choir Cathedral archetype sinkhole (per IDEAS.md Wall-Catching). The player wakes embedded in the wall; meets the substrate-network in their head; escapes (or chooses one of the four escape paths). **This is the player's first direct conversation with the Choir.**
3. **Choir-Touched status** — acquired by encasement, Wall-Catching, or specific quest content. Persistent. Visible to other Choir-Touched and Inquiry-aligned NPCs. Changes the player's standing with the Choir incrementally.
4. **Spore-Wedded village trade** — the player can trade with Spore-Wedded villages for unique substrate-craft items (Choir-Iron weapons that *don't* hurt their wielder despite being anti-fungal; substrate-resistance amulets; etc.).
5. **Senior substrate-elder dialogue** — at sufficient Choir-reputation, the player can enter a Choir Cathedral *peacefully* and speak with an encased elder. These are slow conversations (literal in-game time; the substrate replies on its own pace) but deep. The Choir explains its doctrine in detail; the player can ask hard questions; the substrate engages.
6. **The Pilgrimage to the Wedded** — culmination of the Choir-path. The player travels to the Deepest Cathedral with appropriate offerings, undergoes a multi-stage ritual entry, and meets the Wedded. *This is the most-difficult-to-earn audience with any of the Six gods.* The conversation is dream-mediated; the Wedded speaks slowly; her old face appears and disappears in the substrate around the player.
7. **The Consume ending negotiation** (if the player is on that path) — the Wedded and the player negotiate *how* the Root will be consumed: how slowly, with what dignity, with what concessions to the other gods, with what role for the player after. This is one of the most consequential conversations in the game.

### Reputation system

Choir-reputation is gained by **act, not service.** The Choir does not have a quest-giver hierarchy in the conventional sense; the substrate-collective notices acts and records them in the network's collective memory. Specific acts that gain reputation:

- Allowing or encouraging encasement of the dead
- Donating personal memory through the encasement-bargain
- Refusing to seal or salt-cure a body
- Helping the Choir against the Driving Bloom
- Voluntary Wall-Catching (the *first* Choir-tendril embrace; subsequent ones gain less reputation but are read as deeper acceptance)
- Carrying Choir-Touched status visibly and not seeking removal

Acts that lose reputation:
- Carrying or using Choir-Iron-edged weapons in substrate territory
- Killing Choir tendrils (a hostile act in the substrate-collective's view)
- Sealing or preserving the dead in ways that prevent dissolution
- Helping Pale Curation Catchers
- Aiding or hosting Driving Bloom infection

**Critical mechanic:** Choir-reputation is sticky. Once gained or lost, it persists through the run; the substrate has perfect memory of the player. *There is no faction in CoO that remembers the player's history more reliably than the Choir.*

### How the Choir requests things

The Choir does not give quests through quest-givers. The Choir *requests* through:

- **Dreams.** Choir-Touched players have dreams. Dreams contain suggestions, sometimes specific (*"find the body in the bog and bring it to us"*), sometimes diffuse (*"go to where the substrate is thin"*).
- **Substrate-leakage.** Walking through Choir-substrate produces visible cues — pulsing bio-light, growth patterns, occasional substrate-formed face-shapes. The player learns to read these as the Choir's communications.
- **Encased-elder direct dialogue.** At sufficient reputation, the player can simply *ask* an elder what the Choir wants and receive an answer.
- **The Wedded's direct address.** At the highest reputation, in pilgrimage, the Wedded speaks to the player without mediation. This is rare and weighty.

### Rewards

The Choir's rewards are doctrinally consistent — *substrate-integration*, in escalating degrees:

- **Choir-Iron-resistant gear** — early; weapons or items that work in Choir territory without provoking
- **Substrate-perception abilities** — see through Choir-substrate cell-walls; sense Bloom infection; hear the *sari... sari...* better
- **Reduced Wall-Bound accumulation** (per IDEAS.md voluntary Wall-Catching exploit grammar) — the substrate welcomes the player back faster but the cost-ceiling rises slower
- **Substrate-network route-knowledge** — Wall-Catching destinations the player can request, not just receive (the only way to *control* Wall-Catch routing)
- **Memory-Consumption discounts** — paying the Choir to eat a memory costs less for Choir-aligned players
- **The endgame option:** voluntary integration. Become a Choir node yourself. **The most permanent decision in the game.**

## VIII. Quest spine

### Early-game arc (Tier 1-2; pre-Choir-Touched)

- **The Sill incident** (per Phase 3). Sill hears the *sari... sari...* for the first time; the player investigates. A Choir tendril is found growing in a place it shouldn't be. The player's first encounter with substrate as a *fact*, not a children's story. The Choir is not yet adversary or ally — it is *strange*.
- **The Marrowstye Catcher quest** (per Phase 2 v2). Marrowstye has an unwanted Catcher cell. The player can investigate. The Choir is *not* the obvious solution — the Catchers and the Choir hate each other — but the player can begin reading the Choir-Catcher dynamic here.
- **Lampwell tendril-management.** Lampwell's leadership wants the Choir tendrils pruned back from the beetle hatcheries. The player can take the contract. Doing so loses small Choir reputation; refusing loses small Concord reputation. *First-act faction-tradeoff.*

### Mid-game arc (Tier 2-3; Choir-Touched possible)

- **First Wall-Catch event** — either accidental or chosen. The player escapes by one of the four paths (per IDEAS.md); each path tells the player something different about the Choir.
- **Spore-Wedded village access.** Reach Spivenor or another Spore-Wedded settlement. Begin trading. Learn the village's relationship with the Choir; learn that some catacomb-villagers chose this; some were born to it; some are still uncertain.
- **Encased-elder dialogue.** First in-Cathedral conversation. The substrate's voice. The Choir's doctrine explained at length, by someone who has held it for centuries.
- **The Bloom-cleansing offer.** A Bloomed village is identified; the Choir asks the player to help cleanse it (substrate-network + the player's physical action). Accepting reveals the Choir-Bloom dynamic and gives the player a *Choir-aligned good deed* to weigh against the Choir's larger ambitions.

### Late-game arc (Tier 3-4; high Choir reputation)

- **The Pilgrimage.** Multi-stage journey to the Deepest Cathedral with offerings and ritual preparations. Each stage is a dialogue or test.
- **Meeting the Wedded.** Dream-mediated. The substrate around the player forms her old face occasionally during the conversation. The Wedded does not lecture; she *listens* to the player and *replies*, slowly. She asks the player questions about their own losses, their own choices, their own memories. The conversation is intimate in a way that should be uncomfortable.
- **The Stirring Faction approach.** At some point a senior substrate-elder of the Stirring Faction will approach the player and argue for *immediate* action. The player can side with the Patient Faction or the Stirring Faction (or refuse to engage with internal Choir politics).
- **The Quiet Faction encounter.** Rarer. A player who has reached very high Choir reputation may find themselves contacted (in dream) by the Quiet Faction. They explain their position. They ask the player to consider whether *the Wedded's old kindness* should be honored. **This is the seed of a non-Consume Choir-aligned outcome** — supporting the Quiet Faction may shift the Wedded's position during the Renewal ritual.

### Ending-aligned arcs

- **Consume path.** The player aligns with the Stirring or Patient Faction, negotiates the Consume ending's terms with the Wedded, performs the ritual at the Root. *Or:* the player voluntarily integrates en route — Wall-Bound to 100%, the player becomes a Choir node, the Consume ending happens with the player *as* part of the new mycelial Tree.
- **Preserve path.** The Choir resists. The player must work *against* the Choir to seal the Root. This is the hardest path against this faction — the Choir's substrate-network is enormous and the Choir is patient. Direct confrontation is doomed; the player must outmaneuver, not outfight.
- **Renewal path.** The player needs the Wedded's *agreement.* The Wedded will only agree if the new Tree has substantial mycelial character (Phase 1 v2's "Mycelial Renewal" flavor) — *or* if the player has reached the Quiet Faction and shifted her toward accepting a non-Consume completion. *Both* paths are possible; both are expensive.

## IX. Implementation and writing notes

- **The Wedded's ultimate true position.** Later canon keeps Selen strongly Consume-aligned but conditionally persuadable toward Renewal through the Quiet Faction / mycelial-substrate concession path. Her old kindness is not a reversal; it is the reason the persuasion is possible.
- **What the Wedded and the Rooted actually say to each other.** They communicate constantly through substrate. The content of those communications is mostly unknown to other characters. Are they reconciled? In ongoing argument? Co-conspiring? *Late-game lore-reveal candidate, possibly through the Rooted's dream-channel to the Listening villages.*
- **The Quiet Faction's true size.** Open whether they are a tiny minority or a substantial-but-quiet plurality. If the latter, the Choir's position on the Root may be far less unified than the surface understands.
- **Whether voluntary integration is *fully* reversible.** Per IDEAS.md voluntary Wall-Catching exploit, the cost ceiling rises with each visit. Current design recommendation: integration at 100% is absolute *for the run*; the next run's player can encounter the previously-integrated player as a substrate-elder.
- **The Wedded's name.** Later canon names her **Selen** and makes the loss of that name load-bearing: everyone has forgotten it except the Counter.
- **Whether the Driving Bloom can be folded back into the Choir.** Final canon treats the Bloom as "no one home": it can be biologically suppressed or, after true Renewal, reduced to an ordinary disease, but not reconciled as a faction.

## X. Tonal balance check

- **Biological horror.** Maximum. The Choir is the project's biological-horror anchor faction. Mycelial integration, encasement, the substrate growing toward what it loves, the Wedded's body partly-still-woman-shaped after a thousand years — these are the core images.
- **Cosmic horror.** Layered. The substrate's *kindness* is the cosmic horror — the Choir does not want to harm; it wants to include; the inclusion erases you. The Wedded's old face surfacing in the substrate. The 870-year patient waiting. *"There is room in us for everyone."*
- **Comedy.** Quieter here than in other faction docs, by design. The Choir is not a comedy faction. *One* comedy beat lives at the boundary: the Saccharine Concord's brisk pragmatic trade with the Choir (the Concord delivers bodies for dissolution; the Counter sends Choir invoices; the Wedded refuses to discuss the invoices but the Choir pays). The kindness, again.
