## 2. Depth audit

### 2.1 Mythic-historical stratification

**Quote (bible L23):** *"Every faction in the game is defined by
their relationship to this event."*

**Strong.** The bible commits to event-relativity as a structural
principle — every faction is a stance toward the cataclysm.

**Thin.** No two factions disagree about *when* the cataclysm
happened. There is one unspoken assumption — six hundred years
(Choir L51, "*we have dreamed of the mountain for six hundred
years*") — and nothing else weighs in. Six hundred years from
*whose* calendar? The Choir does not keep walker-time; the
Palimpsest exists in many tenses at once. That the bible's only
date-claim is a Choir self-report should be a feature, not a
default.

**Concrete enhancement (bible §The Cataclysm, append after L23):**

> No two factions agree how long ago the cataclysm was. The
> Choir says *six hundred years,* but the Choir counts in
> fruitings and root-extensions and is rounding upward. The
> Saccharine Concord's founder is dated by the Concord to *the
> twelfth honey-year* — they count from the founding, not the
> ending, and politely decline to convert. The Pale Curation
> dates by intake-tag and refuses to give a year aloud at all,
> on the principle that giving a date to an outsider is a
> reading the outsider has not earned. The Glassblown count from
> *the first cooled glass after the burning,* which gives a
> different number every time the question is asked. The
> Palimpsest, asked, returns the question to the asker: *"how
> long ago does it feel."*

Pulls dating itself into the contested-history zone the bible
already inhabits at the level of *names.*

### 2.2 Unreliable in-world authorship

**Quote (bible L133, attributed):** *"It is not evil. It is an
eraser that believes it is a pen." — The Palimpsest on the
Choir.*

**Strong.** Two attributed cosmic-faction quotes (L43, L133),
and the May 8 audit proposed two more (Concord + Curation).

**Thin.** Brine Communion and Thermoclaves still have no
attributed voice anywhere on disk. The bible's only Brine
sentence is the *they hate the Thermoclaves* clause at L208;
the Thermoclaves' is the same clause from the other side. Two
named factions whose hatred is *load-bearing* worldbuilding,
and neither has been heard.

**Concrete enhancement (bible §How They View Each Other, append
two more attributed quotes after the May 8 pair):**

> *"Salt is older than fire. The fire forgets this every
> generation. It is the keepers' work to remind them, gently
> when we can, with the tide when we cannot."* — Brine
> Communion shore-keeper, recorded marginal in a borrowed
> Concord almanac

> *"The sea has no hearth. We do not begrudge it for being what
> it is. We only ask that what it is, it remain — and not climb
> our shore unannounced."* — Thermoclave second-shift watcher,
> from a hearth-edge inscription

Two single-sentence hate-axis voices. The Brine quote frames
the hatred as a parent's patience; the Thermoclave frames it as
hospitality refused. Each hates the other for a different
reason and would not recognise the other's grievance as the
same fight.

### 2.3 Mutating motifs across cultures

**Quote (bible L121):** *"The beings who belong to the Palimpsest
are those who have become aware of the layering."*

**Strong.** The Palimpsest has a category — *the aware* — that
is not the same as faction membership.

**Thin.** Other factions have their own words for the same
people, and none of those words appear anywhere on disk. A
Choir-aligned mycelial would call a Palimpsest-aware walker
something specifically Choir-flavoured; a Concord factor would
have a trade-term; a Brine keeper would have a sea-term. The
bible just calls them *the aware,* which is the omniscient
narrator's neutral.

**Concrete enhancement (bible §The Palimpsest, append at the
end of "What It Actually Is"):**

> Each faction has its own word for the awakened reader. The
> Choir calls them *the still-warm* — not yet composted, not
> yet fruiting, but already *known to the soil.* The Saccharine
> Concord calls them *flickering customers,* a backroom
> trade-term for buyers whose preferences shift unpredictably
> between visits. The Pale Curation has two words: *partial-
> readers* (admiringly, of an ally) and *over-readers*
> (cautiously, of an outsider whose reading exceeds the
> available text). The Brine Communion calls them
> *changing-water* — a warning, not a description; the kind
> of water a sailor checks twice. The Thermoclaves prefer
> not to name them and gesture instead: a slow turning of the
> open palm toward the fire and away from it. *One hand shows
> two faces.*

Five faction-specific terms, each disclosing a worldview in
one phrase.

### 2.4 Cosmology with internal mechanical logic

**Quote (bible L171-173, on the Annotation and Overwrite):**
*"The Annotation — permanently alter the palimpsest itself.
Write memories that didn't happen. Create echoes of events that
never occurred. Fabricate history."*

**Strong.** The Annotation's "fabricate history" is a real
cosmological commitment — the past is editable.

**Thin.** The bible never specifies the rule when *two readers*
disagree. If the Annotation makes the past editable, what
happens when a Palimpsest-aligned NPC has annotated a location
*differently* than the player would? Does the more-recent
annotation overwrite the older one? Is there a contest? The
metaphysics has a single-author assumption baked in.

**Concrete enhancement (bible §The Manuscript / The Annotation,
append after L172):**

> Two annotations on the same location do not overwrite one
> another. They layer. A Palimpsest reader approaching such a
> place experiences both annotations as simultaneous and is
> required, by the cosmology's internal grammar, to *prefer
> one* — not by belief but by attention. The act of preferring
> dims the unpreferred annotation by a measurable amount; long
> enough preference erases it from anyone who arrives later.
> This is the only way the Palimpsest votes. It does not
> deliberate. It accumulates attention, and forgets what no
> one tends. The horror, for a careful reader, is this: a lie
> annotated and tended is more present in the palimpsest than
> a truth no one has read in a generation.

Adds a vote-by-attention rule and a specific edge case (lies
outvote unread truths) a quest can hinge on.

### 2.5 Apotheosis-shaped themes without aping CHIM

**Quote (bible L218):** *"The healthiest path might be moderate
reputation with both. But the game shouldn't make that feel
satisfying. The moderate path should feel safe — and in a world
this broken, this beautiful, this strange, safety should feel
like cowardice."*

**Strong.** The bible explicitly assigns the moderate path a
*felt cost* — cowardice, not punishment.

**Thin.** The bible specifies the felt cost of cowardice as a
design intent, but not a single experiential signature. What
does cowardice feel like in this game's vocabulary? The Choir
ending has an exhale that does not stop; the Palimpsest ending
has a last sentence that belongs to someone else (May 8
proposal). The neutral path has nothing. A player who arrives
at it deserves a closing image too.

**Concrete enhancement (bible §The Central Question, append
after L218):**

> The moderate ending closes with a small sound. The player
> stands at a crossroads they have stood at before. The Choir's
> tendrils withdraw a finger's width. The Palimpsest's
> overlay flickers and stays settled. Nothing happens. The
> player notices, for the first time, that nothing has been
> happening for a while. The end-screen carries one line:
> *you kept your shape. The world kept its shape. Both of you
> are smaller for it.*

Refuses the gotcha and refuses the elevation. The sentence
*both of you are smaller for it* lands the bible's *cowardice*
intent without using the word.

### 2.6 Linguistic texture

**Quote (bible L228):** *"Dialogue should shift pronoun usage."*

**Strong.** The Choir's pronoun-arc rule is the bible's
clearest dialogue-implementation instruction.

**Thin.** The Choir's grammar has a hole the bible has not
named: it has no passive voice. *"I was absorbed"* is not a
Choir sentence; the Choir has no language for being-acted-upon
because, from the inside, no one is. The bible can require
this without specifying a workaround for the moments the
player needs to *say* the unsayable thing.

**Concrete enhancement (bible §Choir Implementation Rules,
append after L230):**

> The Choir has no passive voice. *"I was absorbed by the
> Choir"* is not a sentence a Choir-aligned NPC can produce in
> their own grammar; it presumes a doer-and-done-to that the
> Choir does not believe in. When a tendril *must* describe the
> moment of joining — an outsider has asked, and the answer
> matters — the Choir reaches for a middle voice it has built
> from substitution. *"I came into the soil and the soil
> received,"* a tendril will say, instead of *"the soil took
> me."* A walker who has learned to listen for this notices
> when a tendril slips: a passive sentence from a Choir mouth
> is the surest sign that the speaker is not yet wholly the
> Choir. Grib (ROTCHOIR L97) uses passives. Mogu does not.

A diegetic test for *how-far-Choir-the-speaker-is,* legible
inside the existing five voices.

### 2.7 Anthropological framing

**Quote (bible L236):** *"Failure states should be permanent
and non-violent. The 'Remember the Forgotten' quests where the
player fails to stabilize a fading memory result in permanent
loss — not death, but forgetting."*

**Strong.** The Palimpsest's failure-state design is sharp.

**Thin.** The bible never describes a Palimpsest-aligned
*settlement* the way May 8 began doing for the Choir. The
Choir grows villages. What does a Palimpsest-aware settlement
look like on a Tuesday morning? The bible implies isolated
readers; nothing on disk says whether they cluster, and if
they do, what shape the cluster takes.

**Concrete enhancement (new bible subsection §Palimpsest-Aware
Settlements, after the §Palimpsest Ending block):**

> Palimpsest-aware settlements do not look like settlements.
> They look like ordinary towns whose residents stay slightly
> longer in doorways than is comfortable, and walk slightly
> further around certain unmarked patches of street. The
> houses are unremarkable; the doors do not match; nothing is
> built tall. There are no public squares — gathering in one
> place, in numbers, *thickens* the layering, and the
> townspeople have learned not to. Children are warned away
> from rooms where someone has died, not for fear of ghosts
> but for fear that the room *remembers too clearly* and a
> child standing inside it long enough may learn the dead
> person's habit of speech. Marriage is announced by the
> couple sweeping the same threshold for a week — a small
> shared act repeated until the threshold *takes* the
> repetition into the layering. The dead are not buried in the
> town. They are buried at distance, in unmarked plots, on the
> theory that a grave is a reading-aid and the dead deserve to
> not be read.

Where the Choir-aligned village's customs are *communal*
(shared pot, twice-naming, fruiting-grove burial), the
Palimpsest-aware settlement's are *spaced* (avoidance,
distance, threshold-time). Each culture's daily texture is the
metaphysics turned into furniture.

## 3. Faction asymmetry — Thermoclaves

Rotation target this run. Last sitting did Pale Curation. Of
the side-factions, Thermoclaves were last touched by the
2026-05-07-1901 chain, which drifted into recursive
cadence-authority taxonomy and proposed nothing player-facing.
This sitting proposes one concrete custom and one specific
historical incident — the texture of the bible's *they hate
each other* line.

**The hearth-name rite.** Every Thermoclave household fire is
unnamed for its first year. On the first anniversary, the
household gathers and the eldest watcher gives the fire a
name. The name is short, specific, and not chosen — it is
*found* by listening: the watcher names what the fire has
already said to them across the year. *Crooked-burn,
ash-evening, second-hand-quiet.* The name is private and is
not shared with non-Thermoclaves; outsiders are told the
household-fire is *the fire,* with no qualifier. To douse a
named fire (even by accident, even when the house is moving)
requires a public expiation: the watcher who let the fire die
must stand at the hearth-edge of a related household for a
fortnight, taking no shifts, and may not be the named-watcher
of any new fire for a year.

**The long minute.** A specific historical grievance, taught
to Thermoclave children at second-shift age. Generations ago,
on a stretch of coast no Thermoclave will name to an outsider,
a Brine Communion crew came ashore at dusk and doused a
watch-hearth that had burned, named, for nine generations.
The fire's name is not given (the name was lost with the
hearth), but the duration is exact: the dousing took *one
minute, by salt and by hand.* The Thermoclaves call this *the
long minute* and use it as the standard against which all
later wrongs are measured. The Brine Communion has its own
account of the same evening; it appears in their songs as
*the cleaning of the western shore* and is sung as a working
song, a thing one whistles while coiling rope. Neither party
considers their account a version. Each considers it the
event.

The hearth-name custom makes Thermoclave hospitality a
specific labour: a guest is asked, on entry, whether they have
ever doused a fire — not as accusation but as introduction. The
long minute makes the Brine grievance historical and ongoing
both: every sailor inherits the song; every Thermoclave child
inherits the count. The Thermoclaves *listen for* the fire's
name; the May 8 Curation reads *by hand,* doubting the eye.
Both are reading-cultures, neither cosmic — together, the
bible's two best candidates for a *third position* the player
can move toward without being absorbed by either axis.

## 4. Contradictions and bleeds

**The bible (L21):** *"It overwrote reality itself, imperfectly,
the way text on a medieval manuscript was scraped off and
written over with the old words still faintly visible
underneath."* The bible's metaphysics is a *manuscript* — a
literary, scribal metaphor. The cataclysm is an act of
overwriting. There is no machine, no substrate, no leak.

**The JSON (L427-428):** *"Beneath matter, beneath energy,
beneath time itself — there is something. A foundation. The
old world built machines that touched it, and the touching
broke both the machines and the substrate. What leaked through
the cracks changed everything."*

**The contradiction.** The JSON imports an entirely separate
cosmology — *substrate, machines, leak* — that has no
referent in the bible. The bible's manuscript metaphor is
*scribal:* an act of writing erased and rewritten by other
writers. The JSON's substrate metaphor is *technological:* a
foundation accidentally cracked by a machine and altered by
what came through. These are not the same shape. A medieval
manuscript is overwritten *deliberately, by readers.* A
substrate is cracked *accidentally, by builders.* The two
accounts assign moral weight to different actors and offer
different prescriptions (read carefully vs. don't build
recklessly).

**Proposed fix.** Don't reconcile them. Treat them as
faction-coded accounts — *the substrate cosmology* is the
Choir's account of the cataclysm, told to a walker because
the Choir prefers a story in which the old world's *hubris*
is the cause and integration is the cure. Add a single
sentence to the bible's §The Cataclysm acknowledging that the
Choir's preferred account is mechanistic and the Palimpsest's
preferred account is scribal, and that *neither has authority
over the other's account* — the bible's narrator-voice is
the only one neutral enough to call it a *cataclysm* and stop
there.

> The two cosmic factions tell the cataclysm differently. The
> Choir tells it as a *substrate* broken by old-world
> machines — a fall caused by hubris, healed by integration
> (RotChoir.json: WhyBad, RepairSubstrate). The Palimpsest
> tells it as a *manuscript* overwritten by an unknown reader
> — a wound caused by reading carelessly, healed only by
> reading better. Both stories are true to the teller. Neither
> is true to the other.

This converts an unflagged contradiction into a deliberate
faction-coded ambiguity, in line with the bible's existing
stance that the cataclysm has multiple incompatible accounts.

## 5. The "merchant's diary" suggestion

A *Brine Communion mariner's marginal,* scribbled in the back
of a borrowed Concord almanac. The mariner has been keeping
the almanac as a personal log on a long coastal run. The
major reveal — about the Thermoclave hearth-name rite — lands
parenthetically, inside an entry about weather.

> Day 9 of the eastern run. Wind veered south-southeast at
> the second bell. The almanac says *expect the calm by
> evening,* which the almanac always says. The almanac is
> wrong about wind in this latitude and right about everything
> else. I keep using it. Mer says I keep using it because I am
> sentimental. I am not sentimental. I am thrifty.
>
> Day 11. Anchored off the small headland north of the
> hearth-towns. Did not go ashore. Mer went ashore for water
> and came back annoyed. The shore-keeper asked her, before
> she said a word, whether she had ever doused a fire. Mer
> said no — meaning *not deliberately,* the way our people
> answer that question — and the keeper let her draw water.
> Mer says the keeper's question is a greeting, not an
> accusation, and that we have misread it for generations.
> She is right. I am writing it down so I do not forget. I
> will probably forget.
>
> (Mer also says that the watch-hearth on this headland has
> a name, and that the name is *first-shift-quiet,* which she
> heard the keeper say to the fire when she did not think she
> was being heard. I do not know what to do with this. I am
> writing it down anyway. It seemed important to the keeper.
> The fire did not seem to mind us being there. I have
> noticed, on this run, that fires never seem to mind us being
> there. It is the keepers who mind.)
>
> Day 13. Calm by evening, exactly as the almanac said.
> Crossed the keepers off the list of *people we cannot work
> with.* Added them to the list of *people who are tired of
> us.* Salt is older than fire — but tiredness is older than
> both, and the keepers' tiredness is, I think, mostly the
> long minute. We sing the cleaning. They count the minute. No
> one will set this right, and the work goes on.
>
> Day 14. Honey wine from the Concord factor at the cove.
> Trade was fair. He asked after our salt. Took two casks.
> Did not ask about the keepers. We did not say. There are
> some things that, from kindness, one does not bring to a
> Concord table.

(298 words.) The reveal — *the watch-hearth has a name, and
the name is first-shift-quiet* — lands parenthetically in a
weather entry. The mariner is writing about wind, not about
the Thermoclaves; the fact sticks because it sits inside the
writer's own admission that they will probably forget. Two
side-pieces also land: that the Brine Communion *knows* about
the long minute (and sings the cleaning anyway), and that
there is a working diplomatic silence between Brine and
Concord about the grievance (*we did not say*). The Brine
register is sailor's prose — short sentences, weather, lists,
a noted-and-moved-on quality — a different grain than the May
8 Concord factor's.

## 6. Anti-style self-check

Quoting one sentence from this audit:

> *"A walker who has learned to listen for this notices when
> a tendril slips: a passive sentence from a Choir mouth is
> the surest sign that the speaker is not yet wholly the
> Choir."*

Does this sound like a TES book? No. The register is plain
modern-prose linguistic-anthropology. The technical content
(*passive voice, middle voice, slipping*) is grammatical
vocabulary, not archaic-fantasy diction. There are no
*verily, hath, by [deity-name],* no apostrophe-laden
proper nouns, no inverted clauses. The sentence describes a
diegetic test — *a walker has learned to listen* — in the
voice of a careful contemporary observer. The construction
*is the surest sign that* is unmarked modern English and
would read identically in a non-fiction essay about a real
language community. No rewrite needed.
