### The etymology of *concord*

The Concord uses the word *concord* with a specificity the other
factions do not register. To a Choir-tendril or a Palimpsest-attuned
walker, the word reads as *agreement-between-parties* — a
commonplace. Inside Concord ledger-language, *concord* is a
**rateable** noun: it names the price two parties have written
themselves into agreeing on. A book-keeper would not say *we are in
concord;* they would say *we have signed concord* (the price is
fixed) or *we are quoting concord* (the price is offered, awaiting
counter-signature) or, for a deal that has gone wrong, *the concord
has spoiled* — the agreement has soured, the price stands but the
parties no longer want it.

This produces a small inventory of working idioms the Concord uses
and the other factions misread:

  - *signed concord* — the price is fixed; both parties have written
    themselves into the agreement and may not exit without paying out.
  - *quoting concord* — an offer is on the table.
  - *spoiled concord* — the agreement was signed and has gone wrong;
    the price stands.
  - *standing concord* — an old agreement no living party signed but
    that has remained binding by inheritance. The Concord's
    relationship to the cataclysm is named, internally, as *the oldest
    standing concord* — an agreement no one alive remembers signing,
    that has nonetheless been priced into every Concord ledger since.

The bible's L185 passage about *what the Saccharine Concord's founder
actually did* — a high-rep Palimpsest revelation — gains a working
edge here: the Concord's foundational price was paid by parties who
did not know they were signing, and the modern Concord's whole
ledgering practice is a centuries-long performance of the position
*we did not sign this; we are nonetheless bound by it.* The Concord's
moral register is not *good faith* but *paid-and-keep-paying.*

**Insertion target:** new short sidebar in the bible, post-L218, in
the same zone as item 14 (the Concord book-keeper pull-quote). Two
paragraphs, ~180 words. Drop-in once the surrounding agenda is
absorbed.

## 6. Anti-style self-check

**Quoted sentence:**

> *"The Concord's moral register is not good faith but
> paid-and-keep-paying."*

Working register, plain-spoken, observational. *Moral register* is a
working-anthropology term, not a heraldic one. *Good faith* is a
flat phrase, not capitalised, not personified. *Paid-and-keep-paying*
is a hyphenated working idiom — the kind of compound a clerk or a
working anthropologist would coin to compress an observation, not
the kind of compound a Tamrielic chronicler would build (those
compounds are nominal-archaic: *sword-of-the-dawn*, *mantle-bearer*).
The verbs are flat: *is not, but.* No archaism, no apostrophe-
fantasy nominal forms, no inversion.

TES rewrite (forbidden): *"The Concord's faith is not the faith of
men, but the faith of the Coin That Keeps."* I did not write that.
The forbidden version's tells: *the faith of men* (universalising
heraldic substitution); *the Coin That Keeps* (capitalised
allegorical proper-noun); *not… but…* in a weighty doublet (TES
doublet rhythm); the implicit personification of *concord* as a
deity-coin. The actual sentence's anti-tells: lower-case throughout,
hyphenated working idiom, the unceremonial *paid-and-keep-paying*
landing as bookkeeping not theology.

Pass.
