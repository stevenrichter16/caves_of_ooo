Sitting 15 lands the form.

### The shoaling-house and the mass-clerk

Every Brine held-depth village maintains a **shoaling-house** —
a low building at the highest reach of the spring tide, half-
submerged at full moon, dry at neap. Inside, on stone shelves
sized for a standard book-blank, are the village's correspondence
with the under-tide. Each book is bound in waxed leather, written
on rag paper that does not pulp under water, filled on the dry
side of the tide-margin with a question, and committed to its
shelf at the start of a tide-cycle.

A book is **left** for a half-cycle, a full cycle, a month-cycle,
or a year-cycle — duration is the question's weight. A quick
question (*will the inland postings have rain by twelfth-month?*)
gets a half-cycle. A question that wants the under-tide to think
on it (*should this child be named Pell or Mell?*) gets a year.
Books left longer are **deep-set** and are usually questions the
village expects only a presence-of-answer to.

When the cycle closes, the book is collected, weighed, and read
by the **mass-clerk**. The book's weight before committal is
recorded. The weight after is recorded. The *difference* — a
few grams, sometimes a fraction — is the message.

### The mass-grammar

The mass-clerk's notebook (itself eventually committed under-
tide) carries the working grammar of mass-gain. The canonical
patterns:

  - **Even gain across the volume** — the under-tide
    *acknowledges*. Not yes, not no; *received.*
  - **Gain concentrated in the spine** — *answering the
    binding* — the question's framing, not its content. A
    correction: the question was wrongly asked.
  - **Gain in the front pages, none in the back** — *yes to the
    first part, silence on the second.*
  - **Loss of mass** — extremely rare. The under-tide has *taken*
    something from the book. The book is not re-committed and
    lives on the *taken-shelf* permanently.
  - **No measurable change** — the most common outcome. The
    under-tide has not yet read; the book is re-committed.

The pattern is the *grammar of the message*, not the message
itself. The message proper is the question the book asked, *now
read in the under-tide's grammatical case*. The Brine doctrine
is exact: the under-tide does not author replies in language. It
*reads* the question back in a tense the asker did not have when
they asked it.

### The under-tide doctrine — neither magic nor metaphor

The under-tide is not a god; it is not prayed to, only written
to, and the writing is in the asker's own hand. The under-tide
gives no information the village did not already have; it returns
the question with a grammatical case attached. The mass-grams
are real — a Concord-trained scale registers the same grams; the
Brine have invited Concord assayers to confirm. What the Concord
cannot confirm is the *grammar* — the pattern's reading is a
Brine craft. The Concord agrees the books gain mass; the Concord
disagrees that the mass is *language*. The Brine doctrine holds
that the disagreement is itself a mass-pattern — the Concord-
Brine seam is, in Brine reading, a question the under-tide is
taking a long cycle to answer.

> **Postings-record of bowl-maker Kelo-of-the-no-shore-mother,

> the new accounting.* Hand: charcoal. Annotations in the margin

> Posting: Threadmarket west-gate dry-walking station.
> Tide-mark of arrival: low.

>
>   1. salt-stone hollow → posting-keeper Mellis:

>      (standard. Mellis has said this six times in two years.
>       I suspect she is sending the same sentence to the same
>       shore by different bowls. This is allowed.)

>      "the tide is the bill that has not been weighed"
>      (NOT a remembered-tense sentence. Pell is asking the bowl
>      what the tide owes her. I gave her the bowl anyway. I will
>      write to the shoaling-house at her home village and ask

>      not have taken it as trade.)
>
>   3. dried-gourd → posting-keeper Tov:
>      "the tide is heavy at home tonight"
>      (the *heavy* word is wrong. Tov knows it is wrong. He

>      gourd and a second one without charge. The second is to
>      hold the *heavy*. He understood without being told.)
>
>   4. wax-lipped clay → no buyer.
>      (No posting-keeper this circuit could meet its price in
>      remembered-tense sentences. It will go to the next
>      circuit. I think it wants a sentence about a river, not
>      a sea.)
> ```
>
> *Margin, smaller ink, plainly later:* the journeyman confirmed
> Pell's bowl is part of a deep-set question — her home village
> has a book in the shoaling-house twelve cycles deep on the
> matter of *what the tide is for, in the standing-water years.*
> I am to walk back next circuit and return the bowl with the
> sentence un-asked, and ask Pell for a remembered-tense
> sentence in trade instead. She will understand.

## 6. Anti-style self-check

**Quoted sentence:**

> *"The Brine doctrine holds that the disagreement is itself a
> mass-pattern — the Concord-Brine seam is, in Brine reading, a
> question the under-tide is taking a long cycle to answer."*

Working register, gloss-form. *Mass-pattern* and *long cycle*
are working nouns the Brine use as casually as a mass-clerk
would. *Reading* is the verb the Brine use about under-tide
correspondence — colloquial, not heraldic. The doctrinal claim
arrives in the same noun-set the Brine use about an ordinary
book on an ordinary shelf.

TES rewrite (forbidden): *"Verily, the Holy Brine teach that the
Discord between Themselves and the Saccharine Concord is itself
a Sign — a Question writ upon the Under-Tide, which That Patient
Sea shall in its own Hour give Answer unto."* Tells: *Verily*;
*Holy Brine* (heraldic adjective); *Discord* (capital-D abstract
substituting for a working disagreement); *Sign*, *Question*
(allegorical capitalisations); *That Patient Sea* (TES *that-
which* personification); *shall in its own Hour give Answer
unto* (mythic-archaic future). The actual sentence: lower case
throughout, no allegorical substitution, *holds* and *answer*
working-register verbs.

Pass.
