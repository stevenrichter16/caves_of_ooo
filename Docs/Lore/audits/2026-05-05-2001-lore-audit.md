## 3. Faction asymmetry — Brine Communion rotates in

### The Brine name for the cataclysm: *the Long Slack*

The Brine Communion does not call it the cataclysm. *Cataclysm* is a
landlubber's word — a single percussive event, sudden, declarative.
The Brine's name for the same event is **the Long Slack**, and the
naming is mechanical, not poetic.

In tide-language, *slack* is the moment between flood and ebb when
the ocean is neither rising nor falling — the brief still hour twice
a day. A working tideman knows the slack is *part of the tide*, not
an interruption of it; it is the hinge the tide swings on. The
Brine teach that the cataclysm was a slack the tide forgot to come
out of. The world stopped moving in the way oceans stop moving
between strokes. Then it did not start again.

This produces a small inventory of working idioms the Brine use
about the world the cataclysm left behind:

  - *the Long Slack* — the cataclysm proper; the still that did not
    end.
  - *the tide's return* — the future-religious term. The Brine do
    not pray for the cataclysm to be undone. They watch for the
    moment the tide remembers it is a tide.
  - *standing-water years* — the Brine's name for the centuries
    since. A tideman would call standing water *bad water* — water
    that does not move stops being water and becomes something
    older, heavier, and less honest.
  - *plaiting against the slack* — the Brine knot-language for
    refusing the standing-water register, used in dispute (the
    1501 §3 *plaited refusal*) and in private grief (a knot tied
    on the day of a death and untied on the day of a remembering).

The bible's only direct mention of the Brine Communion (L208,
*the way the Thermoclaves and Brine Communion do*) frames them as
*hostile* — at war, in the bible's reading, with the Thermoclaves.
This is true in the bible's frame, but in the Brine's own frame,
the war is not theological; it is *a dispute about whether the
slack is a tide or a kind of thirst.* Thermoclaves treat the
standing-water years as the new normal — what they call *thermal
balance* the Brine call *bad water*. The two factions are not
enemies because they hate each other. They are enemies because
they cannot agree on whether the world is between strokes or has
stopped having strokes.

**Insertion target:** new short sidebar in the bible, post-L208, in

paragraphs, ~200 words. Drop-in once the surrounding agenda is

> already paying. The plaiting is what we do *while* paying.

> undated:* — both of you are right. The slack is the bill, the
> tide is the credit. The book is the receipt. The book did not

## 6. Anti-style self-check

**Quoted sentence:**

> *"The Brine teach that the cataclysm was a slack the tide forgot
> to come out of."*

Working register, plain-spoken, mechanical. *Slack* is a working
tide-language term, used here without italics or capital, the way
a tideman would use it. *The tide forgot to come out of* is
animated but not personified — *forgot* is colloquial, the kind of
verb a sailor would use about a stubborn sea, not the kind a
chronicler would use about a deity. *Cataclysm* lower-case, treated
as a translatable term not a heraldic proper noun. The sentence
sits inside a *the Brine teach that…* frame, which honestly marks
the line as Brine-internal teaching, not omniscient narration.

TES rewrite (forbidden): *"The Brine know the truth: the Long Slack
was when the Tide That Was forgot Itself, and so the World was
struck still."* I did not write that. The forbidden version's tells:
*the truth* (universalising heraldic substitution); *the Tide That
Was* (capitalised allegorical proper-noun in the TES *that-was*
construction); *forgot Itself* (capitalised reflexive pronoun
implying deity-status); *the World was struck still* (passive-voice
mythic-archaic with implicit agent). The actual sentence's
anti-tells: lower-case throughout, the unceremonial *forgot to
come out of* (a tideman's grammar, not a chronicler's), no
capitalised proper-nouns, the explicit teaching-frame *the Brine
teach that…* preserving authorial uncertainty.

Pass.
