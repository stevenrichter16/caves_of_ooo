**Mode B — narrow hold (09:15-style).** Narrow §3 deepening

Both modes maintain: no new bible-side absorption-ready item;
no new flagged candidate that grows the standing-flag count.

### 3.2 Mode-switching criteria

Decide between A and B at audit-start, after pre-draft hygiene
checks:

  - **Choose Mode A** when:
    - The most-recent-sitting was Mode B and the chain's recent
      output has been mostly content (multiple Mode B in a row
      drift toward chain-extension by accretion);
    - **OR** no genuinely additive deepening or resolution edit
      is available (verified via hygiene item 7);
    - **OR** the editor's pile is *visibly absorbing* — bible
      mtime moved on a recent fetch but the absorption is
      partial (more pile noise risks confusion).

  - **Choose Mode B** when:
    - The most-recent-sitting was Mode A and a genuinely
      additive deepening or resolution edit IS available
      (verified via hygiene item 7);
    - **AND** the deepening's working-content has a clear
      0501-style hook in a prior rotation that was never
      developed.

  - **Default when uncertain:** Mode A. Less is more durable
    than more-with-noise.

## 4. Narrow §3 deepening — Brine keel-singer's pedagogy

The 0501 §5 sailor's marginalia carried a four-line apprentice
hook:

> *Apprentice's first reading-rehearsal at the next port. He
> has the position right and the breath wrong. The breath is
> the whole reading. Tell him again.*

The pedagogy referenced is unworked — the chain has *position*
(somewhere on the deck) and *breath* (the controlling variable)
named, but no working-content for what either means
procedurally. The 09:57 develops the breath-pedagogy specifically.
**No new working-rule** — only the procedural shape of teaching
an existing one.

### The breath-pedagogy

A keel-singer's apprentice learns the breath in three working-
rehearsals, each tied to a working-port. The first is at the
*next-port* (the apprentice's first port-arrival as a salt-
marked hand); the second is at the *return-port* (the
apprentice's first return to home harbour); the third is at
*any-port* (the apprentice's first solo rehearsal, mid-voyage,
without senior witness).

  - **First rehearsal — the *full breath.*** The apprentice
    breathes in completely, holds at the top, sings on the
    full release. The senior corrects: *too full. The keel-
    song does not need the whole lung. The slow tide does
    not push the whole ship. It pushes the keel-line only.*
    The apprentice tries again. Most apprentices need three
    or four cycles to find the *keel-line breath* — about
    three-quarters of a full inhalation, held at the top for
    one heartbeat, released across two heartbeats. The senior
    measures by ear, not by counting.

  - **Second rehearsal — the *held breath.*** The apprentice
    sings while holding the breath partially (no top-of-
    inhalation pause). The senior corrects: *the hold is
    where the slow-tide finds you. Without the hold, you
    sing the wave, not the tide.* The held breath is the
    hardest of the three; many apprentices take a working-
    year to find it consistently.

  - **Third rehearsal — the *empty breath.*** The apprentice
    sings on the bottom of an exhale, the lungs almost
    empty. The senior says nothing. The apprentice must
    hear themselves — the empty breath teaches the difference
    between *singing the keel-line* and *singing into the
    keel-line.* Most apprentices fail this rehearsal on
    first attempt; the senior does not correct. The third
    rehearsal is the apprentice's own.

### Why the breath, not the position

The 0501 marginalia gave the position right, the breath
wrong. The senior's working-rule is: *the position is where
you stand; the breath is what reaches the water through the
keel.* A position-correct, breath-wrong apprentice produces
sound that does not reach. A position-wrong, breath-correct
apprentice produces sound that reaches but in the wrong
register; correctable. The breath is the whole reading
because the breath is what the slow tide can read.

### What this is, what this isn't

This deepening:

  - Names the **three-rehearsal pedagogy** (working procedure).
  - Names the **keel-line breath** (working-vocabulary, additive
    to 0501's *keel-singing*).
  - Names the **third-rehearsal-is-the-apprentice's-own** rule
    (working-pedagogy parallel to the 0001 §3 Concord *third
    pass — the senior says nothing during the rite*).

This deepening is **not**:

  - A new bible-side candidate.
  - A new working-rule beyond the 0501 keel-singing-vs-bow-
    singing distinction.
  - A pile-extending §5 artifact (no merchant's-diary fragment
    this sitting; the §3 paragraphs are complete in themselves).

Within Mode B narrow-content hold per §3.1.

## 5. Anti-style self-check

**Quoted sentence (from §4 *Why the breath, not the position*):**

> *"The breath is the whole reading because the breath is what
> the slow tide can read."*

Working register, gloss-form. *Breath* and *slow tide* are flat
working nouns the Brine use about the actual physiological
breath and the actual sea. *Read* is the keel-singing working-
verb (per 0501) — what the slow tide does to the keel-song.
The sentence's claim is procedural — *the part of the song the
tide responds to is the breath, not the words or the position* —
not theological. Lower-case throughout. The triple-clause
construction (*breath / whole reading / what the slow tide can
read*) is the kind a senior keel-singer would use to drill the
working-rule into an apprentice.

TES rewrite (forbidden): *"The Breath is the whole of the
Reading — for it is the Breath alone that the Sea, in her
ancient quiet, may yet hear and answer."* Tells: capitalisations
on *Breath*, *Reading*, *Sea*; appositional *in her ancient
quiet* (mythologisation of the sea); *yet hear and answer*
(KJV-archaic future-permissive). The actual sentence: *breath*,
*whole reading*, *slow tide*, *can read* — four working terms,
the procedural rule doing its own work.

Pass.
