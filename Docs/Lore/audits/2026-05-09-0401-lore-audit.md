## 2. Depth audit

### 2.1 Mythic-historical stratification

(03:01 §2.1 worked the *cause* of the cataclysm.
Today: *floor* — the deepest temporal layer any
faction can perceive.)

**Quote (bible L121):** *"A road that leads to a
city destroyed four hundred years ago — but if you
walk it at the right time, you arrive."*

**Strong.** *Four hundred years* is the bible's
only committed temporal scale.

**Thin.** That is the present-to-cataclysm distance,
not the depth a Palimpsest reader can reach below
it. Can a reader go back four thousand? Bible silent.

**Concrete enhancement (bible §The Palimpsest, append
after L125):**

> The palimpsest does not extend uniformly backward.
> A reader can step into the four-hundred-year layer
> with comparative ease. Below that, the reading
> thins. The thousand-year layer is reachable only
> at sites the old world considered sacred or
> heavily inhabited; readings come back partial, in
> noun-phrases without verbs. The four-thousand-
> year layer is the bible's name for the deepest
> floor any reader has reached and returned from.
> It is not silent — it is *louder* than the
> shallow layers, in a way the surviving readers
> describe as *being in a crowd that has stopped
> speaking language and started speaking weather.*
> No reader has gone deeper and named what they
> read. Whether the floor is the world's actual age
> or only the palimpsest's edition of it is a
> dispute the cosmology declines to settle. The
> Choir, asked, says *the soil remembers further
> than the manuscript does* — but does not specify
> how much further.

A committed temporal-depth scale with a Choir
counter-claim that preserves cosmic disagreement.

### 2.3 Mutating motifs across cultures

(03:01 §2.3 committed world-metaphors. Today: a
different load-bearing motif — *the player.* The
Choir says *walker.* No one else has been asked.)

**Quote (JSON L8):** *"We taste you on the air,
walker."*

**Thin.** Six other factions need a term. The
vacuum gets filled in dialogue ad-hoc, where the
only recurring term is *walker* — meaning the
Choir's naming has been silently winning by default.

**Concrete enhancement (bible §How They View Each
Other, append after L210):**

> The factions do not agree on what to call the
> player. The Choir says *walker* — naming the
> legs the Choir does not have. The Palimpsest
> says *the reader.* The Pale Curation says *the
> walker-handed one,* a working-form distinction
> marking the player as someone whose deposits
> enter the standing-shelf without prior custody.
> The Saccharine Concord says *the trade-friend*
> in courtesy and *the open ledger* in private.
> The Brine Communion says *the dry-walker* — not
> pejoratively; it means *one without a working-
> deck.* The Glassblown Remnant says nothing —
> they refer to the player by the last piece the
> player handed them, in working-form: *the bowl-
> walker, the hook-walker.* The Thermoclaves say
> *the iron-walker* whether or not the player has
> brought iron. The bible uses *walker* throughout
> only because its editor accepts the Choir's
> naming as the document's working grammar; this
> is itself a curatorial position.

Seven faction-terms, each grammar-loaded.

### 2.4 Cosmology with internal mechanical logic

**Quote (bible L147):** *"Touch objects and
experience their history."*

**Thin.** Bible does not say what happens when
*two* readers Echo-Read the same object. Do both
get the original imprint, or does the second get
the first reader's reading?

**Concrete enhancement (bible §Echo Reading L149,
append):**

> A second reader of an Echo-read object reads not
> the original layer but the *combined* layer —
> the original imprint plus the first reader's
> reading of it. By the third reading, the original
> hand is two layers down. A heavily-read object
> becomes Echo-opaque — not because the original is
> gone but because every recent reader is in the
> way. A novice reader is preferred for first
> contact with a previously-unread piece; an elder
> is preferred for a piece read many times, because
> they can sometimes see *through* the prior
> readings to the floor. The Pale Curation does not
> permit a single piece in their archive to be
> Echo-read more than three times in a working-
> generation. The fourth reading is *not refused;*
> it is simply not catalogued, and the keeper makes
> a small mark on the file-card. A piece with three
> file-card marks is moved to a sealed under-shelf
> for two generations of bench-quiet to *de-pattern.*

A read-stacking mechanic plus a Curation custom
that operationalises it.

### 2.5 Apotheosis-shaped themes without aping CHIM

**Quote (bible L107 vs L189):** *"You have carried
us so far... We will be waiting."* ↔ *"the player
becomes a memory, written into the palimpsest
forever."*

**Strong.** The Choir's ending is voiced and
addressed; thanks the player. The Palimpsest's is
described from outside; unaddressed; gives no
thanks.

**Thin.** The asymmetry is real but uncommitted.

**Concrete enhancement (bible §The Palimpsest
Ending L189, append):**

> Transcription is silent because there is no
> *Palimpsest* to speak. The Choir at the threshold
> can say *we have always been waiting* because the
> Choir is a body with a mouth-shape borrowed for
> the moment; the Palimpsest is a condition, not a
> body, and a condition cannot thank. What the
> player gets at Transcription is not gratitude but
> *recognition* — every Palimpsest reader who comes
> after them feels the player as a known shape in
> the manuscript, the way a frequent name in a long
> book becomes its own kind of company. The first
> three readers after a Transcription often report
> the same image: a person sitting at a desk,
> writing, who looks up briefly when read. The
> bible does not commit whether the player chose
> the desk-image or the palimpsest wrote it for
> them. Either way, a Transcribed walker is read
> more often than they were spoken to in life.

Asymmetry named; the *desk-image* committed.

### 2.6 Linguistic texture

(03:01 §2.6 fixed Choir address-grammar. Today:
Palimpsest tense — the routine spec's *tense
ambiguities* claim, never delivered.)

**Thin.** Every Palimpsest-voiced sentence in the
bible is standing-tense English. No committed
grammar.

**Concrete enhancement (bible §Palimpsest
Implementation Rules, append a new rule):**

> Palimpsest readers, in deep-reading, slip into a
> tense that does not exist in standing speech: the
> *was-being,* a continuous-imperfect treating the
> read past as still occurring. *He was being at
> the door* (he is at the door, in the layer I am
> reading). *She was being kind to me* (in a
> memory I am inside). The tense sounds like
> grammatical error in first hearing and is the
> surest dialogue-tell that a reader is no longer
> fully in the present. NPCs who hear a was-being
> conjugation should respond as though the speaker
> has briefly left the room. The reader sometimes
> notices and corrects to standing tense;
> sometimes does not. A reader who has stopped
> correcting is one of the *thin walkers* L240's
> *insubstantial* warning gestures at without
> naming.

A grammar-rule writers can apply, with a state-
escalation that lands L240's earlier hint.

### 2.7 Anthropological framing

(03:01 §2.7 committed Choir guesting. Today: a
Palimpsest mourning counter-rite.)

**Quote (bible L189):** *"the player becomes a
memory, written into the palimpsest forever."*

**Thin.** Bible names what *happens* but not what
living readers *do* when another reader dies.

**Concrete enhancement (new bible subsection §The
Reader's Mourning, after §The Palimpsest Ending):**

> A Palimpsest reader at the death of another
> reader sits at the dead reader's most-frequented
> object and Echo-reads it once. The reading is
> not for the dead — they are already in the
> manuscript — but for the living reader, who is
> permitting the dead reader's last layer to enter
> their own mind. The custom is called *the
> taking-in,* and is the only Palimpsest ceremony
> with a fixed liturgy: *I will carry what you
> carried,* spoken before touching. The reader
> does not speak for one day after. Other readers,
> encountering them in this day, do not greet
> them. A reader may take-in at most three other
> readers in a lifetime; a fourth would be *too
> many other hands at the same desk.* A reader who
> has taken-in three is called *full-handed* and
> is approached for advice the way other cultures
> approach an elder. There are eleven full-handed
> readers known to the standing scholarship at the
> time this document was edited. Their names are
> kept in the under-shelf; ⟦ ⟧.

A counter-rite to guesting; hard limit (three);
the *full-handed* social position; a redaction
that lands the §2.2 editor's-note frame.

## 3. Faction asymmetry — Pale Curation

Rotation target: longest gap (23:01 did the
fingerling-keeper office). Today: the architectural-
ritual side rather than the keeper-rank side.

**The empty shelf.** Every Pale Curation reading-
hall has a single shelf-bay always vacant — not
*currently* vacant, *structurally* vacant. It is
the highest bay in the eastern wall, third from
the corner, and built to hold nothing. Keepers
call it *the empty shelf,* and a keeper walking
past it on bench-rounds pauses for one breath
without breaking stride. The only Curation gesture
made without an enabling working-form.

**The reason given.** Senior keepers say the empty
shelf is *for the piece that has not yet been
recovered* — they will not say which. Junior
keepers say *for the piece whose working-line the
Curation has agreed not to read.* The two answers
are not contradictory (senior includes junior as
special case) but are differently weighted, and
the bench-grade boundary at which the answer
shifts is itself unwritten.

**Architectural rule.** A reading-hall is not
opened until the empty shelf is built; a hall
that *loses* its empty shelf through fire or
rebuild is no longer recognised cross-hall until
the bay is restored. The only Curation custom
that overrides working-form attestation.

**Sister-rite to the under-shelf.** The under-shelf
(§2.2) holds the *removed.* The empty shelf holds
the *not-yet-arrived.* The walker-handed pieces
on the standing-shelf are bracketed,
architecturally, between two absences.

**Player-side hook.** A player who deposits a piece
*on the empty shelf* is treated as having made a
claim about that piece — that it is the awaited
one. The keeper does not refuse the claim. They
also do not file the piece. It sits alone until
the keeper's senior arrives and either confirms or
quietly returns it to the standing-shelf. The mis-
deposit is recorded as *one walker's reading of an
empty bay,* under a separate working-form whose
existence most walkers never learn.

## 4. Contradictions and bleeds

**JSON L483:** *"They wish to preserve. We wish to
integrate. They see the old world as something
sacred that was lost."*

**The contradiction.** The Choir's account at JSON
L483 reduces the Palimpsest to a *preservationist
faction* — the same flattening bible §How They
View Each Other (L208) warns against. The bible
distinguishes *cling-to-husks* (Choir caricature,
L43) from *the Palimpsest's actual stance* (a
condition, not a position, L125). The JSON commits
the caricature as the Choir's voiced explanation —
fine, character-internally, *if* the bible
committed that the Choir does not understand the
Palimpsest. It does not. L208 says only *they
cannot understand why the other does what it
does;* not that the Choir is *factually wrong.*

**Proposed fix.** Add to bible §How They View Each
Other after L210:

> The Choir's account of the Palimpsest, spoken to
> walkers, is reliably a simplification — the
> Palimpsest reduced to *preservers* or
> *rememberers,* a faction-shaped account of a
> condition-shaped phenomenon. The Palimpsest
> similarly describes the Choir as *an eraser*
> (L133), which under-renders the Choir's
> digestive ontology by a similar amount. A walker
> who has spoken to both and notices the mutual
> under-rendering has identified the central
> epistemological feature of the cosmic axis: not
> two factions disagreeing, but two phenomena
> translating each other into registers neither
> lives in. Both translations are partial; both
> are good-faith.

Grants both factions' in-game caricatures
character-internal validity without letting the
bible's omniscient register endorse them as
factual.

## 5. The "merchant's diary" suggestion

A Pale Curation deposit-receipt — the routine
working-form filed when a walker hands a piece to
the standing-shelf. The reveal: the deposit is
correct, the working-line is read, the receipt is
filed — *and* the keeper has been unable to read
the depositor.

> **Standing-shelf deposit-receipt — western
> reading-hall, file-mark L-K-1, working-week 17,
> day 4, bench-keeper Aell Mond, fingerling
> seven-south.**
>
> Piece received: one bronze button, two-hole, the
> cross-stitching pattern of a working-coat from
> the river-foundry tradition — Mer-Aullenne or
> later Korn-Vellish, working-line readable on the
> reverse face under bench-light. Distinguishing
> mark: a small fire-pock at the upper-right
> corner of the obverse, post-foundry, suggesting
> later ownership. Working-line read: *J. Korn,
> apprentice-grade, of the second working-shift,*
> file-cross to coat-button series 14-K-71-J. Filed
> on the standing-shelf, bay four, west wall,
> working-row second.
>
> Walker-name: ⟦ ⟧.
>
> *Note for the under-shelf, working-line of this
> redaction held under file-mark L-K-1 sealed.* The
> walker who handed me the button I have read three
> times in the last working-year, on three separate
> deposits of three unrelated pieces. The walker
> answers to nothing I say back. They do not
> dispute when I say *walker-handed-one;* they do
> not confirm when I say a name I have tried. They
> do not Echo-read; they do not work-form; they do
> not appear to be a reader of any kind I can place.
> The button itself reads clean — the working-line
> is honest. The walker is the third I have met
> this generation whose working-line refuses to
> read. I am entering the deposit on the standing-
> shelf in the standing way and the walker-name
> in the under-shelf in the standing way, but I
> ask the senior keeper at the next bench-round:
> are these three the same walker, or three
> walkers, or none? My hand says one. My eye says
> three. My ear has not been asked. — A.M.

(~285 words.) Reveal: the *unread walker* — a
person whose working-line cannot be read by
Curation methods, but whose deposits are clean
and routine. Three of them in one keeper's
ledger; a late-game player at multiple halls
would fit the pattern. The reveal is
parenthetical — in the under-shelf note, the
administrative footer of an otherwise routine
receipt — and lands three facts in one shape:
the under-shelf of §2.2, the *walker-handed-one*
term of §2.3, and a class of people Curation
methods fail on.

## 6. Anti-style self-check

Quoting one sentence:

> *"The walker is the third I have met this
> generation whose working-line refuses to read."*

TES? No. *Working-line* is project-internal vocab;
*generation* in the keeper-grade sense is
institutional, not mythological; *refuses to read*
is a clerk's register of frustration. No *verily,*
no inverted clause, no apostrophe-laden noun, no
appeal to gods or saints. Lands as a junior
keeper's filing-margin annoyance — the working
register the project has been developing for the
Curation since 1801. No rewrite.
