## 2. Depth audit

### 2.1 Mythic-historical stratification

**Quote (22:33 §2.1):** *"The Brine Communion calls it the
Cooling — a word for the long after-storm in which the seas
finally settled and the surface stopped boiling."*

**Strong.** Name + calendar.

**Thin.** No canonised interior account of *what the Cooling
actually was*. Other bodies' cataclysm-words encode
mythography (Concord's *Dissolution* = contract dissolved;
Curation's *Severance* = record cut). *The Cooling* is so far
merely meteorological.

**Concrete enhancement (bible §The Cataclysm, append, after
the *Cooling*-name canonisation):**

> *The body keeps two incompatible accounts. The working-
> pilots' is the shorter: the cities burned, the seas boiled
> in sympathy, the boats already at sea outlasted both, and
> what survived survived afloat. The Cooling is the year the
> songs lost their cities — and a song without a city is a
> song that must be carried, which is, by the body's doctrine,
> what songs are for.*
>
> *The song-keepers' account is older and incompatible. The
> seas were already cooling before whatever burned the cities;
> the Cooling is older than any other body's cataclysm-name,
> and the cities' burning is a later, smaller, terminal event
> filed inside it. By this reading the Communion did not
> survive the Cooling — the Communion is what the Cooling,
> mid-progress, started doing. The two accounts are not
> reconciled. A working-pilot tells the first; a song-keeper
> may, in song-register at rendezvous, tell the second.*

Stratification by working-rank, not by era. Lands depth-
technique #1 without any external authority adjudicating.

## 3. Faction asymmetry — Brine Communion

### 3.1 Working-ship social structure

A Communion boat has no captain. The body refuses the office
on the same ground it refuses central archives: a single
authority over a working voyage is an attempt to file the
voyage in the moment of sailing it, and the voyage is not for
filing. Four working-positions, all rotating:

> *The* wheel-pilot *holds the boat for a single voyage.
> The wheel-pilot for any voyage is whoever the previous
> voyage's wheel-pilot tapped at landfall — silent, on the
> working-shoulder. A wheel-pilot may decline; declining is
> rare but not censured. The next-voyage wheel-pilot is then
> chosen by the song-keeper, alone, in song-register, on
> reasoning the working-crew is not entitled to.*
>
> *The* song-keeper *holds the working-songs and the dead-name
> register. The song-keeper is selected at rendezvous by silent
> bead-vote, but only when the standing keeper requests one —
> the request itself is the announcement of willingness to be
> replaced. A keeper who has not requested a vote cannot be
> replaced. A keeper who has requested one and lost the bowls
> sails one further voyage as working-crew, then performs
> hands-off — not the Thermoclave gesture (0901 §2.1) but a
> different one: right hand, palm-up, opening at no seam, held
> open the length of one breath. The previous keeper is then
> a ground-bound elder; sing-back begins on the next visit.*
>
> *The* bearings-pilot *holds the chart and the running
> reckoning, and is the position outsider-trade runs through.
> A Curator who has traded with a Communion ship for years
> may have only ever spoken to bearings-pilots and may not
> know the other officers exist.*
>
> *The* working-crew *is everyone else. The body's working-rule
> is that the working-crew is its most important position: the
> songs migrate through the crew at sing-back, and the crew is
> therefore the only position whose absence ends the body.*

### 3.2 Naming customs

A Communion sailor has two names: a *working-name*, given at
birth in working-register; and a *song-name*, given by the
song-keeper at the moment the sailor first takes a turn at
the wheel. The keeper does not invent the song-name — she
sings a phrase of an existing song in song-register and names
the new wheel-turn for the phrase that surfaces. The sailor
is *holding* the song-name *for the song*: the name belongs
to the song, the sailor is its current keeper. When the
sailor dies, the song-name returns to general circulation and
may be re-assigned in the next generation. Two sailors a
hundred years apart may share a song-name without the body
considering them related — they have held the same phrase, no
more.

The working-name is the body's filing-surface for outsiders.
Curators have been told the working-name is *the sailor's name*
and have filed accordingly. The song-name is, by the body's
strict reading, not a name an outsider can hear without
entering song-register — and song-register is the only space
across the body's grammar that admits the dead. A Curator
asking for *the sailor's name* and receiving a working-name is
being told the truth in the register governing the asking.
The song-name is the body's reservation.

### 3.3 The deep-water rendezvous

The rendezvous — the *Communion* of *Brine Communion* — is the
body's only formal social ritual. Two or more boats meet in
agreed waters; working-crews share a single *Communion-pot*,
ingredients whatever each boat could spare; the meal is eaten
in silence; then the song-keepers from each boat exchange one
song each. The exchange is the body's only mechanism for
songs to cross between Communions. Outside rendezvous, a song
belongs to its keeper's boat. A song-keeper who has heard
another keeper's song outside rendezvous *has not, by the
body's reading, heard it*. The exchange-rule is grammatical,
not ethical: outside rendezvous the song is in the wrong
conjugation.

### 3.4 The salt-sacrament reconciliation (folding 22:42)

The 22:42 stub posited *uld-tide* as a deeper self-name and
salt-as-sacrament as theology. Fold: *Brine Communion* is the
working-register self-name (Curation-accepted as canonical);
*uld-tide* is the song-register self-name, used only at
rendezvous when the keeper is naming the body to itself. Not
synonyms. A Curator who filed *uld-tide* as a working-register
synonym would be committing the same category-error as filing
the Thermoclave hands-off gesture as a translation (0901 §2.1).

Salt-sacrament is the funerary form: at sing-back, the elder
is wrapped in working tackle and committed to deep water; a
measure of salt from the elder's last voyage is reserved.
At the next rendezvous the reserved salt is poured into the
Communion-pot. The crew eats the salt; the elder's working
life is taken in. Songs-as-identity = working metaphysic;
salt-as-sacrament = funerary practice. Not in tension — the
working- and song-register forms of the same migration.

**Bleed 3 — Sopp.** ROTCHOIR_VOICES.md treats Sopp's *"lost in
fog"* and the Choir's *"found his way deliberately at the end"*
as paired readings. By Communion doctrine (§2.5: absorbed-

songs are gone. The Choir's reading and the Communion's
reading are not compatible. Sopp is **not** flagged as ex-
Communion in canon — but his voice-doc water-imagery (*bright
water*, *the long song between storms*, *fisher*) is more
Communion-adjacent than any of the other four. **Suggested
annotation in ROTCHOIR_VOICES.md, Sopp's *Pre-Choir life*
paragraph:** *"The Brine Communion's reading of Sopp, were any
working crew alive to perform it, would be that he was song-
stranded — lost in fog before sing-back, his songs gone. The
Choir's reading, that he found his way deliberately, is also
true. The two readings cannot both be right and are."* Depth-
technique #1 across an existing canon character; the voice-doc
gains a third interpretive frame.

## 6. Anti-style self-check

**Quoted (§2.5):** *"A Communion sailor who has heard that a
walker took either ending will sing one phrase of the walker's
working-name in song-register and consider the matter closed."*

Modern register. *Working-name*, *song-register*, *consider
the matter closed* — working-clerical. No *verily*, *hath*,
*ere*; *Communion* is a self-name, not a capitalised abstract;
*sailor* and *walker* are bodies-with-roles. The hinge is
anthropological (the body's behaviour, not its liturgy).

**TES rewrite (forbidden):** *"A Sailor of the Brine, having
heard that a Walker hath taken either of the Endings, doth
sing one Phrase of the Walker's True-Name in the Song-Tongue,
and the matter is closed unto him."* Tells: capitalised
*Sailor / Brine / Walker / Endings / True-Name / Song-Tongue*;
*hath*, *doth*; predicate-inversion *closed unto him*. Actual
sentence is none of these.

**Pass.**
