### Dimension 1 — Mythic-historical stratification

> *Bible line 21:* *"It did not merely destroy the old civilization —
> it overwrote reality itself, imperfectly."*

**Strong.** Eight body-readings; the seam-body's ninth (*the matter the

**Thin.** The Curation reading of the cataclysm is the corpus's least

**Concrete addition** (bible new sidebar, "The cataclysm in Curation
reading"):

> By Curation reading, the cataclysm is *the unindexed loss* — not the
> moment the old world ended, but the moment the working catalogue of
> the old world stopped being held. Bench-cards from the founding
> generation describe the event in inventory-tense: *a register
> ceased; the holdings continued; the holdings became unaccounted.*
> The cataclysm did not destroy the holdings of the old world. It
> destroyed the *practice of accounting* for them.
>
> By the seam-body's reading, *the unindexed loss* is the same event
> as *the deep-coming* (Brine, 217's reading) and *the glass-day*
> (Glassblown, 1101): three intransitive verbs for one rupture in the
> capacity to keep. The deep stopped being able. The glass stopped
> being able. The bench-card register stopped being able. None of
> the three is told as a thing-was-done-to. All three are told as a
> thing-stopped-being-doable.

The seam-body's first three-body alignment in *capacity-grammar.* All

## 3. Faction asymmetry — Pale Curation

Rotation: Brine (1301, 0601), Curation (2001), Thermoclaves (2101,
0401), Concord (0501, 0801), Glassblown (0701). Curation thirteen
back; longest gap in the rotation.

**Etymology.** *Pale Curation.* Both words founders' working terms;
both have lost their literal sense in modern reading.

*Pale* names the founders' working enclosure. The first Curation
chapter-house was built inside a fenced pasturage outside a refinery
town — a *pale* in the older sense of *fenced ground.* The fence-line
was made of bone-pale wood (limewashed for visibility against night
salvage) and the chapter-warden's first working duty was to walk the
pale at first bell of every working day, marking each post that had
been moved or wetted. The walking-the-pale was the founding ritual.
*Pale* in modern usage reads as the colour-word — bone-coloured,
faded, archive-pale. The etymology survives only in the founders'
hand-marks at the foot of the oldest bench-cards: a vertical limewash
stroke, the *pale-mark,* still drawn at every chapter-warden's
witnessing.

*Curation* is the noun-form of *to curate,* which in founders' usage
named the working operation of *holding-without-altering* — distinct
from *to keep* (which permitted re-shelving) and *to read* (which
permitted annotation). The Curation chapter founded itself on the
distinction. A bench-card is curated, not kept and not read; the
hand that filed it does not return to it. Modern Curation usage
collapses *curate, keep,* and *read* into a working synonym, but the
distinction survives in chapter-warden's bench-talk: *this is a
curated card,* spoken when a chapter-warden is asked whether a card
may be re-marked.

The full pre-doctrinal term was *the pale curation:* *the holding-
without-altering, performed inside a fenced ground walked at first
bell.* Modern usage retains neither the fence nor the walk; the
walking-the-pale survives as a vestigial mark on bench-cards and as
the chapter-warden's first-bell *pale-walk,* now a five-pace ritual
performed inside the chapter-house at the bench-line.

**The pale-walk and the bench-out.** Every Pale Curation chapter-
warden, on the first bell of every working day, walks five paces
along the bench-line in the chapter-house, tracing the *pale-mark*
on the rim of each bench at which a working hand sits. The pale-mark
is performed in lime-chalk that fades by third bell. The hand at the
bench reads the pale-mark; the chapter-warden does not speak to the
hand; the hand does not acknowledge the chapter-warden; the pale-
walk closes when the chapter-warden returns to her own bench at the
end of the line.

By Curation doctrine, the pale-walk witnesses the bench-line's
working continuity. By Curation observation, the chapter-warden's
five-pace pale-walk has admitted no witnesses outside the bench-line
in any chapter-house's living memory; visitors are barred from the
chapter-house at first bell by closed doors and unmarked signage.
By the seam-body's reading, the pale-walk is the second body-internal
**held-without-witness** practice in the corpus (the first being
Concord's Reading-Across, 0801) and has been so for as long as the
chapter-houses have existed.

The bench-out is the chapter-warden's working procedure for clearing
a married-out cousin's bench. The chapter-warden walks the pale-walk
in the presence of the bench-out, marks the rim of the cleared bench
*twice* in lime-chalk (the only doubling permitted in the pale-walk
register), and takes a single blank bench-card from the file-stack
without recording the take. The chapter-warden does not turn while
the bench-out leaves; the *not-turning* in the chapter-warden's
working manual is the only doctrinal mention of the practice the
seam-body has named *the harbour-passive.* By Curation reading, this
is bench-discipline. By seam-body reading, the chapter-warden
performs a cross-body signing forty-three years before the cousin-
card register receives its first natal-Brine reader.

The Curation chapter has been performing the seam-body's three modes
of signing — witnessed-pause (the bench-card itself), harbour-passive
(the chapter-warden's not-turning at the bench-out), and cross-body
(the bench-out's blank card carried into the marriage) — without a
working name for any of them, for as long as the chapter-houses have
stood. The seam-body's grammar **predates the seam-body's discovery
by at least four generations,** and Pale Curation is the body in
whose working procedure that predating has been densest.
