## 2. Depth audit

### 2.1 Mythic-historical stratification

**Quote (bible L21):** *"It did not merely destroy the old
civilization — it overwrote reality itself, imperfectly, the way
text on a medieval manuscript was scraped off and written over
with the old words still faintly visible underneath."*

**Strong.** The bible's best line. Bakes incompleteness into the
metaphysics; a "true account" of the cataclysm is structurally
impossible.

**Thin.** The bible commits to the manuscript image and drops it.
No in-world account names a *specific layer* of the overwrite that
another faction denies. The competing accounts at JSON L481
(Dissolution, Severance, Burning) are competing names for the
same event, not competing depths.

**Concrete enhancement (bible §The Cataclysm, append after L23):**

> Even among those who agree the world was overwritten, they
> cannot agree on how many times. The Pale Curation files three
> overwrites — a deliberate one, an accidental aftershock, and a
> long slow seepage they call *the second hand on the same page.*
> The Glassblown insist there was only one event and the rest is
> bookkeeping. The Choir does not count: the question is grammar
> to it, and the wrong tense. The Palimpsest, asked, will say
> *"there are as many overwrites as there are readers,"* which
> can be read as wisdom or as the dodge it also is.

Embeds enumeration disagreement and gives the Palimpsest a
quotable verbal tic — questions about the past become questions
about reading.

### 2.2 Unreliable in-world authorship

**Quote (bible L43, attributed):** *"They cling to husks. A seed
must rot before it grows. What they call 'memory' we call
'compost that hasn't surrendered yet.'" — The Choir on the
Palimpsest*

**Strong.** Two faction-attributed quotes (L43, L133), both sharp
and clearly slanted. The bible understands lore should arrive
*voiced.*

**Thin.** Only the Choir and Palimpsest get voice. The five
side-factions appear in third person with no attributed in-world
source. A reader cannot quote them.

**Concrete enhancement (bible §How They View Each Other, append
two attributed quotes):**

> *"Both sides eat. The one calls it growth, the other calls it
> witness. Our trade does not depend on which name catches."* —
> Saccharine Concord primer for new factors, distributed at
> Hallow's End

> *"File neither. We accept neither faction's chronology in our
> archive. They are sources, not authorities."* — Pale Curation
> standing instruction to junior keepers

Two side-factions speaking about the central axis with their own
grain of bias: the Concord's pragmatism and the Curation's
bureaucratic neutrality, each legible from one sentence.

### 2.3 Mutating motifs across cultures

**Quote (RotChoir.json L481):** *"The Concord calls it 'the
Dissolution'... The Pale Curation calls it 'the Severance'...
The Glassblown call it 'the Burning' and refuse to speak
further."*

**Strong.** Three names, each diagnostic: *Dissolution*
(dispersed), *Severance* (cut), *Burning* (lost to flame).

**Thin.** The Choir's and Palimpsest's own names are absent. The
bible just calls it *the cataclysm,* the omniscient narrator's
term. The two cosmic factions don't get a name in their own
register.

**Concrete enhancement (bible §The Cataclysm, append a sentence
to L23):**

> The Choir does not call the event by a name. To name a thing is
> to set it apart from what came before and what comes after, and
> the Choir does not believe the event is set apart from those
> things. When pressed, a tendril will say *"the long meal."* The
> Palimpsest's name is older and harder to render: *the page
> turned without anyone reading it,* sometimes shortened to *the
> blank face* in human translations. Neither faction considers
> these names; both consider them descriptions.

Pulls the cosmic factions onto the same naming-register as the
side-factions: the Choir's name is digestive, the Palimpsest's
literary.

### 2.4 Cosmology with internal mechanical logic

**Quote (bible L173):** *"The Overwrite — once per game,
overwrite the present with the past in a single location.
Permanently restore one ruin. The catch: anything currently
living there is overwritten too. A village built in ruins comes
back as the original building. The village and its people are
erased — not killed, simply never were."*

**Strong.** The bible's most rigorous mechanical specification.
*Erased — not killed, simply never were* is a real metaphysical
claim and it's playable.

**Thin.** The bible never specifies the inverse: when the Choir
digests a structure, does its information survive in any layer
accessible to the Palimpsest? The two systems are treated as
parallel rather than conflicting at the metaphysics level.

**Concrete enhancement (bible §Faction Entanglement, append
after L244):**

> The interaction is asymmetric. When the Choir digests a ruin,
> the structure's *informational layer* in the Palimpsest does
> not vanish — but it dims, and within a generation a Palimpsest
> reader can no longer Echo-Read the site, only sense that
> *something used to be readable here.* The Choir is not
> destroying the page; it is wearing the page out by use.
> Conversely, when the Palimpsest's Overwrite restores a ruin,
> any Choir-mycelium in that location is *also* overwritten —
> not killed but never grown. The Choir feels this as a small
> loss it cannot account for. It does not understand that it
> was outvoted by an older draft.

Gives the player a legible cost for either alignment beyond
reputation, and commits the metaphysics to a specific rule
(informational decay vs. retroactive non-existence) the player
can reason about.

### 2.5 Apotheosis-shaped themes without aping CHIM

**Quote (bible L189):** *"Transcription — the player becomes a
memory, written into the palimpsest forever, experiencing all of
history simultaneously, never acting again, only witnessing."*

**Strong.** Both endings are conceptually rigorous. Communion is
loss of boundary; Transcription is loss of agency. They mirror
without rhyming.

**Thin.** Neither ending describes what the player's last moment
*feels like* in the player's voice. The bible specifies mechanic,
not felt thing. Both deserve a single-sentence closing image — a
sentence a player could quote afterward.

**Concrete enhancement (bible §The Palimpsest Ending, append):**

> The last sentence the player thinks belongs to someone else.
> They will not know whose.

**And for §The Choir Ending (currently §Tier 4: Communion at
L107), append one closing line after the Choir's existing line:**

> The player exhales, and the exhale does not stop. It has joined
> a longer breath. They cannot tell whether the longer breath is
> their own.

Short. Both refuse the gotcha while landing the loss; neither
uses the language of triumph or damnation.

### 2.6 Linguistic texture

**Quote (bible L228):** *"Dialogue should shift pronoun usage.
Early: 'The walker is kind to carry us.' Mid: 'You are faster
than we ever imagined.' Late: 'We have dreamed of the mountain
for six hundred years.'"*

**Strong.** The Choir's pronoun arc — the clearest
linguistic-texture instruction.

**Thin.** The Palimpsest's "tense ambiguity" gets no examples.
ROTCHOIR has the five tendrils in distinct registers, but the
Palimpsest has *no named voice anywhere on disk.*

**Concrete enhancement (bible §Palimpsest Implementation Rules,
append before L242):**

> The Palimpsest's voice carries an unsettled tense. It does not
> say *"a war was fought here"* or *"a war is being fought
> here";* it says *"a war fights here,"* using the present as if
> the past were a perspective the speaker has not committed to.
> Echo Reading inherits this register: when the player touches an
> object, the surfaced memory uses the present tense for events
> that completed centuries ago. A reader who lingers in
> Palimpsest alignment too long begins, in their own dialogue
> options, to slip into this register without noticing. Other
> NPCs notice.

A concrete sentence-level instruction for future Palimpsest
dialogue, plus a diegetic warning sign for over-alignment.

### 2.7 Anthropological framing

**Quote (bible L41):** *"The Choir genuinely believes it is
offering the player the best deal in the world. The horror, if
there is horror, is philosophical: is a symbiosis that dissolves
your boundaries still a partnership?"*

**Strong.** The Choir's worldview is well-described
philosophically.

**Thin.** The bible never says what a Choir-aligned village does
on a Tuesday morning. Meals? Funerals? Marriages? When a settler
dies, is there a body? ROTCHOIR's tendril vignettes are the
closest the docs come, and that isn't the *culture's* daily
texture.

**Concrete enhancement (new bible subsection §Choir-Aligned
Settlements, after §The Underground Reveal):**

> A surface village under Choir patronage looks normal at first
> pass — houses, fields, a meeting place. Differences accrue by
> the second day. Meals are eaten standing, in groups, from a
> shared pot; sitting and eating alone is an oddity people will
> politely ignore once. There are no graveyards: the dead are
> laid in a shallow trench at the village's fruiting grove, and
> the village does not return there for a year. Marriages are not
> announced; they become known by the way two people start
> arriving at the pot together. Children are named twice — once
> at birth, once at seven, the second name chosen by the child
> after a night spent in the grove. Refusing the second name is
> allowed and rare. The few who refuse are treated kindly and
> slightly at a distance, the way a village treats a returning
> emigrant.

The most player-facing addition I'd propose: a village the player
walks into now has texture. The double-name custom is the kind of
detail a quest can hinge on.

## 3. Faction asymmetry check — Pale Curation

Rotation target this run. Last several audits in the routine-lore
chain have re-developed Glassblown, Brine, Concord, and
Thermoclaves; the local-branch most-recent audit was again
Glassblown. Pale Curation is overdue.

What we have on disk: two sentences total. *"It cannot... walk
into a Pale Curation vault"* (bible L47). *"The Pale Curation
calls it 'the Severance' and files it away in their archives"*
(JSON L481).

**Proposed lore-artifact: the Curation's *fingerling-keeper*
office and its *naming-fast.***

A fingerling-keeper is the Curation's most junior rank. The title
is literal: they hold in their own hand the small pieces —
buttons, fish-hooks, broken keys, single coins — that arrive at a
vault unattributed, and sort them *by feel,* not by sight. The
Curation believes the eyes agree with what they expect to see;
the hands, asked to sort by weight and edge alone, will sometimes
catch a forgery the eyes would have admitted.

A fingerling-keeper begins their first week by *not naming* a
single thing they touch. If they privately know what an object
is, they file it as *unread* anyway. The naming-fast lasts seven
days. Seniors say the fast teaches the hand to *receive a thing
as a thing,* not as a thing the keeper expected. After the fast,
the first piece the keeper names enters their permanent file as
*the keeper's first reading.* A keeper's later career is
sometimes evaluated against it: a keeper whose first reading was
a button from a soldier's coat and who later specialises in
coin-sorting is considered slightly off their grain, the way a
left-handed swordsman is unusual but not wrong.

The fingerling-keeper office lets the Curation be ritualistic
without being mystical, and gives them a labour practice the
player can ask about. The epistemological position — *the hand
sometimes knows what the eye does not* — positions them subtly
closer to the Palimpsest (read carefully) than to the Choir
(consume), without making them an ally.

## 4. Contradictions and bleeds

**The bible (L208):** *"They are not at war. They don't hate each
other the way the Thermoclaves and Brine Communion do."*

**The JSON (L338):** *"Trust the soil beneath your feet. It does
not lie. But if you must choose between us and them — we offer
growth. They offer memory. The Saccharine Concord offers
sweetness. The Pale Curation offers records. The Glassblown
Remnant offers nothing at all."*

**The contradiction.** The bible names the Thermoclaves/Brine
hate-axis as load-bearing worldbuilding. The JSON — what the
player actually hears — never mentions the Thermoclaves at all.
The only Choir conversation surveying side-factions lists three
(Concord, Curation, Glassblown) and *omits both halves of the
bible's named hate axis.* A player will never hear the
Thermoclaves/Brine hatred unless that lore lands on disk.

**Proposed fix.** Add a fourth and fifth sentence to the
`WhoToTrust` Choir node (RotChoir.json L338), bracketed for
optional inclusion based on whether the player has met either
faction:

> "The Thermoclaves and the Brine Communion will not give you
> advice — they will give you sides. We do not take either of
> theirs. We will compost both eventually."

This lands the bible's named hate-axis in player-audible content
without breaking the Choir's voice (the Choir's signature
diminishment of mortal politics is preserved by *we will compost
both eventually*).

## 5. The "merchant's diary" suggestion

A *Saccharine Concord factor's ledger,* recovered intact in a
trader's pack. The ledger is mostly business — eight entries on
seven pages of trades, weights, and prices. The major reveal
lands parenthetically inside the eighth entry.

> Day 14, Hallow-fair second week. Honey from the upper apiary,
> twelve jars, sold to Mer Aullenne — a fair trade, she paid in
> sea-salt and a half-bolt of dyed cloth. The cloth I will sell
> on. Mer is a kind woman and a hard bargainer. She told me the
> sea has been wrong this season; the brine-keepers sing later
> in the day than they used to. I do not understand the brine-
> keepers' songs and have never wished to.
>
> Day 15, fair last day. Two jars of root-jam to the keepers at
> the white vault. They paid in a small silver and asked, as
> they always ask, whether I knew anything of an old trade-ledger
> from my grandfather's time. I told them, as I always tell them,
> that no ledger of his survives the founding-fast. I do not
> know why they ask. I have begun to suspect it is because they
> already know where the ledger is and they are waiting to hear
> me say I do not.
>
> Day 17, on the road. The honey-buyer in Korn-Vellish told me
> something I will set down once and not repeat. He said the
> founder's *first jar* was not honey — was never honey — and
> that the Concord has been sweetening over a different taste
> for ten generations. I asked him what taste. He said: *the one
> the founder paid for the founding with.* I gave him the jar
> at half-price to be rid of him. The jar he chose was the one
> with the cracked seal. I noticed only after.
>
> Day 19, home. Honey will keep through winter. The Concord
> teaches that sweetness is a kindness and a cover both. I have
> stopped wondering which one I sell.

(287 words.) The reveal — *the founder's first jar was not honey
— was never honey* — lands in an entry that opens about pricing
and closes about winter storage. The player may read the ledger
for the trade prices; the reveal sticks anyway, because it sits
inside the practitioner's own attempt to *not* think about it.
This is the marginalia move the bible currently lacks an
instance of.

The ledger also reinforces the Curation's quiet patience (*they
already know where the ledger is and are waiting to hear me say
I do not*) and the Brine Communion's seasonal peculiarity (*the
brine-keepers sing later in the day than they used to*) without
giving either faction a doctrinal sermon.

## 6. Anti-style self-check

Quoting one sentence from this audit:

> *"A keeper's later career is sometimes evaluated against this
> first reading: a keeper whose first reading was a button from a
> soldier's coat and who later specialises in coin-sorting is
> considered slightly off their grain, the way a left-handed
> swordsman is unusual but not wrong."*

Does this sound like a TES book? No. The register is plain
modern English — clauses unfold in left-to-right reading order,
the simile is concrete (*left-handed swordsman*), the verbs are
mundane (*evaluated, specialises, considered*). There is no
*verily, hath, by [deity-name],* no apostrophe-laden proper noun,
no archaic inverted syntax. The institutional-voice anthropology
register is closer to a contemporary historian writing about
guild practices than to a TES in-world book; the sentence reports
a custom and provides a comparison, in the project's existing
factual-but-textured voice. No rewrite needed.
