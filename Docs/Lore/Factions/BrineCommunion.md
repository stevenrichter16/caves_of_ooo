# The Brine Communion

> *The Brine carry the sea inside them, even inland.*

---

## What they are

The sea-people, but not only by the sea. The Brine fish the rising tide
(never the falling), recover what the tide returns, read the salt-
patterns left on stones, and write to the gone-deep. They live in
small coastal villages and in inland postings, and the inland Brine —
the *dry-walking* — outnumber the coastal Brine three to one.

They speak in a way that disorients outsiders. Their sentences open
with a tide-mark (*low, the boat returns; full, we sing*). They have
three first-person pronouns at three different depths and switch
between them mid-sentence. *Who is speaking* is, in Brine grammar, a
question with multiple correct simultaneous answers.

They do not raise their voices. They do not litigate. When something
they have promised does not happen, they do not blame each other —
they blame *the tide*.

---

## What they believe

That every body, every object, every promise has a depth at which it
would lie perfectly still. They call this the **held depth** — the
still place where a thing would be *kept*. Find it, and the thing is
preserved. Miss it, and the thing disperses.

This is, for the Brine, both physical and moral. Real water has a
held-depth layer (a measurable stratum at which currents thin and
objects stay). Real promises do too. A promise kept at its own held
depth is *deep-kept*; a promise that did not reach its depth before
the speaker's attention slipped is *un-deeped* — neither broken nor
fulfilled, only unfinished.

The Brine far from the sea — the dry-walkers — keep the held depth
*in the body itself*. Doctrine teaches that the held depth is wherever
*you would be still enough.* They can be kept by a deep room, a
cellar, the hour before a rainstorm. The keeping is partial, but it
is real.

The Brine read the *under-tide* — the stratum below the moving sea, the
salt that does not move with the moon — as a literate party. They write
to it. It writes back, slowly, by gaining mass in the books they leave
in shallows. They consider this neither magic nor metaphor.

## A starter NPC

You will likely meet a **bowl-maker** — an inland Brine sub-population
who walks the roads gathering vessels (cups, mugs, hollow stones,
dried gourds) and trades them to dry-walking Brine in exchange for a
single sentence in the *remembered tense*: a sentence about water
absent from the room, like *the tide is highen at home tonight*.

The bowl-maker keeps the sentence the way the home shore keeps a
reproach — silently, without answer. A bowl-maker who collects more
sentences than they can hold begins *un-bowling* — releasing them back
to the air in the order received.

If you give a bowl-maker a sentence in the remembered tense, she will
give you a bowl. She will not tell you which bowl was meant for you;
she will simply place one in your pack while you are looking elsewhere.

## Where they live

On the coast, in small held-depth villages. Each has a designated
**held-depth shore** — the place where bodies, eventually, are
committed.

Inland, in **postings** — Brine merchants, scholars, and travellers
who maintain a single bowl on a windowsill at the home tide, refilling
it at high tide forever. The bowl evaporates over weeks; the bowl is
not poured out.

The most senior Brine sites are **the Coastal Embayment** (Tide-Reader
Nesh's body of water) and the unnamed inland pond where the founder
recognised the held depth in *fresh* water two hundred miles from any
shore — and the Communion was born.

## Going deeper

If you want to know more:

- **Going-deep.** The Brine apotheosis. A swim from a Brine-tended
  shore until the Communion calls you back, or doesn't. Walkers who
  turn back of their own accord — the *risen-late* — are the most
  honoured.
- **The deep-letters.** Replies from the gone-deep, recorded by
  multiple tide-witnesses, archived as *bound knots*. The Communion's
  only canonical scripture — a scripture that is a *hash, not a text*.
- **Tide-Reader Nesh of the Coastal Embayment.** Senior figure;
  forty-one years a tide-reader. Has computed the going-deep tides for
  one hundred and eleven walkers.
- **Mer-Iss.** The founder. Possibly *deep-kept* rather than dead.
- **The salt-warped book.** The Brine's most-cited artifact: a
  pre-cataclysm book that gained mass while sitting on a Brine
  windowsill, returned to the tide, and refused.
- **Salt-names.** Seven canonical patterns of salt deposit that name
  the things the tide is ready to carry. A reader claiming an eighth
  is *going-deep on the rumour*.
