# Faction Territories

> The six factions are not merely *organisations*. Each holds land —
> maintained borders, administered infrastructure, recognised by every
> other faction as *theirs*. The world has no king and no central
> authority, but it does have countries.
>
> This document covers: where each faction's territory lies, who
> legitimately crosses it and why, known trade routes, and the
> *standing exceptions* — places where one faction's people have
> settled or work inside another's land. These exceptions and the
> territory lines are the texture of the world between quests.

---

## How territory works

Faction territory is not a hard wall. There are no customs-posts on
the high road, no border guards demanding papers. What there is: each
faction maintains infrastructure — roads, wayhouses, market-stalls,
chapter-houses, hearth-camps — within its claimed land, and the
social expectation that outsiders passing through behave according to
the host faction's conventions. A visitor who ostentatiously ignores
local convention (refusing to price clauses in Concord land, refusing
to bank a fire for a witness in Ember Order camps) is not arrested.
They are simply noticed, and remembered.

The factions recognise each other's claims through three mechanisms:

1. **The Salt-Hinge conventions** — a set of unwritten cross-faction
   rules older than the current senior leadership of any faction.
   Includes hand-off protocols (what happens when a trade or document
   crosses a border), border-witness rules (each faction can station
   a witness at the edge of its territory to observe what leaves it),
   and the half-redemption right.
2. **Claim-markers** — physical objects, faction-specific, placed at
   territory edges. Curation claim-markers are tagged stone plinths.
   Concord markers are balance-stones. Brine markers are tide-bowls
   filled from the nearest water. Ember Order markers are cold-fire
   stones (a small quench-fire, deliberately extinguished, marked
   with a stroke per year of keeping). Glassblown markers are
   unfired sand-column stubs. The Choir does not use markers; Choir
   ground is identified by the soil's response to boots.
3. **Custom** — some borders are older than living memory and are
   simply known. No one disputes where Brine coastal land ends and
   Concord hinterland begins; that line was drawn two hundred years
   ago by a tide-witness and a reckoner simultaneously, and neither
   faction has questioned it since.

The Sealhand Crusade does not respect this system. They hold land they
have taken by force (the Burnt Houses and the northern flats), but no
other faction officially recognises their claim. They are, in the
language of the Salt-Hinge conventions, *occupants without hand-off
rights*. This matters practically: any trade done in Sealhand-held
land cannot be half-redeemed at the Salt-Hinge.

## Territory by faction

### The Pale Curation

**Core land:** The old ruin-belts — areas where pre-Overwriting
infrastructure is densest. Chapter-houses cluster near the richest
ruins. The Hyphal Surveys Office (four days northwest of the
Salt-Hinge) is the largest; there are seven others distributed across
the world.

**Borders:** Curation borders are defined by their chapter-house
catchment areas. Land within a day's walk of a chapter-house is
administered by that chapter-house; the Curation surveys it, tags
artifacts found on it, and maintains the roads that lead to it.

**Character of the land:** Quiet, procedural. The buildings are stone
and vaulted. Roads are maintained to surveyor standards (level, marked
at intervals with accession-number plaques for notable features). The
pace is slow. Loud commerce feels wrong here, and Concord apprentices
who arrive in Curation land for the first time find the silence
disorienting.

**Legitimate reasons to be there (non-Curation):**
- Delivering an artifact for cataloguing
- Seeking a Curation witness for a cross-faction document
- Accessing the road network (Curation roads are the best-maintained
  in the world)
- Study — the Curation permits any non-affiliated scholar to read in
  tier-1 vault reading rooms, by appointment
- Employment (see: *Concord exception* below)

---

### The Saccharine Concord

**Core land:** Market towns and the roads between them. The Concord
is the only faction whose territory is defined not by ruins or
geography but by *infrastructure it built*. The Salt-Hinge is its
crown jewel; the other market towns are smaller but similarly
constructed — a balance-stone at the centre, a Reckoner's chair, a
ring of licensed stalls.

**Borders:** Concord borders are traced by the road-toll system. Every
road the Concord maintains has toll-markers at its entry points. The
toll is nominal (a quarter sweet-mark or a priced greeting); the point
is the marking of entry, not the revenue.

**Character of the land:** Loud, dense, economically legible. Everyone
is pricing something. The inns are good. The bread is expensive. The
gossip is informative and often accurate because the Concord charges a
premium for false information (single-incident dishonesty costs a
vendor their stall licence for a season).

**Legitimate reasons to be there (non-Concord):**
- Trade (this is essentially universal — the Concord's land is the
  world's economy)
- Transit (Concord roads connect every other faction's territory)
- Seeking credit or debt-settlement
- Accessing the half-redemption system

---

### The Brine Communion

**Core land:** Coastal regions and the inland river networks. Brine
territory is the only faction-land defined hydrologically — it follows
water. Every navigable river with a Brine wayhouse on its bank is
Brine territory for the length of the wayhouse's influence. The coast
is theirs where they have committed dead to it; they consider
uncommitted coastline *unclaimed water*, not their land.

**Borders:** Tide-bowls at the high-water mark define coastal
territory. Inland, the border follows the last wayhouse's sight-line
to the next — if two wayhouses can see each other, the land between
them is Brine.

**Character of the land:** Damp, slow, deeply attentive. Brine
wayhouses are built low, against flooding. The ritual pace of
the Communion infects the land — visitors slow down, without
understanding why, within a few hours of arrival.

**Legitimate reasons to be there (non-Brine):**
- Fishing rights (the Brine sells seasonal licences)
- River-barge passage (non-negotiable for inland trade; Brine pilots
  are required on Brine-territory rivers above a certain draft)
- Recovering objects that washed up on Brine-held coast (the Brine
  has a formal *found-at-shore* return protocol with every other
  faction)
- Meeting a tide-reader for a deep-letter consultation

---

### The Ember Order (Thermoclaves)

**Core land:** The Ember Order's territory is the most unusual because
it is *camp-based, not contiguous*. Each cohort camp claims the land
within a day's walk of its outermost numbered fire. Two cohorts close
enough together produce a corridor of Ember Order land between them.
The Ninth Hearth (Keeper Suul's camp, nine days northeast of the
Salt-Hinge) is one of a chain of eight cohort camps in the highlands
that together make a corridor of contiguous Ember Order territory.
Outside that corridor, Ember Order land is a scatter of isolated
circles.

**Borders:** Cold-fire stones — claim-markers that are also memorials
to fires that went out. A camp without a cold-fire stone at its
boundary is a recent camp or a temporary one.

**Character of the land:** Highland, spare, focused. Every building in
an Ember Order camp is oriented toward a numbered fire. The warmth of
a well-kept camp is noticeable from outside the boundary. Visitors
who arrive at a camp and do not acknowledge a fire (even a small bow
toward the nearest numbered hearth) are considered rude without
exception.

**Legitimate reasons to be there (non-Ember Order):**
- Warmth and shelter (the Ember Order does not turn away cold
  travellers — this is doctrinal, not courtesy)
- Banking a fire as a witnessed act (a non-Ember Order person can
  *have* a fire banked by an Ember Order witness; this is a service
  the Order provides and prices modestly)
- Road-transit (the highland corridor is the only reliable route
  between the northern flats and the southern market towns in winter)
- Seeking a fire-tally witness for a cross-faction document

---

### The Glassblown Remnant

**Core land:** The salt-flats, where the Choir cannot grow. The
Glassblown specifically settled geography hostile to the Choir — not
as a political statement but as a practical response to the Burning.
Their territory is defined by the salt, which defines them.

**Borders:** Unfired sand-column stubs, placed at intervals along the
salt-flat edge. A visitor crossing a salt-flat into Glassblown land
will feel the Choir's absence in the soil — silence underfoot, where
everywhere else there is the faint attentiveness of mycelium.

**Character of the land:** Dry, bright, quiet in a different way from
Curation land. Curation quiet is procedural; Glassblown quiet is
*chosen*. The workshops radiate low constant heat. The people are
genuinely hospitable but speak less than outsiders expect. A silence
from a Glassblown person is not a refusal or a rebuff; it is a full
sentence in their grammar. Outsiders who fill silences with talking
are tolerated without comment.

**Legitimate reasons to be there (non-Glassblown):**
- Purchasing glass goods (the only place in the world to buy
  properly-made Glassblown work)
- Refuge from the Choir (the salt-flats are the world's most
  reliable Choir-free zones; the Glassblown accept any refugee from
  Choir territory without question)
- Seeking a Glassblown letter-of-passage (their *unfired letter*
  system is the only cross-faction correspondence method the Choir
  cannot intercept — the salt prevents mycelial reading)

---

### The Sealhand Crusade

**Core land:** The Burnt Houses and the northern flats, seized over
forty years of expansion. The northern flats are salted dead now —
the Choir cannot enter, but neither can most crops. The Crusade
sustains itself through tribute from villages it has absorbed into its
territory.

**Borders:** Ash-markers — rings of ash burned into the ground at
territory edges. Each ring is refreshed every seven days by a
Crusader patrol. The ash smells of the Sealhand's specific salt-and-
pine accelerant.

**Character of the land:** The most deliberately inhospitable
territory in the world for any non-Crusader. Roads are patrolled.
Travellers are questioned on entry. The three questions (are you a
reader? have you touched Choir ground in the last seven days? do you
carry any layered artifact?) are asked at every ash-marker by patrol.

**Legitimate reasons to be there (non-Sealhand):**
- Officially: the Sealhand permits transit for any traveller who
  passes the three questions
- Trade: the Crusade needs food; it buys grain and meat from Concord
  merchants who are willing to enter (some are, for the markup)
- Diplomatic: the Curation has sent two formal observers who have not
  returned; the Concord sends a factor quarterly who does return; the
  Brine has sent no one

## Trade routes

The world's commerce runs on six main routes. Each crosses at least
two faction territories, making every significant trade journey a
diplomatic exercise.

| Route | Factions crossed | Primary goods | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| The High Road (east–west) | Concord, Brine (at the delta), Curation (at the chapter-house spur) | All general goods | The world's spine. Best-maintained section is Concord-administered. |
| The River Road (south coast, inland) | Brine, Concord | Fish, salt, deep-letters | River-barge only for most of the route; Brine pilots required. |
| The Vault Road (chapter-house spur) | Curation, Concord | Artifacts, catalogued goods, scholarly materials | The road from the Salt-Hinge north to the Hyphal Surveys Office. Patchy in sections. |
| The Ember Corridor (highland north–south) | Ember Order, Concord (southern terminus) | Fuel, kept ash, highland game | The only reliable winter route between north and south. |
| The Salt-Flat Way (east loop) | Glassblown, Concord (at the flat's edge market) | Glass goods, salt-flat minerals | Passable only in dry season on foot; mule-caravan in all weather. |
| The Old Road (northern flats) | Sealhand (occupied), formerly Concord | — | The Concord closed the northern road formally three years ago after the second Sealhand ash-marking. Now used only by Sealhand patrols and desperate couriers. |

## Standing exceptions

Standing exceptions are places where a faction's people have settled
or hold employment *inside another faction's territory*, by mutual
agreement — or at least by long-standing tolerance. Each exception
is a source of local politics, cross-faction drama, and character
texture for the player.

---

### Exception 1 — The Concord Enclave inside Curation Territory

**What it is:** The Pale Curation's chapter-houses have an unusually
high Saccharine Concord population living and working inside their
territory. In the Hyphal Surveys Office's market-town (the settlement
around the chapter-house), approximately one in three permanent
residents holds a Concord affiliation. They run the inns, the
stalls, the grain-storage, and the road-maintenance contracts.

**Why it happened:** The Curation is procedural, not economic. Its
surveyors are good at filing things and terrible at running a market.
When the Hyphal Surveys Office expanded three generations ago, it
drew a large population of scholars and surveyors whose daily needs
(food, lodging, equipment) exceeded what the Curation's own culture
could provide. The Concord moved in to supply it, and never left.

**How it works now:** The Concord enclave holds its own internal
market (on Concord calendar, not Curation calendar) inside Curation
land. The chapter-house witnesses Concord trades by standing protocol.
The Concord pays a nominal territory-use fee in sweet-marks; the
Curation files the payments and does not discuss them. The Concord
population does not interfere with cataloguing; the Curation
population does not interfere with commerce. Both factions prefer
this arrangement to the alternative.

**Tensions:** Three active ones.

1. *The road-toll dispute.* Concord road-maintenance workers have
   begun placing toll-markers on the Vault Road sections they
   maintain, which the Curation did not authorise. Director Ven has
   not yet formally objected in writing; Senior Assayer Veshen has not
   sent the unauthorised toll revenue to the chapter-house as required.
   Both are waiting for the other to move first.

2. *The Concord-Sealhand transit problem.* When Sealhand columns
   pass through the Vault Road, the Concord factors in Curation land
   are the ones being asked for trade. The Curation refuses to witness
   any Sealhand transaction; the Concord officially refuses them credit
   but has no formal rule against a factor in another faction's
   territory making a private sale. A. (the junior cataloguer) filed
   two instances of Concord-Sealhand exchange inside Curation land
   last season. Director Ven has the reports; she has not filed them
   in a tier above the intake desk.

3. *The enclave children.* Children born in the enclave to a Concord
   father and a Curation mother (or vice versa) have no formal
   faction-affiliation protocol. The Concord considers them *enrolled
   pending first-market* (their faction is determined by their first
   independent trade). The Curation considers them *not-yet-entered*
   (the field is blank until they choose). In practice, most choose
   neither until they leave the territory, and some never do.

**Player relevance:** The enclave is where the player is most likely
to find a Concord contact while on a Curation quest, and vice versa.
Side quests in this area involve the road-toll dispute and the
enclave-children question. A. is the source of information on all
three tensions; she files things.

---

### Exception 2 — Brine Wayhouses on Ember Order Corridor Roads

**What it is:** Three Brine wayhouses operate permanently inside the
Ember Order's highland corridor — the only routes between the northern
flats and southern markets in winter. The wayhouses serve river-
tributary traffic (the highland rivers feed into the coast), but the
Brine personnel who run them live year-round on Ember Order land.

**How it works:** The Brine wayhouse staff maintain a *home tide* —
a bowl of water from the nearest river source, refilled weekly,
attended daily. The Ember Order permits this; the Brine does not
interfere with the cohort fires, and the Ember Order does not
interfere with the tide-bowls. There is an informal annual exchange
where the senior Brine wayhouse-keeper brings a vessel of coastal
water to the nearest cohort fire, and the hearth-keeper lights a
small temporary fire from it and lets it go out immediately. Neither
faction has written down what this exchange means. Both consider it
significant.

**Tensions:** One recurring one. When the Brine wayhouse staff
hold a *going-deep* ceremony (committing a wayhouse worker's body
to the nearest river at an unfavourable tide), the Ember Order has
a grammatical problem: a death without a fire-witness, recorded
only in a tide. Their solution — quietly, over three generations —
has been for a cohort keeper to stand at a distance from the
river-bank during Brine ceremonies, not as a witness (which would
require participation in the Brine's grammar) but as a *fire that
happens to be near*. The Brine tide-witnesses know. They do not
object. The keeper does not enter the water.

---

### Exception 3 — Curation Observers in Glassblown Salt-Flats

**What it is:** Two Pale Curation surveyors hold permanent residency
at the Cool Kiln, by long-standing invitation. They are the only
permanent non-Glassblown residents of any Glassblown workshop
cluster.

**Why it exists:** The Glassblown produce objects the Curation cannot
replicate or adequately describe from the outside. The Cool Kiln's
paste-mother (currently Tulen Niemensa-Sopellen) extended a formal
invitation to the Curation sixty years ago: *send two surveyors who
will not talk. we will show them what we make. they may file what
they see.* The Curation accepted. The two surveyors rotate on a
seven-year residency cycle. They are the only Curation employees
forbidden from writing margin notes (this is in the invitation's
terms, which A. has read and filed in the intake desk at tier-3).

**Character:** The two surveyors are changed by the residency. Every
Curation surveyor who has completed a Cool Kiln rotation returns
speaking less. Their reports are shorter. Halen — who was the first
rotation's second surveyor — writes fewer margin notes now than
she did twenty years ago, and the notes she does write are longer
in *deliberate silence* (mid-line dots used for things she has
chosen not to say, rather than things she doesn't know).

## World dynamics between quests

When the player is not on a quest, these territory lines and
exceptions produce the world's ambient texture:

- **On the high road:** The player will pass Concord toll-markers
  regularly. Paying them (a quarter sweet-mark) is habit; not paying
  them is noticed within the next market-town. A Sealhand column on
  the road is visible from half a mile — torches, organised march,
  salt-tools on the packs.

- **In Curation territory:** The quiet is deliberate. Concord vendors
  in the Hyphal Surveys market-town feel slightly out of place, which
  they don't acknowledge. A.s unofficial sub-annotations occasionally
  appear pinned to bulletin boards where they technically should not be.

- **On Brine rivers:** The current is always described in the
  remembered tense. A Brine pilot will tell you what the river carried
  last season before she tells you the current rate.

- **In Ember Order camps:** The fires tell the social mood. A
  well-kept numbered fire means the cohort is stable; a fire burning
  high and wavering means a banking is overdue; a cold-fire stone
  that smells recent means a death in the last month.

- **In Glassblown salt-flats:** The silence is not the Curation's
  procedural quiet — it is the sound of people who have chosen every
  word they do and do not say. The Curation surveyor-residents can
  be identified by their reduced speech and the longer-than-usual
  pause before they answer any question.

- **Near Sealhand territory:** The ash-markers are visible from the
  road. The smell of salt-and-pine accelerant carries. Locals near
  Sealhand borders have developed habits — carrying no books, keeping
  no Choir-grown plants, wearing no glass objects that might be called
  *layered artifacts*.

## Border tensions between specific faction pairs

Not all borders are equally calm. Below are the pairs with active
friction, the cause, and the current state.

---

### Curation ↔ Sealhand — *the burning border*

**Cause:** The Sealhand has burned three Curation chapter-houses in
eleven years. The Curation has formally refused to witness for the
Sealhand (unprecedented in its history). Their borders, where they
are adjacent, are the most actively dangerous crossing in the world.

**Current state:** The Curation's nearest chapter-house to Sealhand
territory (a smaller one, two days' ride from the Burnt Houses)
has been operating behind closed intake for two seasons — it accepts
no new artifacts and is quietly moving its tier-1 vault holdings
deeper into the network. Director Ven has not announced this. A.
has filed a note about it at tier-3 intake.

**For quests:** A player carrying a Curation letter of introduction
near Sealhand-held land is a *target*, not a neutral traveller.
Sealhand patrols who see the Curation seal will ask the fourth
unasked question (*are you here to recover something?*) even if the
player passes the standard three.

---

### Concord ↔ Sealhand — *the closed credit line*

**Cause:** The Concord formally revoked Sealhand credit access four
years ago, after the Sealhand destroyed a Concord trade-stall in the
Salt-Hinge's northern annexe. The Sealhand had, in their accounting,
not destroyed it — they had *sealed* it, as it was selling layered
artifacts. The Concord did not accept this reading.

**Current state:** The Concord's factors do still operate near
Sealhand territory (privately, without credit extension). The official
stance is no-trade; the practical reality is selective cash-trade.
Veshen has not named any factor who has traded with the Sealhand in
the last year. She knows. She has not filed it.

**For quests:** A player trying to move Concord goods through Sealhand
land (or vice versa) has to navigate both factions' conflicting
positions. The Sealhand will tax the goods (a salt-tithe of ten
percent of visible value); the Concord will not officially acknowledge
a trade that passed through Sealhand territory — which means it cannot
be half-redeemed.

---

### Brine ↔ Sealhand — *the tide question*

**Cause:** The Sealhand has been salting coastal approach zones near
two Brine-held shores, in an attempt to seal substrate-leaks that
emerge at those shores. The Brine's position: the tide is their
theology, and a salted shore is a *dead held-depth* — a concept the
Brine grammar does not have a word for, because it was thought
impossible. Nesh has used the phrase *wrong-kept* in her reports;
the Brine has no stronger term of condemnation.

**Current state:** The Brine have not declared war. They have
positioned five tide-witness stations along the contested shoreline
and are recording every tide in detail. The Choir, aware of the
salting, has sent a slow voice to a tidal cave near the largest
affected shore and has been in quiet conversation with Nesh for
two seasons. The Sealhand does not know this is happening.

**For quests:** A player who discovers the Brine–Choir communication
at this shore has a piece of information that changes the political
calculus significantly. The Concord would pay well for it. The
Curation would file it at tier-1 with extreme care.

---

### Ember Order ↔ Sealhand — *the witness refusal*

**Cause:** The Ember Order will not light a fire for the Sealhand,
and will not witness a Sealhand banking. From the Ember Order's
doctrinal position, an unwitnessed banking is an extinguishing; a
Sealhand camp cannot sustain its fires by Ember Order grammar.
This is not a practical threat (the Sealhand has its own fire
practices) but is a profound *social insult* — being told your fires
are grammatically not fires.

**Current state:** The Sealhand has not yet moved militarily against
Ember Order territory, possibly because the highland corridor is
too valuable as transit. There have been two incidents where Sealhand
columns passing through Ember Order land attempted to bank fires at
cohort camps without asking for a witness; both times the Ember Order
keepers walked away from the fire until the Sealhand left.

**For quests:** The player who earns Ember Order trust (and gains the
right to be a banking witness) holds an unusually powerful card. A
Sealhand who genuinely wants to bank a fire — not on doctrine, but
because it is cold and they are alone — has nowhere to go except to
a faction-sympathetic traveller.

---

### Glassblown ↔ Sealhand — *the silence that unsettles*

**Cause:** The Sealhand has burned three Glassblown workshops. The
Glassblown response — rebuilding without speaking, folding back
Sealhand letters without opening them — is one the Sealhand's
grammar does not contain a category for. The Crusade is doctrinally
equipped for resistance, negotiation, defiance, and submission. It
is not equipped for *we have heard your fire and are not answering it*.

**Current state:** The Sealhand's next step against the Glassblown is
unclear even inside the Crusade. Anselm has visited the salt-flats
once, personally. He sat at the Cool Kiln's outer boundary for two
hours and was not spoken to, and left. Sealmaster Iro, asked, said
only: *the quiet ones are not quiet about the same thing we are*.

**For quests:** A player who understands Glassblown grammar (earned by
time at the Cool Kiln) can read the Glassblown's silence about the
Sealhand as communication. The unfired child at the workshop doorway
has, each time a Sealhand party has come and gone, formed and held
a shape for the exact duration of their visit and then folded it back.
No one has ever asked her what it was.

## Notes for quest design

- **Cross-border quests** work best when the faction-crossing has
  a *legitimate stated reason* the player can use if questioned. The
  existing reasons above are the canonical set; quest designs can
  add to them but shouldn't contradict them.
- **The Concord enclave** (Exception 1) is the richest single
  location for side quests: three active tensions, multiple
  characters in conflict (A., Director Ven, Veshen, enclave-children
  NPCs), and the Sealhand-Concord exchange problem as a slow-burn
  main-quest thread.
- **Cold-eye check for new quests:** If a quest requires a player to
  enter faction territory without a legitimate reason, check whether
  the quest is better served by *providing* a cover-reason (a letter
  of introduction, a Concord factor's transit pass, a tide-reading
  appointment) than by making the crossing invisible. The
  cover-reason is almost always more interesting than ignoring the
  border.
- **The standing exceptions are living tensions**, not resolved
  settlements. Each one can generate a side quest or a world-state
  shift if pushed.
