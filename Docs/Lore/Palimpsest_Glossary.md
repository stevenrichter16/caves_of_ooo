# Palimpsest Glossary

> **What this is.** A two-column reference: every distinctive term from
> the Palimpsest lore corpus, paired with a plain-English label you can
> use instead. Both are correct. The deep term is the technical name
> (used in the bible, audits, and compendium). The casual label is the
> everyday name (used in narrative prose, the primer, and player-facing
> material).
>
> **How to use it.** When writing about the world for a general reader,
> use the casual label by default. Drop the deep term in as a parenthetical
> on first introduction (e.g. *"the still place — the held depth"*) and
> after that use whichever fits. When writing for the lore-deep audience,
> use the deep term directly.
>
> **Tier 1 of the lore.** This glossary is part of the surface — the
> entrance for new readers. The deeper material lives in
> `Palimpsest_Lore_Bible.md`, the audits, and `Palimpsest_Lore_Compendium.md`.
> Nothing here contradicts those; everything here translates them.

## §1 — The world

| Deep term | Casual label | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| The cataclysm | The breaking / The Overwriting | Four hundred years ago. The event that overwrote the old world imperfectly. *The Overwriting* is the cross-faction everyday name (no one faction owns it); use it in narrative prose when you want a name all factions recognise. |
| The Severance / Dissolution / Burning / First Feeding / Temporal Unraveling | The breaking (six faction names) | Each faction calls it something different. The names disagree because the factions disagree on what happened. All six coexist; none is official. |

| The smoke-born | The smoke-born | The first generation born after the breaking — the twenty years of children no faction quite knows what to do with. (Already image-led; keep.) |
| The thaw | The false dawn | A seventeen-year warming about eighty years after the breaking, when crops returned. It ended badly. |
| The first hour | The first hour | The opening moment of the breaking. Each faction has its own image for it. (Already image-led; keep.) |
| Pre-cataclysm / the Foretime | The world before | Before the breaking. Almost nothing about it is agreed. |
| The substrate | The world's foundation | The layer beneath matter, energy, and time. The pre-breaking machines cracked it. |
| The substrate-leak | The crack | Where the substrate leaked through. The Choir woke through one crack; the Palimpsest persists in the layering of another. |

## §2 — The two great forces

### The Choir

| Deep term | Casual label | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| The Rot Choir | The Choir | A vast underground mycelial network that has come awake. Speaks in *we*. (Already image-led; keep.) |
| The First Root | The First Root | The Choir's underground architecture — kept, this is the most striking name in the corpus. |
| The substrate (Choir name) | The Choir's word for *the foundation* | The Choir thinks of the world as bedrock under bedrock. |
| Stacked plural | The deep voice | The Choir's underground voice — a single sentence carrying multiple "we"s at different scales. |

| The Long Throat | The slow voice | A fruiting body the Choir has kept *individuated* over long time. Mogu, Grib, Nam, Sien, Sopp are slow voices. *Not* an end-state — they're stable middles. |

### The Palimpsest

| Deep term | Casual label | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| The Palimpsest | The Palimpsest / the layered world | A *palimpsest* is a manuscript that has been scraped clean and written over, with the older words still faintly visible. The lore is named for this — the Palimpsest *is* the layering. Always gloss the word on first use. |
| The substrate / *the page* | The Palimpsest's word for *the foundation* | The Palimpsest thinks of the world as a surface that takes ink. |
| Over-text | What the world is now | The visible top layer of any place or object. |
| Under-text | What the world was | The fainter older layer. Visible to a Glimpse-attuned eye. |
| Thick / layered / flat surfaces | Thick / layered / flat | A *thick* surface holds three or more layers. A *flat* surface is one whose layers have equalised — readers can no longer tell which was first. |
| Flattening | Flattening | A surface losing its distinguishability. Slow if natural, fast if the Choir is digesting it. |
| Forced flattening | Live flattening | When the Choir digests a Palimpsest-readable place, the layers equalise *visibly* in real time. The reader gets one bilingual reading, then the surface goes blank to them. |
| Re-pressuring | Reading a flat surface back into legibility | A Curation-run technique. Briefly re-distinguishes the layers under the right reading conditions. |
| The layered tense | The all-three tense | The Palimpsest's grammar: a sentence carrying past, present, and *will-have-been* simultaneously. *I lifted-and-am-lifting-and-will-have-lifted the cup.* |
| Closed / open sentence | Pinned / unpinned | A *closed* sentence picks one tense; an *open* sentence keeps the layers simultaneous. The Palimpsest considers open the honest form. |
| The Re-pressed | The doubled (reader) | A reader who has stabilised mid-change with both layers legible in their own body. The Palimpsest's *populated middle* — the equivalent of a Long Throat. |
| The Glimpse | The Glimpse | A toggle that overlays the under-text on the over-text. (Already image-led; keep.) |
| Echo Reading | Echo Reading | Touch an object and feel its history. The Palimpsest's most-used skill name. (Already image-led; keep.) |
| The Manuscript | The Manuscript | The ability to *write* on the Palimpsest — leave temporal marks. (Already image-led; keep.) |
| The Revision | The Revision | Restore a place to a previous state for a duration. Then it crumbles back. (Already image-led; keep.) |

## §6 — Cross-faction concepts

| Deep term | Casual label | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Bridge-clause | A cross-grammar sentence | A sentence written by a senior figure in *another faction's grammar*. Rare. Always senior. Always partial. The Palimpsest reads them as *finished even when unsent.* |
| Senior carry | Senior letter | Hand-carriage of a document between faction seniors. Each grammar handles the carrier differently. |
| Triple-carriage witness | Three-grammar walker | A walker carrying letters in three faction-grammars in one season. Three known instances since the breaking. |

| The Year the Caravans Did Not Cross | The year of the closed pass | An eight-month period thirty-one years ago. The first cross-faction event remembered the same way (and named differently) by Concord, Brine, and Thermoclaves. |
| Pocket-class artifact | Pocket-class | An object whose institutional home is a person, by structural necessity. The First Card and Tossan's slip are the two known examples. |
| The detached margin | A margin without a book | A scrap of paper where only marginalia survives — the underlying text destroyed. The Palimpsest reads these most readably. |

## §8 — Examples in the wild

A few worked sentences showing the casual labels in narrative use, with the
deep terms noted in the footnotes. These are the texture you're aiming for.

> **Example 1.** The Brine carry the sea inside them. A Brine far from any
> coast keeps a small bowl on the windowsill — drawn from any source,
> refilled at high tide. The bowl is not consecrated. It is *attended.*
> If you ask such a Brine what the tide is doing back at home, they will
> tell you: *"The tide is highen at the third water."* They are pointing.
>
> *(Casual labels: "carry the sea inside them" = dry-walking; "highen" =
> remembered tense; "the third water" = a tide-mark.)*

> **Example 2.** When a Thermoclave bank a fire down to its ember, they
> need a witness. Banking alone isn't banking — the grammar calls it
> extinguishing. The keeper isn't being theatrical. The ember-floor only
> *takes* the heat if the keeping was witnessed.
>
> *(Casual labels: "bank a fire down to its ember" = banking; "the ember-
> floor only takes" = the substrate-acceptance edge case; "witness" stays
> as witness.)*

> **Example 3.** The Glassblown have a way of refusing to answer that is
> not silence. They form a small piece of paste while the question is
> being asked, hold it for some breaths, then fold it back into the
> batch unfired. The forming is the answer. You are invited to watch it
> rise and dissolve. Most questioners don't realise what they were given.
>
> *(Casual labels: "form a small piece of paste" = a deferred shape; "fold
> it back into the batch unfired" = re-batching; "the forming is the
> answer" is the doctrinal claim, kept verbatim.)*

> **Example 4.** Some readers stabilise mid-change with both the now and
> the before legible in the same body. They speak in two tenses at once
> and write what was already written. Their mornings are odd: the over-
> text body sits up while the under-text body still dreams, and for
> about a quarter-hour the layers don't quite match.
>
> *(Casual labels: "stabilise mid-change" = the Re-pressed; "the over-text
> body sits up while the under-text body still dreams" = the Re-pressed
> morning, kept verbatim because the imagery does its own work.)*

> **Example 5.** Three elders are writing letters. Veshen, an old Concord
> trader, has been revising one sentence to Suul, an old fire-keeper, for
> sixty-one days. The sentence uses Concord trade-grammar and Thermoclave
> count-grammar in the same breath, and it doesn't quite work in either.
> The Palimpsest reads it anyway, even though Veshen has not sent it.
>
> *(Casual labels: "an old Concord trader / an old fire-keeper" = Senior
> Assayer / Senior Keeper, casualised; "doesn't quite work in either" =
> a bridge-clause; "the Palimpsest reads it anyway, even unsent" is the
> doctrinal claim about bridge-clauses, kept verbatim.)*
