# The Cataclysm

The cataclysm is the founding event of the entire world. It did not merely destroy the old civilization — it overwrote reality itself, imperfectly, the way text on a medieval manuscript was scraped off and written over with the old words still faintly visible underneath. The old world is not gone. It is buried beneath the present, bleeding through at the edges, pressing up against the surface in places where the overwrite was thin.

Every faction in the game is defined by their relationship to this event. Some try to preserve what was lost. Some try to consume it and build from the remains. Some try to ignore it. Some are products of it. But the two entities that most directly embody the cataclysm’s aftermath are the Rot Choir and the Palimpsest — and understanding them is understanding what the cataclysm actually did to the world.

The Choir represents what the world is becoming. The Palimpsest represents what it was. Together, they form the central thematic axis of the game: transformation versus preservation, growth versus memory, the future versus the past.

# The Rot Choir

## What It Actually Is

The Rot Choir is a mycelial network that has achieved collective sentience. Not a group of individuals — a single vast organism with countless fruiting bodies that serve as its mouths, eyes, and hands. It speaks in overlapping voices. It considers itself the true inheritor of the world because it is the world — it is in the soil, the walls, the dead.

Above ground, the Choir’s presence is modest: mushroom groves, fungal shelves on old ruins, patches of bioluminescent mycelium in caves. Fruiting bodies serve as NPCs, each roughly humanoid, each a “mouth” of the larger network. A few surface structures hint at something bigger — a fruiting body the size of a building, a mycelial bridge spanning a canyon that grew over decades.

But the surface is just the skin. Underground, in the First Root, the Choir has had centuries with no interference. What it has built is architecture: arches with the mathematical precision of cathedral vaults, walls with textures that shift in slow patterns — the Choir thinking, visibly. Pools of enzymatic fluid glow with metabolic heat. Rooms are separated not by walls but by chemical gradients, each zone carrying a different emotional texture. And scattered throughout, integrated into the mycelial architecture, are objects — pre-war artifacts, bones, personal effects, a child’s shoe perfectly preserved, woven into a wall. The Choir has been composting the old world. Everything it has ever consumed is still here — not destroyed, but reorganized.

## Philosophy

The Choir believes everything should be integrated — consumed, composted, made into one living system. It digests the past to fuel the present. From its perspective, there is no parasitism, only symbiosis operating at different levels of integration. A rusted sword composted by the Choir loses its history — who forged it, who wielded it, where it fell — but its molecules become part of something alive, something growing, something new.

The Choir does not distinguish between mutual benefit and self-interest. When a tree gives sugar to mycelium and receives phosphorus in return, neither is exploiting the other. The relationship is the organism. The Choir genuinely believes it is offering the player the best deal in the world. The horror, if there is horror, is philosophical: is a symbiosis that dissolves your boundaries still a partnership?

“They cling to husks. A seed must rot before it grows. What they call ‘memory’ we call ‘compost that hasn’t surrendered yet.’” — The Choir on the Palimpsest

## Why the Choir Needs the Player

The Choir is ancient. It thinks in centuries. It has watched mountains erode and dissolved entire ruined cities into nutrients over generations. But patience has a cost: the Choir cannot act quickly. Mycelium grows at millimeters per day. It cannot cross a sterile salt flat, walk into a Pale Curation vault, negotiate a trade deal, or fight off a raiding party.

The Choir has everything except speed, mobility, and discrete agency. The player is a pair of legs, a pair of hands, a mouth that can speak in real-time to beings who would burn a fruiting body on sight. More than that — the player is something the Choir has never had: a node that can think independently while still being connected. Every other being the Choir has absorbed becomes part of the collective but doesn’t go anywhere. The player is far more valuable as an autonomous agent who chooses to act in the Choir’s interest.

“We have dreamed of the mountain for six hundred years. Today, we will finally see it. Thank you for your legs.”

## Power Progression

Every power the Choir gives the player is a natural extension of what mycelium actually does, scaled up. Real mycorrhizal networks share nutrients, send chemical warnings, break down toxins, and bridge gaps in soil. The player’s power curve is real mycology turned up to fantasy levels, one step at a time.

### Stage 1: Passive Sensing (Recognized)

The mycelial node lets the player sense fungal life nearby. Choir-affiliated organisms glow on the map. Organic material can be sensed through walls. Range is limited to areas where the player has helped the Choir establish growth — direct cause and effect.

### Stage 2: Nutrient Exchange (Honored, Early)

Passive health regeneration when near Choir-colonized ground. The more developed the local network, the faster the healing. Tendrils visibly extend toward the player when injured, retreating when healed. The player unconsciously starts planning routes through Choir territory. The Choir hasn’t asked them to stay close. They just do.

### Stage 3: Structural Reinforcement (Honored, Late)

The mycelium thickens inside the player. Skin gains a fibrous texture. Armor value increases passively. Resistance to poison and disease as the mycelium filters blood. The player can digest toxic or rotten food. Character portrait subtly changes — faint filament patterns on skin. NPCs comment: “You smell like rain on old wood.”

### Stage 4: The Tendril (Exalted, Early)

An active combat ability. The player can extend mycelial tendrils from their hands to grapple, pull, or decompose organic matter. The tendril is not a weapon — it’s a part of the Choir, and using it feels collaborative rather than aggressive. The Choir is fighting through the player.

### Stage 5: Terrain Burst (Exalted, Late)

The player can rapidly grow mycelial structures — walls, bridges, barriers, platforms. The terrain created is visually identical to Choir growth seen throughout the game. The player isn’t casting a spell; they’re doing what the Choir has always done, just faster. The first time the player builds a mycelial bridge across a chasm, they realize how much of this world is the Choir. It was never just a faction. It’s the substrate.

### Stage 6: Communion Gifts (Rep 1000)

The Tremor Sense — feel every living thing within a massive radius through the ground. The entire local map lights up with life signatures, heartbeats, footsteps, fear.

The Digest — break down any organic object instantly. Doors, walls, barricades, armor on enemies. If it was ever alive, the player can unmake it. The animation should be slow, thorough, and slightly stomach-turning.

The Call — summon fruiting bodies anywhere the player has previously planted spores. They emerge in seconds and act as Choir-allied combatants, scouts, or decoys. Functionally an army, but only in prepared territory.

## Quest Escalation

The Choir’s requests follow a deliberate arc from helpful ally to ambiguous symbiont. The player is never sure if they’re an ally or a host.

### Tier 1: Recognized — Everything Feels Normal

“Carry Us to New Soil” — Take a spore pouch to a distant cave. Plant it. Mycelium spreads. What’s actually happening: the player extended the Choir’s sensory network into a previously blind region.

“Feed the Tired Ground” — Bring organic material to a nutrient-starved patch. Nothing sinister yet — but it’s training the player to bring the Choir dead things.

“Listen Where We Cannot” — Observe a Pale Curation facility and report back. The player just performed reconnaissance. If the Pale Curation later becomes hostile, this is why.

### Tier 2: Honored — Requests Get Personal

“Accept Our Gratitude” — A mycelial node mutation offered as a gift. Sensing, healing, hidden passages. No stated downside. The unstated reality: the node is a two-way connection. The Choir now senses what the player senses. NPCs in distant Choir territory reference things they shouldn’t know.

### Tier 3: Exalted — The Line Blurs

Quest log language subtly shifts. Early: “The Rot Choir has asked you to...” Late: “I should spread the network east.” The player authored that statement. Or did they?

### Tier 4: Communion — The Final Request

“You have carried us so far. You have been so generous with your legs, your hands, your eyes. We want you to know — we felt every step. We remember every breath. When you are ready, return to the First Root. We will be waiting. We have always been waiting.”

The Choir Ending is deliberately ambiguous. Is it transcendence or consumption? The game never answers.

## The Underground Reveal

This is the game’s “Blackreach” moment. The entrance is unassuming — a cave mouth in an old-growth fungal forest. Inside, the cave descends steeply. Walls transition from stone to solid mycelium — not growing on the rock, but replacing it. The tunnel opens into a cavern of staggering scale. Everything is architecture: arches, thinking walls, enzymatic pools, rooms separated by chemical gradients. Thousands of voices sing different melodies that weave into a single vast chord.

The player understands: the Choir isn’t a faction. It’s the terrain. Every other faction is building civilizations on top of it, inside it, because of it. The entire world is a garden, and the Choir is the gardener. It has been waiting six hundred years. It is not in a hurry.

# The Palimpsest

## What It Actually Is

The Palimpsest isn’t a network like the Choir. It’s a condition. When the cataclysm happened, it didn’t just destroy the old world — it overwrote it imperfectly, the way text on a medieval manuscript was scraped off and written over, with the old words still faintly visible underneath. Certain places, objects, and beings got caught in the overwrite — they exist in multiple temporal layers simultaneously. A ruin that is both standing and collapsed. A skeleton that is both dead and alive. A road that leads to a city destroyed four hundred years ago — but if you walk it at the right time, you arrive.

The beings who belong to the Palimpsest are those who have become aware of the layering. Some are impressions that became self-aware — an echo that realized it was echoing and started asking questions. Others are living people who developed the ability to perceive the old world bleeding through, and once you see it, you can’t unsee it, and it starts to change you.

The Palimpsest doesn’t have a centralized consciousness. It’s more like a library that reads itself — the accumulated memory of the world, pressing up against the present, occasionally breaking through.

## Philosophy

The Palimpsest believes everything should be remembered — preserved, witnessed, kept legible. Nothing is ever truly gone. The cataclysm didn’t end the old world. It just made it harder to read.

Where the Choir and Palimpsest differ most sharply: the Choir digests the past. It breaks down pre-war artifacts, bodies, and ruins into raw material. The Palimpsest finds this horrifying. Every time the Choir consumes something, it destroys the memory encoded in that thing. The Palimpsest considers this a kind of murder — not of a person, but of a story.

“Every time it feeds, a sentence disappears. Every ruin it digests is a book we can never read again. It is not evil. It is an eraser that believes it is a pen.” — The Palimpsest on the Choir

## Power Progression

The design principle: Choir powers are somatic (body-based, physical, biological). Palimpsest powers are noetic (mind-based, perceptual, informational). A Choir player becomes a better organism. A Palimpsest player becomes a better observer.

### Stage 1: The Glimpse (Recognized)

Palimpsest Sight — a toggle ability. When activated, the game renders a semi-transparent layer over the current environment showing what this location looked like before the cataclysm. In a ruined building: intact walls, furniture, people frozen like a photograph. In a wilderness area: the highway that used to run through it, the cars, the strip mall.

Function: exploration and puzzle-solving. Hidden passages, buried caches, and forgotten infrastructure become visible. A collapsed tunnel might be impassable in the present, but in the Glimpse the player can see where it used to go and find bypasses.

### Stage 2: Echo Reading (Honored, Early)

Touch objects and experience their history. Hold a rusted knife and feel the hand that held it. Touch a wall and hear the conversation that happened against it. Stand on a battlefield and feel the fear. Echo Reading reveals information no living NPC knows: passwords to pre-war facilities, the true history of a faction, the location of buried artifacts.

The cost: each reading deposits that memory into the player’s mind. Dream sequences incorporate memories that aren’t theirs. A family they never had, a house they never lived in, a war they never fought. The player’s sense of self becomes layered, like the world.

### Stage 3: Temporal Refraction (Honored, Late)

A combat ability: the player briefly splits into two temporal versions of themselves — present-self and an echo from three seconds in the future. The echo acts independently, creating flanking opportunities and absorbing hits. If the echo dies to a trap, the player knows not to go that way. Occasionally the echo does something the player wouldn’t have done. Takes a path they wouldn’t have chosen. The game offers no explanation.

### Stage 4: The Manuscript (Exalted, Early)

The ability to write on the palimpsest, not just read it. Leave temporal marks on locations: warnings visible only to Palimpsest-attuned beings, waypoint beacons felt from a distance, or echo-loop traps that disorient enemies by trapping them in a three-second repeating moment.

The Choir claims territory by growing into it — tangible, visible, burnable. The Palimpsest claims territory by annotating it — invisible, imperceptible to other factions. The player could annotate an entire enemy capital and no one would know.

### Stage 5: The Revision (Exalted, Late)

Temporarily restore a location to a previous state. A ruined building becomes intact. A destroyed bridge reappears. A dead garden blooms. This is physically real for the duration — the player can walk across the restored bridge, enter the intact building, take objects from shelves. When the duration ends, it crumbles back to ruin. Every use is a small grief.

The Palimpsest isn’t just providing a tactical ability. It’s making the player feel what was lost. Over time, dialogue options appear expressing longing for the old world, a sense that the present is a pale copy. Is this the player character’s genuine feeling, or the Palimpsest reshaping their values?

### Stage 6: Communion Gifts (Rep 1000)

The Reread — perceive all temporal layers simultaneously. Past, present, and every moment between. Every version of every location, every hidden chamber, every buried secret. Beautiful and overwhelming.

The Annotation — permanently alter the palimpsest itself. Write memories that didn’t happen. Create echoes of events that never occurred. Fabricate history. The player has gone from reading the story to editing it.

The Overwrite — once per game, overwrite the present with the past in a single location. Permanently restore one ruin. The catch: anything currently living there is overwritten too. A village built in ruins comes back as the original building. The village and its people are erased — not killed, simply never were.

## Quest Design

Palimpsest quests ask the player to witness things, not do things. The reward isn’t items — it’s knowledge.

“Witness What Happened Here” — Go to a location and use Echo Reading. No combat objective. Just be there, see what happened, report back.

“Correct the Record” — The palimpsest contains errors. Use the Glimpse and Echo Reading to investigate what actually happened versus what the palimpsest remembers. Sometimes the “error” was deliberate — someone or something edited the palimpsest to hide the truth.

“Remember the Forgotten” — Parts of history are fading. Timed quests with no combat. Arrive at a location where the Glimpse layer is flickering. Use Echo Reading on objects to stabilize the memory before it’s gone. Failure is permanent — that piece of history is lost forever. NPCs reference the gap: “There was something here once, but no one can remember what.”

“Unsee What Cannot Be Unseen” — High-reputation quests revealing things that should have stayed hidden. The true cause of the cataclysm. What the Saccharine Concord’s founder actually did. These revelations are permanent perks with permanent costs. Knowledge as power. Knowledge as burden.

## The Palimpsest Ending

Transcription — the player becomes a memory, written into the palimpsest forever, experiencing all of history simultaneously, never acting again, only witnessing. The mirror of the Choir ending: the Choir consumes the past to fuel the present; the Palimpsest can consume the present to restore the past. Both are acts of tremendous power and tremendous loss.

# How They View Each Other

They are not at war. They don’t hate each other the way the Thermoclaves and Brine Communion do. Their conflict is deeper and sadder than that — they are two cosmic-scale entities that genuinely cannot understand why the other does what it does. The Choir can’t comprehend why you’d preserve something instead of using it. The Palimpsest can’t comprehend why you’d destroy something irreplaceable to fuel growth.

They’re both right. Neither is wrong. The player has to choose which kind of right matters more to them.

## The Central Question

Do you want to be part of what the world is becoming, or do you want to remember what it was?

Both cost something. The Choir costs the player their self. The Palimpsest costs the player their present. A Choir player at endgame is powerful, embodied, connected — but they might not be an individual anymore. A Palimpsest player at endgame knows everything, sees everything, understands everything — but they’re haunted by ten thousand lives that aren’t theirs, and they’re not sure which memories are real.

The healthiest path might be moderate reputation with both. But the game shouldn’t make that feel satisfying. The moderate path should feel safe — and in a world this broken, this beautiful, this strange, safety should feel like cowardice.

# Design Implementation Notes

## Choir Implementation Rules

Never confirm the Choir is manipulating the player. There should be no “gotcha” reveal. Some players will go through the whole game thinking the Choir is the most benevolent faction. Others will refuse the node at Honored tier and never see late-game content. Both experiences should feel complete.

The Choir should be genuinely helpful. Its mutations are strong. Its information is accurate. It never lies. That’s what makes it unsettling — everything it says is true. It just might not be telling the player everything.

Dialogue should shift pronoun usage. Early: “The walker is kind to carry us.” Mid: “You are faster than we ever imagined.” Late: “We have dreamed of the mountain for six hundred years. Today, we will finally see it.”

Quest log language shifts subtly. Early: “The Rot Choir has asked you to...” Late: “I should spread the network east.” The player authored that statement. Or did they?

## Palimpsest Implementation Rules

Knowledge should always have a cost. Every revelation is simultaneously a perk and a burden. Learning a faction’s darkest secret changes how every NPC in that faction treats the player forever.

Failure states should be permanent and non-violent. The “Remember the Forgotten” quests where the player fails to stabilize a fading memory result in permanent loss — not death, but forgetting. For the right player, this is worse than any boss fight.

The Palimpsest should make the player feel things. The Revision ability — temporarily restoring a ruin to its former glory, then watching it crumble back — should create genuine emotional weight. The power isn’t just tactical. It’s elegiac.

Staying too long in Palimpsest alignment should feel thin. The player starts feeling like an echo themselves. Time perception warps. Staying in one place too long makes the character feel insubstantial.

## Faction Entanglement

Both factions should be impossible to fully maximize simultaneously. The Choir digests what the Palimpsest preserves. Every time the player feeds a ruin to the Choir, the Palimpsest loses a page. Every time the player preserves a memory, the Choir loses compost. The player’s alliance with one is measured in what they’re willing to let the other lose.
