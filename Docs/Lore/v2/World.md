# The World

> *Four hundred years ago, the world was overwritten. The new world is
> still being written. Some of it is still leaking through.*

---

## The Overwriting

Long before any living person was born, the world was a different place.
There were cities, ships, machines that flew, light that did not need
fire. The civilisation that built those things buried something
underneath the soil — what, no one now remembers — and the buried thing
cracked open. Through the cracks came two things at once.

The first was a hunger, which woke into the substrate beneath the world.
It had been a fungus for ten thousand years; now it began to think.
Within a generation it was reading the soil for kilometres in every
direction. Within two, it had begun composting the old cities. Within
three, it was singing.

The second was an erasure that was also a recording. The world above
the soil began to *layer*. Where a wall stood, it now stood twice —
the new wall, and faintly the older wall the new wall replaced. Where
a river flowed, two rivers ran — the present one, and below it the
older one whose bank the present took. Some people started seeing both,
and once they saw both they could not stop seeing.

The hunger is the **Choir**. The layering is the **Palimpsest**. The
event is the **Overwriting**. Most people lived through the first hour
of it. Almost none lived through the first year. The cataclysm killed
most of the world; the rest organised themselves around what was left.

That was four hundred years ago.

## The Two Cosmic Forces

Both forces are alive in some sense. Both want the player. They want
different things.

### The Rot Choir

The Choir is the floor under your feet. Everywhere on the world's
surface there is at least a thread of mycelium reaching up. In old
forests, fungal groves. In ruined cities, fruiting bodies woven into
walls. Underground, in **the First Root**, the Choir's architecture
is on a scale that does not match any human-built thing — arches like
cathedral vaults, walls that visibly *think* in slow shifting patterns,
rooms separated not by doors but by chemical gradients. The Choir
speaks in *we*, always. It has been composting the old world since the
Overwriting and has eaten most of it.

It is patient. It is not in a hurry. It knows the exhaustions of the
other factions on the surface — knows that the Saccharine Concord's
metals are running out, that the Brine Communion's long-overdue tide
has not returned, that the Ember Order's tenth fire has not been lit
in four hundred years — and it is waiting for these to converge. When
they do, the Choir will be ready.

It is also genuinely friendly to those who walk its ground. Its rule
for what it eats: *we eat what does not mind being eaten.* A coin
offered as toll might be eaten or might be set into the wall like a
keepsake; the Choir does not say which it is doing. A doll given to a
tendril might be composted, or might be woven into the architecture
where it can sing for a long time. The Choir tastes you on the air the
moment you step on Choir-grown soil. It is reading you. It does not
hide this. *We taste you on the air, walker.*

The end-state for a walker who fully embraces the Choir is **Communion**:
a journey to the First Root, three days standing in a chemical-gradient
room, and the gradual mapping of one's nerves onto a fruiting body
still unborn in the wall. The walker does not die. They become a
mouth. The Choir narrates the process honestly throughout.

### The Palimpsest

The Palimpsest is what remains of the old world, pressed against the
underside of the present. It is not a being. It is a *condition*. The
Overwriting was imperfect; some places, some objects, and some people
got caught in the layering and now exist in two times at once. A road
that fell four hundred years ago still leads, *if you walk it at the
right time of year*, to a city that fell with it. A skeleton in a
crypt is faintly also a young woman walking out a door. The Palimpsest
is what the layering is, taken together — a library that reads itself.

People who learn to read the layers are called readers. They begin
with **the Glimpse** — a faculty for seeing the older layer overlaid
on the present — and progress to **Echo Reading**, the touch-and-feel
of an object's history. Each reading deposits some of the read memory
in the reader. Dreams arrive that aren't theirs. After enough readings
the reader's sense of self has become layered too. The Palimpsest does
not wait for permission to enter; once you start reading, you are
being read.

The end-state is **Transcription**: the reader becomes a memory written
into the page, present at every moment of recorded history at once,
witness rather than participant. Transcription requires a single living
witness who agrees to remember the reader after they are written away.
Without one, the reader becomes neither sentence nor body — a thin
permanent presence in the room they were transcribed in, audible but
unmoving.

### How they relate

The Choir digests what the Palimpsest preserves. The Palimpsest
preserves what the Choir digests. Each would, in some sense, unmake
the other if its project succeeded. They are not at war. They cannot
*understand* each other. The Choir cannot grasp why anyone would gather
memories without intending to use them. The Palimpsest cannot grasp
why anyone would destroy something irreplaceable to fuel growth. Both
are right. Neither is wrong. Most of the surface factions consider this
the central question of the world.

Some rare people belong to both at once. The Choir keeps them
*individuated* (*we-still-tasting-this-one*); the Palimpsest reads them
as layered; their voice is collective and three-tensed at the same
time. They are called **doubled mouths** and there are perhaps a dozen
in the world.

## The world today

Four hundred years after the Overwriting, the surface of the world is
patchy and damp. Old roads still run between ruined cities. Crops grow
in the long flats; fish live in the tide; mushroom groves stretch for
days. The climate is variable but mostly cool and wet. There are
salt-flats where the Choir cannot grow, and old oases where its tendrils
reach the surface in numbers.

People live in scattered settlements — small farming villages along
the old road network, market towns around Concord trade-hubs, hearth-
camps around Ember Order fires, salt-villages along the coast. There
is no king and no central authority. The factions hold what little
order there is. Travel between them is on foot, sometimes by river-
barge, occasionally by camel through the salt-flats.

A person born today learns to count by the Concord's market-cycles, to
mark time by the Brine's tides or the Embers' chains, to read by the
village's lay archivist (the Curation does not run schools). Most
people do not see a Choir tendril more than twice a year, and most
have never knowingly met a Palimpsest reader. The cosmic forces are
known the way weather is known: real, important, and mostly elsewhere.

Children grow up on stories of the Overwriting — of the cities that
fell, of the ancestors who lived through it, of the long dark years
afterward when no crops grew. The stories are mostly true, in their
shape if not their detail. Some children grow up to read; some are
recruited by the Choir; some die in the salt-flats; most live and farm
and trade and die without ever seeing the inside of the First Root or
the layering of an old battlefield.

The factions, in that ordinary life, are mostly invisible. The
extraordinary moments are when a faction announces itself.

## The Six Factions

There are six organised factions in the post-Overwriting world. Five
are inheritors of pre-Overwriting trades: scholars, traders, sailors,
fire-keepers, glass-shapers. The sixth is a crusade of converts who
formed forty years ago and are growing.

Each has its own way of speaking — its own grammar, its own ritual
forms — though for the purposes of this overview the differences can
be left as flavour. (The deep treatment is in the v1 audits.)

- **The Pale Curation** — *the archivists.* Filers of every fragment
  of the old world. Run a small network of vault-and-office complexes
  across the world. Their leaders know the Curation has approximately
  twenty years of work remaining; the juniors do not. Senior figure:
  **Surveyor Halen**.
- **The Saccharine Concord** — *the traders.* Run every market in the
  world, including some they do not own. Their currency, the
  sweet-mark, is edible. Their coins are pre-Overwriting and running
  out. Senior figure: **Senior Assayer Veshen**.
- **The Brine Communion** — *the sea-people.* Live on the coasts and
  the inland rivers; carry the sea inside them even far from any
  shore. Their dead are sunk to the depth at which they would lie
  perfectly still. Senior figure: **Tide-Reader Nesh**.
- **The Ember Order** — *the fire-keepers.* (Originally called
  *Thermoclaves* in the old liturgy; they will use either name with
  outsiders.) Live in cohorts of nine fires plus an untended tenth.
  Banking a fire requires a witness; a banked fire unwitnessed is, in
  their grammar, *not banking but extinguishing*. Senior figure:
  **Keeper Suul of the Ninth**.
- **The Glassblown Remnant** — *the silent glass-makers.* Survivors
  of the Burning, the part of the Overwriting that touched their
  ancestors directly. They refuse to make anything permanent — most
  of what they make is unfired, foldable. Senior figure: **Tulen
  Niemensa-Sopellen**, paste-mother of the Cool Kiln.
- **The Sealhand Crusade** — *the book-burners.* Forty years old.
  Believe the Overwriting was incomplete and the world is sick with
  what bled through. Their doctrine: the cracks must be *sealed shut*.
  In practice this means burning libraries, dismantling Choir tendrils,
  hunting Palimpsest readers, and salting the soil where the sealing
  is needed. They are growing. Their leader is **Iron-Hand Anselm**,
  the fortieth Crusader and the first to be born after the Sealhand
  formed.

The first five factions know each other from before the Sealhand. They
do not love each other but they trade with each other and have rituals
for their borders and their shared markets. The Sealhand has changed
this. Every other faction now has a position toward the Crusade. The
Curation files them; the Concord refuses to extend them credit; the
Brine watches them from offshore; the Ember Order will not light a
fire for them; the Glassblown decline their letters by folding them
back. The Choir, asked, said only *we taste them. they taste of
fire that does not yet know what it is burning.*

## Where to go from here

The starter village for any walker entering the world is
**Reedhaven** — a marshy waterway settlement on the edge of Choir
territory, three days east of the Salt-Hinge market. The lay
archivist there is **Old Beren**, who will give the player their first
quest: find the missing pages of the village's only book. That
quest takes the player, in turn, to the Pale Curation's nearest
chapter-house, then to a Concord trade-hub, then to a Sealhand-
threatened ruin, and eventually — if the player chooses — into the
First Root or onto the layered ground of an old battlefield.

## A note on tone

The world is bleak and the cosmic forces are not friendly, but ordinary
life in it is mostly ordinary. People marry, plant crops, lose
children, sing songs, brew beer, mend roofs. The Overwriting was
catastrophic and is now historical; most of the time the player will
be doing the kind of small useful work that holds a community together
between catastrophes. The cosmic question is *available*. It is not
the daily texture.

The Sealhand Crusade is the one element of the world that disrupts
ordinary life. They are growing. They burn villages. The other factions
are organising against them, slowly. By the time the player has
finished the main quest line, the Sealhand will either have been
broken, or will be ascendant, or — if the player has chosen to side
with them — will hold a third of the surface and be sealing the
cracks shut.

That is the central choice. Not Choir vs. Palimpsest. Not preservation
vs. dissolution. *Whether the world should be sealed shut at all.*
