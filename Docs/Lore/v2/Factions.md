# The Six Factions

> Five inheritors of the pre-Overwriting trades, plus one new crusade
> that is changing everything.

---

## 1. The Pale Curation — *the archivists*

> *Filing every fragment of the old world before it is lost. Twenty
> years of work remaining.*

The Curation are scholars and bureaucrats. They run a small network
of vault-and-office complexes — *chapter-houses* — across the world.
Their work is procedural: every artifact gets a tag, every tag has a
class, every class goes in a vault, every vault has a register.

The senior leadership knows a hard fact the juniors are not told. The
cataclysm-ruins are finite. At current intake, the Curation has
**twenty years of work left**. After that, they will be a body that
maintains what it has filed, not a body that files. The senior
leadership disagrees — quietly, in writing — about what to do when
the work runs out.

Their grammar carries a distinctive on-record voice for things that
have been observed and filed, plus several conventions for marking
*what was deliberately not entered*. (A single mid-line dot, in
Curation prose, is read as *the field has been deliberately not
entered*, not as *unknown*.) For surface purposes the only thing the
reader needs to know is: the Curation distinguishes carefully between
what has been catalogued and what has not.

**Senior figure:** **Surveyor Halen**, a Tier-11 cataloguer at the
Hyphal Surveys Office. Filed the doll-in-the-wall recovery (accession
11-K-3318) and writes the unauthorised footnotes that the Director
prefers not to see.

**Starter contact:** **A.**, a junior cataloguer who keeps writing
things down truthfully when the institution would rather she didn't.
Reachable at any chapter-house intake desk after-hours.

**Where they sit:** strategic neutral. The Curation will witness
anything for anyone, which is why every other faction trusts them
slightly. The Sealhand Crusade is the first faction in living memory
the Curation has refused to file for.

## 2. The Saccharine Concord — *the traders*

> *Every debt can be balanced. Every coin is older than your
> grandfather.*

The Concord run every market in the world. Their currency, the
**sweet-mark**, is a compressed honey-grain disc you can eat after
spending — a denomination that is also a calorie. Their best market
is **the Salt-Hinge**, a four-mornings-a-week meeting of all six
factions where the half-redemption convention applies (any market
trade can be re-opened next market-day at half its original price,
no reason given).

The senior assayers know what the apprentices are not told. **Concord
coins are not minted — they are gathered.** Every coin in circulation
pre-dates the Overwriting. The coins are running out. The senior
assayer, **Veshen**, estimates Foretime stock will be functionally
exhausted in **eleven years**. The Concord has never operated without
Foretime alloys.

Their grammar requires a *price-tag* on every obligation-clause (*I
will meet you at the Salt-Hinge, ¼*) — the fraction is the opening
cost of the meeting. Concord children learn this before they learn
full sentences. Outsiders rarely speak it correctly. Outsiders who
fail to tag a clause are pricing the conversation as *gift*, which
the Concord has no separate word for.

**Senior figure:** **Senior Assayer Tobir Veshen**, third-tooth (an
assayer who has lost the front teeth and works by the molars).
Thirty-one years in continuous service. Has never been wrong about an
alloy.

**Starter contact:** **Brem the apprentice**, age 19, in the
Reckoner's chair at a Salt-Hinge stall. Learning to *hear* a trade by
tallying its priced-clause tails.

**Where they sit:** the Concord is everywhere. They are not
politically aligned. They will trade with anyone — except the
Sealhand, whom they refuse credit on principle. (When pressed, Veshen
gives a single-sentence reason: *one cannot price a thing that aspires
to be unfileable.*)

## 3. The Brine Communion — *the sea-people*

> *We carry the sea inside us, even inland.*

The Brine live on the coasts and along inland rivers; they fish the
rising tide (never the falling), recover what the tide returns,
write to their gone-deep dead, and read the salt-patterns on stones.
Their inland membership — the **dry-walking** — outnumbers the
coastal Brine three to one. A dry-walking Brine keeps a small bowl of
water on a windowsill, drawn from any source, refilled at the home
tide. The bowl is not consecrated. It is *attended.*

They believe every body, every object, and every promise has a depth
at which it would lie perfectly still — a held depth at which the
thing is, in their word, *kept*. They commit their dead to a Brine
shore at an unfavourable tide, so the deceased's account is over-paid
when the tide takes them.

Their canonical scripture is the **deep-letters** — replies from the
gone-deep, recorded by tide-witnesses on shore. A swimmer surfaces
once, speaks the reply once, and four to seven witnesses each write it
from memory in their own hand. The bound knot of versions is the
scripture. *Consensus across the knot* is what the gone-deep said;
*divergences* are what the witnesses said. A scripture that is a hash,
not a text.

**Senior figure:** **Tide-Reader Nesh of the Coastal Embayment**.
Forty-one years a tide-reader. Has computed the going-deep tides for
one hundred and eleven walkers. Forty-three were called back; sixty-
eight went-deep. She visits seven of the gone-deep on the ceremonial
calendar.

**Starter contact:** **a bowl-maker** — an inland Brine who walks
roads gathering vessels (cups, mugs, hollow stones, dried gourds) and
trades them for sentences in the *remembered tense*: sentences about
water absent from the room.

**Where they sit:** the Brine are slow to act and fast to remember.
They will not war for the Sealhand or against them. But the long-
overdue tide — the cataclysm-tide that did not return four hundred
years ago — is on their mind. Nesh believes it is on the same clock as
the Concord's coins.

## 4. The Ember Order — *the fire-keepers*

> *We keep nine fires. The tenth tends itself.*

(Older liturgy: *Thermoclaves*. The Ember Order is the everyday name;
older speakers and visitors who use *Thermoclaves* are not corrected.)

The Order live in cohorts of nine numbered fires plus an untended
tenth. Each fire has a name, a tally, a temperament, and a keeper.
Each cohort has a senior who carries a small slate of strokes — one
per day-of-keeping — forward each morning. They do not *kindle* fires;
they *carry* them, ember to ember. *Kindling* happens once — the
founding fire — and is heretical to use of any subsequent fire.

The most important fire is the **tenth**, which is not lit, not kept,
and older than the camp. The Apprentice's Catechism asks whether the
tenth is *a keep, a quench, or a cold* and accepts the apprentice's
silence as the answer. Probably the tenth is a pre-Overwriting
substrate-leak; the Order will not commit to this in writing.

Their grammar is fire-aspect: every important verb takes one of four
particles indicating the speaker's relationship to fire. **Banking** —
reducing a fire to its kept ember without extinguishing — is the
hardest verb and *requires a witness*. A keeper banking unwitnessed is,
in their grammar, *not banking but extinguishing*. The grammar
enforces it.

**Senior figure:** **Keeper Suul of the Ninth** — forty-seven years a
keeper, tends the oldest active fire in the present count. Was
apprenticed at fourteen to **Erro**, who tended the tenth at the
moment of the Overwriting and did not survive. Suul is *lineage-
orphaned* — taught a count she cannot pass on under her teacher's
name.

**Starter contact:** **Renne**, a smoke-born apprentice (the bridge
generation born in the first twenty years after the Overwriting). She
is sixteen, keeps an unauthorised hearth-log in pencil margins, and
knows more than she lets on.

**Where they sit:** the Order maintains relations with everyone. They
are not at war. But they refuse to light a fire for the Sealhand
Crusade, on the doctrinal grounds that the Sealhand is *cold-coming*
in a way no carrying can warm.

## 5. The Glassblown Remnant — *the silent glass-makers*

> *We were burned. Now we refuse to make anything permanent.*

The Glassblown were the fuel of the Overwriting fire — their ancestors
burned into glass during the cataclysm, and their descendants shape
glass paste in workshops that double as memorials. Most of what they
make is *unfired* — paste before commitment, foldable, reversible. The
firing of a shape is a serious commitment; senior shapers fire perhaps
ten shapes in a lifetime.

They speak less than any other faction. Their silences are not empty —
their grammar has a fourth verb mood (the **unsaid mood**) that marks
sentences they choose *not* to finish, and treats the choosing-not-to
as positive content. Outsiders feel they missed half of every Glassblown
sentence. In fact every half is present.

Their long-distance correspondence is the **unfired letter** — a
parcel of glass-paste sealed in wet cloth, carried by hand. The
recipient unfolds it, reads the shape, and either fires it (agreement)
or folds it back into their own batch (refusal). A Glassblown's archive
is composed entirely of fired shapes received from others. There is no
record of any disagreement, anywhere, ever.

The end-state for a Glassblown is **unfiring** — the petitioner's
blood is mixed into a batch, the batch is shaped by others into
ordinary vessels (cups, lenses, salt-spoons), fired, and distributed.
The petitioner walks away and does not return. *They are not in the
cup; they are what the cup is, by a fraction.*

**Senior figure:** **Tulen Niemensa-Sopellen**, paste-mother of **the
Cool Kiln**. Sixty-eight years a Glassblown. Has fired only eleven
shapes in her career. Her name means *the-shape-that-is-not-yet* +
*the-not-yet-naming-of-it*.

**Starter contact:** **an unfired** — a Glassblown child who has not
yet been given their adult shape-name. Sitting in a workshop doorway,
forming and folding paste while strangers ask her questions.

**Where they sit:** the Glassblown do not fight, do not vote, do not
litigate. They fold back. The Sealhand does not yet know what to do
with them. The Crusade has burned three Glassblown workshops; each
time, the surviving Glassblown rebuilt without speaking. The Sealhand
is unsettled by this in a way they do not have a word for.

## 6. The Sealhand Crusade — *the book-burners*

> *The Overwriting was incomplete. We finish it.*

Forty years old. The newest faction, and the only one that did not
inherit a pre-Overwriting trade.

The Crusade's doctrine, in their own words: *the world was only
half-broken. Both halves now bleed into each other. The Choir feeds
on what bleeds in; the Palimpsest reads it. Both are sicknesses that
must be sealed. The cracks must be shut. The reading must stop. The
old world must be finished.*

In practice this means burning libraries (the Pale Curation's
chapter-houses in particular), dismantling Choir tendrils where they
can be dismantled, hunting Palimpsest readers as *infected*, and
**salting the soil** in any place where the bleeding-through is
strongest. They are organised, well-equipped, and growing. They
recruit from every other faction. Their converts are usually fervent
in proportion to the depth of the loss the converter survived.

The Sealhand's apotheosis is **the Sealing** — a Crusader who has
sealed enough cracks (by salt, by fire, by the destruction of layered
artifacts) is, by Sealhand doctrine, *finished*. They are anointed with
salt and ash and given the title **Sealmaster**. Two Sealmasters are
on the world today.

The Sealhand's danger is not their numbers — there are perhaps three
thousand of them across all the surface — but their *certainty*. They
do not negotiate. They consider every other faction either an obstacle
or a target. The Concord's eleven-year alloy crisis, the Curation's
twenty-year end-date, the Brine's missing tide — the Sealhand interprets
all of these as *the world's natural healing*, the cracks closing under
the weight of their work. They are accelerating.

**Senior figure:** **Iron-Hand Anselm**, the fortieth Crusader and the
first born after the Crusade's founding. Forty-one years old. Lost his
left hand in a Palimpsest reading at fifteen — which is why he is
*Iron-Hand*. He believes, with absolute sincerity, that the world will
be saved when the last reader stops reading and the last tendril is
cut.

**Starter contact:** none. The Crusade does not place itself for
recruitment in starter villages; it shows up at their gates.

**Where they sit:** the antagonist. The first faction the player will
hear about with fear. The faction that has burned a Curation chapter-
house, the faction that hunts readers, the faction whose torches the
player will see across the marsh on the third or fourth day.

## How they fit together

There is no central authority in the world. There is no Sultanate, no
king, no government. The factions hold what little order there is.

Before the Sealhand, the five inheritor-factions had a workable peace
— rituals for borders, conventions for the shared market at
Salt-Hinge, half-redemption rules, hand-off protocols, the unspoken
agreement that the Curation could witness anyone. The Choir and the
Palimpsest were *cosmic*, not factional. Each surface faction had a
position toward them, but not a war.

The Sealhand has changed this. The Crusade does not respect the old
borders. They burn what the Curation files, which forces the Curation
to choose. They forbid trade in pre-Overwriting goods, which costs the
Concord. They destroy salt-tides, which violates the Brine. They will
not be witnessed, which the Embers consider a foundational offence.
And they have begun trying to seal the substrate-cracks themselves,
which the Choir has, twice, opened the soil to swallow them for.

The world was not at war forty years ago. It is now slowly becoming
one. The player arrives at this moment.
