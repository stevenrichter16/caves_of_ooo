# The Opening

> The first hour of play. Reedhaven, Old Beren, and the missing pages
> of the village book. The Joppa equivalent.

---

## You wake in Reedhaven

You wake on a thin straw mattress in **Lin's inn**, in a small upstairs
room with a window that opens onto the western marsh. It is just after
dawn. The marsh is full of mist; the herons are calling. Through the
floorboards you can hear Lin downstairs, sweeping. She has been awake
for an hour.

You have a small purse of sweet-marks (six and a half), a change of
clothes, a knife, a waterskin, and a scrap of paper with one sentence
on it in handwriting you do not recognise:

> *Find the missing pages, traveller. Old Beren will know what to do.*

You don't remember coming to Reedhaven. You remember the road. You
remember a man with an iron hand passing in the opposite direction.
You remember the smell of smoke from a place that was not yet in
sight.

You go downstairs.

---

## Lin's inn

Lin is sweeping the common room. She is perhaps twenty-eight, sharp-
eyed, with a weariness that does not extend to her hands. She has set
out a piece of apple matz on the bar — Reedhaven's signature dish, a
wedge of dense apple-and-rye flatbread — and a small bowl of the
village's marsh-rye porridge.

She will charge you a quarter sweet-mark for breakfast. If you say
nothing about price, she will name the price; if you offer a
quarter-mark first, she will accept and you will have spoken your
first **priced clause** in the world.

After breakfast Lin will ask if you have business in the village. If
you mention Old Beren, her face will close slightly. *He's been
waiting for someone,* she will say. *I don't know what for, but he's
been waiting since the rains turned. His cottage is on the south bank,
the one with the marsh-rye plot. Don't go in his back room without
permission. He's not unkind, but it's the only thing he asks.*

She will not tell you anything else. She will not gossip about Beren.
She will, however, tell you about **Marl** out on the porch, who has
seen things on the high road, and **Pell** the bookbinder, who knows
Beren and may have a message for him.

---

## Old Beren

Old Beren's cottage is a single-room building on the south bank, with
a marsh-rye plot behind it and a low stone bench in front. Beren is
sitting on the bench, watching the herons in the channel. He is in his
late seventies, slight, dignified, with hands that have done thirty
years of careful work and now do small careful work.

He turns to you when you approach. He does not stand.

> *"Ah. You have come. Sit, traveller — the bench is dry today."*

He does not ask you who you are. He does not ask why you have come.
He has been waiting; you are who he has been waiting for. He pours
you a cup of marsh-rye tea from a pot that has been keeping warm on a
small flat stone beside him.

He will tell you, in his careful way:

- That **the village's only book** — a hand-stitched volume in his
  cottage's back room, going back over a century — is **incomplete**.
- That **pages have gone missing** — three pages at known locations,
  and a fourth that was never bound in. He suspects more.
- That he was a **Pale Curation surveyor** for thirty years, and he
  has been retired for fifteen, and he came home to Reedhaven
  expecting to die quietly. He has not died yet. He is annoyed about
  this in a small dry way.
- That a young scholar from the **Hyphal Surveys Office** — the
  nearest Curation chapter-house, four days northwest of the
  Salt-Hinge — passed through Reedhaven six weeks ago with a list of
  the missing pages and a plan to find them. The scholar walked into
  the marsh and did not return.
- That Beren has been waiting for the next traveller who came through
  Reedhaven, because he is too old to walk a four-day road and the
  next traveller is *who he has been waiting for*. That is you.

He will give you:

1. **A list of the four missing pages** (where they are believed to
   be; what they are believed to contain).
2. **A small marsh-rye reading** — a quick lesson in reading marsh-
   rye stalks for water-content. (This is functionally Echo Reading-
   lite. Plant the seed for the player's later Palimpsest awakening,
   if they choose that path.)
3. **A letter of introduction to A.**, the junior cataloguer at the
   Hyphal Surveys Office. *She will help you. The senior cataloguers
   may not. Trust A.*
4. **The first sweet-mark of his pension**, formally placed in your
   palm with a small bow — the Concord-style first-sweetness, signalling
   that Beren considers you a fellow traveller now, not a stranger.

He will not give you the village's book. The book stays in the
cottage. He explains: *the book is the village's body. The pages are
hands. We bring the hands home.*

---

## The four missing pages

Beren's list, as he gives it to you:

| # | Where | What it is believed to contain |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | The Drowned Library, two days south of Reedhaven | A recipe — *the apple matz Lin makes, but the older form* |
| 2 | The Hyphal Surveys Office, four days northwest of the Salt-Hinge | A name — *the name of the village's first marsh-mother* |
| 3 | The Salt-Hinge market itself, in a Concord debt-record cabinet | A contract — *the agreement between Reedhaven and the Concord* |
| 4 | Unknown — *the lost scholar found something and did not return; the page she found is not on the list because we do not know what it is* | Unknown |

The first three pages are recoverable by the patient walker. The
fourth is the main quest.

Beren does not say the fourth is the main quest. He simply says: *find
the others first. The fourth will come.*

---

## What you should do next

Three doors are open from this conversation. Each leads into a
different faction's introduction.

### Door 1 — South to the Drowned Library

The closest. Two days south, through the marsh. The Drowned Library
is a sunken pre-Overwriting library; the upper floor is wadeable.
This is the Palimpsest's introduction. You will likely meet **a
first-time Echo-Reader** there — a young walker who has just
discovered the Glimpse on a battered field-stone where two roads
cross.

You can try to read for yourself. You will likely fail your first
attempt. You will succeed by the second day.

**This door teaches you what the Palimpsest is.**

### Door 2 — West to the Salt-Hinge market

Three days west. The cross-faction market — Concord, Curation, Brine,
Embers, Glassblown all converge here four mornings a week. This is the
Concord's introduction (and a partial introduction to all six
factions). You will meet **Brem the apprentice** in the Reckoner's
chair, learn to *hear* a trade, and gain access to the Concord debt-
record cabinet that holds page #3.

If you stay an extra day, you can also see **Reckoner Tossan** set her
slip on the central balance-stone and walk away. (She does this on
fourth-day mornings, every other month.)

**This door teaches you how the world economically works.**

### Door 3 — Northwest to the Hyphal Surveys Office

Seven days, by way of the Salt-Hinge. The longest walk, and the most
politically loaded. This is the Curation's introduction. You will
meet **A.** (with Beren's letter of introduction), **Surveyor Halen**
(if you earn it), and possibly **Director Ven** (if A. cannot keep
you off her desk).

You will also, if you walk this road, see your first **Sealhand
Crusader** column. They are not yet hostile to you. They will become
hostile if they think you are a Reader, and will become very hostile
if they see Beren's letter.

**This door teaches you what the Curation is — and introduces the
antagonist.**

---

## Hidden — Door 4 — the Choir

Not on Beren's list, and not on the obvious map. As you walk any of
the three doors above, you will pass at least one Choir tendril —
most likely the **new tendril** at the footpath crossing two days
east of Reedhaven (off the road to the Salt-Hinge). The tendril will
greet you. You can offer it a coin and learn what the Choir does with
it. You can ask it about the Sealhand. You can simply pass.

If you offer the tendril the **lost scholar's name** (which you do
not yet know), the tendril will tell you something true. You will
not yet have a way to know the lost scholar's name. The tendril is
patient.

**This door is open from the start. It will teach you what the Choir
is, on the Choir's schedule — not yours.**

---

## What the village asks of you

Beren has framed the quest as *find the missing pages*. He has not
told you the quest is, in fact, four cascading quests — and that the
fourth is the main story of the world. Here is what he will not yet
tell you, but is true:

- **Page #1 (Drowned Library)** is recoverable in three days. It will
  teach you the Palimpsest exists and that Reading is real.
- **Page #2 (Hyphal Surveys Office)** is recoverable in nine days. It
  will introduce you to the Curation's politics and to A.
- **Page #3 (Salt-Hinge debt-record)** is recoverable in five days
  with cooperation from the Concord. It will introduce you to the
  Concord's metaphysics.
- **Page #4 (the lost scholar's discovery)** is the *thing she walked
  into the marsh and did not return for*. The lost scholar is
  alive — barely — in a Sealhand-held ruin between the Burnt Houses
  and the high road. Recovering page #4 means rescuing her, and
  rescuing her means *deciding which side you're on*.

When you have all four pages, Beren will bind them into the village
book. The village will be made whole. And then — if you have not
already chosen — the world will ask you for its choice.

---

## A note for the player who will not play heroically

There is also a fifth way through the opening, which Beren will not
mention because he does not approve.

You can decline the quest. You can stay in Reedhaven, work for Lin in
the inn, learn to read marsh-rye, marry a villager, raise children
through the next forty years. The Sealhand will pass through, twice,
and you will hide in the marshes. The lost scholar will not be found.
The village's book will not be made whole. You will live and die a
small useful life, the way most people in the world do.

The world will continue without you.

If this is the playthrough you want, Beren will not be angry with you.
He will be a little disappointed. He will continue to wait. When you
die — sometime in your sixties, in a winter that comes early — Lin
will lay you out on the bench in front of his cottage, and Beren will
sit beside you for a day, and then he will go back to waiting.

The world is large. It does not require you. It will, however,
remember whether you came.
