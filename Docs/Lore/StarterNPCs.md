# Starter NPCs

> Eight ground-level NPCs: seven faction-faces plus the village
> quest-giver who introduces the world. The people a player would
> meet *first* — not the elders, not the senior figures, not the
> doctrine-makers. The shopkeepers, apprentices, foundlings, road-
> walkers, and one retired surveyor waiting on a bench.
>
> **Status: Tier 1 introductions, not bible canon.** These NPCs are
> *introduced by the surface*, not promoted from the audits. They
> extrapolate from existing material but are not load-bearing on the
> deeper corpus. Future audits or content commits may refine, replace,
> or canonise any of them.

---

## Old Beren — The Lay Archivist (Reedhaven)

**Faction.** None (retired Pale Curation; unaffiliated now).
**First met.** Sitting on a low stone bench in front of his cottage on
Reedhaven's south bank, watching the herons in the channel.

Beren is in his late seventies — slight, dignified, with the careful
hands of someone who did thirty years of precise work and now does small
precise work. He was a Curation surveyor. He came home to
**Reedhaven**, a marshy waterway village three days east of the
Salt-Hinge, expecting to die quietly. He has not died. He is mildly
annoyed about this.

He keeps the village's only book — a hand-stitched volume of recipes,
prayers, marsh-songs, and contracts going back a century — in his
cottage's back room. The book is **incomplete**. Pages are missing.
He has been waiting for someone to arrive who could go and find them.
That is the player.

He does not explain the whole world. He explains the problem in front
of him: *four pages are missing. I know where three of them are. I am
too old to walk the roads. You are not.* He gives the player a list,
a letter of introduction to a Curation junior named A., and the first
sweet-mark of his pension — formally placed in the palm, with a small
bow.

> *"Ah. You have come. Sit, traveller — the bench is dry today."*

He does not ask who the player is. He was waiting for whoever came.
His preferred greeting about the weather is accurate. He teaches the
player to read marsh-rye stalks for water content — a kind of Echo
Reading made plain. He tells you the missing pages matter *the way
the village's hands matter to the village's body.*

He is the Joppa equivalent. The Mehmet. The door into the world.

**Status: introduced by v2 redesign (not yet load-bearing on the v1
bible).** See [`v2/Opening.md`](../v2/Opening.md) for the full quest
structure.

## A. — Junior Cataloguer (Pale Curation)

**Faction.** Pale Curation.
**First met.** At a chapter-house intake desk, after-hours, when she
should have gone home but hasn't.

A. is the junior cataloguer who wrote the margin-note on the doll-in-
the-wall — the one asking why the Choir preserved that one when it
consumed everything else. She is the same A. who keeps writing the
unauthorised sub-annotations no one signs off on. Her full name is not
on file; she signs her initial.

She is ~22, tireless, soft-voiced, and quietly furious at the
institution she serves. She works late because she does not trust the
day surveyors to write things down truthfully. She has good
handwriting and a small horde of pencils. She likes you immediately if
you bring her something interesting. She does not like Director Ven.

> *"I am writing this down. Yes, the Director told me not to. I am
> writing it anyway, because somebody in this office has to be
> writing things down truthfully or the archive is just shelves."*

What she offers: cataloguing services for any artifact you bring her,
plus an unofficial walnut-ink note saying what she actually thinks it
means. The unofficial note is the more useful document.

## Brem the Apprentice (Saccharine Concord)

**Faction.** Saccharine Concord (Salt-Hinge market).
**First met.** Sitting in the **Reckoner's chair** at a busy market
stall, listening to a trade he is not party to.

Brem is ~19, in his third year of apprenticeship. The Reckoner's chair
is the apprentice's training role — sit, listen, tally the *priced
clause tails* used by both parties to a real trade, then read your
tally aloud at the close. If you're within a quarter-credit of the
actual price, you've *heard the trade*. Brem has heard 11 trades. He
needs ~50 to advance.

He is bright, fast, and entirely unaware that the Concord he is
training to serve is metaphysically running out of time. (Senior
Assayer Veshen knows. The juniors are not told.)

> *"That trade was — eight and three-quarter sweet-marks, plus an
> unbilled greeting? They didn't say it but it was there. Was that a
> bow? Did you see the bow? It was a bow."*

What he offers: market gossip, an honest assayer's eye on any coin you
show him, and conversation in the priced-clause register. He will
charge you a quarter-credit for the conversation and forget to
collect it.

## The Bowl-Maker (Brine Communion, inland)

**Faction.** Brine Communion (a dry-walking bowl-maker on the road).
**First met.** Walking a road with a sack on her back, picking up
abandoned cups, mugs, hollow stones, gourds.

The bowl-makers are an inland Brine sub-population whose only trade is
the *windowsill bowl* — vessels gathered from the road and given to
dry-walking Brine in exchange for a single sentence in the
**remembered tense** (a sentence about water absent from the room).

This particular bowl-maker is older than she looks — perhaps 35, with
wind-burnt hands and a small smile that comes slowly. She does not
introduce herself. She is collecting both bowls and sentences. She has
collected too many sentences and is beginning to *un-bowl* — release
them back to the air in the order received.

> *"The river is highen at my home tonight. The lead is heaviest at
> the third water. The bread was scarcen today."*

(These are the three sentences she released this morning. They are
not hers.)

What she offers: a bowl, found, unfired, that she will place in your
pack when you are looking elsewhere. She does not tell you which bowl
was meant for you. The Concord cannot price what she does. The
Curation files her under *intentional un-cataloguing.*

## A Smoke-Born Apprentice (Thermoclaves)

**Faction.** Thermoclaves (eleventh-fire cohort).
**First met.** At her cohort's day hearth, in the last hour before
her shift ends. She is sitting cross-legged on the rock between fires.

She is sixteen — old enough to keep but not yet to bank — and
**smoke-born**, meaning she belongs to the bridge-generation born in
the first twenty years after the breaking. The senior keepers handle
her gently. She handles them more gently than they realise.

She keeps a hearth-log in pencil margins. The log records what the
senior keepers will *not* write down: gap-burns, the private griefs of
keepers who have lost too many fires, conversations between her
hearth-keeper and tendrils that pass through the soil-side. She knows
her log is unauthorised. She knows it is also the only honest record
the cohort has.

She has not been given her station-name yet (that comes at first-
quench). Her hearth-name — the family-only one, predating the chain —
is **Renne**.

> *"Yesterday a Choir tendril came to the soil-side and asked
> Tossin if our kept ember and the Brine's held depth were the
> same thing sampled differently. She said yes, immediately.
> She had been waiting to be asked."*

What she offers: the contents of her hearth-log, if you earn her
trust. She is the most accessible voice the Thermoclaves have. Most
travellers don't realise it.

## An Unfired Child (Glassblown Remnant)

**Faction.** Glassblown Remnant.
**First met.** In the doorway of a workshop, sitting in the morning
sun, with a small ball of paste in her hands.

She has not yet been given her shape-name. (Glassblown children are
named for the kind of vessel they are first asked to attempt; until
that day, they are *the unfired*.) She is perhaps eight. She speaks
when she chooses, and chooses rarely.

If you ask the unfired a question, she will form a shape between her
palms while you talk. She will hold it for the length of your
question, then fold it back into the batch beside her. The shape rises
and dissolves. The forming is the answer. Most travellers don't
realise this.

She is a child but not a fool. She has watched her grandmother fire
sixty grief-markers. She knows what every blank field in a marker
means. She will not tell you. The not-telling is the content.

> *"…"*
>
> (She has just answered your question. It was a very good answer. You
> didn't catch it. She does not mind.)

What she offers: silence as a positive datum. If you are willing to
sit with her in the morning sun and not press, she will eventually
form a small fired shape — a single finger-ring, a small lens, a salt-
spoon — and put it in your hand. She picked it for you. You will not
know what it means until later.

## A New Tendril (Rot Choir)

**Faction.** Rot Choir (a young surface fruiting body, not yet a slow
voice).
**First met.** On a footpath that crosses Choir ground, where the soil
visibly shifts beneath your boots.

The tendril is roughly humanoid in shape — bipedal, bark-skinned,
glowing faintly along its filaments. It is recent — months old, not
seasons. Its voice is not yet stable. It speaks in *we*, like all the
Choir, but the *we* is thinner here than at older sites.

It will offer to *taste* you on the air, which is the Choir's
canonical greeting. It will report what your blood tells the soil
about you (mostly true things; occasionally things you didn't know).
It will not refuse a coin you offer in toll. Whether it eats the coin
or sets the coin into the wall depends, by Choir doctrine, on whether
the coin *minds being eaten*. The tendril will not tell you which it
did.

> *"We taste you on the air, walker. You are warm. You are alone but
> not lonely. You have been carrying something that is not yours. The
> soil tells us this. We are not asking. We are reporting what we
> have read."*

What it offers: passage across Choir ground without molestation, in
exchange for any small offering. Information about the soil's recent
weather. A vague, unsettling friendliness that turns out to be sincere
within the limits of the Choir's grammar.

## A First-Time Echo-Reader (Palimpsest)

**Faction.** Palimpsest (a young walker who has just discovered the
Glimpse).
**First met.** Sitting on a stone, eyes closed, both hands flat on
the stone's surface, listening.

She is perhaps 24 and was not, until this morning, anything special.
Then she touched a battered field-stone in a place where two roads
crossed and the world *layered* under her hands — the present field,
the older battlefield, the older-still grain field that came before
the war. She has been on the stone for six hours. Her water flask is
empty.

She is going to be a Palimpsest reader. She does not yet know what
that costs.

When she opens her eyes she will look at you a moment too long, and
not focus quite the same way she did this morning. She will tell you
what the stone said — accurately, in present tense, in someone else's
voice. The voice is not hers. The voice is not anyone she has ever met.
The voice is the field, twice, before what's there now.

> *"There was a battle here. It was a small battle. The third son of
> a farmer fell in the second hour. I am not a farmer. I have never
> had three sons. Why do I know his name."*

What she offers: a guide, briefly, into a layered place — if you can
keep her from wandering off. (Re-pressed readers wander.) She does not
know yet that she is becoming Re-pressed. The doubled tense will start
appearing in her speech in three to four months. By then she will have
forgotten this conversation, but not the field-stone.
