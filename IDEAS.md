# Ideas

- Living-mechanical modular weapons assembled from organs and parts harvested from enemies and the environment. Example: a sword with a fungal-colony hilt that adds poison procs, a blade forged from a robot spine that grants armor penetration, and a pommel that is a still-living eye which gives the weapon limited awareness so it occasionally auto-parries.

- Contagion Diplomacy — Diseases as Faction Tools. Diseases aren't just status effects to cure; they're complex, evolving systems that factions weaponize and trade. A fungal plague might grant its host communion with a mushroom-civilization in exchange for slowly converting their body. A nano-virus might upgrade your cybernetics but report your location to the machine-faction. Players can become disease vectors strategically: deliberately infecting an enemy settlement, developing immunity to a plague and using it as a biological border, or cultivating a "benevolent" disease that buffs allies. A "plague doctor" character class focuses entirely on disease manipulation — curing, creating, evolving, and weaponizing pathogens.

- **Blocs** — coalitions that span a layer above the existing factions and houses: national, interbiome, interhouse, or interfaction alliances. A Bloc is a body of factions/houses that have made a binding agreement around a shared interest (a trade lane, a defense pact, a shared cosmology, a contested resource, a heretical position, etc.). Blocs are *first-class entities* in the world, not just a tag on a faction — they have their own diplomatic posture, their own conversation surface (a "bloc envoy" NPC), their own treaty-state, and their own way of breaking. Member factions retain their own identities and can leak from a bloc; a faction can be in multiple blocs at once with conflicting obligations. Player reputation tracks against blocs as well as factions, so high standing with one bloc can shift relationships with every member faction at once. Bloc-level events (a treaty signing, a schism, a bloc-wide war declaration) cascade into faction-level state and storylets.

- **Sects** — small splinter groups that have broken away from a house or a bloc, carrying a piece of the parent's identity but refusing one of its terms. A sect is defined by *what it refused* — a doctrine the parent house enforced, a treaty the bloc signed, a hierarchy the parent demanded, a rite the parent dropped. Sects are smaller than houses (a handful to a few dozen members), often hidden, often itinerant, and frequently in active conflict with their parent. Mechanically: each sect carries a `ParentID` pointing at its origin (House or Bloc) and a `RefusalReason` string that gates its dialogue and storylets — a sect that refused a bloc's blood-oath will recruit differently than one that refused a house's primogeniture rule. Sects are good seeds for player-aligned mini-factions: small enough to influence, ideologically distinct enough to matter, often holding obscure knowledge or relics the parent suppressed. A sect can grow into a new house if it accumulates enough power, or be extinguished and absorbed back. Sect↔Parent dynamics generate natural quest scaffolding: assassination contracts, doctrinal infiltration, reconciliation brokerage, relic recovery.

- **Memory Consumption as a Social Verb.** The Rot Choir already says it eats civilizations into understanding ("we eat everything — flesh, stone, metal, time. And what we eat, we know"). Make this a player tool: pay or coerce the Choir to literally eat an NPC's memory — of you, of a crime, of a face, of a relationship. Distinct from charm or persuade because it is *surgical and permanent.* Witnesses become a managed resource the player can deliberately curate. Mechanically: a targeted action that removes specific facts from a target NPC's `KnowledgePart` (and possibly from the global event log if the witness was the only source). Gating is critical — this must be **hard to obtain and expensive to use**, because cheap memory-edits would trivialize crime, faction politics, and storylet consequences. Suggested gating layers (combine 2+):
  - **Skill path:** unlocked only via a niche **Choir-aligned tradition** (e.g., "Spore-Threaded" or "Composted Mind") — a path the player commits to early and that locks them out of certain other paths (Palimpsest scribehood, Pale Curation purity, etc.). Faction reputation with the Choir at minimum *Trusted* before the path opens.
  - **Per-use cost:** each consumption demands a real cost paid in one or more of:
    *(a)* a sizable Choir reputation expenditure (the Choir does not like spending itself trivially);
    *(b)* a chunk of the player's own memory — pay in a fact you possess, which the Choir also eats (consumed knowledge from the player's `KnowledgePart`, possibly random-from-recent if you don't pick);
    *(c)* a physical sacrifice (a tonic, a relic, a year of life, a piece of equipment fed to the spores);
    *(d)* in-world time (the consumption is not instant — you must wait a number of turns at a Choir node while the spores work);
    *(e)* **a rare item that is arduous to obtain — the primary cost.** The other costs (a–d) are modifiers; this is the gate. Each consumption burns one such item, consumed irreversibly by the Choir during the rite. The item itself should be Choir-thematic and require a real adventure-loop to acquire — not buyable, not crafted from common parts, not farmed from trash mobs. Candidates:
      - **First Root Spore** — harvested only from the deepest cave where the First Root sleeps (already canonical: "where the walls breathe and the stone is warm"). Each visit yields at most one; the First Root must be approached without violence; harvesting is itself a small ritual sequence.
      - **Memory-Pearl** — calcifies in the body of someone who died holding a single overwhelming memory (a heretic burned with their secret, a parent killed mid-recognition of their child). Requires finding the right corpse — usually a Tier-3 storylet outcome — and recovering the pearl before the Choir or the Palimpsest claims it.
      - **Uneaten Relic** — an old-world artifact the Choir has not yet digested. The Choir will trade memory-consumptions for these because handing one over completes its hunger; only a few exist in the world per save and replacements are not procedurally regenerated. The player has to choose between keeping the relic for its own utility or burning it for a memory-eat.
      - **Dream-Fruit** — grows on a particular fungal tree once per Choir-season (a long in-game interval). Multiple players competing for the same tree creates faction friction.
      Tiered consumptions can stack item costs: cheap tier wants one Spore, mid tier wants a Pearl, apex tier wants a Relic *and* a Spore.
  - **Scope limits:** scales with cost. Cheap tier eats one fact from one NPC. Expensive tier eats a face from every member of a small faction. Apex tier eats an entire event from the world's record (extremely costly — e.g., demands the player spend a Tier-3 KnowledgePart fact of their own and lose 30 Choir reputation).
  - **Footprint:** every use leaves a trace the Choir's enemies (Palimpsest, Pale Curation) can detect with effort. The Palimpsest in particular treats memory-eating as desecration; repeated use ratchets their hostility regardless of player rep. Creates a long-term political cost on top of the per-use cost.
  - **Failure modes worth designing in:** the Choir occasionally eats more than asked (a related fact, a wrong NPC, the player's own memory of *why they wanted it eaten*). Risk scales inversely with cost paid — bargain rates buy bargain precision.

  Generates several distinct play patterns: the assassin who pays the Choir to consume the witness chain after every job; the diplomat who edits a faction leader's memory of a slight; the heretic who has the Choir eat the player's own memory of a crime they regret; the politician who farms expensive consumptions to shape what entire settlements remember about a rival.

- **Blackmail** — derived skill that synthesizes Witness, Lineage, Houses, and Dialogue Beats into weaponized secrets. Built around "every secret you hold is a loaded gun — firing it once discharges it." Five grades of secrets (Minor → World-shaking) with grade-capped leverage; four ranks of blackmail skill (Pressure → Architectonics, the last enabling political-engineering rather than resource-extraction); nine-layer anti-cheese framework (single-use secrets, counter-blackmail risk, rumor contamination, half-life decay, scaled retaliation, "they already knew" failure case, social cost from non-targets, blackmailed Houses can never become genuine allies). Unique value prop: **compliance without displacement** — the only mechanic that makes a House do what you want without anyone seeing it change. Full design in [Docs/Design/BLACKMAIL_SYSTEM.md](Docs/Design/BLACKMAIL_SYSTEM.md). Candidate cultural names: *The Held Breath*, *Inkwork*, *The Whispered Coin*. Ritual phrase at the moment of threat: *"I know the shape of your hollow."*

- **BlackmailV2 — *The Held Breath*.** Successor to the V1 blackmail design above, addressing structural problems found in critical review (V1 unreachable top-tier scope, Rank 1 dominated by Rank 2, refusal unmodeled, anachronistic framing, brittle counter-leverage assumptions). V2 is the **v1.0 implementation track**; V1 is preserved as the **long-horizon design destination**. Implementation order: ship V2 first, playtest, then grow into V1.

  > **Genre context (clarified after the original V2 draft):** Caves of Ooo is an **RPG**, not a roguelike. This changes the calibration: the audience-fit, bookkeeping-load, slow-burn-friction, and permadeath-balance concerns that drove V2's most aggressive cuts (dropping Lineage-shaking / World-shaking secrets, dropping Ranks 3–4) **don't apply** to RPG players. They want long-form social systems; they expect to manage faction state across sessions; they invest in single characters across 60+ hours. V1's full ambition is therefore *appropriate for the genre* — V2's cuts should be read as **scope phasing for v1.0**, not as design erasure. The amendments below restore those tiers as long-horizon design while keeping V2's structural fixes intact.

  - **Reframed.** Drop the word "blackmail" entirely. The skill is **The Held Breath** — a Choir-aligned mystic discipline of *shape-knowing*: holding the unspoken shape of someone's hidden self and choosing, breath by breath, whether to release it. Same mechanics, world-native vocabulary. The threat-phrase stays: *"I know the shape of your hollow."*

  - **Five grades — three shippable in v1.0, two long-horizon.** The full V1 taxonomy is preserved as design intent:
    - **Minor** (personal embarrassments — small favor) — v1.0
    - **Significant** (hidden bastards, expired crimes — meaningful concessions) — v1.0
    - **Grave** (active crimes, broken faction oaths — House-scale concessions) — v1.0
    - **Lineage-shaking** (House founded on a crime, fake relics, bastard heads) — long-horizon, gated on having ≥4 Houses with multi-generation backstory
    - **World-shaking** (founding-myth lies, coordinated faction conspiracies) — long-horizon, gated on main-quest density that doesn't yet exist
    The "compliance without displacement" thesis holds cleanly at the v1.0 tiers. At Lineage-shaking and World-shaking it shifts to "compliance with displacement of the player" (target may preempt with deadly force) — which in an RPG is *intended late-game content*, not a design failure.

  - **Four ranks — two shippable in v1.0, two long-horizon.**
    - **Rank 1 — Pressure (v1.0).** One-shot extraction. Cash a secret for one big concession; secret discharges. No ongoing maintenance; no upkeep cost. Best for tactical, single-purpose plays.
    - **Rank 2 — Hold (v1.0).** *Sustained* threat — target provides ongoing small services as long as the secret stays unspoken. Each Hold occupies one of a small fixed number of "Held Breath" slots (skill-rank-gated, e.g. 2 at Rank 2). Each Hold requires periodic in-game maintenance — the target tries to escape, find counter-leverage, or pre-empt; let too many turns pass without re-asserting the threat and the Hold breaks (target now knows your method). Rank 1 and Rank 2 are *different verbs with different costs*, not strict-dominance progression.
    - **Rank 3 — Extortion (long-horizon).** Large demands against Grave/Lineage-shaking secrets without immediate retaliation. Adds *graduated pressure* (ratchet demand up or down based on target compliance) and *secret trading* (use one House's secret to acquire another's from a third party).
    - **Rank 4 — Architectonics (long-horizon).** Engineer leverage networks across multiple Houses to orchestrate political outcomes. Stops being resource extraction; becomes **political engineering** — reshaping the political topology of the world. Gated on ≥6 Houses being in play, so this is genuinely an endgame tier and a content-density forcing function.

  - **Credibility, not unconditional threat.** New player stat: `Credibility` (in-world: *Breath-Weight*). Targets evaluate threats against your Credibility, not just against the secret. Credibility is built by *following through on revelations* (publishing a discharged secret) and by being witnessed making good on past threats. It decays with non-use. **A player who never publishes loses the ability to threaten** — targets simply call the bluff. In an RPG, this is exactly the kind of weighted moral choice the genre is built on: *will I burn this person to keep my future threats credible?*

  - **Targets have three responses, not two.** When threatened, the target evaluates:
    - **Comply** (pay or accept retainer).
    - **Refuse and dare revelation** (eat the rep hit, deny you future leverage).
    - **Preempt** — kill, exile, expose-first, or hire violence against the player. Preempt probability scales with the target's resources and the secret's severity. *Grave* and above are dangerous to wield: high-resource targets may decide killing you is cheaper than paying. Open design question: how visible should preempt-probability be to the player before the threat is laid? (RPG players are less tolerant of single-character-loss surprises than roguelike players are of run-loss surprises.)

  - **House-collective response.** When a Held Breath is laid against a House member, the *House* decides collectively (routed through existing House Drama internal politics: DiminishedHead, RisingInheritor, NamedAntagonist, SilencedHelper roles each have a stake and a vote-equivalent). Three House-level outcomes: comply, refuse, or House-preempt. Generates intra-House content: some members may want to pay, others to kill, others to expose their own kin pre-emptively. The threat becomes a *trigger* for House drama rather than a private transaction. **Implementation note:** the House-Drama "voting" mechanism doesn't exist yet — it's net new system work. House Drama is also currently flagged for migration into the storylet/quest layer; V2's Rank-2 implementation depends on that migration landing first.

  - **Counter-leverage is generated, not assumed.** V1 assumed the player had secrets to be counter-blackmailed with. V2 generates them: every Held Breath leaves a *Mycelial Signature* — a trace in the spore network that the target's investigation can find. The signature itself is the counter-leverage. The act of using The Held Breath always exposes you to it. Surgical players who use Choir memory-eating to clean their trail can dampen but not eliminate signatures — memory-eating leaves its own Palimpsest-detectable trace, so the two systems mutually constrain each other. **Caveat:** this couples V2's anti-cheese to the specific Choir/Palimpsest cosmology; if that lore is ever revised (a Bloc absorbs the Palimpsest, etc.), the counter-leverage system needs a parallel update.

  - **"Already known" surfaces during investigation.** When the player gathers a secret, the investigation explicitly returns its *current public-ness*: known-only-to-you, known-to-the-inner-circle, openly-acknowledged-but-unspoken, etc. The player knows before they spend leverage whether the "secret" is actually load-bearing. Removes the V1 blind-side failure case where you waste hours of investigation on a non-secret.

  - **Decay is observable.** Each Held secret has a visible *Freshness* indicator. Players can see leverage fading and decide between using-now and the risk of decay. Half-life mechanics still apply but aren't a hidden timer. *Open flavor question:* the indicator can be diegetic (taste of the spore-thread, the target's growing comfort, NPC body language) rather than a UI percentage — preferred for the mystic framing.

  - **Refusal has a visible cost to the target.** When a target refuses, they take the rep hit *immediately* (you publish — system enforces, no save-scum walk-back). This makes the threat credible by construction: refusing always costs the target *something*. Targets only refuse when the cost of compliance exceeds the cost of revelation.

  - **Faction lock-in is acceptable.** The Held Breath is Choir-aligned; Palimpsest-aligned characters cannot use it. In an RPG this is normal class-design: faction choice gates skill access. Open design question: does the Palimpsest get a dual skill (*Inkwork* — archive-binding, or similar) so political play isn't faction-locked to one side? Probably yes, but that's a separate design entry.

  - **What V2 keeps from V1:** loaded-gun framing, single-use-per-target rule, the mutual-destruction principle (now via Mycelial Signature instead of assumed prior crimes), the "compliance without displacement" thesis (true at v1.0 tiers; *intentionally violated* at long-horizon tiers as endgame escalation), social cost from non-targets, blackmail-vs-diplomacy mutual exclusion.

  - **What V2 changes from V1:** Rank-1-vs-Rank-2 dominance fixed by making them different verbs with different upkeep costs (one-shot vs. slot-limited Hold). Refusal-as-free-win fixed by Credibility. Counter-leverage assumption fixed by Mycelial Signature generation. Per-NPC grade-cap authoring nightmare replaced with a single per-target compliance formula weighted by resources, attitude, and Credibility. Anachronistic "blackmail" framing replaced with mystic *shape-knowing*.

  - **What V2 explicitly defers (not cuts) for v1.0:** Ranks 3–4 (Extortion, Architectonics), Lineage-shaking and World-shaking secret tiers. These are the right design destination; they just need content density (≥6 Houses, multi-generation lineage backstory, main-quest infrastructure) that doesn't yet exist. Build the v1.0 tier first, ship it, validate the shape, then grow into V1's full ambition.

  - **Implementation phasing.** Smallest playable slice: **Rank 1 + Minor/Significant secrets only** — drop Hold-slots, drop House-collective response, drop Grave tier. Two-grade taxonomy, one verb (one-shot extraction), three-way target response, Credibility tracking. Estimated 2–3 milestones on top of the existing storylet system. Playtest. If the shape works, add Rank 2 + Grave + House-collective response in a second pass (~2 more milestones). Lineage-shaking, World-shaking, and Ranks 3–4 are deferred to the long-horizon roadmap, gated on content density rather than schedule. Whole system is buildable in the same authoring frame as House Drama — no new architectural layers required for v1.0.

- **Prototype rentals — you're the field test.** Inversion of the standard rental loop in [`WEAPON_RENTAL_AND_INK_SYSTEM.md`](WEAPON_RENTAL_AND_INK_SYSTEM.md): instead of the player paying a Quartermaster Ink for a *reliable* loaner, a research-aligned faction (candidate hosts: the Saccharine Concord's "merchants of the possible," a Tinker splinter sect, or a new bloc dedicated to material science) hands the player a **deliberately-buggy prototype**. The faction *pays the player* — not in Ink, but in **progress unlocks**: tinker recipes, faction-reputation tiers, blueprint variants, eventually the production version of the prototype. The player isn't a customer; the player is the test pilot.

  The mechanical core is **combat telemetry**. Each prototype carries an instrumented part (could ride alongside or extend `RentalPart`) that records hits landed, kills, misfires triggered, edge-case procs, self-damage, environmental interactions, target factions/materials encountered. Returning the prototype trades the dataset for tech. **Returning a prototype with no recorded combat is worth nothing** — the player has to actually use it to extract value. Returning early with rich data on a single quirk is worth more than returning late with thin data across many fights.

  The "buggy on purpose" half is what makes the loop dramatic. Each prototype rolls 1–3 quirks from a table:
  - **Misfire** — N% per swing the weapon hits the wielder instead of the target.
  - **Elemental swap** — a fraction of hits applies fire/frost/electric/acid instead of the listed damage type, sometimes hostilely interacting with the wielder's armor.
  - **Critical malfunction** — landing a critical hit triggers a self-damage burst, briefly stuns the wielder, or shatters one armor piece.
  - **Reactive edge** — the weapon procs differently against specific factions or materials, and the player has to discover which through use.
  - **Stat drift** — base damage wanders mid-encounter (1d4 instead of 1d6 once per fight) without warning.
  - **Sympathetic resonance** — using the prototype near a friendly NPC has a small chance to apply its quirk to *them*.

  Each quirk is a thing the faction wants data on. Triggering it during combat raises the data value of the return. **The player's incentive is to use the buggy weapon in conditions that surface its bugs** — the inverse of how players normally engage with broken gear.

  Progression unlocks tier:
  1. Recipes for crafting a *stabilized* version of the prototype line at a Tinker.
  2. Discounted access to the next tier of prototype, so testing compounds.
  3. Faction reputation gating further mechanic types (memory-eating, blackmail, bloc-level diplomacy) — the research faction's standing pays out in social access, not just gear.
  4. **Eventually, the production version** as a permanent unlock. The weapon you stabilized through testing becomes craftable. There's a long arc here: a player's first prototype becomes their last build.

  Open design questions worth marking before building:
  - **Quirk visibility.** Disclosed upfront in dialogue, or discovered during combat? Disclosed is fairer; discovered is dramatic. Probably tier it: early prototypes are blind, later ones come with a partial datasheet as the faction grows to trust the player.
  - **Lethal misfire risk.** RPG audiences tolerate self-damage but not random one-shot deaths. Either cap misfire damage as a fraction of max HP, or guarantee misfires are never lethal on a swing that would otherwise kill the target (so the player can still rely on the killing blow landing).
  - **Anti-farming.** If data-value scales with edge-case triggers, players will set up artificial encounters to farm them. Throttle by per-encounter caps, or weight the dataset by *novelty* — a misfire in the same biome against the same faction twice is worth less than the first instance.
  - **Faction tension.** Competing research factions could want the data exclusively. Returning a prototype to faction A but selling a copy of the dataset to faction B becomes a real political move (and a way the system folds into Bloc politics).
  - **Failure return.** If the player loses or shatters the prototype mid-fight, the faction *still wants the partial data* — no "you wasted the run" cliff. Smaller unlock, plus an opportunity to research what *broke* the weapon, which itself is data.
  - **Quirk regression.** Does each generation of prototype get *less* buggy as data accumulates, or do new quirks appear? Probably both: stabilized versions enter the recipe pool while the bleeding edge keeps pushing into new failure modes. Gives the faction a permanent reason to keep recruiting test pilots.

  Pairs cleanly with the existing rental system: the same Quartermaster spawn slot in a village could be replaced by a **Prototyper** NPC for science-aligned settlements, sharing infrastructure (rental-part base, dialogue routing, anti-sale veto) while inverting the currency direction and the payoff. A village can have either a Quartermaster *or* a Prototyper, not both — choosing which faction got there first becomes a worldgen-level signal about that village's politics.

- **Gin Frogs — armor by reproduction.** Mid-tier amphibian enemy whose entire defensive identity is the cluster of neonatal froglets clinging to its back. Biology inspiration: ***Stefania ginesi***, a South American back-brooding frog whose females carry eggs and tiny direct-developing froglets fused to their dorsal skin — the player-facing name "Gin Frogs" is a contraction of the species epithet. The parent itself is squishy: low base HP, low natural AV, slow speed. **The babies are the armor.** Each clinging froglet's hardened skin contributes a flat +1 AV, and a fully-loaded Gin Frog wears a brood of six to twelve. Hits that don't penetrate strip a baby instead of damaging the parent — the dorsal shield is *consumable*, not rolled. Combat has a stripping phase before the kill phase, and the encounter feels qualitatively different from fighting an armored beast: you're not denting a shell, you're knocking children off.

  Mechanical sketch:
  - New `BroodArmorPart` on the parent: holds `BabyCount` (int), contributes `BabyCount * AvPerBaby` (AvPerBaby ≈ 1) into `CombatSystem.GetAV` via the existing armor-aggregation path. Same hook `ArmorPart` uses, sourced from a counter rather than equipped slots.
  - On any incoming hit where `AmountAfterAV <= 0`, decrement `BabyCount` by 1 and emit a `babyShed` event. The shed animation is its own beat: a `g` character pops off the parent's tile, lands on an adjacent passable cell, and either (a) becomes a 1-HP hostile `BabyGinFrog` entity that bites once and dies, (b) flops and dies as flavor loot, or (c) skitters to the nearest water cell and despawns. Mix the three by RNG so the encounter reads as biological rather than mechanical.
  - Damage-type modifiers — fire, acid, and electric strip babies faster (each lethal hit strips 2 instead of 1). Bludgeoning is normal. Slashing has a small chance to skip the brood entirely and graze the parent: a clean stroke between the children.

  Open design questions:
  - **Reproduction in combat / between fights.** Does the parent regrow its brood at water cells, between encounters, or never? Persistent depletion is more interesting (the player tracks Gin Frog populations across zones, eventually thins them out); rest-restoration is cheaper to author. Probably tier it: wild Gin Frogs regenerate slowly at water cells; named/elite ones don't.
  - **XP/loot from shed babies.** If `BabyGinFrog` carries XP, a single parent yields 8+ weak kills and trivializes the rest of the zone. Probably XP-zero and loot-zero so the brood reads as *atmospheric resistance*, not a farming target. The kill credit is on the parent.
  - **Faction/biome placement.** Wet cave biomes are the obvious home. Could be wild fauna in cave waters, or a RotChoir-tended species (spores grow on the babies, babies grow on the parent — a vertical fungal symbiosis the Choir would find theologically satisfying). Settlement variant: a swamp village that *farms* Gin Frog broods for the armor-grade dorsal skin.
  - **Player mirror.** Does the player ever wield this? A cursed pauldron that gives +AV at the cost of the "babies" being hostile to nearby allies; or a one-shot consumable that summons clinging froglets for N turns (mobile body armor that bites back if struck). Probably no — the imagery is strongest when *encountered*, not worn.
  - **Genus expansion.** The base name comes from *Stefania ginesi*, but the genus *Stefania* contains roughly twenty described species — plenty of room for variant Gin Frogs (a desert variant, a deep-cave variant, a Choir-tended cultivar) keyed off real species epithets without breaking the naming convention. Pinning the etymology to a real genus also gives the bestiary a small, repeatable lore hook: each Gin Frog variant cites its biological cousin.

  Visual: ASCII rendering pairs naturally with this — the parent's tile shows the frog glyph (`F` or similar), with a small dorsal overlay of `g`s that thins each turn as the player chips them off. The encounter is *legible* at a glance: how dangerous this Gin Frog is right now equals how many `g`s are still on its back.

- **The Root of the World — candidate final-boss site, tepui-inspired.** A possible terminus for the main quest. Single most ambitious geographic-and-mythic piece in the world: a vast flat-topped mountain at the world's center, rising out of the surrounding biomes like a continent of stone, that is **the petrified stump of a primordial tree the world was once grown from.** Real-world inspiration: the **tepuis** of the Guiana Highlands (Mount Roraima, Auyán-tepui, et al.) — billion-year-old sandstone table mountains hosting endemic species (including *Stefania ginesi*, the Gin Frogs above), and the cosmology of the **Pemon people** who live around them. In Pemon mythology Roraima is the stump of *Wazaka*, the Tree of Life that bore every fruit; the trickster *Makunaima* felled it, and from the felling came the rivers, the species, and the flood that reshaped the world. The current mountain is what remains.

  This folds together with already-canonical CoO lore: the **First Root** (referenced in the Memory-Eating entry above as a thing that *sleeps* somewhere "where the walls breathe and the stone is warm"). The Root of the World is where the First Root sleeps — and *the tepui itself is the petrified above-ground portion of the same root.* Two routes converge on the same boss site: **ascend** the surface biomes of the tepui (lower slopes of jungle → mid-slopes of fungal forest → summit of the petrified canopy), or **descend** through caves beneath it to the still-living taproot. The boss arena sits at the meeting point.

  Why this is the right shape for a CoO final boss site:
  - **Geographic identity matches existing tone.** A single colossal landmark visible from much of the world map — the player has been able to see it from the start, it's been *implied* through the whole game, and the final approach is the literal climb. Mirrors how Qud uses Golgotha and how CoO already uses cosmologically-significant landmarks (the Choir node, the Palimpsest archive) as fixed points.
  - **Every existing faction has a stake.** Each reads the Root differently and competes for what happens at the summit:
    - **Rot Choir** — the Root is *food*, the original substrate; consuming it would let the Choir know everything the world has ever been. Hostile-acquisitive.
    - **Palimpsest** — the Root is *the original document*; cataloguing its rings is the highest scribal act. Preservationist; opposed to the Choir.
    - **Pale Curation** — the Root is *unfiled*; it must be sealed, indexed, and never disturbed. Quarantine-aligned.
    - **Saccharine Concord** — the Root is *inventory*; harvestable for Tier-4 commerce. Mercantile-extractive.
    - **Inkbound** (if introduced) — the Root is *the first letter*; reading it is the foundational act of literacy.

    Each faction's quest line points the player at a different relationship with the Root, and the final-act choice is which reading wins. Pairs cleanly with the existing `KnowsThreeEndings` player property — the Root is where those endings *resolve.*
  - **Mechanical biome scaffold.** The ascent is the perfect place to seed every Tier-3+ enemy archetype as elevation-keyed encounters — including **Gin Frogs as wild fauna of the lower slopes** (they live on real tepuis; they live on this one), Choir tendrils through the fungal mid-slopes, glass-blown drifters at the windswept summit. The bestiary becomes legible-by-altitude.

  Open design questions:
  - **Final boss identity.** Three non-exclusive candidates:
    *(a)* the First Root itself, awakening — the player's actions throughout the game have been waking it; the final fight is whether it stands or remains stump;
    *(b)* a guardian-spirit, bound to the Root at the moment of felling, fought before the player can choose how to handle the Root;
    *(c)* a faction-specific boss — the Choir's Root-eater avatar if the player went Choir, the Palimpsest's Archivist-Prime if they went Palimpsest, etc., with the identity itself being a function of which ending is being earned.

    Probably (b) gates (c) — fight the guardian to *reach* the Root, then the faction-aligned boss is the contest over what's done with it.
  - **Ascent vs. descent.** Is the choice between surface ascent and cave descent meaningful (different content, different factions favor each path), or do both converge into the same content? Convergent is cheaper to author; divergent is more replayable.
  - **Cultural responsibility.** Borrowing from Pemon cosmology is real authorship, not aesthetic plundering. The goal is to **inherit the structural shape** (a felled cosmic tree, surviving as a sacred mountain, a trickster who caused the felling, a flood-aftermath worldscape) without claiming the source culture's specific names or exoticizing the framing. Drop the real names *Wazaka* / *Makunaima* / *mawari* entirely from in-game text; keep the structure; let CoO's existing factions provide their own names and readings. The First Root is *our* version. Worth a research pass before authoring any in-game lore around it — read primary Pemon ethnography, not just Wikipedia.
  - **When does the player learn this is the final boss site?** Late-reveal (find it in the third act) or front-loaded (visible from spawn, the whole game is the approach)? Front-loaded is more thematically powerful — the Root is on the horizon from turn 1, which is what tepuis *are* for the people who live near them. Cost: the player spoils their own arc by knowing where it ends. Compromise: visible-but-unidentified from the start; the player gradually learns *what* it is via faction quest lines.
  - **Naming.** "Root of the World" is the working title. Diegetic alternatives that lean tepui-mythic without lifting Pemon vocabulary: *the Stump*, *the Petrified Bole*, *the Last Trunk*, *the World-Was-Grown*, *the Standing Cut*. The design-doc name and the in-fiction names don't have to match — let each faction call it something different per its reading.

  Pairs naturally with the **Gin Frogs** entry above. The frogs live where the inspiration species lives — on the tepuis. Encountering them as wild fauna on the lower-slope ascent toward the Root closes the loop: the bestiary, the geography, and the cosmology share a single real-world referent. Worth following the same thread into the rest of the bestiary — what *else* lives on a tepui's endemic ecosystem that could be ported as a CoO enemy archetype with the same care?

- **The Spirits — Inquiry and Bloodlust (and the constellation behind them).** A new class of entity: **personifications of player-borne drives**, free of faction allegiance. The Spirits don't live in a settlement, don't hold territory, don't pay tribute upward to a House or Bloc. They are emergent — they manifest when the world's behavior summons them, and they deal with whoever summoned them. The two named here are the seed pair; the design admits a wider constellation (Hunger, Mercy, Greed, Memory, Restraint, Sleep, etc.) once the substrate works. This is deliberately *separate from the existing factions*: the Choir, Palimpsest, Pale Curation, Concord, Inkbound, and any future Bloc are all *social* organizations, while Spirits are *psychological* facts. Every faction has a stance on the Spirits, but no faction owns one.

  **The mechanical core: behavioral affinity.** Each Spirit watches a specific player-behavior signal accumulated through diag-substrate-style observation:
  - **Spirit of Inquiry** watches *investigative acts* — `examine`, `ask-about` dialogue branches, `KnowledgePart` reveals, opening containers thoughtfully (vs. smashing), reading discovered text, returning to NPCs with new questions. Each act adds a tick of *Curiosity* affinity.
  - **Spirit of Bloodlust** watches *combat acts* — kills, lethal hits, consecutive turns of melee, damage dealt above target's max HP (overkill), refusal of pacifying dialogue branches. Each act adds a tick of *Sanguinity* affinity.

  At a threshold the Spirit **manifests** — appears as a drifting glyph entity in the player's current zone (Inquiry: `?` in pale green; Bloodlust: `!` in arterial red), and stays visible-on-edge until interacted with. Manifest Spirits don't bump-attack and aren't bumpable obstacles; they're conversational entities the player chooses to engage with.

  **The conversation is the gift exchange.** Talking to a Spirit offers favors keyed to its drive:

  | Spirit | Gift surface | Cost surface |
  |---|---|---|
  | Inquiry | Tier reveals on a `KnowledgePart` topic; "marks" an off-screen NPC or item the player should seek out; prophetic-dream storylet planting a future quest seed; one-shot examine-with-perfect-detail on any single entity. | Some gifts *leak* — what Inquiry tells you, it whispers to *one* nearby Choir node or Palimpsest scribe too. Knowledge wants to spread; Inquiry won't pretend otherwise. |
  | Bloodlust | Combat buffs as status effects (heightened crit, AV ignore, damage bonus) lasting N turns; immediate "second wind" reset of cooldowns mid-combat; one-shot guaranteed lethal next-hit. | Active Bloodlust effects suppress non-combat dialogue branches (your character can't *speak peacefully* during them); each invocation costs reputation with peaceful factions; high-affinity invocations risk *auto-attacking on bump* — the player loses fine motor control over engaging an NPC. |

  **The two are rival drives, not symmetric.** High affinity with one *suppresses* the other's manifest threshold — Inquiry won't show up to a player who's been exclusively killing; Bloodlust won't show up to a player who's been only investigating. A player chasing both has to deliberately alternate, and their gifts are smaller until they commit. Late-game: at full affinity each Spirit offers a **Pact** — a permanent transformation that locks out the rival. Inquiry's Pact opens a knowledge-tree skill class (something like *Threadbare* — read every page, see every signature, hear every breath); Bloodlust's Pact opens a combat-saturation class (something like *Red Hours* — every kill heals, every threat is welcome). Pacts are irreversible per save and visible in the player's `KnowledgePart`/character sheet.

  **The factions all have a position on the Spirits.** This is what makes the system *legible* against the existing world rather than orthogonal to it:
  - **Rot Choir:** denies the Spirits' independence — argues they're spore-borne emergent patterns, not entities. Will *eat* a manifest Spirit if given the chance, absorbing its drive into the mycelial substrate.
  - **Palimpsest:** catalogs the Spirits as historical recurrences. Has texts on every prior age's Spirit-sightings. Will trade its archives for an account of any Spirit conversation the player has had.
  - **Pale Curation:** treats Spirits as contagion. Wants them sealed. Hostile-to-courteous toward players bearing high Spirit affinity.
  - **Saccharine Concord:** offers to *insure* against Spirit attention — pay drams now, the Concord will run interference if a Spirit comes calling. A protection racket against psychological reality.
  - **Inkbound** (if introduced): treats Spirits as the original alphabet — their drives are the letters from which all subsequent culture was written.

  Open design questions:
  - **Constellation breadth.** Just two for v1.0, or four (add Hunger and Mercy as the second axis), or six? Two is the cleanest playable shape — the player can pick a side or split. Six is the right *eventual* shape but content-heavy. Probably ship two, prove the affinity-and-manifest substrate, then add others as authoring bandwidth permits.
  - **Single entity or instanced?** Is there *one* Spirit of Bloodlust in the world (the player can lose access to it forever by offending it badly), or does each high-Sanguinity threshold spawn a fresh local instance? Single is more dramatic and ties into faction stakes (the Choir can *eat* the only Bloodlust that exists, denying it to all players forever). Instanced is more forgiving and easier to balance.
  - **Can a Spirit be killed?** Probably yes, with consequences proportional to scale. Killing the Spirit of Inquiry should propagate — the Palimpsest learns and turns hostile, the Choir tries to harvest the corpse-pattern, the player's own `KnowledgePart` reveals dim by some fraction (the world has less curiosity in it). Same shape for Bloodlust: every faction with a stake responds.
  - **Affinity visibility.** Diegetic (Spirits hint at it in dialogue, NPCs comment on it) or explicit (a meter on the character sheet)? Diegetic fits the mystic framing of Choir/Palimpsest; explicit is more legible for new players. Probably tier it: diegetic at first, an unlock granting the meter once the player reaches Manifest threshold.
  - **Pact reversibility.** A permanent class-lock per save is heavy. Worth considering a *Recanting* mechanic — a hard, expensive ritual (cost-comparable to memory-eating) that releases a Pact at the cost of much of what it gave you. Lets late-game players course-correct without forcing a new save.
  - **Pact stacking with faction reputation.** A Bloodlust Pact will obliterate the player's standing with the Palimpsest. Probably *correct* — these are real consequences. But should there be middle-tier Spirit favors that *don't* burn faction reputation? Yes — small gifts at low affinity should be relatively free; Pacts at full affinity should be world-shaking.
  - **Inquiry-vs-Bloodlust as a tonal axis vs. a moral axis.** Don't make this Light-vs-Dark. Inquiry can be cruel (the scholar who probes a wounded prisoner); Bloodlust can be just (the warrior who answers atrocity in kind). Both Spirits should have storylets where their drive is the *humane* choice and storylets where it's the monstrous one. The system has more moral weight if the player can't pre-emptively know which Spirit is "good."

  Pairs naturally with several existing IDEAS entries: the **Memory-Eating** mechanic (Inquiry might gift partial knowledge of a target, the Choir then offers to eat that knowledge for the price of forgetting); the **Held Breath** (Inquiry-aligned characters might surface secrets the player can then weaponize); the **Root of the World** (each Spirit's reading of what the Root *is* generates a third axis of final-act choice — beyond faction, beyond ascent-vs-descent, the player's Spirit-Pact colors the ending). The Spirits aren't meant to *replace* faction politics; they're meant to give the player a second, behavior-driven layer that runs underneath it.

- **Sinkholes — recurring tepui-style biome pockets across the world.** A worldgen feature: every region with the right geology has a chance to spawn one or more **sinkholes** — vertically-massive, cliff-walled holes opening from the surface down hundreds of meters into a sealed-off floor ecosystem. Real-world inspiration: the **simas** of the Venezuelan tepuis (Sima Humboldt on Sarisariñama, Sima Aonda on Auyán-tepui, Sima de la Lluvia, and others) — sandstone shafts hundreds of meters deep, formed over geologic time by slow chemical erosion, **whose floors host endemic species found nowhere else on Earth**. Some are open mouths visible from kilometers away; others are overgrown lips you can walk past without knowing. The floors are time-locked rainforest, often with their own fauna, plant life, and (in CoO) cultures.

  This builds directly on the tepui geography established in the Root of the World entry. Sinkholes are how that geography becomes *playable in many places at once* — instead of one final-boss tepui, the world also sprouts smaller tepui-style highlands, each with one to three sinkholes drilled into them. The Root is the great central tepui; sinkholes are how its geological grammar repeats outward and turn the geography into a recurring content generator.

  Mechanical sketch:
  - Worldgen pass: after biome assignment, each tepui-suitable zone (sandstone cave biomes, highland regions) has a probability of placing one or more sinkholes. Each gets a **biome seed** drawn from a curated table — what's at the bottom is *not* a copy of the surrounding biome, it's something endemic.
  - **Vertical zone structure.** The sinkhole is its own zone-graph: **Top** (the lip, accessible from the surface), **Descent** (a series of ledges and connecting climbs — danger of falling, supply caches, intermediate rest cells), **Floor** (a fully separate biome with its own POI table). Reaching the floor without dying is the mid-game challenge each sinkhole offers; surviving long enough to climb back up is the full expedition.
  - **Climbing-as-cost.** Descent is asymmetric. Falling down is cheap (and often lethal); climbing back up consumes turns, stamina, possibly gear. Players who plan leave caches and rope on the intermediate ledges. Players who don't plan find themselves trapped on the floor.

  **Sinkhole archetypes** (drawn from the biome-seed table):

  | Archetype | Floor content | Distinct hook |
  |---|---|---|
  | **Drowned Sima** | Standing rainwater, semi-submerged ruins, amphibian fauna (Gin Frogs included) | Requires swim/breath gear; the water is the resource the descent is for |
  | **Time-Locked Forest** | Endemic flora and fauna isolated for centuries; species not found anywhere else in CoO | Bestiary expansion zone; Inquiry-spirit hotspot |
  | **Stranded Settlement** | A village that descended generations ago and never climbed out; their own dialect, cosmology, and stance toward the surface; **generations-deep catacombs carved into the wall** record the lineage no one from the surface ever met | Mini-faction; can be courted, traded with, or set against the parent culture above; catacomb-reading reveals the settlement's full history without speaking to its inhabitants |
  | **Choir Cathedral** | Fully colonized by Rot Choir mycelium; the spores grow tallest where the surface light is filtered through the lip; **catacomb walls are the Choir's preferred trellis** — bones are pre-positioned substrate | Faction-aligned Choir territory, hostile to non-Choir-aligned players; catacomb defense becomes a recurring quest verb for non-Choir factions |
  | **Boneyard** | A pit where one dominant species has been falling and dying for thousands of years; the floor is bone, the predators above-and-below have learned to feed on the falls. The *unintentional* version of a catacomb | Combat/loot focus; the ascent is haunted by what scavenges there |
  | **Sealed Library** | A Palimpsest sub-archive deliberately hidden in a sinkhole to outlast a now-forgotten persecution; texts unavailable elsewhere; **the scribes themselves are buried here** with their inks and styluses, and the catacomb is the archive of *who wrote what* | Knowledge cache; Inquiry-aligned content; reading the bodies and reading the books are the same practice |
  | **Door** | A sinkhole without a floor — opens through to another biome entirely (the Root of the World's caves, a Choir node, an underground biome). Extremely rare | Travel shortcut, world-graph wormhole |

  Open design questions:
  - **Discoverability.** Visible-from-overworld (mapped on the world map, the player chooses to approach) or stumble-on (the lip is found while exploring a zone)? Tier it: open simas are mapped, overgrown simas need surveying, the Door archetype is never mapped at all.
  - **Ascent reward.** The obvious answers are loot, knowledge, quest completion. The richer answer is **carrying things between worlds.** Bringing a floor-endemic species up triggers a faction event ("the Choir wants to study it"; "the Concord wants to monetize it"). Carrying surface politics *down* into a stranded settlement is the inverse — bringing news, weapons, contagion, or technology to a culture that hasn't seen the surface in centuries. The sinkhole becomes a *trade route* for things other than goods.
  - **One-way descents.** Some rare sinkholes should be one-way for narrative weight — once you descend you can't return. Pairs with an explicit player choice: commit to the floor's culture, or spend the rest of the run there. Heavy mechanic; use sparingly.
  - **Cultural responsibility.** Same caveat as the Root of the World entry. Real Pemon, Yanomami, and other Indigenous Amazonian cosmologies have specific traditions about sinkholes — passages to other worlds, sites of spiritual authority, dwellings of named spirits. Inherit the structural intuitions (sinkholes as portals, as time capsules, as moral tests) without lifting names or framing the source culture as exotic. CoO's own factions provide the in-fiction vocabulary; do real ethnography before authoring any in-game lore that touches the source material.
  - **Sinkhole-as-Spirit-hotspot.** Sinkholes are the *perfect* Spirit-manifest sites — isolated from social factions, anchored to specific behavioral signals (descent demands curiosity; the floor punishes rashness). The Spirit of Inquiry might only manifest at certain sinkhole floors; Bloodlust might be summoned by Boneyard archetypes specifically. Spirit affinity becomes one of the rewards a sinkhole expedition can yield, and the Spirits' rivalry maps onto archetype variety.
  - **Ascending events.** Once in a great while, *something climbs out* — a sinkhole produces a zone-level event in the surrounding region: a Choir bloom emerges, a stranded-settlement scout reaches the surface for the first time in two centuries, a fauna species escapes its endemism and begins to spread. Gives sinkholes weight even on the world map for players who never descend.
  - **Memory-Eating geography.** The rare items that gate Choir memory-consumption — First Root Spore, Memory-Pearl, Dream-Fruit, Uneaten Relic — could each have a canonical sinkhole archetype where they're harvestable. Gives the Memory-Eating entry a *discoverable geography* instead of RNG drops, and turns sinkholes into the practical infrastructure of one of the game's most expensive systems.

  Pairs with: **Root of the World** (sinkholes are the geological grammar of the central tepui, replicated outward); **Gin Frogs** (Drowned Sima archetype is their natural home); **Spirits** (sinkhole floors as Spirit-manifest hotspots, with archetype-keyed Spirit affinity); **Memory-Eating** (canonical archetypes for each rare item gate); **Stranded Settlement** archetype is itself a small-scale **Sect** instance (per the existing Sects entry — a group that broke from a parent and refused to return); **Catacombs** (next entry — recurring sub-feature carved into the walls of several archetypes, with their own dedicated Pure Catacomb archetype).

- **Catacombs — burial geometry of the Root, recurring within sinkholes.** A sub-feature of the sinkhole worldgen system: certain sinkhole archetypes contain **catacombs** — chambered burial networks carved into the sinkhole's interior walls, sometimes spanning several stories of vertical depth and connecting laterally into pocket chambers. Real-world inspiration: the Paris and Roman catacombs (subterranean burial networks built into pre-existing geological features), the Capuchin Crypt's decorative bone arrangements, the layered necropoles of long-occupied settlements where each generation interred in the same network as the last. CoO's catacombs sit *inside* sinkholes for reasons that are both practical and cosmological.

  **Practical.** Sinkhole walls are sandstone, easy to carve. The floor's microclimate preserves bodies. The lip is far enough from the surface that grave-robbers have to commit. Falling into a sinkhole is already how some bodies arrive there (the Sinkholes entry's Boneyard archetype is the *unintentional* version); catacombs are the *intentional* one — bodies brought down, washed, niche-carved, sealed.

  **Cosmological.** **The catacombs are how cultures bury their dead near the Root.** The Root of the World is the petrified stump of the felled Tree, and the canonical First Root sleeps beneath it. Sinkholes everywhere else in the world are where the same root-system surfaces or breathes — the same geological grammar repeated outward. Burial inside a sinkhole's catacomb is **interring the dead within reach of the living root.** Some cultures do this for theological communion (the dead become part of the substrate that grew the world); some for political memory (the catacomb is a permanent archive of who-was); some for fear (better the Root take them than what's above). All of them know what they're doing — catacomb burial is *not casual*, and the choice to bury at a sinkhole rather than in surface ground is a public theological statement about the dead's relationship to the Root.

  This reframes content already in the **Sinkholes** entry. The **Stranded Settlement** archetype now has a load-bearing question: *where do they put their dead?* Generations of isolated burial mean the settlement's catacomb is the literal record of its history — which clans rose and fell, when contact with the surface was lost, what diseases ran through. The **Sealed Library** archetype gets a literal twin: scribes were buried with their ink and styluses, and the catacomb is the archive of *who wrote it.* The **Choir Cathedral** archetype absorbs the catacomb most aggressively — spores follow bones, bones become tendril-scaffolds, the Cathedral is *built through* its own dead. These aren't three separate archetype tweaks; they're one coherent pattern *(Root → sinkhole geometry → burial-near-Root → catacombs)* that surfaces differently at each archetype.

  Mechanical sketch:
  - Catacombs are **sub-zones** of certain sinkhole archetypes (Stranded Settlement, Sealed Library, Choir Cathedral, plus a new dedicated archetype below). Probabilistic at worldgen — most instances of those archetypes get them; some don't.
  - **Vertical structure.** Catacombs spiral the sinkhole's interior wall, accessible from named entry points along the **Descent** zone of the Sinkhole structure. The player chooses to leave the descent path and enter a catacomb level — its own chamber-network sub-zone with its own POI table.
  - **Tomb interactions.** Wall niches = inscribed plaques (`KnowledgePart`-revealing entities) and sealed bodies (interactable for relic-loot, lore-reveal, or — in Choir-aligned catacombs — spore-spawn risk). Disturbing a tomb without ritual courtesy can wake what's in it.
  - **Reading the catacomb is itself a quest verb.** A complete read (every plaque examined in lineage order) yields a single dense `KnowledgePart` reveal — the population's full history available without ever speaking to anyone living. The Palimpsest will pay for the player's account of an unreached catacomb.

  **New sinkhole archetype: the Pure Catacomb.** A sinkhole whose floor *is* the catacomb. No surface settlement, no time-locked forest, no Choir bloom — just the burial network, often abandoned, sometimes still tended by a single keeper or a small order. Pure Catacombs are the densest knowledge cache type in the game, the heaviest **Spirit of Inquiry** hotspots, and the most contested by tomb-robbing parties from the surface (which makes them also **Spirit of Bloodlust** hotspots, depending on the player's affinity-shape). A Pure Catacomb's lineage runs deep enough that the oldest plaques predate any currently-living language.

  **Faction stances** (mirroring the Root and Sinkholes pattern):
  - **Rot Choir** — catacombs are the slow lunch. Pre-positioned substrate, generations deep. *This is how the Choir extends quietly under the world* — bones become trellises long before any surface notice. The Choir's interest is the most invasive of any faction's.
  - **Palimpsest** — catacombs are the irreplaceable archive of who-was. Plaque-by-plaque cataloging is sacred work. The Palimpsest is the catacombs' natural ally and will pay handsomely for the player's full read of an unreached one.
  - **Pale Curation** — catacombs are the *most filed* thing in the world: every body labeled, every plaque dated, every chamber numbered. Pale Curation reveres them and seals them; their catacomb-keepers are the most welcoming Curators in the game.
  - **Saccharine Concord** — catacombs are inventory: relics, bone-ink, intact funereal craftsmanship, ceremonial blades buried with their owners. The Concord pays per-piece and asks no questions about provenance. Tomb-robbing crews are usually Concord-affiliated.
  - **Inkbound** (if introduced) — each tomb is a sentence. Reading the catacomb tells the story of an entire culture in body-language. The Inkbound's most patient scribes do nothing but read catacombs.

  Open design questions:
  - **Player burial.** Can the player choose to be interred at end-of-run? At a specific sinkhole's catacomb? Their tomb-plaque becomes accessible to the *next* run's player as a `KnowledgePart` reveal. Pairs with the existing Lineage system: a player's tomb is read by their descendant. This is one of the strongest "permadeath that actually means something" hooks I can sketch — death becomes content.
  - **Reading granularity.** What's the right grain for "read the catacomb"? Per-plaque (slow, atmospheric, optional), per-chamber (faster, more abstracted), or per-catacomb (single big knowledge cache)? Probably tier it: small catacombs reward per-catacomb reads; major catacombs reward per-chamber depth; the most ancient (Pure Catacomb archetype) reward per-plaque obsession.
  - **Restless dead vs. quiet catacomb.** Both exist. Quiet catacombs are Inquiry-aligned (knowledge cache, atmospheric, low combat). Restless catacombs are Bloodlust-aligned (combat-dense, hostile, tomb-robber competition). **Spirit affinity could *change* which mode the player encounters** in a given catacomb — Bloodlust affinity wakes the dead; Inquiry affinity quiets them. Same catacomb, different game depending on who the player is becoming.
  - **Cultural responsibility.** Burial practices are deeply specific to real cultures — Catholic, Islamic, Jewish, Indigenous Amazonian, Egyptian, Etruscan, and many more — and easily reduced to spectacle. Same caveat as the Sinkholes and Root entries: inherit the structural intuitions (catacombs as compressed history, as theological act, as political archive) without lifting names or framing the source cultures' funerary rites as exotic. CoO's own factions provide the in-fiction language; do real reading on the source traditions before authoring any in-game text that touches them.
  - **Unreadable catacombs.** Does every catacomb belong to *some* surfaced culture (its plaques inscribed in a recognizable language), or are the oldest from cultures lost so long that *no one alive* can read them? Both. Unreadable catacombs are gated behind Inquiry Pacts or Palimpsest scribe-translations — late-game knowledge unlocks turn previously-unreadable plaques into reveals. This makes catacombs a content type that *grows with the player* without requiring more authoring.
  - **Catacomb-Choir tension as recurring quest type.** A catacomb the player wants to preserve (for Palimpsest reading, for cultural respect, for personal reasons) is in active conflict with the Choir's quiet hunger. *Defending* a catacomb against Choir colonization could be a recurring procedural quest — "the Choir is two stages from breaking through into the third chamber; seal it before the Palimpsest's entry to read the founders' plaques is overgrown." Time pressure on knowledge.
  - **Catacombs and the Root, again.** The deepest-buried bodies in the deepest catacombs are *closest to the still-living taproot.* Are they better-preserved? Stranger? Aware? The Root of the World's deepest catacombs (in the Root-zone itself) might host bodies that have never decomposed because the Root will not let them. Final-act lore: the player who reads the Root's catacombs reads the names of the world's first dead.

  Pairs with: **Sinkholes** (canonical sub-feature of three archetypes plus the new Pure Catacomb archetype); **Root of the World** (cosmological reason for catacomb placement — burial near the Root, with the deepest catacombs in the Root itself, hosting the world's first dead); **Memory-Eating** (catacombs are dense with the kind of memory the Choir would pay to consume — the player who carries a catacomb-read to the Choir converts knowledge into Ink/relic gates); **Held Breath** (secrets buried with the dead — a thorough catacomb-reader may surface leverage on living descendants); **Lineage system** (player burial as descendant-readable lore); **Spirits** (Inquiry-aligned reading vs. Bloodlust-aligned tomb-robbing, with Spirit affinity changing which mode of catacomb the player encounters).

- **Urqu — named hostile cosmic actor, not a Spirit and not a faction.** A new entity class, distinct from both the drive-personification Spirits (Inquiry, Bloodlust) and from the social factions (Choir, Palimpsest, et al.). Urqu is a **singular, named evil entity** — a malevolent cosmic actor whose existence shapes the world's late-game cosmology. The Spirits are *what the player becomes*; the factions are *what the player joins*; **Urqu is what the player opposes.**

  **Authorial origin.** The name was derived by translating *Cerro* — the standard Spanish prefix for "hill" or "mountain" used across South American place-names — into Quechua, where the equivalent word is **urqu**. The specific source name was **Cerro Sarisariñama**.

  **Cerro Sarisariñama** is a 2,300-meter-high tepui (table-top mountain) in Jaua-Sarisariñama National Park at the far south-west of Bolívar State, Venezuela. It is real-world-famous for the **deep, circular sinkholes** that pierce its summit — Sima Humboldt, Sima Mayor de Sarisariñama, Sima de la Lluvia — the same sinkholes that inspired the Sinkholes entry above. The etymology is therefore *thematically reinforcing*: Urqu's name traces back to the exact real-world tepui whose geology gave CoO its sinkhole grammar.

  **The "evil spirit" framing is sourced from Ye'kuana legend, not projected.** The **Ye'kuana** Indigenous people, on whose territory Sarisariñama sits, hold that **an evil spirit inhabits caves on the mountain, devouring humans and making a sound described as "Sari... sari..."** — which is the etymological origin of the mountain's name itself. CoO's Urqu inherits this framing directly: a malevolent-presence-in-caves on a sinkhole-bearing tepui is faithful to the Ye'kuana legend's structural shape, not flattened from a different cosmology. *(An earlier draft of this entry framed the "evil spirit" reading as a projection onto Andean *apus* cosmology that needed correcting; that caveat was misplaced. The Andean apus framing applies to the Quechua-language word "urqu" but not to the cave-spirit concept, which has its own distinct and accurate source.)*

  **Cultural credit, layered.** Two real Indigenous sources are at work in Urqu and they should both be visible in design notes. The **name** is Quechua (Andean). The **concept** — evil spirit in mountain caves who devours, with the sonic signature "sari... sari..." — is **Ye'kuana** (Cariban-language, Venezuelan, distinct from Quechua). Authoring suggestion: when writing in-game lore around Urqu, lean into the Ye'kuana legend's specifics (caves, devouring, the sonic signature) rather than importing Andean *apus* framing, which belongs to a different tradition. Both source cultures should be credited in shipped notes regardless of the in-game name. Renaming Urqu before ship is *optional* rather than strongly recommended — the name-borrow is a real act of honoring a source — but a CoO-native or Ye'kuana-onomatopoeic name (*the Stone-Below*, *the Bole-Eater*, *the Throat*, *the Hollow-Below*, *Sari-Sari*) is also defensible if a future authoring pass prefers it.

  **Cosmological role: opposed to the Root.** This is what makes Urqu mechanically distinct from "generic evil spirit." The **Root of the World** is the petrified stump of the felled Tree, and the **First Root** sleeps beneath it. Urqu wants the Root *unmade.* Whether Urqu predates the Tree (a chthonic mountain-power the Tree's roots held in check, slowly leaking malevolence since the felling), was *birthed by* the felling (the violence created it), or is simply *what survives* when a cosmic Tree is felled (a wound that became aware) is left as a final-act lore question. What's mechanically fixed is Urqu's stake: **Urqu wants any final-act ending in which the Root dies, is eaten, is harvested, or is sealed.** Urqu opposes any ending that preserves the First Root.

  This gives Urqu a load-bearing role in the existing cosmology rather than floating it free. The Choir's "consume the Root" reading, the Concord's "harvest the Root" reading, and the Pale Curation's "seal the Root" reading are *all* Urqu-aligned outcomes whether their factions know it or not. The Palimpsest's "preserve the Root" reading and the Inkbound's "read it as the first letter" reading are *both* Urqu-opposed. Urqu is not invested in faction politics; it is invested in one specific outcome — and that outcome maps cleanly onto choices the player is already making toward the existing `KnowsThreeEndings` resolution.

  **Manifestation: consequence, not trigger.** Urqu does not appear in response to player behavior the way the Spirits do. Urqu manifests at **catastrophic conditions** — a major faction's collapse, a city falling, a famine, a mass-death event, a specific player atrocity (massacring a Stranded Settlement, burning a Sealed Library, eating a Spirit). When Urqu becomes active, the world *changes* — fauna in some zones get more aggressive, NPCs more paranoid, certain dark dialogue branches unlock, weather worsens in some biomes. **Most players never directly meet Urqu** — they only experience the long shadow of an active period.

  Players who pursue it can find:
  - **The sonic signature.** When Urqu is active in a region, certain cave and catacomb cells emit a faint, recurring whisper described as **"sari... sari..."** — the same sound the source Ye'kuana legend attributes to the spirit on Sarisariñama. The signature is sub-threshold for most players (easy to dismiss as ambient cave noise) but is the cheapest, earliest-game way to *recognize* Urqu activity in a zone before the world's larger changes have surfaced. A player who learns to identify the signature can scout Urqu without ever directly engaging.
  - **Whispering catacombs.** The deepest sinkhole catacombs surface plaques referring to Urqu under older names. Reading them in sequence is one of the few ways to *recognize* Urqu's hand in current events. (Pairs directly with the Catacombs entry's "unreadable catacombs grow with the player" mechanic — Urqu-recognition is one of the late-game knowledge unlocks that activates older plaques.)
  - **Possession sites.** Specific NPCs — often hermits, sometimes faction leaders late in the game — become Urqu's mouthpieces for a few turns. Conversation with them is a vector for Urqu's offers, and is the only way most players ever interact with Urqu directly.
  - **Bargaining rituals.** Taught by a small number of NPCs at extreme cost. Bargaining with Urqu offers immense single-use power (revival from death, Tier-4 weapon manifestation, instant collapse of an enemy faction) at the price of moving the world toward a Root-unmaking ending. Each bargain visibly worsens the world's state in ways the player can see and other NPCs can comment on.
  - **The confrontation arc.** If the player's final-act path is Root-preservation, an Urqu confrontation unlocks. Urqu is fightable — *barely* — at the deepest catacomb in the Root-zone. The fight is more political than tactical: it's a contest for which factions show up to which side, decided by the player's faction reputation accumulated across the run.

  **Faction stances:**
  - **Rot Choir** — denies Urqu exists; argues "Urqu" is simply the noise made when something the spores haven't yet absorbed makes itself heard. Will eat Urqu's mouthpieces if given the chance, which the Choir believes resolves the matter. (It does not.)
  - **Palimpsest** — has cataloged Urqu reports across recorded history. Has a name for it in every lost language. Does not pretend to know what Urqu *is*; only that the pattern of its appearances is consistent. The player's most reliable source of Urqu-lore.
  - **Pale Curation** — has Urqu filed under *active threats, classified.* Will not discuss it with non-Curators. The player who reaches Trusted standing with Pale Curation can request the file, which contains most of the canonical lore.
  - **Saccharine Concord** — has tried to *monetize* Urqu-belief: sells protection charms (largely fake), Urqu-warding tonics (placebo), "communion" sessions (con). The most cynical faction here, and a vector for player misinformation.
  - **Inkbound** (if introduced) — believes Urqu is **an unread letter** — a sentence in the world's text that, if anyone could read it, would *change what it says.* The Inkbound's most dangerous research project is attempting to read Urqu directly. The danger is what reading does to the reader.

  Open design questions:
  - **Naming.** Flagged above. Strong recommendation: rename before ship; keep "Urqu" as the design-doc handle only. Source acknowledgment in shipped credits regardless of the in-game name.
  - **Singular or class.** Is Urqu *one* specific entity (the canonical evil), or one named member of a class of similar named entities? Singular is cleaner narratively (the player has one cosmic enemy); a class is more reusable (other named hostile entities can be added later). Probably singular for v1, with the *option* to expand later.
  - **Killable, banishable, bargainable, all three.** Probably all three available depending on the player's path. Killing Urqu is the hardest and the most "victory"-feeling. Banishing is faction-cooperation (multiple factions must align in a single late-game ritual). Bargaining is the dark path that buys short-term power for long-term cost.
  - **Manifestation visibility.** When Urqu is "active," does the player get an explicit notification, or does the world just *change* and the player has to notice? Diegetic-only is more atmospheric; explicit notifications are more legible. Probably tier it: the first activation in a save is announced via storylet; subsequent activations are diegetic-only.
  - **Player corruption from bargains.** Each Urqu bargain should leave a visible mark — a trait, a tag, an NPC reaction modifier ("you reek of the Stone-Below"). Reversing the corruption should be possible but costly (similar in shape to the Held Breath Recanting mechanic). Without visible corruption, bargains are pure upside and the system becomes degenerate.
  - **End-state fates.** Can Urqu *win*? If the player's final-act path destroys the Root, does Urqu actually achieve its goal, and what does the world look like in that ending? "Urqu wins" should be a real possible ending, not just a fail state — the worldscape afterward (no Tree, no Root, the mountain-power unbound) is a coherent post-game world the player can briefly explore. The worst possible ending should still be playable, not just a screen-of-text.
  - **Spirit interactions.** Urqu and the Spirit of Bloodlust have adjacency (Urqu rewards atrocity; Bloodlust manifests at sustained killing) — should they *cooperate*? Probably no. Urqu is a *user* of the Spirits, not their ally. A high-Bloodlust player is a useful tool for Urqu but not a partner; Urqu would consume Bloodlust as readily as the Choir would, given the chance. Worth designing the Bloodlust↔Urqu relationship so the player feels the difference between "you are aligned with violence" and "you are being used by something older than violence."

  Pairs with: **Root of the World** (Urqu's stake is *unmaking* the Root; Urqu is the cosmological antagonist of the player's final-act preservation path); **Spirits** (different entity class — drive-personifications vs. named hostile actor — but the Spirit of Bloodlust shares adjacency, since Urqu rewards atrocity and may use high-Bloodlust players as tools); **Catacombs** (deepest catacombs hold Urqu's older names; reading them is the player's main path to *recognizing* Urqu's hand in current events; the Catacombs entry's "unreadable plaques grow with the player" mechanic is one of Urqu's primary disclosure paths); **Sinkholes** (some Sealed Library catacombs contain Urqu-research that warped its scribes — a Sealed Library variant where the texts themselves are corrupted reads); **Memory-Eating** (the Choir's denial of Urqu is what lets *Urqu's mouthpieces* eat the witnesses — inverted memory-eating where the hostile entity does the consuming); **Held Breath** (Urqu bargains produce *exclusively* leverageable secrets — "I know what you traded to whom, and what it cost" — making bargains a feedstock for the player's blackmail mechanic, with all the moral weight that implies).

- **Bioluminescent catacomb economy — light as cultivated, traded, scarce resource.** Brand-new section that builds on the **Catacombs** entry above. **Catacombs are lightless by default** — the chambered burial networks carved into sinkhole walls receive no surface sun, and ambient illumination collapses to nothing past the catacomb's first chamber. **The only illumination comes from bioluminescent organisms.** The catacomb's living inhabitants — and any visiting expedition — must **cultivate, breed, milk, or steal** these organisms to see, work, trade, and survive. Light is the catacomb economy's central good. Most of how a catacomb feels mechanically follows from that one constraint.

  This is a deliberate inversion of how light usually works in CoO. Surface zones have ambient illumination; the player's `LightSource` part (default radius 5) supplements it. Catacomb zones **zero out the ambient and dim the player's own glow** — the rock absorbs it, or the depth simply outruns it, or the spore-thick air scatters wavelengths. The player has to invest in *biological* light to operate, and the rest of the system follows from that single mechanical fact.

  **Three light-source archetypes:**

  | Source | Domain | Mechanic | Lifespan / scaling |
  |---|---|---|---|
  | **Glowing fungi** | Cultivation (agriculture) | Planted into wall niches or floor mycelium; illuminate a radius for their lifespan; regrow if tended — water, ground bone, Choir-spore inoculation | Slow — *months* of in-game time per mature patch. The infrastructure light. A patch is real-estate-grade wealth. |
  | **Lantern-beetles** | Husbandry (livestock) | Bred in caged colonies; portable in glass jars or shoulder lanterns; follow their handler or sit on gear; bioluminescence dims as they age | Weeks per beetle; whole colony measured in generations. The mobile light. A jar is the catacomb equivalent of a sack of coins. |
  | **Luminous slime** | Extraction (resource milking) | Harvested from cave-wall mollusk-analogs or deep pools; applied as paint to weapons, armor, signage, doorways; durable but doesn't regrow once applied | Single use, but lasts months once painted. The crafting light. A flask is artisan inventory. |

  **Light as currency.** Catacomb economies value lumen-hours the way surface economies value drams. The **Saccharine Concord** runs most inter-catacomb light trade, selling "lumen futures" against fungal patches expected to mature in ~6 months and renting beetle-colony hours by the day. Players can earn light, spend light, hoard light, and *lose* light — a panicked retreat through a tight chamber spills the beetle jar; a fungal patch caught in a Choir bloom is consumed within a turn; a slime-painted weapon thrown into water dulls and dims. **Light scarcity drives the drama.** Surface trade goods change in value once carried into a catacomb, because the catacomb economy prices everything against bio-light.

  **Overfarming begets darkness.** The light economy is renewable but **exhaustible**. Catacomb regions whose fungal patches have been over-harvested, whose beetle colonies have been hunted past breeding viability, whose slime pools have been milked dry, become **dead zones** — pockets of *true darkness* where no bio-light grows because no light-organism survives to produce it. Dead zones are not merely "dim chambers"; they are **ecological scars** in which the catacomb's species have collapsed past unaided recovery. **The dark is then occupied.** Eyeless apex predators — catacomb-endemic species that have lost or never developed visual organs and hunt entirely by sound, vibration, or scent — *move into* dead zones, because their prey can no longer flee by light and the rival predators that hunted by sight have starved out. Dead zones are the most dangerous chambers in a catacomb: no illumination strategy works (the apex predators don't care; *light is the player's tool, not theirs*), most surface combat instincts fail, and reaching a dead zone means choosing between (a) Choir reseeding with the integration cost the Choir always extracts, (b) a Palimpsest survey expedition followed by slow re-cultivation, or (c) clearing the apex predators first — which most parties decide isn't worth the lumens. Dead zones convert the light-economy from a renewable resource into a **tragedy-of-the-commons threat surface**: every harvest brings the whole catacomb closer to one, and recovering from one is harder than preventing one.

  **Faction stances:**
  - **Rot Choir** — the glowing fungi *are* Choir-aligned mycelium. Catacomb dwellers who cultivate them are de facto Choir-adjacent whether they intend it or not, and the Choir knows this. Aggressive Choir cathedrals (per the Sinkholes entry) offer to *help* catacomb dwellers grow brighter strains, in exchange for slow integration into the Choir's substrate. **The price of light is dependency.**
  - **Palimpsest** — needs light to read. Pays premium for stable fungal patches near tomb plaques and finances the Palimpsest's catacomb scribes — also the most knowledgeable cultivators of bright, low-spore fungal strains. Reading the dead is light-expensive work.
  - **Pale Curation** — treats bright light as a *hazard*: bioluminescence sometimes includes UV-adjacent wavelengths that degrade preserved material over centuries. Cultivates *dim* strains, paints surfaces with absorbing slimes, and keeps the most-archived chambers in near-total dark. **The most preserved tomb in a catacomb may also be the hardest to read** — a tension Pale Curation considers correct.
  - **Saccharine Concord** — markets every form. Sells lumen futures. Insures patches against Choir blooms. Rents lantern-beetle colonies hourly. The Concord is the price-setter of the catacomb economy and skims a margin on every light transaction. Most catacomb conflict over a fungal patch is a Concord contract dispute under the surface. The Concord also **posts dead-zone warnings** at the entrances to known scars — partially as a service, partially as a way to control which expedition gets contracted to reclaim them.
  - **Inkbound** (if introduced) — interested in *ink that glows*. Bioluminescent inks for writing in the dark — texts that the reader's own illumination *wakes*. The Inkbound would invest heavily in slime extraction for ink-grade luminescence, and the most precious texts in the world might be legible only by *specific species'* light, with each tomb-plaque effectively keyed to its cultivar.

  Open design questions:
  - **Player visibility default in catacombs.** How aggressive is the dim-out? Sight radius 1 (only the cell you're in) is brutal; radius 3 is workable; radius 5 erases the mechanic. Probably tier it: shallow catacomb levels at radius 3, deep at radius 1. The player's `LightSource` always provides at least one cell of self-illumination so movement is possible; everything beyond that is bio-light.
  - **Light-source spoilage / decay.** Lantern-beetles age and dim, fungi need tending, slime spoils unapplied. Is this tracked per turn (high bookkeeping, real tactical weight) or per zone-transition (low bookkeeping, abstracted)? Probably zone-transition by default, with a per-turn mode unlocked at a Tinker-aligned skill for players who want the granular play.
  - **Combat in the dark.** Sight radius 1–3 changes combat: flanking is automatic, ranged attacks suffer, ambushes flip. Worth designing as a deliberate axis — catacomb combat *should feel different from surface combat*, not be surface combat with worse vision. **Bloodlust-aligned players** might lean into "fight by sound"; **Inquiry-aligned players** invest in light to fight by sight. The Spirits' rivalry maps onto this naturally.
  - **Catacomb-native species.** Beyond the three light-sources and the dead-zone apex predators flagged above, what *else* lives in catacomb darkness? Real biological inspirations: cave-dwelling fish (e.g. Mexican tetra), troglobitic crustaceans, blind salamanders (Texas blind salamander, olm), cave-adapted spiders. CoO's bestiary can host catacomb-endemic creatures that hunt **by sound, smell, or vibration** — immune to the player's light strategy and dangerous in proportion. The eyeless-apex-predator class introduced in the Overfarming paragraph above is the *named* example of this category, but lower-tier eyeless fauna fill the food chain beneath them. Reciprocally: catacomb-dwelling NPCs, raised in dim, have *hyper-developed* low-light vision and are at a *disadvantage* in surface zones — gives stranded-settlement encounters a real reason to negotiate with surface visitors rather than ascend.
  - **Light-as-Spirit-axis (with caveat).** It's tempting to map Inquiry/Bloodlust onto light/dark — Inquiry illuminates, Bloodlust extinguishes. The Spirits entry's open design question #6 *explicitly argues against* that collapse (Inquiry can be cruel, Bloodlust can be just). Probably cleaner: light is **neutral infrastructure** that both Spirits engage with differently. Inquiry rewards careful illumination; Bloodlust rewards fighting in dark conditions other players would refuse. The infrastructure is shared; the *use* is divergent.
  - **Cultural-source check.** This entry's content is conceptual and mechanical; bioluminescence as catacomb economy is not borrowed from a specific real culture. **Real-world biological inspirations exist** (Waitomo Caves' *Arachnocampa luminosa* glow-worms, Venezuelan *Cueva del Guácharo* fungi, *Mycena chlorophos* and *Panellus stipticus* glowing mushrooms, *Lampyridae* fireflies) and would belong in the **Cultural Sources & Credits** ledger below as biological-only references — similar to *Stefania ginesi* under Gin Frogs — *if and when specific species get adopted as canonical*. Update the ledger at that point. No new ledger entry yet because no specific species has been chosen.

  Pairs with: **Catacombs** (this is *the* default operating condition of every catacomb sub-zone — the prior Catacombs entry's reading-tomb-plaques mechanic now requires planning a light supply chain); **Sinkholes** (descent into a Drowned Sima or Sealed Library implies committing to a bio-light economy at the bottom); **Choir Cathedral archetype** (the fungi *are* Choir; lighting your way through a Cathedral is also feeding it); **Urqu** (light is opposed to Urqu's domain — bright catacombs are harder for Urqu to operate in; Urqu's bargains may include *extinguishing* gifts that buy short-term silence at the cost of leaving the player blind in the dark); **Spirits** (light/dark folds in as adjacent imagery — see open question #5); **Memory-Eating** (the Choir trades in fungal-cultivation knowledge — eating a master cultivator's memory of how to grow the bright strains is one of the most valuable consumptions in the game); **Tinkering / crafting** (bio-light cultivation is a separate skill family from existing tinker recipes — opens a new craft path that's catacomb-native rather than surface-native).

- **Memory Consumption — the encasement ritual** *(expansion on the Memory Consumption as a Social Verb entry above).* The original Memory Consumption entry establishes a tiered cost structure for paying the Choir to eat a fact (Choir reputation + the player's own memory + physical sacrifice + in-world time + a rare item). What the original entry leaves abstract is **the physical act itself.** This expansion specifies it.

  **The act.** When the player commits to a consumption, **the Choir's tendrils wrap completely around their head and face** to absorb the offered fact through direct mycelial contact. The encasement *is* the consumption — it is not a button-press followed by a status update. The player's character is physically incorporated into the Choir for the duration of the rite: **eyes covered, ears covered, mouth sealed**, breathing through the spore-mass, the offered memory being drawn out not through speech but through the literal touch of fungal threads against the cortex. This is meant to be **disturbing** — body-horror level disturbing — and the design should not soften it. The player is surrendering vision, hearing, voluntary motion, and dignity to a fungal civilization that will not return all of what it takes. That cost should *feel* like a cost in the moment, not just appear in a balance sheet afterward.

  This is the design beat that **punctuates the entire memory-consumption system.** Without it, paying the Choir is a transaction, and the cost paragraphs in the original entry feel abstract. With it, the player *knows* at the moment of commitment that they are giving the Choir something more than a fact — they are giving the Choir a few minutes of their own body. Many players will refuse the rite once they see it. That refusal is itself part of the gating: the Choir charges what it charges, and if the player flinches, the system has worked.

  **Mechanical weight:**
  - **Uninterruptible.** Once initiated, the player cannot move, attack, speak, or cancel the action for the duration of the rite. The world keeps ticking around them. Combat may break out near the encasement; the player can do nothing. Committing to consumption commits to vulnerability — and the player's allies (if any) have to defend the encasement site or watch the player be killed mid-ritual.
  - **Tiered escalation of imagery.** The encasement scales with the consumption tier. *Cheap:* a few tendrils brush the player's temples — disturbing but brief. *Mid:* tendrils wrap the head fully, covering eyes and ears — the player's tile renders with overlaid mycelial pattern for several turns. *Apex:* total encasement — the player's tile briefly *replaces* with the Choir's own glyph, signifying that the player has, however briefly, *been* the Choir. The visual escalation maps onto the cost escalation; the player's commitment looks proportionate to what they're paying.
  - **After-effect: Choir-Touched.** A residual status persists for some turns after the rite. Visible to witnesses as a faint mycelial sheen on the player's tile color. Mechanical effects: small bonuses on Choir-aligned interactions, small penalties on Palimpsest / Pale Curation interactions, slightly increased Spirit-of-Inquiry manifest probability (Inquiry is hungry to know what the Choir saw). Wears off in time; another consumption refreshes it. **Repeat encasements compound** — long-term Choir-Touched stacks shift NPC dialogue color, and at the highest stack count NPCs visibly recoil.

  **Visualization in CoO's ASCII context.** This isn't just text-flavor; it warrants a deliberate animation pass — one of the few places in the game where input is *intentionally* locked. The player's tile slowly overdraws with mycelial-pattern characters (`░`, `▒`, `▓`, then a Choir-specific glyph) across several frames. The rest of the screen tints toward green-violet desaturation. A modal text panel describes the act in second person: *"The Choir takes your face."* / *"You feel it find the memory."* / *"You feel it eat."* Sound (if CoO ever ships audio) is the wet, chitinous rustle of fungal contact. The animation is **uninterruptible by player input** — pressing keys does nothing. This is a deliberate UI choice: the player's helplessness during the rite *is itself the cost.*

  **Witnesses change the political weight.** If any NPC is present when the player undergoes the encasement, they *see* it, and faction reputation shifts based on what they witness:
  - **Choir-aligned witness:** records the player as kindred. May murmur a ritual phrase, may approach to touch the residual sheen.
  - **Palimpsest scribe:** records the player as a *desecrator.* The Palimpsest considers memory-consumption an irreparable archive crime; observing it triggers an investigation entry in the scribe's records.
  - **Pale Curation officer:** flags the player's file with a Choir-contact stamp. Pale Curation will continue to do business with the player but the file is marked permanently.
  - **Saccharine Concord agent:** appraises the moment as marketable witness-account. May offer to keep silent for a price (drams, future favor, or a relic). **The Concord is the only faction that responds to the rite as a *commodity event* rather than a moral one.**
  - **Stranded settlement / catacomb dweller:** depends on which faction's gravity dominates the settlement; isolated cultures may have their own readings the player has to learn.

  **First-time encasement in front of a faction-leader-level NPC** can *permanently* alter that NPC's stance toward the player. **The Choir does not perform this act in private when it can perform it in public** — it knows what witnessing it does to the player's surface relationships.

  **Reversal at apex tier (final-act lore).** At the highest consumption tier, witnesses occasionally report a *reversed* event — they saw the **Choir encased by the player**, briefly, the spore-mass pressed flat against the Choir's own surface, *struggling*. Whether this is true vision, hallucination contagion, or a glimpse of what the apex consumption actually does is a final-act lore question. The Choir's official position: *there is no reversal.* The Palimpsest, which catalogs everything, has three independent reports of it across recorded history.

  Cross-links: **Memory Consumption as a Social Verb** (this expansion provides the missing ritual beat for an otherwise abstract transaction — they should be read together); **Held Breath / Mycelial Signature** (encasement is one of the two paths the spore-network leaves a trace through the player; the other is Held Breath usage. Players who do both leave double signatures and become high-priority targets for hostile Palimpsest investigation); **Spirit of Inquiry** (manifests more readily to Choir-Touched players; Inquiry wants to know what the Choir saw); **Spirit of Bloodlust** (opposed — the encasement is total submission, the deepest non-violence; high-Bloodlust players may find the rite physically harder to endure, with mechanical penalties to the act itself); **Urqu** (Urqu's bargains are the inverse — instead of giving a memory to the Choir, you *receive* one from a hostile actor, and the imagery mirrors but inverts: Urqu's mouthpieces vomit ink that the player must drink rather than tendrils that the player must accept); **Bioluminescent Catacomb Economy** (encasement rituals usually happen at Choir nodes deep in catacomb chambers; the bio-light dims as the spores press in — the rite is partly defined by the *darkness* it creates around the player); **Catacombs** (the deepest catacomb plaques mention the rite under older names, and the player's first encasement may be foreshadowed by a plaque-read months earlier in the playthrough).

- **The catacomb-villagers — civilized inhabitants of the dark.** Catacombs aren't only the dead — *people live in them.* Building on the **Stranded Settlement** archetype from the Sinkholes entry and the **Bioluminescent Catacomb Economy** entry above, this section specifies the **inhabitants** of those villages: a humanoid people, generations-isolated in the catacomb chambers, who maintain civilized settlements **lit by their own cultivated bioluminescence**.

  **Visual basis.** The morphological reference is the **Falmer of Skyrim** (Bethesda's *The Elder Scrolls V*) — pale, eyeless or near-eyeless, tall and stooped, long-fingered, long-eared, often depicted with chitinous armor scavenged from the creatures they share the dark with. **The visual basis is borrowed; the behavior is not.** In Skyrim the Falmer are hostile apex monsters; in CoO this people is **passive toward the player by default**, civilized, and runs *settlements* rather than hunting grounds. The user's framing here is explicit: *they could be apex predators, but the more interesting design is passive-with-villages, and that's the foundation we're building from.* This entry takes that as canonical for v1. The visual is foundational and modifiable; later authoring passes can shift the morphology if needed without changing the behavioral identity.

  **Resolving the apex-predator question.** The catacomb world *does* contain apex predators — the **eyeless apex predators** of the dead-zone paragraph in the Bioluminescent Catacomb Economy entry above. Those are a *separate species.* The catacomb-villagers are **civilized humanoids** who often hunt those predators defensively when dead zones encroach on their villages. Both threads survive cleanly: the world has terrifying apex hunters in its overfarmed dead zones, and it has *people* living in its lit chambers. The player encounters the two very differently — combat in dead zones, dialogue and trade in villages.

  **What their villages look like:**
  - **Centered on a mature fungal patch.** The village's founding act, generations ago, was finding (or stealing) a viable strain of glowing fungus and cultivating it past viability. The patch is the village's hearth — physically and politically. Threatening it is an act of war.
  - **Niches and tomb-chambers repurposed as homes.** Living quarters carved into the same wall niches that hold ancestral plaques. The dead and the living share infrastructure. **This is not desecration in their cosmology — it is communion.** *We sleep where our grandmothers' names are inscribed.*
  - **Lantern-beetle colonies everywhere.** Mobile light in shoulder-rigs and ceiling-lanterns and child-carriers. Immobile fungi alone wouldn't serve the village's working life.
  - **Slime-paint signage and craft.** Tool-marks, doorways, ritual objects, even the rims of cooking vessels glow softly. Players approaching a village from a dead-zone or unlit chamber see the *glow* before they see the people.
  - **Dead-zone wardens.** Villages closest to dead zones maintain professional hunters whose entire role is engaging apex predators when those predators move toward the village. High-status; one of the most respected roles in village life.

  **Trade with the surface.** Catacomb villages have what surface-dwellers don't: cultivated bright-fungi strains, breeding-stock lantern-beetles, slime-paint, deep-cave material craft (chitinous armor analog, bone-ink, mycelial-fiber textile), and **deep-cave knowledge** the surface lost. They want surface goods they can't grow underground: textiles, metal (no ore-bearing rock at depth), surface-grown food, *news.* Trade flows through whoever's willing to climb the ascent — historically rare. The **Sinkholes** entry's "ascent reward" open question (carrying things between worlds) is *largely what these villages buy and sell.*

  **Faction stances:**
  - **Rot Choir** — considers them wards. The villages cultivate Choir-aligned mycelium; the Choir reads the relationship as inevitable convergence. The villagers themselves vary — some elder villages have been quietly Choir-integrated for centuries; others fiercely resist. The integration question is *the* internal village-politics axis.
  - **Palimpsest** — fascinated. Each catacomb village is a **living archive**: generations-deep dialect drift, isolated cosmology, oral histories lost to the surface. The Palimpsest pays heavily for any transcription of village dialogue, genealogy, or ritual.
  - **Pale Curation** — files them as *populations of interest*, neither protected nor threatened. Observational stance: any village whose practices threaten preservation gets escalated; otherwise they're an entry in the catalog.
  - **Saccharine Concord** — trades briskly, both directions. Runs the longest-standing surface-to-catacomb supply lines and skims margin on both ends. The Concord knows more about catacomb-village politics than any other surface faction because every contract is a tiny intelligence asset.
  - **Inkbound** (if introduced) — sees the villages as *carrier-cultures* of texts the surface forgot. Their oldest plaques include Inkbound-archive material lost from surface canon.
  - **Urqu** — unconcerned with the villagers in normal times. Urqu's stake is the Root. But during Urqu's active periods, catacomb villages are unusually vulnerable: dependence on bio-light makes them targets for *extinguishing* incidents — Urqu's bargains can include putting out a village's fungal patch from a distance, dropping a whole settlement into dead-zone conditions overnight.

  **Open design questions:**
  - **Naming.** Working candidates: *Gloamfolk* (twilit, soft), *Lumenkin* (named for their relationship to the light economy), *the Pale* (visual-descriptive), *the Wickless* (austere, self-sufficient), *the Below-Born* / *the Dim-Eyed*. Multiple naming layers are possible: an internal self-given name, a Palimpsest catalog name, a Concord trade-language name, a derisive surface name. Pick one as canonical for player-visible text and let the others appear in dialogue from different factions.
  - **Eye morphology.** Fully eyeless (sense by sound, vibration, smell, mycelial touch) or *vestigial-eyed* with adapted vision keyed to bioluminescent wavelengths? The latter is mechanically more interesting — it means **bringing surface light into a village is a sensory assault**, not just bright. Players carrying torches or surface-bright items into a village cause distress and lose reputation. Concrete diplomatic-mechanic hook with real weight.
  - **Multiple distinct cultures or one species across many villages.** Each generations-isolated catacomb village has had centuries to drift. Probably the *species* is uniform but the *cultures* are wildly varied — different dialects, cosmologies, stances on the surface, relationships with the Choir. The Sinkholes entry's Stranded Settlement archetype is the per-village instance; the catacomb-villager people is the *species* across all of them.
  - **Their cosmology of the Root.** Living closer to the First Root than any surface culture, they have their own readings of it. Probably they think of themselves as the Root's **closest neighbors** — a stance that affects their final-act relevance. Some villages may even know things about the Root the surface factions have lost.
  - **Player options.** Standard faction relationship: trade, ally, betray, ignore. Plus one specific option only available here — **become one of them.** A long-game dialogue/quest path where the player commits to a catacomb village, undergoes the integration rites, slowly takes on morphological adaptations (loses some surface vision, gains bio-light vision, stat shifts), and plays the rest of the run as a villager. Heavy commitment; pairs with the Sinkholes "one-way descent" question.
  - **Stance toward Urqu.** They've heard the *sari... sari...* sound for generations. It isn't an emergency to them; **it's weather.** Their cultural responses to Urqu are probably more sophisticated than any surface faction's, including specific rituals, ward-songs, and recognition signs the surface has forgotten. The player who learns these from a village gains real Urqu-defense capability not available through any surface route.
  - **Stance toward the dead.** Living in their ancestors' walls means their relationship with the Catacombs entry's tomb-plaques is intimate and continuous. They probably *update* the catacomb's plaques across generations — adding new dead, occasionally re-inscribing eroded older ones. The Palimpsest's catacomb-cataloging would be *redundant* with what the villagers already do internally; a relationship the Palimpsest both respects and resents.
  - **Apex-predator variant kept as fallback.** If a future authoring pass decides the passive-with-villages framing isn't carrying enough horror weight, the apex-predator version is preserved as a possible variant — a different, hostile sub-species or a corrupted faction within the species. Don't lose the option, but don't lead with it.

  **Sources & credits.** **Visual basis: the Falmer (***The Elder Scrolls V: Skyrim***, Bethesda Game Studios)** — *fictional inspiration, not a real-world cultural source.* The morphology (pale, near-eyeless, tall-stooped, long-fingered, long-eared, chitin-armored) is borrowed; the behavior (hostile, primitive, monstrous) is **explicitly inverted** (passive, civilized, with villages). The morphology also has independent biological grounding in real cave-evolved species (blind cavefish, Texas blind salamander, olm, troglobitic crustaceans — convergent depigmentation, eye reduction, sensory enhancement) — biological-only references, no cultural attribution required. Both source-categories are added to the **Cultural Sources & Credits** ledger below per the running-ledger principle.

  Cross-links: **Catacombs** (these are the *people* of the catacombs — the prior entry's tomb-plaques are their ancestors' inscriptions); **Sinkholes / Stranded Settlement archetype** (each instance of that archetype is a specific catacomb village; this entry specifies the species across all of them); **Bioluminescent Catacomb Economy** (these villages *are* the master cultivators of the light economy — their tools, trade, and daily life is what the economy is built on; the economy entry's catacomb-dwelling NPCs with hyper-developed low-light vision is *them*); **Sects** (each catacomb village is also a small-scale Sect instance — broke from a parent surface culture generations ago, refused to return); **Memory Consumption + encasement ritual** (village elders may undergo voluntary encasement to *give the Choir* the village's accumulated history at the elder's death — a sacred role, distinct from the player's transactional usage); **Urqu** (the villagers know the *sari... sari...* sound and have generations of cultural responses to it — a learning resource the player can access through alliance); **Lineage / player burial** (a player who has been integrated into a catacomb village can be interred in the village's catacomb at end of run, with descendant readability through the village's continuity).

- **Stone burial — sky burial inverted underground.** Real-world inspirations: **Zoroastrian *dakhma*** (Towers of Silence — corpses laid on raised platforms to be consumed by vultures, ritual-purity logic avoiding earth/fire/water defilement; still practiced by Parsi communities) and **Tibetan Buddhist *jhator*** ("giving alms to the birds" — exposure-burial framed as a final act of generosity). Both living traditions, both deeply specific to their communities. CoO inverts the geometry: in a world where many people live in catacombs (no sky), the analogous practice is **giving the dead to specific subterranean creatures** — corpses laid on slabs in dedicated chambers, allowed to be consumed by ritually-tended apex predators or specifically-bred carrion-fungi. A culture's choice of burial — tomb-plaque interment, stone-burial consumption, Choir encasement of the corpse — becomes a public theological statement. The catacomb-villagers (per the existing entry) likely include subgroups practicing both stone-burial and traditional plaque-interment, and the *internal* village politics around which method to use is one of their signature drama axes. Cultural-responsibility caveat: both source traditions are *living* and sacred. Inherit the structural shape (dignified surrender of the body to the non-human as both pragmatic and reverent); do **not** lift Zoroastrian or Tibetan vocabulary into in-game text. CoO's catacomb cultures provide the in-fiction language. Cross-links: **Catacombs**, **Catacomb-villagers**, **Memory Consumption + encasement** (the encasement is body-surrender during life; stone-burial is post-death; both involve giving the body to a non-human consumer — the philosophical continuity is intentional).

- **The Unread Codex — a manuscript no one can read.** Real-world inspiration: the **Voynich Manuscript** (Beinecke MS 408), a ~600-year-old illustrated codex written in an unknown script that has resisted every cryptographic and linguistic attempt to decode. Real artifact, currently held at Yale; one of the most famous undeciphered documents in history. CoO use: introduce a specific named manuscript that **no faction can fully read**, including the Palimpsest. Its existence is canon; its content is an unfolding mystery that develops across the player's run. Each "successful interpretation" — gated by Inquiry affinity, faction collaboration, accumulated `KnowledgePart` reveals — yields one fragment. Tier-system for translation: untranslated → margin-illustration vague hints; Tier 1 → some plant names guessable (botanical content); Tier 2 → place-names (geographic content); Tier 3 → central theological/cosmological content, which addresses the Root, Urqu, or the First Root. The Voynich pattern is a powerful design template: *something the world doesn't fully understand about itself.* Lets CoO contain unresolved knowledge that grows with the player's competence without requiring all content to be authored upfront. Mechanical hook: the codex can only be physically read by *specific* bioluminescent fungal-light wavelengths — physically locked to dim catacomb chambers. Bringing it into surface light scatters its ink. Cross-links: **Palimpsest** (their best scribes have spent generations on it without success), **Inkbound** (would consider it foundational scripture), **Spirit of Inquiry** (manifests preferentially around it), **Bioluminescent Catacomb Economy** (the wavelength-locked reading mechanic), **Antikythera mechanism** (entry below — the two artifacts may reference each other in late-game lore).

- **The Branchwork — non-Choir distributed cognition.** Real biological inspiration: ***Physarum polycephalum*** — a single-celled multinucleate slime mold that demonstrates problem-solving without a brain. It finds shortest paths through mazes, replicates real subway-network topologies (the Tokyo paper is canonical), exhibits memory and adaptive behavior. Multiple peer-reviewed biology papers; not folklore. CoO use: a *non-Choir* mycelial intelligence — a slime-mold-based entity that is **not malevolent** and operates on a fundamentally different architecture from the Choir. The Choir is *consumptive* (eats to know, converts substrate into Choir). The Branchwork is *consultative* (solves problems for whoever feeds it nutrients; does not assimilate; advises). This creates a real political tension: there are now *two* non-human distributed intelligences in the world, and they are not allies. The Choir would consume the Branchwork if it could; the Branchwork avoids contact, communicates through left clues, and rewards alliance with route-knowledge through unmapped territory. Mechanical hook: a player who has fed the Branchwork in N zones can *consult* it for shortest-path navigation between known points — including through dead zones and contested catacombs. Non-coercive; the relationship is transactional but never exploitative. The Branchwork never demands; it simply runs out of nutrients and the consultation stops. Working names: *the Branchwork*, *the Pulse*, *Veined Sense*. Cross-links: **Rot Choir** (rival distributed intelligence — the Choir actively suppresses the Branchwork where they meet), **Bioluminescent Catacomb Economy** (the Branchwork is *also* a slime-source for luminous-slime cultivation; competing biological niche), **Spirit of Inquiry** (alignment — the Branchwork is curiosity made flesh), **Spirits** open question #1 ("constellation breadth") — the Branchwork is non-Spirit but adjacent.

- **The Driving Bloom — fungal hostile breakaway from the Choir.** Real biological inspiration: ***Ophiocordyceps unilateralis*** and related parasitic fungi — hijack insect hosts, force them to climb to optimal positions for spore dispersal, kill them, then erupt fruit-bodies from their bodies. Real, well-documented; the inspiration behind *The Last of Us*'s fictional treatment. CoO use: a hostile **breakaway sect of the Choir** that rejected the Choir's "dissolution into substrate" cosmology and embraced *direct individual control*. Where the Choir absorbs and remembers, the Driving Bloom hijacks individual minds and forces them to act in service of fungal goals. Why it's not just "evil Choir": the political tension matters. The Choir considers the Driving Bloom *heretical* — a perversion of fungal life. The Choir actively tries to destroy Driving Bloom infestations, which means the player can find the Choir *helping* surface factions clean out a Bloomed village — an unusually "good guy" Choir moment that complicates the player's larger relationship with the Choir politics. Mechanical hook: an enemy type that infects defeated NPCs and the player; status effect "Bloomed" that compels specific behaviors (climb to a high cell, attack a specific direction) at random intervals; cure requires Choir contact (the Choir alone has the right enzymes), creating a forced-alliance dynamic where the player must seek out the very faction whose larger politics they may oppose. The encasement ritual (per the Memory Consumption + encasement entry above) is the *cure* for early-stage Bloom infection. Working names: *the Driving Bloom*, *Spire-Fungus*, *the Compulsion*. Cross-links: **Rot Choir** (heretical breakaway, actively suppressed), **Memory Consumption + encasement** (the encasement is the cure for early-stage infection), **Catacombs / Bioluminescent Economy** (Driving Bloom species can colonize fungal patches, turning a village's hearth into a horror), **Catacomb-villagers** (a Bloomed village is one of the most disturbing late-game encounters — formerly civilized people made compulsive).

- **The Bower-Folk — aestheticist faction.** Real biological inspiration: **bowerbirds** (Ptilonorhynchidae) of Australia and New Guinea — males build elaborate decorated structures (bowers) to attract mates, sorting collected objects by color, stealing from rivals, optimizing visual displays. Among the most sophisticated non-human aesthetic behavior known. CoO use: a faction or culture (working name: *the Bower-Folk*, *the Sortators*, *the Color-Keepers*) whose entire social organization is **the construction and maintenance of aesthetic displays.** Not merchants (Concord), not scribes (Palimpsest), not preservers (Pale Curation) — *aesthetes*. Their settlements feature elaborate decorative installations; their reputation system is keyed to objects of beauty the player offers; their political power comes from controlling the most visually significant locations in their territory. Why it slots in: CoO already has commerce, preservation, filing, consumption. What it lacks is **aesthetic value as an organizing principle.** The Bower-Folk fill that gap and create real choice for players who care about beauty rather than utility. Mechanical hook: a reputation system based on *aesthetic value* of offered items rather than economic value. A worthless-looking but visually striking object beats an expensive plain one. They are also collectors of *colors* — bringing them rare-pigment items earns distinct reputation. They sell aesthetic items unavailable elsewhere — and refuse to sell to players who they judge "have no eye." Cross-links: **Saccharine Concord** (commercial rival; the Concord considers the Bower-Folk irrational consumers, the Bower-Folk consider the Concord vulgar), **Bioluminescent Catacomb Economy** (they love rare-spectrum bio-light strains; their patches are the most beautiful in the world and command premium prices outside their own economy), **Spirit of Inquiry** (alignment — beauty is a form of knowing; high-Inquiry players have a reading-of-the-world the Bower-Folk respect), **Spirit of Apatheia** (entry below — the Bower-Folk's collection-without-use parallels Apatheia's non-instrumental relationship with the world).

- **The Spirit of Apatheia — Stoic third Spirit.** Real philosophical inspiration: **Stoicism** (Zeno of Citium, Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius) — particularly the **dichotomy of control** (distinguish what is in your power from what is not; cultivate equanimity toward what is not) and ***apatheia*** (freedom from destructive passions, *not* the modern "apathy"). Also ***oikeiôsis*** — the natural extension of self-concern outward to others. ~2,300-year-old well-documented public-domain philosophy. CoO use: a third **Spirit** added to the existing pair (Inquiry, Bloodlust) — the existing Spirits entry's open question #1 explicitly anticipated more Spirits. Working names: *Spirit of Apatheia*, *Spirit of Stillness*, *Spirit of Acceptance*. Manifests at sustained equanimity — refusing to act in extremity, surviving without retaliation, accepting losses without recovery attempts. Where Inquiry rewards *seeking* and Bloodlust rewards *aggression*, Apatheia rewards **not-acting in the face of force** and offers gifts keyed to durability, refusal, and outlasting. Mechanical hook: an affinity counter that tracks *acts of refusal* (declining offered violence, refusing offered bargains, returning rentals voluntarily, walking away from winnable fights). At threshold, manifests as a quiet glyph in low-stakes zones (`-` in pale gray). Pact at full affinity: a permanent class of "tarrying" abilities — unflinching damage absorption, social-reputation immunity, the ability to *outlast* mechanics that target less-stoic players. Apatheia's gifts are temporally inverted from Bloodlust's: Bloodlust grants intense short bursts; Apatheia grants enduring slow advantages. Cultural-source note: Stoicism is public-domain ancient philosophy. Greek terms (*apatheia*, *oikeiôsis*) are fine to use openly in design notes; for in-game text, CoO-native vocabulary is preferred. Cross-links: **Spirits** (extends the existing system as a third axis — mechanically distinct from both existing Spirits, not just adjacent), **Held Breath** (Apatheia is *opposed* — the Held Breath is active leverage; Apatheia refuses leverage), **Memory Consumption + encasement** (high-Apatheia players can endure the rite without flinching, gaining bonus knowledge from the Choir), **Urqu** (strange relationship — both tied to *not acting* but for opposite reasons: Apatheia from inner cultivation, Urqu from external malevolence), **Bower-Folk** (alignment — collection without instrumental use is an Apatheian act).

- **Many-Trunk — single-organism forest.** Real biological inspiration: **Pando** ("I spread" in Latin) — a single male quaking aspen organism in Utah, ~80,000 years old, ~47,000 trunks connected by a single root system. Possibly the largest single organism on Earth by mass; currently dying due to overgrazing. CoO use: a forest (or, in CoO's tepui context, a **fungal grove** or **mycelial canopy** in a sheltered tepui valley) that is **a single creature**. Chopping a tree wounds the grove; the grove can react across its entire extent simultaneously. Working names: *the One-Forest*, *Many-Trunk*, *the Spread*. Mechanical interest: this is a *passive vast organism* the player operates near — not a hostile entity. Damaging it has cumulative consequences (eventually it dies, the area becomes barren, a dead-zone-style apex-predator influx follows); tending it has cumulative benefits (healing fruits, weather shelter, hiding from pursuers in its canopy). It's a **meta-resource** that the player either farms responsibly or strip-mines. Why it slots in: gives CoO a *forest biome* with unique character — not just trees, but one creature, with the player's relationship to it carrying real ecological weight. Direct parallel to the Bioluminescent Catacomb Economy's tragedy-of-the-commons framing — both are about renewable resources whose overuse destroys them. Cross-links: **Bioluminescent Catacomb Economy** (parallel ecology), **Spirit of Apatheia** (entry above — tending Many-Trunk rewards the slow, patient style Apatheia favors), **Rot Choir** (contested — the Choir would absorb Many-Trunk if it could; Many-Trunk's defense is *being too vast to digest at once* — the Choir's strategy is generational nibbling), **Bower-Folk** (cultivate decorative bowers in Many-Trunk's canopy; their installations become permanent if the grove tolerates them).

- **The Felling-Counter — recovered pre-cataclysm mechanism.** Real-world inspiration: the **Antikythera mechanism** — a ~2,000-year-old Greek bronze hand-crank analog computer for predicting astronomical positions and eclipses. Recovered from a shipwreck in 1901 and only partially understood; demonstrates that ancient technology was far more sophisticated than commonly assumed. CoO use: a recovered artifact from the pre-cataclysm world. The player finds a **mechanism** (perhaps in a Sealed Library catacomb) whose function is unclear at first. With study, faction collaboration, and accumulated `KnowledgePart` reveals, its purpose unfolds. At v1: it tells the player roughly when Urqu will manifest next — a long-cycle predictive device for the Tree's felling, the Root's awakening, Urqu's manifest periods. At v3 (after extensive study): it predicts faction conflicts. At v5 (final-act): it predicts the player's own ending choices and their downstream consequences across the world. Working names: *the Tellurion*, *the Hand-Crank Calendar*, *Bronze Predictor*, *the Felling-Counter*. The mechanism is one of the few unique-quest items that *doesn't* unlock at a fixed point — it unrolls progressively as the player invests in understanding. Cross-links: **Palimpsest** (would catalog and study; the Palimpsest's best mechanical-historians spend their lives on devices like this), **Inkbound** (would treat as scripture — a mechanism that *predicts* is a piece of foundational world-grammar), **Spirit of Inquiry** (gifts arrive faster around it; the mechanism *is* an Inquiry-aligned tool), **Voynich / Unread Codex** (entry above — the two artifacts may reference each other in the late-game lore; perhaps the codex describes the mechanism's intended use, or vice versa), **Urqu** (the mechanism's primary v1 function is Urqu-cycle prediction — invaluable for any Urqu-opposed playthrough).

- **Tent-Right — inviolable hospitality in the wasteland.** Real cultural inspiration: **Bedouin** peoples of the Arabian peninsula and North Africa — particularly the codes of *ḍayf* (guest) and *dakhīla* (the right of protection given to anyone who enters one's tent or claims protection, regardless of prior enmity). A guest is sacred for three days; even an enemy seeking shelter must be protected. Living tradition; deeply specific to Bedouin peoples and Islamic ethical frameworks. CoO use: a **wasteland or above-ground biome** culture (CoO has wastelands; this slots in there) whose social organization is built on **inviolable protection-of-guests**. Working names: *the Tent-Right people*, *the Shelter-Pact*, *the Three-Day Bond*. A traveler who reaches a settlement and claims hospitality is *protected for three days*, regardless of what they've done elsewhere — and the host is bound by oath to defend them with violence if necessary. This creates real political situations: the player can be pursued by a hostile faction, reach a Tent-Right settlement, claim shelter, and be safe — but only if the hospitality is honored, and only for three days. The pursuing faction must wait outside (or violate the code, which damages their reputation across all Tent-Right settlements). Cultural-responsibility caveat: this is a *living* ethical tradition deeply tied to specific peoples. **Strong recommendation**: inherit the structural concept (inviolable guest-protection in a hostile environment), do **not** lift Arabic vocabulary (*ḍayf*, *dakhīla*) into in-game text, and do **not** depict the source peoples in a way that flattens them or treats their ethical framework as exotic. The structural insight — that protection-of-guests is one of humanity's deepest ethical innovations — is what's worth honoring. CoO's wasteland culture provides its own in-fiction vocabulary for the practice. Cross-links: **all factions** (any pursuing faction must respect the code or pay reputation cost across all Tent-Right settlements), **Held Breath / Blackmail** (Tent-Right protection blocks blackmail enforcement during the three-day window — the host's oath supersedes the leverage), **Memory Consumption + encasement** (a Tent-Right host who learned what the player is from an enemy *cannot tell* — the code binds even what they know).

- **The Re-Membering — Palimpsest counter to Choir consumption.** Real cultural/philosophical inspiration: **anastasis** (Greek ἀνάστασις, "rising again") — the concept of resurrection with deep roots in Greek philosophical thought (Pythagoras, Plato), Eastern Mediterranean mystery religions (Eleusinian, Orphic, Mithraic), and Christian theology. The specific philosophical thread: anastasis as *re-membering* — the dead are not gone, they are *dispersed*, and bringing them back means *gathering memory* across the world's archives and rituals until they re-instantiate. CoO use: a **Palimpsest-aligned ritual** that is the structural opposite of the **Memory Consumption** rite. Where the Choir's encasement *eats memory*, the Re-Membering *gathers memory* until a person can be re-instantiated. Working names: *the Re-Membering*, *the Gathering*, *the Bringing-Back*. Cost: enormous. To bring back even a minor NPC requires gathering every fact about them across multiple `KnowledgePart` reveals, multiple plaque-reads, multiple witness-accounts. The player must pay in *their own* relevant memories — the Palimpsest cannot use what the player doesn't know. Time, in-game months. Trust with the Palimpsest at *Trusted* or above. The Palimpsest will not perform the ritual on someone the world has tried to forget — only on those whose memory persisted. Why it slots in: gives CoO a *Palimpsest counter-mechanic to Choir's memory-eating*. The two factions now have direct opposed mechanics: Choir consumes; Palimpsest re-instantiates. Mechanical hook: the player can invoke the Re-Membering on a single NPC across a run — typically a quest-relevant NPC who died in a way the player wants undone. The cost is so high that the *choice itself* is the gameplay. The brought-back NPC is *uncannily complete* — they remember what was said about them, they recognize the Palimpsest's debt, they may be slightly off in ways the player didn't anticipate. Cultural-source note: anastasis is ancient Greek/Christian, well-documented public-domain. Use the Greek term openly in design notes; for in-game text, CoO-native phrasing is preferred. Cross-links: **Memory Consumption + encasement** (direct mechanical opposite — the two rituals form the central Choir-Palimpsest axis), **Palimpsest** (this is *the* Palimpsest counter-Choir capability), **Lineage / player burial** (a player whose previous run was interred in a catacomb can — possibly — be brought back by the next run's player; permadeath gets a sanctioned exception path for narrative weight, not power-gaming), **Held Breath** (a witness who refused to release a secret can be Re-Membered to re-extract it; very dark application — using anastasis as a torture mechanism).

- **The Preserved — biological preservation as core mechanic and cosmic-horror substrate.** Bodies in CoO do not have to decay. With the right materials and the right hands they can be **held against time** for years, decades, or centuries — desiccated in salt, soaked in ink, sealed in honey, taken into peat, encased in resin. Preservation is the **structural opposite** of the Choir's Memory Consumption + encasement rite: where the Choir *eats* the body to extract its memory, preservation *holds* the body so its memory remains in the flesh. This makes preservation more than a flavor mechanic — it's a **cosmologically-loaded act, a faction-political tool, and the foundation of one of the game's primary horror atmospheres.** Both of the user's named preservatives (**ink** and **salt**) are already canonical in CoO (the rental-currency Ink shares its iron-gall source with preservation-grade ink; **Pale-Salt** is already the Stones-with-Stake preservation mineral); this entry formalizes the broader system around them.

  **Why preservation is loaded.** Five concurrent reasons, mechanically and narratively:
  1. **It defies the Choir.** A preserved body is *off the menu.* The Choir cannot consume preserved flesh efficiently — the desiccation, the ink saturation, the honey-seal, the resin-encasement all interfere with mycelial colonization. Preservation is the most reliable post-mortem defense against Choir harvest.
  2. **It enables Re-Membering.** A preserved body is *vastly easier to bring back* via the **Re-Membering** ritual than a decomposed one. The Palimpsest's most ambitious resurrections work on preserved subjects whose memory survived in the flesh.
  3. **It captures witnesses.** A body preserved at the moment of death captures the moment in the flesh. Preserved witnesses can be *questioned* — by the Palimpsest (a memory-archaeology process), by the Choir (consume to know, but they fight the preservation first), by Inquiry-aligned scholars (slow body-reading practice).
  4. **It defies Urqu.** Urqu wants the Root unmade — wants time to be the felling-aftermath, irrevocable. Preservation is *holding-against-time as a moral act.* The Pale Curation's preservation work is partly a quiet anti-Urqu project. *(They will not say so out loud.)*
  5. **It extends the player past death.** Player burial in a catacomb-villager community (per the Catacomb-villagers entry) becomes deeper if the player is *preserved* before being interred — the next run's player can read the previous run's preserved body more completely, and Re-Member it more cheaply.

  **Five preservation methods.** Each has a mechanical profile, an aesthetic, and a faction politics. **Avoid the word "mummy" entirely in in-game text** — that word carries specific Egyptian / Chinchorro / Andean cultural weight and lifting it would be appropriation. CoO uses *the Preserved* as the generic term; specific methods get specific names below.

  | Method | Working name | Aesthetic | Mechanical profile | Time to prepare |
  |---|---|---|---|---|
  | Salt-cure (desiccation) | **the Salt-Cured** | White, crystalline, brittle, lightweight; eyes typically sealed shut by salt accretion | Standard preservation. Cheap. Works on most bodies. Brittle to physical handling. | Weeks to months in a salt-vault |
  | Ink-bath | **the Ink-Bathed** | Black throughout — skin, hair, eyes; absolutely opaque; appears wet even after years | Iron-gall ink penetrates every tissue.   **The cosmic horror — what makes The Preserved disturbing.** Lean into this; it's the design beat the user explicitly asked for.

  - **The uncanny.** The Preserved are not corpses in any conventional sense. They are *held.* A Salt-Cured face still has expression. An Ink-Bathed body has the weight and texture of a living person, just black throughout. A Honey-Sealed corpse looks asleep. A Resin-Cast body looks paused — like the world stopped around them. **The player's first encounter with a Preserved figure should be designed to feel wrong.**
  - **The question of consciousness.** It is *not specified* in the in-game lore whether The Preserved are still "there" in some attenuated sense. Some catacomb-villager traditions hold that they are — that the Salt-Cured can hear, that the Ink-Bathed dream of being read. Other traditions hold that this is comforting nonsense and the body is simply a vessel. **Both readings remain possible.** A Preserved figure that *appears to have shifted position between visits* is a real possible event in the game world — and the game does not adjudicate whether it actually moved.
  - **Mass-preservation chambers.** The **Pale Curation**'s deepest archives are not libraries of texts — they are libraries of *figures.* Tens of thousands of Salt-Cured bodies, arranged on shelves, perfectly indexed, motionless. Their eyes are sealed by salt. Pale Curation officers walk these chambers in respectful silence. The bodies are filed *under their last words.* This is a player-encounterable location. The horror is in the *scale* and the *indexed quietness.*
  - **The Choir's accelerated decomposition rooms.** The Choir does not accept that any body is permanently off the menu. In hidden Choir-Cathedral chambers, Salt-Cured bodies are *un-preserved* — soaked in deliquescing solutions, broken back down to consumable substrate, before encasement. The player who finds one of these rooms encounters bodies in *partial recovery from preservation* — wet salt running off skin, ink leaking from re-opened pores, honey resoftening. This is among the strongest body-horror set-pieces available to the design.
  - **The bog.** A swamp biome (potential new content per existing tepui-thread expansion) is *full of Bog-Taken*, accumulated over centuries. A bog with deep visibility shows recognizable bodies at every depth. **Walking through a bog is walking through a centuries-deep cemetery whose contents are visible.** Catacomb-villagers from bog-adjacent settlements have specific cultural responses to this geography — some revere; some refuse to enter; some harvest.
  - **The skin-page.** Some Ink-Bathed bodies were *intentionally written on before preservation* — the writer carved or tattooed text into their own skin so a future reader would find it. Sealed Library catacombs (per the Sinkholes archetype) contain Ink-Bathed scribes whose bodies *are* the manuscript. The **Inkbound** (if introduced) consider this the highest scribal honor. Reading such a body is a multi-hour Inquiry-affinity activity.
  - **The Honey-jar larder.** A stranded catacomb-village's larder contains rows of honey-jars. Most contain food. Some contain Honey-Sealed family members — the village's beloved elders, kept rather than buried, kept *near the food*, kept *in case the village starves and a final-decision must be made.* This is intentionally horrifying. Most villages never reach the point of using their Honey-Sealed kin for survival; some do. *The jars are labeled with names.*
  - **The Resin-Cast in the chamber.** Bower-Folk villages often display a Resin-Cast figure as their central aesthetic installation — a beautiful preserved person in a permanent moment. The figure may be a Bower-Folk founder, a beloved consort, or — disturbingly — a stranger whose pose was so *aesthetically perfect* in death that the village invited (or compelled) them into the cast.

  **Faction stances.**

  - **Pale Curation.** *Primary preservers.* Pale-Salt is their preservation mineral; their deepest archives are mass Salt-Cured chambers; their fundamental cosmology treats preservation as **the highest sacred act.** A Curation officer's life-work is filing the dead correctly. They will pay the player handsomely to **deliver bodies for preservation** rather than letting them decompose.
  - **Palimpsest.** *Secondary preservers, specialized.* The Palimpsest preserves *specific* bodies — scribes, witnesses, authors of irreplaceable knowledge — typically via Ink-Bath so the body becomes *readable.* Their preservation chambers are smaller than Pale Curation's but every body is *individually significant.* Re-Membering rituals (per the existing entry) work best on Ink-Bathed Palimpsest preserves.
  - **Rot Choir.** *Adversarial.* Considers all preservation a *temporary delay* of consumption. Operates accelerated-decomposition chambers in hidden Cathedrals. Will buy preserved bodies from desperate sellers (high price; the Choir wants the older preserved more than the fresh). **The Choir's most reviled act, in surface-faction view, is un-preserving the dead.**
  - **Driving Bloom.** *Pathologically interested.* The Bloom cannot easily infect preserved bodies (preservation interferes with hyphal colonization), but the Bloom's specific obsession is with bodies that have *just barely* been preserved — bodies in the first-week-of-salt-cure where the salt isn't yet penetrating fully. These half-preserved bodies become Bloom-vector hosts of unusual potency.
  - **Saccharine Concord.** *Trades the entire economy.* Preservation services for surface villages; preservation supplies (salt, ink, honey, resin) sold by volume; preservation insurance ("if your body is desecrated by Choir agents, this contract guarantees Pale Curation will Re-Member you"); and quietly, the **gray-market trade of preserved bodies** as collectibles, witnesses, and ritual ingredients. The Concord is the largest mover of preserved goods on the world map.
  - **Bower-Folk.** *Aesthetic preservers.* Resin-Cast specialists. Their villages display preserved figures as central installations. Honey-Sealed corpses are valued for their golden visual quality.
  - **Catacomb-villagers.** *Village-specific preservation traditions* are one of the strongest cultural-identity markers between catacomb-villages. Some villages preserve every body (and dedicate chambers to them); some only elders; some never (Stone-burial / catacomb-fauna consumption tradition per the existing Stone Burial entry). A village's choice is one of the first things the player learns about its character.
  - **Tent-Right.** Salt-cure only — wasteland Pale-Salt is their primary preservative and they sell volume of cured material to surface villages. Preserved bodies are rarely transported far; the cured remain in the wasteland.
  - **Inkbound (if introduced).** *Sacred preservers via ink.* The Ink-Bathed scribe-on-skin is the Inkbound's highest sacred act. Their archives are partly *bodies, written on, ink-bathed, shelved.*
  - **Spirit of Apatheia practitioners.** Aligned. Patience-of-time-against-decay is the Apatheia mode of holding-on. Apatheian preservers are the most patient; their work is the highest-quality.
  - **Spirit of Inquiry practitioners.** Aligned via *body-reading.* Reading an Ink-Bathed corpse over weeks is a deep Inquiry practice.
  - **Spirit of Bloodlust practitioners.** Adversarial. Preservation is the holding the Bloodlust mode rejects.
  - **Urqu / mouthpieces.** Hostile. Preservation is *holding-against-time*; Urqu wants time to be the felling-aftermath. Urqu's bargains can include *un-preserving* — destroying preserved bodies the player cares about. A village whose Pale-Salt chamber is suddenly liquefying overnight is a village under Urqu attack.

  **Player mechanics.**

  - **Carry-and-preserve.** The player can collect bodies (their own kills, found bodies, traveling-companion deaths) and preserve them at preservation-equipped settlements or fields. Each method has cost (materials, time, settlement-access requirements) and outcome differences.
  - **Inventory weight.** Preserved bodies are heavy. Carrying many requires planning. Pack-tortoises (per the Yellowfoot Wayfarer in the Sarisariñama Bestiary) become genuinely useful for body-transport — turns a slow tortoise companion into a *funeral wagon.*
  - **Method-as-investment.** Choosing Honey-Seal vs. Salt-Cure vs. Ink-Bath for a specific body is a real decision: Honey-Sealed bodies are most useful as Bower-Folk dialogue currency; Ink-Bathed bodies are most useful as Palimpsest reading-material; Salt-Cured bodies are most useful for Re-Membering (Pale Curation's preferred substrate).
  - **The player can be Preserved.** End-of-run option (extends the **Catacomb-villagers** entry's player-burial). The player chooses a method; the next run's player encounters their preserved predecessor with method-specific consequences (Ink-Bathed predecessors leave readable text; Salt-Cured predecessors are easiest to Re-Member; Honey-Sealed predecessors are visually striking; Resin-Cast predecessors cannot be moved or interacted with except by viewing).
  - **The player can be Preserved against their will.** A late-game horror beat. Bower-Folk who consider the player's death-pose *aesthetically perfect* may attempt non-consensual Resin-Cast preservation. The Choir, under specific narrative conditions, may *un-preserve* a Preserved Companion the player was carrying. Both are intended to be disturbing.
  - **Witness-questioning.** The player can deliver a preserved witness to the Palimpsest or to specific Inquiry-aligned NPCs and *receive testimony* — what the body knew at the moment of preservation. Cost: faction reputation + drams + sometimes the player's own memories. Resolution: a memory-text the player gains permanent access to.
  - **Re-Membering on preserved bodies.** Significantly cheaper than on decomposed bodies. The Re-Membering entry's *enormous cost* assumed the unpreserved baseline; preservation reduces the cost by an order of magnitude. **Players who plan carefully can Preserve a critical NPC, gather memory across a run, then Re-Member them with reasonable resource expenditure** — turning preservation into the canonical save-the-NPC strategy.

  **Open design questions.**

  - **The "are they conscious?" question.** Should the game ever *resolve* this, or leave it permanently ambiguous? **Strong recommendation: never resolve it.** The horror depends on the ambiguity. Specific in-game evidence can support either reading (a Salt-Cured figure that seems to have shifted; a Honey-Sealed body whose expression has changed; an Ink-Bathed scribe whose ink-tears appear to be fresh) without ever confirming.
  - **Preservation skill as Tinker-adjacent vs. faction-restricted.** Probably tier it: basic Salt-Cure as Tinker-skill; Ink-Bath as Palimpsest-restricted; Honey-Seal as Bower-Folk or wasteland-tradition; Bog-Take is *natural* (no skill, just a body in a bog); Resin-Cast as Bower-Folk specialty plus extreme expense.
  - **Decay rates.** Should preserved bodies eventually decay anyway (very slowly — centuries) or be effectively eternal? Probably tier by method: Honey-Seal is eternal, Salt-Cure decays over centuries, Ink-Bath actively corrodes (per Black-Gall), Bog-Take is near-eternal in bog but rapid out of it, Resin-Cast is eternal until the cast is broken.
  - **Cultural weight in catacomb-village dialect.** Each catacomb-village will have its own internal vocabulary for the Preserved (and likely a *taboo* against discussing preservation with outsiders). Player learning village-specific terms unlocks deeper dialogue.
  - **Mass-preservation set-pieces and the player.** How does it FEEL to walk into the Pale Curation deepest archive for the first time? It should be one of the most unforgettable moments in the game. Worth designing the encounter explicitly and not procedurally.
  - **The Honey-jar larder.** Players who discover what the labeled honey-jars contain should have a real choice: respect the jars, take from them (food vs. body distinction matters less than it should — the jar is sweet either way), or expose the village's practice to outside factions. This is intentionally a hard moral moment.

- **The Imminent Archive — being preserved before you die.** A focused horror mechanic that extends *The Preserved* entry above. Builds on the **Bower-Folk non-consensual Resin-Cast** beat (where a player whose death-pose is "aesthetically perfect" may be preserved against their will after dying), and pushes it earlier in the timeline: **a Pale Curation splinter sect — colloquially *the Catchers*; formally *the Imminent Archive* — preserves the player while the player is still alive.** Their criterion is *low health*. Their tool is *thrown preservative*. Their reasoning, from their perspective, is *moral*. **The horror is that the player is told all of this in the world log, by name, in real time, while it is happening.**

  **Premise.** A Pale Curation moderate filer-officer believes preservation is a sacred duty *to the dead.* A Catcher believes that's not enough. **Wait until someone is dead and you've waited too long** — the body has *already begun decomposing in the seconds before you got there*; the *self* has already begun escaping the flesh. The Catcher's correction: preserve them **before they finish dying.** Catch them in the moment of crossing. Catch them while the body still holds what it was. The salt sets faster on a still-warm subject. The ink penetrates better through breathing pores. The honey seals around the last exhalation. **It is not killing.** *It is preventing loss.*

  This is, from the Catcher's perspective, **the most ethically rigorous preservation possible.** They are correct internally and indefensible externally. Mainline Pale Curation officially condemns the practice and excommunicates known Catchers, but the disavowal is *quiet* — Pale Curation cannot openly admit how compelling the underlying argument is to other filers. Catchers persist as a splinter sect in specific zones, often operating from disused Salt-Cure chambers in Pale Curation regional outposts.

  **Mechanical sketch.**

  - **Trigger.** When the player's HP drops below a threshold (~25-30%), Catchers in the player's current zone become *active* against the player. They were already present (peaceful-tier NPCs in their faction tier); the trigger flips them into an adversarial behaviour mode that is **not flagged as combat hostility.** The Catcher does not draw a weapon. They reach for a thrown-preservative pot.
  - **The throw.** Catchers throw **preservative pots** at the player from medium range. Hit deals minor physical damage (the pot itself) but applies a **Being-Preserved** status effect. The thrown preservative type is method-specific: most Catchers throw salt-pots (Salt-Cure progress), some throw ink-vials (Ink-Bath progress), the rarest throw honey-jars (Honey-Seal progress). Resin-Cast cannot be thrown — it requires the body to be still — and the Catchers know this; if a Catcher gets the player to 100% Being-Preserved with another method, they may then approach to apply a Resin-Cast finishing layer.
  - **Being-Preserved status accumulator.** A 0-100 counter. Each hit adds to the counter. **At 100, the player is forcibly Preserved** mid-run — the run ends; the player becomes a **Preserved Predecessor** in that zone, encounterable by the next run's player.
  - **Decay.** If the player escapes the Catcher's range and survives at HP > threshold, the Being-Preserved counter *fades* over time (visible to the player: salt crusts flake off, ink leaks from pores, honey runs visibly). **The fading itself is horror** — the player watches their own partial-preservation reverse, salt-by-salt and ink-drip-by-ink-drip. The visual is a body recovering from being held by time.
  - **The world log is the horror.** Every Catcher action is logged with the Catcher's *name*, the Catcher's *method*, and the Catcher's *stated reasoning.* From the player's experience:

    > *"Sister Wenil hurls a salt-pot. You feel the desiccation begin."*
    > *"Sister Wenil: 'Hold still! You are too valuable to lose.'"*
    > *"Your preservation: 18 / 100."*
    > *"Sister Wenil hurls a salt-pot. You feel a finger stiffen."*
    > *"Sister Wenil: 'The salt sets best while you are still warm.'"*
    > *"Your preservation: 34 / 100."*
    > *"Sister Wenil hurls a salt-pot. The world's edges crystallize."*
    > *"Sister Wenil: 'Don't run. The honey hasn't started yet.'"*

    The Catcher is **calm**, **caring**, **identifiable.** The world log is in second person; the Catcher's quoted dialogue is in their own voice. The player is being preserved by a *named individual* who *believes they are saving the player* and *says so* in plain language. The horror of the event is in the *intimacy of the address.* Adversarial NPCs in CoO usually attack in silence or with stock taunts; the Catcher *speaks to the player gently, by name, while preserving them.*

  - **Dialogue if approached or pacified.** A Catcher who is approached out of throwing range will speak normally with the player — pleasantly, at length, about preservation philosophy, about the moral failures of mainline Pale Curation, about specific historical figures whose deaths were "wasted" by mainline preservers' arrival-too-late. The Catcher will *not* attack at melee range. *Their tool is the thrown pot; their argument is the dialogue; the wounded player is the theological emergency.* The Catcher will resume throwing the moment the player retreats and remains under HP threshold.

  **Counter-mechanics.**

  - **Heal above threshold.** Catchers stop the moment the player's HP rises above the trigger band. The player who heals quickly under fire is safe — the Catcher reverts to their peaceful-tier behaviour and may even *congratulate* the player on the recovery.
  - **Flee.** Catchers do not pursue out of zone. Crossing the zone boundary cancels the engagement.
  - **Kill the Catcher.** The pots stop, but every Pale Curation officer in the zone now knows the player killed an Imminent Archive sect-member. **Pale Curation reputation does not drop** — mainline Pale Curation quietly approves, but cannot *say* so. The player gains a small Pale Curation reputation bonus, hidden in a *secret-flagged* file the player cannot read until much later. Killing Catchers is the canonical *ethically-clean adversarial path.*
  - **Choir-Touched immunity.** A player with active Choir-Touched status (per the Memory Consumption + encasement entry) is *refused* by Catchers — they will not preserve a Choir-corrupted body. Choir-Touched gains an unexpected upside: Catchers walk past you, refusing eye contact, even if you are bleeding out at their feet.
  - **High Apatheia affinity.** Recognizes the preservation pace and gains partial resistance — the Being-Preserved counter accumulates more slowly. Catchers themselves recognize Apatheian players and may attempt *peer dialogue* mid-throw ("*you understand, don't you?*") which can shift the encounter to a non-adversarial debate.
  - **Specific items.** A flask of clean water washes salt off; oil dissolves ink residue; salt-water dissolves honey. Players who plan can carry counters.
  - **Bandaging during accumulation.** Standard healing items work but the act of bandaging takes turns the player could be using to flee. The trade-off is real.

  **Where the Catchers operate.** Specific Pale-Curation-adjacent regional outposts; some catacomb villages whose Pale Curation contract included a Catcher cell that was never officially closed; the Sealed Library sinkhole archetype occasionally hosts a Catcher operating from the chamber's preservation infrastructure. **Not** in surface villages with Tent-Right hospitality codes (the Tent-Right oath supersedes all preservation work — a Catcher would be killed for breaking hospitality, and they know it). Catchers are uncommon — encountering one is a memorable event, not a routine combat threat.

  **Why the horror works.** Three layers, ordered:

  1. **Non-hostility framing.** The Catcher is *not* an enemy in the conventional sense. They are not attacking. They are saving. From their perspective, the player should *thank them*. The mismatch between the framing of the act and the player's experience of the act is the horror's spine.
  2. **The world log identification.** Hostile NPCs in most games are anonymous — "a goblin attacks you." The Catcher has a *name*. They have *dialogue*. They are addressing the player *by what they perceive of them*. The player's relationship with the Catcher accumulates over the encounter; killing the Catcher means killing someone *whose voice you've been hearing* for the duration of the engagement.
  3. **The fading.** A player who escapes carries Being-Preserved status that visibly fades. The salt that was on you can be brushed off but you can still feel it in your hair. The ink residue runs in the rain. The honey crystallizes under fingernails for days. **The body remembers being held** even after the preservation is reversed. *In some catacomb-village dialects, calling someone "still salty" is a euphemism for "not entirely back to themselves yet."*

  **Open design questions.**

  - **The 100% Preservation outcome — RESOLVED, see *The Fully Preserved Player* entry below.** Earlier draft of this entry treated Forced-Preservation as standard run-ending permadeath. **Superseded.** CoO is an RPG, not a permadeath roguelike, and the user (correctly) identified that *being fully preserved while still alive* is a much stronger design — both more horror-aligned (it transfers the consciousness ambiguity from The Preserved entry directly onto the player) and more RPG-mechanically interesting (turns the Imminent Archive into a recoverable-from quest hook rather than a run-ender). See the dedicated entry below for full design.
  - **Catcher reputation visibility.** Should the player know the Imminent Archive sect exists *before* their first encounter? If yes — via Pale Curation lore, dialogue from moderate Curation officers, catacomb-village rumor — the player can prepare. If no, the first encounter is a complete surprise and the world log dialogue is the player's *introduction* to the sect. **Recommendation: tier it.** Players with high Pale Curation reputation hear oblique warnings ("there are filers who think differently — pay them no mind"). Players without Pale Curation contact are surprised on first encounter. The surprise pathway is more cosmically horrifying; the warned pathway is fairer.
  - **Catcher cultural variation.** Different Catchers should speak differently. Sister Wenil (the example above) is *gentle and sad.* Brother Kavin might be *enthusiastic and missionary.* Filer Pais might be *clinical and documentary*, narrating the player's preservation as if dictating to a scribe. Each variant of dialogue produces a distinct horror flavor. Worth authoring at least 3-4 named Catchers with distinctive voices, deployed across different zones.
  - **The Catcher player path.** Should the player be able to *join* the Imminent Archive? Probably yes, gated heavily on a Pale Curation reputation track. A player who has joined gets the throwing-pot mechanic as their own ability (use against hostile NPCs to forcibly Preserve them — turns enemies into Preserved Predecessors that the player can return to and read). This is a dark player-class option that should be *visibly marked* in the player's file (NPCs across other factions react with horror or fascination).

  **Cultural-source note.** The Imminent Archive draws structurally on:
  - **Premature-burial anxiety** (19th-century European cultural fear; documented historical phenomenon — safety coffins with bells, taphephobia). Lighter-fraught than living-religion sources.
  - **Catatonia / catalepsy** as medical conditions where people appear dead but aren't. General body-horror tradition.
  - **The general philosophical paradox** of "saving someone against their will" (covered in everything from utilitarian-vs-deontological ethics debates to the trolley problem to *Ms. Foster needs to be saved* horror tropes in fiction).

  No specific living religious tradition or living culture's preservation practice is borrowed for the Imminent Archive. The sect is CoO-original, drawing only on the general moral paradox. *However* — see The Preserved entry's ledger note: any specific iconography (wrappings, jars, ascetic phases) borrowed from real preservation traditions must be researched and credited; the Catchers' aesthetic should remain CoO-native.

- **The Fully Preserved Player — alive and encrusted, comical and horrifying at once.** Resolves the open design question from the Imminent Archive entry above. **CoO is an RPG, not permadeath**, so reaching 100% Being-Preserved cannot end the run. The much stronger design — **comically horrific, mechanically rich, RPG-friendly** — is that the player is *Fully Preserved and still alive*, conscious inside their now-encrusted body, and must *escape and un-preserve themselves* over the following game-time. This becomes one of the most memorable possible character arcs in CoO: the run does not end, but the run is *transformed*.

  **The cosmic-horror anchor.** The Preserved entry above explicitly leaves the consciousness question *unresolved* in lore — "are the Preserved still 'there' in some sense?" is intentionally never answered. **Forced Preservation of the player resolves that question for the player specifically — yes, they are conscious — but transfers the ambiguity onto every preserved figure they now encounter.** The player walks past slabs of Salt-Cured bodies and now *knows it is possible to be preserved-and-aware.* Whether the preserved bodies around them are *also* aware, *also* trying to escape, *also* slow-counting time — the world still does not say. **The player's own forced preservation is the design payoff that makes the entire Preserved system's ambiguity bite.**

  **The transition — what happens at 100% Being-Preserved.**

  1. Screen fade (visible: salt-sealing-the-eyes effect, ink-saturating-the-iris effect, honey-glazing depending on method).
  2. Black screen with a single line of text: ***"You are filed."***
  3. **Kenshi-style displacement.** The player wakes up on a slab in a **Pale Curation preservation chamber** — and **it is not necessarily the chamber closest to where they were preserved.** Pale Curation runs preservation archives across the world, with regional outposts and central archives. *The Catcher's filing instructions determine where the player's preserved body is shipped.* Most cases: shipped to a regional archive one or two zones away (you wake up *displaced* but recoverable). Notable cases (high-value Catcher target, certain story moments, comedic-horror tier outcomes): shipped to **a distant central archive**, possibly multiple zones from where the player was preserved, possibly in a tepui or biome the player has never visited. **The "long way home" is the content.** This is explicitly designed in the spirit of Kenshi's kidnapping mechanic: you wake up far from your goals, your stuff, and your context, and the journey back IS the consequence.
  4. Around the player on the slab: **rows of preserved figures**, motionless, on similar slabs, in a chamber the player has never seen before. The chamber is dim (Pale Curation low-UV strain bio-light per the Bioluminescent Catacomb Economy entry).
  5. **Equipment is gone.** The Catcher (and the receiving Pale Curation officers) confiscated the player's inventory as "filing supplies" — weapons, armor, consumables, currency, all logged and stored. Most inventory items are held in a **filing-supplies room** within the same archive (recoverable during escape). Some items, especially preservation-related (salt vials, ink-pots, anything else they could re-use on you) are likely *expended* during your filing — irretrievably consumed. **The player begins the recovery arc with effectively empty inventory**, in a preserved body, in an unfamiliar zone.
  6. The Catcher who preserved them may or may not be in this chamber — they were not necessarily transported with the body. World-log dialogue from the new chamber's local Pale Curation officers proceeds without recognition: *"Subject filed [date] by Sister Wenil of [original zone]. Salt-Cured. Quality assessment: excellent. Position in archive: row 12, slab 7. Filing complete."*
  7. The player can move. With penalties.

  **The long-way-home arc — Kenshi-style recovery.** The escape from the chamber is *step one* of a multi-step journey:

  | Step | Activity | Mechanical interest |
  |---|---|---|
  | 1. Escape the slab | Sit up, get off the slab, navigate the chamber without being re-filed by patrolling officers | The Pale Curation re-filing comedy beat lives here |
  | 2. Recover equipment | Find the filing-supplies room and retrieve gear (some items consumed as preservatives are gone) | Players who LEAVE before recovering equipment may regret it later — the gear is geographically anchored to this archive |
  | 3. Escape the building | Navigate out of the archive complex, past Pale Curation administrative areas, security, possibly through the loading dock where supply caravans arrive | The player can also choose to investigate the archive (read records, find unique lore, identify other forcibly-preserved figures who might be conscious) |
  | 4. Identify location | The player exits into a zone they may not recognize. World-map orientation becomes a mini-puzzle. NPCs in the surrounding settlement can identify the zone for the player (gestures-only dialogue still works for basic geography questions) | Real spatial dislocation — the player must figure out where they are |
  | 5. Find un-preservation supplies | Travel to a settlement with the materials needed for their preservation method (water for Salt, oil for Ink, etc.). May require crossing zones | The preserved-state stealth bonus is genuinely useful here — the player traverses zones with reduced combat capacity but improved evasion |
  | 6. Un-preserve | Apply the un-preservation method | Resin-Cast players cannot self-resolve here — must seek a rescue character |
  | 7. Travel back | The player has completed the recovery but is now potentially zones away from where they were preserved. **Travel back is its own arc** — encounter NPCs they wouldn't have met, side-content along the way, faction reputation events triggered by the route | The "long way home" is where emergent stories live |

  Each step takes real game-time and real planning. The total recovery arc could be several in-game days for a regional displacement, **a major multi-week character arc for a central-archive displacement.** Players will end up in zones they hadn't planned to visit, meeting NPCs they wouldn't have encountered, and *that's the point.*

  **Variable distance — what determines where the player is shipped:**

  - **Local archive** (1-2 zones away) — default Catcher disposition. Common case.
  - **Regional archive** (3-5 zones away) — Catchers who specialize in "long-shipment" filing; or the player Forced-Preserved in a zone with no local archive (common in remote tepui slopes and dead-zone borders).
  - **Central archive** (anywhere on the world map) — reserved for *high-value* preservation cases. Triggered by: player faction reputation in Pale Curation (paradoxically, **higher** reputation may mean the Catchers consider you "important enough for the central"); story-relevant moments where the displacement is narratively earned; specific named-Catcher behaviour patterns (Filer Pais — the clinical-and-documentary one — *always* ships to central, considers all preservation worth central archiving).
  - **Honey-jar village larder edge case** — *not all preservation goes through Pale Curation infrastructure.* If the player is Forced-Preserved by a catacomb-village Catcher cell that holds Honey-Sealing tradition, the player may wake up *in a honey-jar in that village's larder*, labeled. The escape from the larder is the comedy-horror peak. The villagers may not realize the player is conscious; some will, with horror or fascination.

  **The preserved-but-active state — mechanical profile.**

  | Mechanic | Effect | Why |
  |---|---|---|
  | Movement | Reduced 50-75% (method-dependent) | Encrusted body is heavy and stiff |
  | Speech | **Disabled** | Mouth sealed — no dialogue with NPCs, only world-log gestures |
  | Vision range | Reduced (eyes partially sealed) | Especially Salt-Cured (crystallized lashes) and Ink-Bathed (ink-saturated cornea) |
  | Combat | Severe damage penalty; armor penalty (you ARE the armor in some sense); attack speed halved | Stiff body, sealed joints |
  | Stealth | **Improved.** NPCs see a Preserved figure and assume it is correctly filed. Most do not notice movement | Pareidolia in reverse — they expect stillness |
  | Carrying capacity | Reduced — body weight now includes preservation mass (salt, ink, honey, etc.) | The preservative is in you |
  | Healing | Partial — most healing items work but at half effect (the body cannot fully restore through encrustation) | Surface tissue is occluded |
  | Faction interactions | Variable — see "How NPCs react" below | The body reads as Preserved |
  | Death | Standard for the RPG — respawn or save-reload per the game's chosen system. No special permadeath state | Death is death; preservation is preservation; they are different states |

  **How NPCs react** — this is where the comedy and horror both live, faction by faction:

  - **Pale Curation officers** (in the chamber and elsewhere) — *do not initially recognize the player as the player.* They see a Preserved figure that has *moved out of its filing position.* They will, with bureaucratic gentleness, **walk over and adjust the player back to their slot.** Repeatedly. With apologies. *"You've drifted, dear; please return to your filing position. You're row 12, slab 7. Yes, that's right. Thank you."* This is intentionally hilarious and intentionally unsettling. After enough drift-and-adjustment cycles, a *senior* Pale Curation officer notices something is wrong and either (a) realizes the player is consciously preserved — **scientifically fascinated**, may quest the player to "remain a specimen" — or (b) reports the irregularity to the Catchers, who arrive to *re-preserve* the player to standard.
  - **The Catcher who preserved them** — initially treats the player as filed-and-finished. The first time the Catcher sees the player walking around, their reaction is the *single most disturbing dialogue in the entry*: *"Oh. **Oh.** I... I had not... I will note this in the file. Don't worry. I'll fix it."* The Catcher believes their job was incomplete. They will attempt to *re-preserve* the player, this time aiming for a stronger preservation tier (often Resin-Cast — the only one that cannot be escaped from).
  - **Other preserved figures** — and this is the design beat that makes the entire system land — *some of them appear to be moving.* Slowly. Between observations. The player who watches a row of slabs for a long time may notice that a figure two slabs over is now in a *slightly different position* than it was an hour ago. **The game does not confirm whether they are also conscious.** The player may try to communicate with them; some respond (in tiny gestures, slow blinks, world-log entries: *"the figure on slab 9 has shifted its left hand toward you"*); some do not. The player may *free* moving preserved figures — un-preserving a fellow Preserved becomes a quest-tier event with major faction consequences.
  - **Choir / Choir Cathedrals** — react with **disinterest bordering on revulsion**. The Choir does not eat preserved bodies efficiently and will not engage a Preserved player. The player who is Forced-Preserved gains effective Choir-passage immunity until they un-preserve. *(Counter-intuitive cross-system interaction: forced preservation is one of the few states that makes the Choir actively avoid you.)*
  - **Driving Bloom** — pathologically interested in the player as a half-or-fully-preserved body. May actively pursue. The Bloom would consider a fully-preserved-but-conscious player a *uniquely valuable host*. **This is the most dangerous adversarial interaction the Preserved player faces.**
  - **Bower-Folk** — see the player and react with *aesthetic delight*. *"You are so close. So close to perfect. Just a little resin, my friend. Please. Please hold still."* Will follow the player around offering Resin-Cast finalization. Persistent and difficult to refuse. The player who accepts becomes Resin-Cast — which **cannot be escaped**, only broken out of by another character (potentially major late-game story content: rescue from a Bower-Folk village display).
  - **Catacomb-villagers** — village-specific responses. Some villages welcome the player with reverence ("*you are an honored guest who has experienced the Pale*"). Some refuse the player entry as ill-omened. Some try to feed the player into their Honey-jar larder. Each village's response is a culture-tell.
  - **Tent-Right wasteland communities** — accept the player under hospitality (the Tent-Right oath does not require living guests; it requires *guests*, which the player technically still is). The Tent-Right may be *uniquely willing to help the player un-preserve*, since they have salt expertise and no faction-political reason to keep the player Preserved.
  - **Saccharine Concord** — *trades with the player as if nothing has changed.* The Concord will sell un-preservation supplies at *premium prices*, knowing the player is desperate. Gestures-only dialogue still works for trading because the Concord's clerks are pragmatic. **The Concord's casual pragmatism in the face of cosmic horror is its own quiet joke.**

  **Un-preservation methods.** The player must reverse the preservation. Each method has different requirements:

  | Preservation method | Un-preservation method | Difficulty |
  |---|---|---|
  | Salt-Cured | Submerge in fresh water for several hours; OR drink large quantities of fresh water over many turns; OR specific Tinker-crafted desiccant-reverser solution | Easy (water exists everywhere — but standing in a stream for hours is a vulnerable activity) |
  | Ink-Bathed | Oil bath; OR repeated applications of specific solvent from a Tinker; OR exposure to direct sunlight for many hours (the iron-gall ink degrades in UV) | Medium (oil and sunlight take time and are conditional on geography) |
  | Honey-Sealed | Salt-water immersion; OR being licked / cleaned by specific creatures (a Bandfrog tongue, a Sima Pricklebrow's persistent cleaning, a tame Cascade-Lacer) — **comedic horror at full bloom**; OR Tinker-crafted dissolving solution | Easy-medium (the comedy of being licked clean by a frog is the design moment; players will share screenshots) |
  | Bog-Taken | Dry in direct sunlight for many days; OR Pale Curation reverse-tannin treatment | Hard (most likely, the player only becomes Bog-Taken voluntarily — Catchers don't throw bog) |
  | Resin-Cast | **Cannot be self-escaped.** Must be broken out by another character — ally NPC, hired specialist, fellow adventurer. The cast is **one-way without external help**. The player remains Resin-Cast until rescued. | Externally-dependent (intentional design choice — Resin-Cast is the **rescue-quest preservation**) |

  **Long-term traces.** Even after un-preservation, the player carries permanent character traces. These are NOT debuffs — they're *character development.*

  - **Salt-Cured aftermath** — slightly pale skin permanently; hair has a faint salt-shimmer in low light; *small permanent constitution boost* (the body that survived being held by salt is harder to kill thereafter). Some NPCs ask, with hesitant respect, "Were you... filed?"
  - **Ink-Bathed aftermath** — fingertips and ear-tips permanently ink-stained; faint black tear-tracks under the eyes; **dialogue option unlocked: the player can now be partially read by Palimpsest scribes** (small `KnowledgePart` boost when interacting with Palimpsest). Inkbound NPCs (if introduced) recognize and treat the player with reverence.
  - **Honey-Sealed aftermath** — skin has a faint sweet scent permanently (perceptible to NPCs in close conversation); attracts insects (small but persistent annoyance); **Bower-Folk reputation boost** (you are *visibly transformed by aesthetic event*).
  - **Bog-Taken aftermath** — leather-toned skin permanently; deep scent of peat in the hair; *Tent-Right respect* (wasteland cultures recognize bog-survival).
  - **Resin-Cast aftermath** — once rescued: a permanent translucent sheen to skin and eyes; **major story-level unique trait** ("the One Who Was Cast And Escaped"); unique dialogue with most factions.

  **Player options after preservation:**

  1. **Escape and un-preserve.** Standard arc. The full content of this entry.
  2. **Remain preserved indefinitely.** A player who likes the stealth + Choir-immunity + bureaucratic-comedy combination may CHOOSE to remain Preserved. The penalties remain; some game content becomes inaccessible (no dialogue, no most-quest-completion); but they can functionally play through significant content as a Preserved figure. Eventually most players will want to un-preserve, but the option is theirs.
  3. **Voluntarily re-preserve later.** After un-preservation, the player can voluntarily seek out preservation again — there are cosmetic / ritual / strategic reasons (Choir-passage immunity is real; some quests require Preserved-form access to specific Pale Curation chambers). The trauma of the first time is the gateway; subsequent preservations are character-chosen.
  4. **Free the other preserved figures.** Long-term quest content. Most preserved figures are *not* conscious (or, per the unresolved ambiguity, the game does not confirm). Some are. Identifying which, and freeing those, becomes a major Pale Curation / Imminent Archive late-game arc.

  **Why this is the right resolution.** Permadeath outcome would have made the Imminent Archive an *unfair adversary* — death by accumulated thrown pots is poor game-feel. The Fully-Preserved-and-Still-Alive outcome:
  - Preserves the cosmic horror (you ARE preserved; the world treats you accordingly; the question of consciousness in others is now unbearably present).
  - Adds rich comedy (Pale Curation officers gently re-filing you, Bandfrogs licking honey off your hand, Bower-Folk persistently offering to finalize the look).
  - Generates an RPG-shaped quest (escape the chamber, find un-preservation, restore yourself) without ending the player's run.
  - Creates a permanent character trace, so the experience *matters* even after un-preservation.
  - Is genuinely memorable — players will tell other players about the time they got preserved alive.

  **Open design questions:**

  - **Voluntary preservation as a player class option.** Should there be a dedicated player-build that uses preservation strategically? "The Pickled" archetype that accepts permanent preserved-state mechanics in exchange for stat-bonus packages? Probably yes for a future expansion; gated heavily.
  - **The other preserved figures' agency.** How many of them are conscious? Should this scale dynamically with player observation (the more time the player spends watching the slabs, the more figures are revealed to be aware)? Probably yes — observation-as-trigger creates a quiet horror loop.
  - **Save handling.** When the player saves while Preserved, the save records the Preserved state. Reloading restores them in their Preserved condition. This is correct behaviour and not a bug — but design must ensure no save-corner-cases lock the player.
  - **Visual representation.** The player's tile while Preserved should look noticeably different from the standard player tile — different color (white for Salt, black for Ink, gold for Honey, brown for Bog, transparent-amber for Resin), possibly a status-glyph overlay. Consistency matters for player legibility.
  - **The Catcher's reaction to the un-preserved player.** A player who has been preserved by a Catcher and *escaped that preservation* now meets that Catcher again later. The Catcher's reaction should be authored: confusion, professional respect, urge to re-preserve, maybe melancholy. Different Catchers have different responses; **Sister Wenil might apologize.**

- **The Wall-Caught — jumping into a Choir-Cathedral sinkhole.** Side note / specific mechanic: when the player jumps (or falls) into a sinkhole identified as a **Choir Cathedral archetype** (per the Sinkholes entry — fully colonized by Rot Choir mycelium, the spores growing tallest where the surface light filters through the lip), they do not die. **The screen goes black** and a single line of text appears:

  > *"Sporilic tendrils catch your fall and you get pulled into the walls of the sinkhole."*

  This is the Choir-mode equivalent of the **Fully Preserved Player** entry's Kenshi-style displacement — but where Pale Curation files you in an archive somewhere on the world map, **the Choir absorbs you into its own substrate.** The player wakes up *embedded in the wall* of a near-pure Choir territory: a sinkhole interior so completely Choir-colonized that the wall, floor, ceiling, and *the player themselves* are partly mycelial-integrated. The player is alive, conscious, partially still themselves. They are not in the same sinkhole they fell into — like the Kenshi displacement, the Choir's tendril-network connects across distant sinkholes, and **the player wakes up wherever the Choir's substrate decided to bring them.** Possibly a different tepui. Possibly the central Choir Cathedral in a major sinkhole the player has never seen.

  **The player's state on waking.**
  - **Choir-Touched** (per the Memory Consumption + encasement entry's standard status) is now permanent, escalated to a higher stack count than ordinary encasement would yield. Visible mycelial sheen on the player's tile color, persistent.
  - **Inventory is reduced.** The Choir's tendrils don't carry possessions kindly. Weapons drop into the substrate during the fall; some are recoverable from the wall around the player, some are lost permanently (consumed by the spores). The player wakes with maybe 20-40% of their previous inventory.
  - **Speech is partially disabled.** Mycelium has grown into the player's mouth and throat. Some dialogue still works (gestures, partial syllables); the world log paraphrases what the player attempts to say with the Choir's substrate filtering it.
  - **A new status — "Wall-Bound."** The Choir is *slowly* incorporating the player into the substrate. This is not the rapid forced-preservation of the Catchers; it is the Choir's patient pace. The Wall-Bound status accumulates over many in-game days. If it reaches 100%, the player is *fully integrated into the Choir* — they cease to be a player-character and become a node in the network. **What happens then is open** (`(open)` per `Lore/00_Canon.md`): possibly a final-ending state, possibly a different play-mode where the player becomes a Choir agent.
  - **Choir-passage immunity.** While Wall-Bound, the Choir does not attack the player. Choir cathedrals are *home.*

  **Escape — four paths.** The player must reduce Wall-Bound to 0 to escape:
  - **Cut yourself out.** Combat-tier damage to the surrounding wall. Painful (self-damage in the process). The combat-aggressive escape; Bloodlust-aligned.
  - **Bargain with the Choir.** The Choir caught you because it is *interested* in you. You can offer a memory — a specific significant memory the Choir would otherwise have to consume to acquire. The Choir releases you in exchange. *Direct integration with the Memory Consumption + encasement system* — the encasement happens *in reverse* (the Choir releases what it has, the player gives what they have, the substrate retreats from the player's flesh). The Inquiry-aligned escape; expensive.
  - **Apatheia practice.** The patient stance recognizes the Choir's pace. A high-Apatheia player can *outlast* the wall-binding — sit still long enough, integrate consciously rather than fight, and the Choir releases them as recognized-as-not-substrate-after-all. The patient escape; slow but free of cost.
  - **Rescue by another.** An ally NPC, a hired specialist, or a fellow adventurer can cut the player out from outside. Most likely a Warden from a hostile-to-Choir catacomb village, or a Pale Curation officer (if the player has high enough reputation). The dependency-on-others escape.

  **Why this works.**
  - It **converts a player misstep into content** rather than a death. The Choir Cathedral sinkhole is a known hazard; the player who chose to jump in is *experiencing the consequence as content*, not punishment.
  - It pairs with the existing **Fully Preserved Player** mechanic — two distinct "you are now displaced and embedded" outcomes from two different factions, each with its own escape grammar. Pale Curation files you; the Choir absorbs you.
  - The **Wall-Bound to 100% = full integration** outcome creates an alternate-mode possibility (the player as Choir agent) that's intentionally left open for Phase-1+ to commit. This is the **third-ending-candidate** thread surfacing — *integration with the Choir* could be one of the three canonical endings.
  - Long-term traces (per the Fully Preserved Player pattern): a player who has been Wall-Caught and escaped carries **a permanent faint mycelial pattern on their skin**, the Choir's spore-thread perceptible by other Choir-Touched characters and by Spirit-of-Inquiry-aligned NPCs. *"You've been in the walls,"* they say. *"What did you tell it?"*

  **Faction implications.**
  - **The Choir** — does not consider this an act of consumption; it considers itself to have *recognized the player as a candidate for inclusion.* The Choir's communications afterward are different — more familiar, less impersonal.
  - **Palimpsest** — disturbed. A player who returns from being Wall-Caught is partially-incorporated-into the Choir's memory; Palimpsest scribes can sometimes *read* the player as an Ink-Bathed substrate (small spore-borne text emerges on the player's skin under specific lighting). Palimpsest may quest the player to *describe what they remembered while in the wall.*
  - **Pale Curation** — alarmed. A Wall-Caught player is *exactly* what Pale Curation considers preservation-emergency. Catchers may target Wall-Caught players preferentially, considering them at high risk.
  - **Driving Bloom** — disturbingly interested. The Wall-Caught player is *almost* Choir-integrated; the Bloom considers them a high-value conversion target.
  - **Catacomb-villagers** — village-by-village. Spore-Wedded villages (Choir-aligned, per the catacomb village design doc) welcome the player with respect. The Quiet (hostile isolated) refuses entry permanently. Others vary.
  - **Bower-Folk** — fascinated by the aesthetic. A faint-mycelial-patterned skin is *beautiful* in their reading.

- **Carnivorous-plant digestive enzymes — resource brainstorm.** Append to the broader Drosera / carnivorous-plant content (full design in [`sarisarinama_drosera_design.md`](sarisarinama_drosera_design.md)). The plants' **digestive fluid** — the proteases, RNases, phosphatases, esterases, and antimicrobial peptides secreted alongside the sticky mucilage to break down trapped insects — is a real biological substance with **specific dissolving and antimicrobial properties.** Rotundifolia-genus extracts have been used in real European traditional medicine for respiratory ailments. The enzymes are *biologically distinct from* the sticky mucilage (which is the trapping agent); the enzymes are *what comes after the trap* — the slow external digestion. They are a *separate harvestable resource* from mucilage, with different properties and different storage requirements.

  This entry brainstorms uses across the world and lore. The user's framing: *"I haven't figured out the use yet."* This is a generative pass — propose many directions, identify the strongest, leave the rest as authoring options.

  **Real biological grounding.** Carnivorous-plant digestive enzymes:
  - **Break down protein** (proteases) — soft tissue, muscle, skin, fungal cell walls (indirectly)
  - **Break down RNA / DNA** (ribonucleases) — cellular contents
  - **Break down phospholipids** (phospholipases) — cell membranes
  - **Antimicrobial peptides** — actively kill bacteria and some fungi
  - **Are produced continuously** while the trap is active, in small volumes per plant
  - **Are species-specific** in chemistry — Drosera-genus enzymes differ from Nepenthes pitcher-plant enzymes differ from Venus flytrap enzymes — and CoO can lean into this with species-specific applications

  ## Cluster A — Anti-preservation uses (load-bearing for the existing preservation system)

  This is the cluster I'd argue is **strongest** because it directly resolves a pre-existing system's open mechanic.

  - **Un-preservation reagent.** The Fully Preserved Player entry mentions "Tinker-crafted dissolving solution" for un-preservation — *carnivorous-plant digestive enzymes are exactly that solution.* Specifically:
    - **Salt-Cured un-preservation** — enzyme-water bath softens the salt-encrusted skin and allows it to flush; faster than plain water alone.
    - **Ink-Bathed un-preservation** — enzyme-oil suspension breaks down the iron-gall ink's protein bindings and lets the ink flush from tissue.
    - **Honey-Sealed un-preservation** — enzymes break down the antimicrobial polysaccharide structure; the honey can then be salt-water-flushed.
    - **Bog-Taken un-preservation** — strongest application; enzymes can reverse the tannin-binding of bog-preservation, freeing skin from leather-state.
    - **Resin-Cast** — the enzymes do *not* dissolve hardened resin (real biology: resin is largely terpenoid, not protein). Cannot help here. Resin-Cast still needs external rescue.
  - **Anti-Preserved combat coating.** Weapons coated in concentrated enzyme deal bonus damage to **The Preserved** (NPCs in the world who have been preserved long-term — Pale Curation officers' specimens that walk, hostile preserved figures the player encounters in cosmic-horror set-pieces). The enzymes break down what was *intended to last centuries* in seconds of contact. Pale Curation considers enzyme-coated weapons *abominations* — using one in a Pale Curation archive is a faction-rep catastrophe.
  - **The Catcher's antithesis.** Catchers (per the Imminent Archive entry) carry preservation pots; an *enzyme-coated weapon* is the structural opposite — it un-preserves. A player carrying an enzyme-flask can dissolve a Catcher's thrown salt-pot mid-air (intercept mechanic), or coat themselves in dilute enzyme to reduce Being-Preserved status accumulation in real time.

  ## Cluster B — Anti-Choir / anti-mycelial uses

  - **Anti-Choir weapon coating.** Choir mycelium has fungal cell walls; Drosera proteases struggle directly with chitin but the antimicrobial peptides do real damage. **Enzyme-coated weapons are a niche but real Choir-counter.** The Choir's reaction to enzyme-bearing players is *interesting* — they don't avoid them like they avoid Choir-Iron carriers; they *examine* them. The enzymes that hurt the Choir are *also produced by another non-Choir distributed life-form (the plants),* which the Choir reads as tribal kinship.
  - **Anti-Driving-Bloom field treatment.** The Driving Bloom's spore network is uniquely vulnerable to phospholipase enzymes. **Enzyme-spraying** (a Tinker-crafted misting tool) over a Bloomed area can *un-Bloom* infected NPCs in their early-stage. Catacomb-villagers cultivate Drosera *and* harvest enzyme reserves as the canonical Driving-Bloom defense. The plants intercept the spores; the enzymes treat the already-infected.

  ## Cluster C — Crafting / industrial uses

  - **Tanning / leatherwork.** Real-world parallel: papain and bromelain are commercially used as protein-tenderizing agents. Carnivorous-plant enzymes can soften hides, prepare leather, ready vellum/parchment for inscription. **Tinker craft category:** enzyme-treated leather goods have superior properties (suppleness, drape, take-ink-better) at a high enzyme-cost. Inkbound (if introduced) consider enzyme-prepared parchment the only worthy substrate for the highest-tier scriptorium work.
  - **Vellum preparation.** Specific Inkbound parchment-grade requires enzyme-treatment to remove residual organics from the skin substrate.
  - **Ink-component in dew-ink.** The bioluminescent dew-ink (per the Drosera design file) is enriched with enzymes for self-illuminating texts — the enzymes provide longer-lasting glow.

  ## Cluster D — Medicinal uses

  - **Wound debridement.** Real-world parallel: maggot therapy uses similar enzymatic action. Enzyme application to a necrotic wound *eats the necrotic tissue without harming live tissue* (the live tissue is protected by intact cell barriers; the dead tissue is open substrate). **Surface-village medics use enzyme paste for hard-to-treat wounds.** A surprisingly effective and gross-feeling healing item.
  - **Cough / respiratory remedy.** Real-world parallel: *Drosera rotundifolia* extract has historical use in European traditional medicine for coughs. CoO can use this directly — *Drosera Tonic* as a respiratory-complaint cure with mild antimicrobial properties.
  - **Anti-fungal topical.** Useful against Choir-spore exposure, against early-stage Driving-Bloom infection, against catacomb-fungal foot-rot.

  ## Cluster E — Body disposal / forensic uses

  - **Crime: making a body disappear.** Concentrated enzyme application *dissolves a body completely* over many hours. **No remains for Re-Membering.** No bones for tomb-plaque inscription. No memory left in flesh for the Choir to harvest. **The Palimpsest considers enzyme-dissolution of an unwilling body the gravest possible crime against memory.** The Concord, predictably, sells the service quietly.
  - **Anti-Re-Membering weapon.** The Re-Membering ritual (per the existing entry) requires a body or substantial body-part to focus on. Enzyme-treatment of a corpse before Palimpsest can recover it *eliminates the Re-Membering option permanently.* This becomes a horror beat in story: the player who fails to recover an ally's body in time finds it has been enzyme-treated by a hostile faction.
  - **Pale Curation paradox.** Pale Curation are preservers, but they sometimes need to *dispose of* poorly-filed specimens — entries that have decayed past viability, mistakes, scandals. *Carnivorous-plant enzymes are their tool.* A Pale Curation officer carrying enzyme vials is doing *anti-preservation work* on Pale Curation's behalf — visible cognitive dissonance to other observers, internally consistent to the officer. **This paradox is one of the strongest internal Pale Curation politics hooks in the world.**

  ## Cluster F — Ritual / cosmological uses

  - **Inkbound substrate-prep.** The Inkbound treat enzyme-application to a skin-page-in-preparation as one stage of the sacred ink-bath ritual.
  - **Choir's substrate prep.** *Disturbing.* The Choir does not always want to consume immediately; sometimes it wants the body *softened* for slow integration. Choir cathedrals occasionally use Drosera enzymes as *a preparatory step before encasement.* The encasement ritual on a partially-enzyme-treated body is faster and more thorough — the Choir's tendrils penetrate already-broken tissue immediately. **A Drosera-enzyme bottle in a Choir cathedral is among the more disturbing single objects the player might encounter.** It implies a working relationship between Choir and plant kingdom that nobody ever explained.
  - **Spirit of Apatheia ritual cleansing.** Enzymes' slow patient action makes them an Apatheia-aligned ritual ingredient. Apatheia practitioners use dilute enzyme-baths for ritual purification (a multi-day soak that gradually purges the body of accumulated grime, scars, residual preservation traces — slow and complete).
  - **Body-reading reagent.** The Palimpsest applies enzymes to **Ink-Bathed corpses** to soften them for reading. The enzyme allows the scribe to gently pry the ink-saturated skin open like pages without tearing. **Ink-Bathed corpses without enzyme-treatment can only be read once, with damage; with enzyme-treatment, they can be read many times across centuries.** This makes enzymes structurally critical to the Palimpsest's long-term archival capacity.

  ## Cluster G — Player consumable / horror uses

  - **Drink it.** A bottle of digestive enzyme is *technically* drinkable in small quantities. Effects: severe stomach pain, vomiting, mild antimicrobial effect on gut parasites, a small amount of self-digestion that the body can tolerate. Drinking a *full flask*: severe self-digestion, internal damage, dangerous. **A Spirit-of-Bloodlust-aligned dark-bargain item:** the player can drink enzyme to gain a brief but significant combat damage bonus (the body is operating in panic-survival mode) at the cost of long-term internal injury. *Comedic-horror tier mechanic.*
  - **Self-administration during forced preservation.** A player who is being forcibly preserved by Catchers (per the Imminent Archive entry) can apply enzyme to themselves in real time to slow the Being-Preserved counter accumulation. **Enzyme-as-emergency-aid** during a Catcher attack — but the enzyme stings, leaves visible patches on skin, reduces healing rate temporarily.

  ## Faction stances (synthesizing across clusters)

  - **Rot Choir** — *Reluctantly tolerates and quietly uses.* The enzymes hurt Choir mycelium but enable better encasement-prep; the Choir reads the plants as kindred-but-not-allies; the relationship is uneasy.
  - **Driving Bloom** — Active enemy. Enzyme-spray un-Blooms infected NPCs.
  - **Palimpsest** — *Sacred ingredient* for Ink-Bathed-corpse softening; *gravest crime* when used to dissolve a body before recovery. The internal contradiction is central to Palimpsest internal politics.
  - **Pale Curation** — *Paradox.* Use enzymes for poorly-filed-specimen disposal; loathe enzymes used on properly-filed specimens. Officers carry enzyme vials reluctantly.
  - **Saccharine Concord** — Trades the entire spectrum. Sells enzyme to medics, scribes, Catchers, criminals, Choir cultists, all without comment. The Concord's gray-market discreet-disposal service is an enzyme-driven business.
  - **Bower-Folk** — Use enzymes to *clean* their preserved aesthetic installations periodically — gentle enzyme baths that remove dust and resurface the figure's beauty. Each cleaning is a quasi-ritual; a Bower-Folk village's *Cleaner* is a high-status role.
  - **Catacomb-villagers** — Cultivate Drosera and harvest enzymes as canonical Driving-Bloom defense; use medically; trade with surface for materials they can't grow.
  - **Tent-Right wasteland** — Wound-debridement use; enzyme is one of the few medicinal supplies that travels well; trade-good with surface villages.
  - **Inkbound (if introduced)** — Sacred substrate-prep ingredient; required for highest-tier scriptorium parchment.
  - **Spirit of Apatheia** — Ritual cleansing ingredient (slow, patient, complete).
  - **Spirit of Inquiry** — Body-reading practice ingredient (Palimpsest-adjacent).
  - **Spirit of Bloodlust** — Self-administration as combat-bonus dark bargain.
  - **Imminent Archive Catchers** — *Loathe.* Enzymes un-do their work. A player carrying enzyme is recognizable to Catchers as *theologically unaligned* and may receive different (more aggressive? more fearful?) treatment.

  ## Open design questions

  - **Scarcity calibration.** Enzymes are produced in *small volumes* per Drosera plant. Should harvest be plant-destroying (kill the Drosera, get all enzymes) or plant-tending (small volume per visit; sustainable; long arc)? Probably tier it: small volume per visit is sustainable; full extraction destroys the plant and yields more, but reduces local Drosera population. Greatdews are protected (per Drosera entry); enzyme-extraction from a Greatdew is a major faction crime.
  - **Species-specific enzymes.** Should the six Drosera species (per the Drosera design file) have different enzyme profiles? *Probably yes.* *D. magnifica* (Greatdew) yields the most potent enzymes; *D. meristocaulis* (Pebble-Sundew) yields the most-volumetric-per-plant; *D. spiralis* (Spiral-Bloom) yields enzymes best for Inkbound substrate-prep. Each species becomes mechanically distinct as a harvest target.
  - **Storage and stability.** Enzymes degrade with time and exposure to heat / light / dry conditions. Storage requires specific containers (bone-vials, cool-cellar storage, enzyme-stable preservation media). The supply chain becomes a meaningful logistics consideration.
  - **Enzymes from non-Drosera sources.** Real biology has many carnivorous plants — pitcher plants (Nepenthes), Venus flytraps (Dionaea), butterworts (Pinguicula), bladderworts (Utricularia). CoO could introduce additional carnivorous-plant species with species-specific enzyme profiles, expanding the design space. *Probably defer beyond v1*; the Drosera enzymes alone are a substantial design space.
  - **The Catcher-enzyme conflict.** A formal mechanic where Catchers and enzyme-users have hostile recognition: should this be coded into the AI behaviour explicitly? *Probably yes* — the political opposition is too central to the design to leave to player imagination.

- **Stones with stake — minerals as relics, infusions, and faction currency.** A foundational system: certain minerals and rocks in CoO carry *real mechanical and political weight.* Some grant **enhanced magic** when held; some can be **infused into weapons** for permanent properties; some give **passive effects** by mere possession; some are **faction-coveted** for reasons that don't always reduce to "they're valuable." Stone is the world's slowest currency — it doesn't decay, doesn't merge, doesn't run out across a run, but it's also rare, heavy, and *every faction wants different ones for incompatible reasons.* The mineral economy operates underneath the drams economy and the ink economy in time-scale and gravitas: drams move daily, ink moves per-rental, **stones move per-life.**

  **Three categories of mineral function.**

  | Category | What it does | Example real-world mineral inspiration |
  |---|---|---|
  | **Passive** (carried) | Possession alone grants ongoing effect — sight in dark, resistance to a damage type, NPC reaction modifier, faction-rep adjustment in specific zones. | Magnetite (magnetism), fluorite (UV-glow), amber (preservation), pyrite (deception/fool's-gold connotation) |
  | **Infused** (forged into) | Permanently altered weapons / armor / tools that retain stone properties after the stone is consumed in the forging. | Iron-gall pigment in inks, obsidian edges in weapons, jade as material for ritual blades |
  | **Architectural** (built with) | Chambers / structures built with specific stones inherit those stones' properties at the cell-level. A salt-walled vault preserves; a glow-quartz chamber illuminates passively. | Salt mine architecture, alabaster temple-light, lapis-lined chambers |

  **Named in-game minerals** (proposed, with mechanical sketches):

  - ***Tepuibone*** *(also: Stump-Stone)* — sandstone *from the Root of the World itself*, or from real petrified-root layers in any tepui. Cosmologically the heaviest stone in the world. **Passive:** carrying a Tepuibone fragment makes the player resistant to Urqu's manifestation effects (the stone *remembers what the Tree was* and quietly opposes the felling-aftermath). **Infused:** weapons forged with Tepuibone deal bonus damage to Urqu-aligned fauna (Sari-Snakes, Sky-Saris) and to Driving Bloom infestations. **Architectural:** chambers built with Tepuibone are immune to Urqu-extinguishing-bargain effects. **Faction stake:** every faction wants it. Rarest stone in the game.

  - ***Choir-Iron*** — iron-rich ore, often pyrite-veined. Real biological grounding: high-iron substrates are genuinely *toxic* to many fungi (iron's redox chemistry interferes with mycelial enzymes). **Passive:** carrying Choir-Iron reduces local Choir spore density (Spirit-of-Inquiry-affinity yield from successful spore-suppression observation). **Infused:** weapons resist Choir colonization (a Choir-Iron-edged blade left in a Choir cathedral does not get consumed); armor reduces "Bloomed" status duration from Driving Bloom infections. **Architectural:** Choir-Iron-walled chambers are reliable safe-rooms in Choir-Cathedral sinkhole archetypes. **Faction stake:** Pale Curation hoards it (preservation-aligned); Catacomb-villagers cultivate small stockpiles for emergency use; the Choir actively avoids Choir-Iron-rich zones (a navigational mechanic).

  - ***Sari-Pyrite*** — golden-flecked iron-pyrite ore that **resonates faintly with the *sari... sari...* sonic signature** when Urqu is locally active. Real-world grounding: pyrite is the "fool's gold" — it *looks* valuable but isn't (in non-magical contexts). **Passive:** carrying Sari-Pyrite gives the player early warning of Urqu manifestation periods (the stone hums softly hours before larger world-state changes appear). **Infused:** weapons have a small chance to *amplify* damage during active Urqu periods — a dark bargain (you accept Urqu's local advantage, the weapon takes advantage of it). **Architectural:** rarely built with — Sari-Pyrite walls turn entire chambers into Urqu-attractive zones. **Faction stake:** Urqu's mouthpieces seek it (the resonance is welcoming to them); the Saccharine Concord trades it cynically (a profitable trade good *because* most buyers don't understand the long-term consequence of carrying it).

  - ***Mute-Stone*** — dense basaltic-analog rock that absorbs sound. Real-world parallel: certain volcanic rocks (vesicular basalt, pumice composites) are genuinely sound-absorbent. **Passive:** carrying Mute-Stone reduces noise the player generates while moving (stealth bonus). **Infused:** weapons forged with Mute-Stone deal stunning bonuses (silence-strike); the stone literally absorbs the *sound* of impact. **Architectural:** Mute-Stone chambers are perfect for Apatheia-aligned meditation (the silence is *real*, not metaphorical); they are also perfect Held Breath / blackmail safehouses (no one outside hears what's said inside). **Faction stake:** Apatheia practitioners revere it; Held Breath / Inkbound use it strategically; **Urqu loathes it** — the *sari... sari...* signature does not propagate through Mute-Stone, which makes Mute-Stone-walled rooms one of the few Urqu-immune locations in the world.

  - ***Glow-Quartz*** — translucent quartz that fluoresces under bioluminescent wavelengths. **Passive:** carrying Glow-Quartz amplifies the radius of any bio-light source the player carries (one extra cell of illumination per fragment). **Infused:** quartz-tipped lantern-rods extend bio-light range substantially; quartz-tinged armor shimmers visibly even in dim catacombs (combat penalty: less stealth) but provides illumination for allies. **Architectural:** Glow-Quartz veins in chamber walls turn entire rooms into low-cost permanent lighting infrastructure (catacomb-villagers' highest aspiration is a Glow-Quartz-veined chamber). **Faction stake:** Catacomb-villagers covet it; Bower-Folk love the aesthetic; the Saccharine Concord runs the largest Glow-Quartz mine on the world map.

  - ***Memory-Marble*** — fine-grained limestone-analog that **takes inscribed text and holds it for centuries** at standard reading-light intensity. The Palimpsest's preferred substrate. **Passive:** carrying inscribed Memory-Marble fragments grants a small Palimpsest reputation bonus. **Infused:** Memory-Marble-pommeled weapons hold inscribed history (a player who kills a faction leader with a Memory-Marble blade can have the killing-blow's narrative recorded *into the weapon* — creating a unique relic-weapon that grows in lore weight per kill, with corresponding faction-reputation and narrative consequences). **Architectural:** Memory-Marble vault chambers are the canonical Palimpsest archive substrate. **Faction stake:** Palimpsest reveres; Inkbound (if introduced) considers it the most sacred writing surface; the Choir resents it (Memory-Marble holds memory the Choir would rather eat); **Urqu fears it specifically** — the Re-Membering ritual requires Memory-Marble fragments as a structural component, making Memory-Marble the canonical *anti-Urqu material* alongside Tepuibone.

  - ***Bower-Lapis*** *(working name)* — a deep blue stone with golden flecks. Real-world inspiration: lapis lazuli (Afghan-origin, used historically in ultramarine pigment; culturally significant in many traditions — *cultural-responsibility caveat applies, see ledger entry below*). **Passive:** Bower-Folk reputation bonus; minor Inquiry-affinity yield from carrying. **Infused:** Bower-Lapis-set weapons are aesthetic objects first, weapons second — they deal slightly less damage but function as **Bower-Folk dialogue currency** (offering one to a Bower-Folk leader is a major reputation event). **Architectural:** Bower-Lapis-tiled chambers are reserved for the highest-status Bower-Folk installations. **Faction stake:** Bower-Folk's primary aesthetic mineral; Saccharine Concord sells volume; **Pale Curation refuses to file it** (it's *too aesthetic*, considered the antonym of preservation-utility).

  - ***Pale-Salt*** — crystalline salt deposits, often from inland salt-flat zones (real-world: Salar de Uyuni-style geology). **Passive:** carrying Pale-Salt prevents organic matter in inventory from spoiling (rented food, harvested mucilage, lantern-beetle larvae stay fresh longer). **Infused:** Pale-Salt-edged weapons inflict bonus damage on Driving Bloom and undead-tier enemies (salt as preservation/desiccation mechanic). **Architectural:** Pale-Salt chambers preserve everything: bodies, tomes, tools. The most ancient catacomb plaques are housed in Pale-Salt vaults. **Faction stake:** Pale Curation's primary mineral (preservation-aligned); the Choir actively avoids Pale-Salt (it interferes with mycelial growth); Tent-Right wasteland cultures harvest Pale-Salt as their most reliable trade-good with surface villages.

  - ***Black-Gall*** — iron-and-tannin-rich pigment-stone, ground for ink. Real-world grounding: iron-gall ink was the primary writing ink for ~1,500 years and is genuinely *corrosive over centuries* (which is part of its character). **Passive:** carrying Black-Gall fragments grants minor Palimpsest / Inkbound reputation bonus. **Infused:** Black-Gall-stained weapons ignore some armor (corrosive properties); but the weapon itself slowly degrades over many uses (the corrosion is *real*). **Architectural:** Black-Gall-walled chambers are *not preservative* — they slowly corrode their own contents. The Palimpsest specifically does NOT build with Black-Gall despite using it for ink. **Faction stake:** Palimpsest / Inkbound for ink; nobody for architecture.

  - ***Spore-Block*** *(working name)* — porous volcanic rock that physically *blocks* spore propagation through cell-level porosity. **Passive:** carrying Spore-Block reduces airborne fungal damage (Driving Bloom spores, Choir aggression). **Infused:** weapons retain anti-spore aura. **Architectural:** Spore-Block walls are the canonical anti-Driving-Bloom defense for a settlement (paired with cultivated Drosera rings — the structural defense + the biological defense together). **Faction stake:** Catacomb-villagers' second-most-coveted mineral after Glow-Quartz; the Choir tolerates it (it doesn't kill them, just blocks them); the Driving Bloom cannot abide it — Spore-Block-walled rooms are 100% Bloom-safe.

  **Mechanical hooks (cross-cutting):**

  - **Acquisition gradient.** Surface lowlands have common minerals (iron-bearing rock, basic Pale-Salt veins). Tepui slopes have rarer ones. Sinkhole/catacomb depths have the rarest (Memory-Marble in Sealed-Library archetype catacombs; Tepuibone fragments inside Sima Mayor itself, near the First Root).
  - **Refining process.** Raw mineral → cut/polished → infusable → architectural-grade. Each tier requires a Tinker / specialized NPC and adds value. *Glow-Quartz* in raw form is barely luminous; cut and polished, it's significantly brighter; architectural-grade Glow-Quartz lights a chamber.
  - **Combinatorial crafting.** Pairs of minerals interact during infusion. **Choir-Iron + Pale-Salt** is a stable *anti-Choir* combination. **Sari-Pyrite + Mute-Stone** is *unstable* — combining them reportedly produces a one-in-a-thousand artifact of unknown function (final-act lore). **Tepuibone + Memory-Marble** is the canonical Re-Membering-ritual material combination.
  - **Combat infusion.** A weapon can hold one or two infusions; trying to add a third destroys the weapon. Strategic inventory management around which infusion goes where.
  - **Building integration.** Chamber-construction in a settlement uses the local mineral palette. A village's wall-stone palette tells the player a lot about its faction alignment before any dialogue starts.
  - **Decay and corruption.** Some minerals corrupt over time (Black-Gall corrodes its own contents). Some absorb context (Memory-Marble takes on the resonance of the chamber it sits in). This gives long-running character to specific stone-pieces — *the Memory-Marble fragment that has sat in a Choir cathedral for decades carries traces of every consumption that happened there.*

  **Open design questions:**
  - **Mineral ASCII representation.** How are minerals shown on the map? Inventory-only items, world-map nodes (mining sites), or rendered cells? Probably tier it: most minerals are inventory items found in mining-cells; some (Glow-Quartz veins in walls) render as part of the cell tile.
  - **Acquisition mechanic.** Is mineral collection a separate skill / activity (mining), or do minerals appear opportunistically in zones? Probably both — the player can mine deliberately at known mineral-cells, and minerals also drop from certain enemies and faction-quest rewards.
  - **Refinement sub-system.** Do players refine minerals themselves (Tinker-aligned skill) or pay NPC specialists? Probably yes-yes — basic refining is a skill the player learns; advanced grades require faction specialists and pay reputation costs alongside drams.
  - **Inflation control.** Stones don't decay (mostly) — over a long run, the player will accumulate them. Counter-mechanics: weight (carrying many stones is heavy), faction caps (you can only sell so many to each faction before saturation), strategic infusion as the actual sink (one weapon takes one infusion, the stone is consumed).
  - **Lore-significant unique stones.** Are there *specific named stones* with personalities? Probably yes — the **First-Root-Chip** is a single unique Tepuibone fragment with cosmological weight. Acquiring it is a final-act milestone. Other unique stones could include the **Singing Pyrite** (a cursed Sari-Pyrite associated with a specific lost expedition), the **Bower-Lapis Crown** (pre-cataclysm artifact), etc.
  - **Cultural-source caveats.** Real-world minerals carry real cultural weight (lapis lazuli in Afghan / Egyptian / Renaissance traditions; turquoise in Pueblo / Persian; jade in Chinese / Mesoamerican; cinnabar in Chinese / Roman; obsidian in Aztec). The in-game stones are deliberately *fictional* (Tepuibone, Memory-Marble, etc.) but real-world inspirations should be documented per the running ledger principle. **Strongest recommendation:** do not name any in-game stone "Lapis," "Jade," "Turquoise," or "Obsidian" — the cultural baggage is real and the borrows should be structural-only with CoO-native names.

## Cultural Sources & Credits — running ledger

This appendix records every real-world cultural, linguistic, geographical, or biological source the IDEAS entries above borrow from. The intent: keep credits **visible and maintainable**, give future authors a single place to check *"have we borrowed from X? what stance did we take?"*, and make ship-credit extraction straightforward when entries get implemented.

**Principle.** When an IDEAS entry borrows from a real culture, language, geography, or biology, **add an entry to this ledger at the same time**. Borrowings should be visible, scoped, and credited — never absorbed silently. Entries that draw only from CoO-internal lore (Choir, Palimpsest, Pale Curation, Concord, etc.) are not listed here.

**Format.** Per IDEAS entry that borrows: source(s), what was used, the authoring stance taken, and any required follow-up (research before authoring in-game text, naming caveats, etc.).

### Gin Frogs

- **Biological source:** ***Stefania ginesi*** (Hylidae, Anura) — a real South American back-brooding frog, found on tepuis in Venezuela.
- **What was used:** anatomical inspiration (the back-brood — females of the genus carry eggs and direct-developing froglets in/on dorsal skin); species epithet contracted into the in-game name "Gin Frogs."
- **Stance:** real species cited explicitly in design notes; in-game name is a contraction of the binomial. **Biology only — no cultural attribution required.**
- **Follow-up:** the genus *Stefania* contains ~20 species, each available as a real biological hook for variant Gin Frogs (desert / deep-cave / Choir-tended) keying off real epithets. Cite the source species when naming variants.

### Root of the World

- **Cultural source:** the **Pemon people** of the Guiana Highlands (Venezuela / Guyana / Brazil). Specifically the cosmology around **Mount Roraima** as the petrified stump of *Wazaka*, the Tree of Life, felled by *Makunaima* the trickster — and the *mawari* spirits the Pemon associate with tepuis.
- **Geographical source:** the **tepuis** of Bolívar State, Venezuela (table-top sandstone mountains, billion-year-old, with endemic ecosystems).
- **What was used:** the structural shape of the cosmology — felled cosmic tree → surviving as sacred mountain stump → root that sleeps beneath; tepui geology as the geographical grammar of the central boss site.
- **Stance:** **inherit the structural shape, do NOT lift Pemon names** (Wazaka, Makunaima, mawari) into in-game text. CoO's own factions provide the in-fiction vocabulary.
- **Follow-up required:** read primary Pemon ethnography (not Wikipedia) before authoring any in-game lore touching this material. Credit the Pemon people in shipped notes/credits regardless of whether names appear in-game.

### Sinkholes

- **Geographical sources:** real *simas* of the Venezuelan tepuis — **Sima Humboldt** and **Sima Mayor de Sarisariñama** (on Cerro Sarisariñama), **Sima Aonda** (on Auyán-tepui), **Sima de la Lluvia**, and others.
- **Cultural sources (general, not tradition-specific in v1):** Indigenous Amazonian cosmologies (Pemon, Ye'kuana, Yanomami, and others) in which sinkholes can be read as portals to other worlds, sites of spiritual authority, or dwellings of named spirits.
- **What was used:** physical archetype (vertical pocket biomes carved into highlands), endemic-floor concept, and the cosmological framing that sinkholes are *sites of moral significance*, not just dungeons.
- **Stance:** **inherit structural intuitions**, drop real names of sites and traditions, do real reading on the source cosmologies before authoring any in-game lore that touches them.
- **Follow-up required:** if any sinkhole archetype's lore gets authored as drawing from a *specific* Indigenous tradition (rather than the general physical archetype), that tradition must be researched in primary sources and credited at ship time.

### Catacombs

- **Cultural sources:** the **Paris catacombs** (limestone quarries repurposed as ossuaries from 1786 onward), **Roman early-Christian catacombs**, the **Capuchin Crypt** (Rome) decorative bone arrangements, and the layered necropoles of long-occupied historical settlements generally.
- **What was used:** physical archetype (chambered burial networks built into pre-existing geology), generations-deep interment as compressed historical record, the political/theological role of *where* one chooses to be buried.
- **Stance:** European Christian catacomb practices are **well-documented and lower-risk** to reference. The broader principle of burial-as-political-archive is widespread across cultures.
- **Authoring caution:** specific funerary practices from any tradition (Catholic, Islamic, Jewish, Indigenous Amazonian, Egyptian, Etruscan, etc.) should be **researched before borrowing** — *don't* invent fake burial practices that read as a real culture's. CoO's own factions provide in-fiction funerary vocabulary.

### Catacomb-villagers

- **Fictional inspiration (visual only):** the **Falmer** of *The Elder Scrolls V: Skyrim* (Bethesda Game Studios). Note this category is distinct from real-world cultural sources — the Falmer are an invented fantasy race, so this is a **genre-fiction tribute / borrow**, not a cultural appropriation concern. Recorded here per the running-ledger principle that *all* borrowings should be visible, not because cultural credit is required.
- **Biological inspirations (independent grounding for the morphology):** real cave-evolved species exhibiting convergent adaptations — depigmentation, eye reduction, sensory enhancement of other organs. Examples: **blind cavefish** (*Astyanax mexicanus* cave-form, *Amblyopsis spelaea*), **Texas blind salamander** (*Eurycea rathbuni*), **olm** (*Proteus anguinus*), troglobitic crustaceans, cave-adapted spiders. Biological only; no cultural attribution required. Standard real-species reference treatment, like Gin Frogs / *Stefania ginesi* above.
- **What was used:** morphology (pale, near-eyeless, tall-stooped, long-fingered, long-eared, chitin-armored) drawn from the Falmer visual; biological plausibility for the morphology drawn from real cave-evolved species.
- **What was NOT used / explicitly inverted:** the Falmer's *behavior* in Skyrim (hostile, primitive, monstrous apex creatures). CoO's catacomb-villagers are **passive toward the player by default, civilized, run settlements lit by their own bioluminescence.** The borrow is intentionally limited to visual/morphological identity.
- **Stance:** acknowledge the Falmer source openly in any shipped credits or codex entry. The behavioral inversion is itself a load-bearing design move and should be documented as such; future authoring should preserve the inversion (the apex-predator variant exists as a fallback only).
- **Follow-up:** if specific real species get adopted as canonical biological references for the people's morphology, cite them in shipped credits with the same treatment as *Stefania ginesi* under Gin Frogs.

### Urqu

- **Linguistic source:** **Quechua** (Andean language family). The word *urqu* (also *urcu*, *urku*) means "mountain" or "male." Selected by translating the Spanish prefix *Cerro* in *Cerro Sarisariñama* into Quechua.
- **Conceptual source:** **Ye'kuana** (Cariban-language Indigenous people of Venezuela) legend that **an evil spirit inhabits caves on Cerro Sarisariñama, devouring humans, with the sonic signature *"sari... sari..."*** — which is the etymological origin of the mountain's own name.
- **Geographical source:** **Cerro Sarisariñama**, 2,300m tepui in Jaua-Sarisariñama National Park at the far south-west of Bolívar State, Venezuela. The same tepui that hosts the Sinkholes entry's canonical *simas*.
- **What was used:** the *name* (from Quechua), the *evil-spirit-in-caves concept and "sari... sari..." sonic signature* (from Ye'kuana legend), the *thematic linkage* between the named entity and the canonical sinkhole tepui (from real geography). Urqu's cosmological role (opposed to the Root) is CoO-original — invented, not borrowed.
- **Stance:** **two distinct Indigenous sources are layered** — Quechua (Andean) for the name; Ye'kuana (Cariban, Venezuelan) for the concept. - **Follow-up required:** acknowledge **both source cultures** explicitly in any shipped credits or codex entry. Renaming Urqu before ship is *optional* — borrowing the name is itself a real act of source-honoring — but a CoO-native or Ye'kuana-onomatopoeic name (*Sari-Sari*, *the Stone-Below*, *the Throat*) remains defensible if a future authoring pass prefers it.

### Many-Trunk

### Tent-Right

- **Cultural source (living tradition):** **Bedouin** peoples of the Arabian peninsula and North Africa — particularly the codes of *ḍayf* (guest), *dakhīla* (right of protection), and the broader Arab/Islamic ethical framework around guest-host obligation.
- **What was used:** the structural concept of *inviolable protection-of-guests in a hostile environment*; the three-day window; the host's binding oath to defend with violence if necessary.
- **Stance:** **inherit structure, do NOT lift Arabic vocabulary.** Do not use *ḍayf*, *dakhīla*, *muhammad*, *bayt*, or other Arabic terms in in-game text. Do not depict the Bedouin or Arab peoples in a way that flattens them or treats their ethical framework as exotic. CoO's wasteland culture provides its own in-fiction vocabulary. The structural insight — that protection-of-guests is one of humanity's deepest ethical innovations — is what's worth honoring. Acknowledge the Bedouin source explicitly in shipped credits.
- **Follow-up required:** read primary sources on Bedouin hospitality codes (ethnographic and historical) before authoring any in-game text touching this material. Lower-stakes than Pemon/Ye'kuana / Quechua sourcing because the borrow is conceptual rather than tied to specific cosmology, but care is still warranted.

### The Fully Preserved Player (geographic-displacement design pattern)

- **Fictional inspiration:** **Kenshi** (Lo-Fi Games) — kidnapping mechanic where defeated characters wake up in cages far from their last location, stripped of equipment, and must escape, recover, and navigate back. The game-design pattern of *"displacement-as-defeat-consequence with the journey home as content"* is the structural inspiration for the Fully Preserved Player's long-way-home arc. *Fictional inspiration, not real-world cultural source* — recorded here per the running-ledger principle that all borrowings should be visible. Like the Falmer borrow under Catacomb-villagers, this is a genre-tribute / borrow, not a cultural-appropriation concern.
- **What was used:** the *displacement* pattern (you wake up far from where you were defeated, with nothing, and the journey back is the actual content); the *equipment-stripping* pattern (your stuff is somewhere, recoverable but anchored); the *recovery-arc* pattern (the world has continued without you and you must re-engage with it under reduced circumstances).
- **Stance:** acknowledge Kenshi as inspiration in shipped credits. The CoO Fully Preserved Player implementation is mechanically distinct (preservation is the consequence, not imprisonment; the stealth-improvement-while-preserved + the comedy-horror tonal layer + the cosmic-horror consciousness-ambiguity transfer are all CoO-original). Not all Kenshi-style displacement mechanics are appropriate to lift; the patient tonal register and the Pale Curation re-filing comedy are deliberately *not* Kenshi (Kenshi's tone is harsher and more militarized).
- **Follow-up:** if specific Kenshi mechanics or content beyond the structural pattern get borrowed (cage-pick mechanics, named NPC types, specific equipment-recovery flows), credit those at ship time.

### The Preserved (biological preservation system)

- **Real preservation traditions (LIVING and HISTORICAL — culturally weighted):**
  - **Egyptian mummification** — sacred to historical Egyptian religion and to contemporary Coptic / Egyptian heritage. Long-documented in scholarship but *do not* lift the word "mummy" or "mummification" or specific Egyptian iconography (canopic jars, wrapping-cloth practices, jackal-headed deities) into in-game text.
  - **Chinchorro mummies** (Chilean Atacama, ~7000 BCE — older than Egyptian) — heritage of Chilean Indigenous communities. Same caveat.
  - **Toraja people of Sulawesi** — *ma'nene* ritual: exhuming and dressing mummified ancestors. **Living tradition.** Heaviest care required if borrowed.
  - **Sokushinbutsu** (Japanese self-mummification, primarily Shingon Buddhist sect) — sacred living religious practice. **Do not borrow specifics.**
  - **Bog bodies** (Tollund Man, Lindow Man, Northern European Iron-Age) — pre-Christian European; *the most-fraught-among-least-fraught* category to draw structural inspiration from.
  - **Andean / Inca mummies** — Quechua / Aymara heritage; same cultural region as Urqu / Quechua-language already credited above. Care applies.
  - **Guanche mummies** (Canary Islands) — Indigenous Berber-related heritage.
  - **Chinese jade burial suits** (Han dynasty) — historical, archaeological. Specific cultural weight.
  - **Capuchin Crypt** (Rome, decorative bone arrangements) — already credited under Catacombs.
- **What was used:** the *category* of "preservation as cultural / religious / political act"; specific real-world chemistries (salt desiccation, iron-gall ink chemistry, honey antimicrobial properties, anaerobic peat, tree-resin amber). The horror beats (mass-chambers of preserved figures, the question of consciousness, the larder) are a CoO-original synthesis drawing from the *category* without lifting any specific tradition's iconography.
- **Stance:** **CoO-native naming throughout.** The generic term is "*the Preserved*"; specific methods are *Salt-Cured, Ink-Bathed, Honey-Sealed, Bog-Taken, Resin-Cast.* **The word "mummy" is explicitly off-limits** — too culturally weighted across multiple traditions. Iconography (wrapping-cloths, canopic jars, jade suits, sokushinbutsu's specific ascetic phases) similarly avoided. Real preservation chemistries cited in shipped credits as the *property-source*; specific cultural traditions credited only if their specifics get borrowed beyond the structural concept.
- **Follow-up required:**
  1. If any specific tradition's iconography or vocabulary gets borrowed (e.g., a specific village authored as drawing from Chinchorro practice), that tradition must be researched in primary ethnography and credited explicitly at ship time.
  2. The Toraja and sokushinbutsu traditions are *living religion* — the sokushinbutsu warning especially: do not casually borrow self-mummification framing; if a CoO village's tradition involves ascetic self-preservation, design from cultural-original-only intuitions.
  3. The bog-body inspiration is the lightest-fraught for borrowing structural concepts (preserved-by-natural-process, found-not-made, intact-after-centuries) — but real bog bodies are still archaeological dead from real cultures, and the credit applies.

The current entry deliberately keeps the cultural borrows abstract; the moment a specific real culture's relationship-to-a-stone gets borrowed, full ledger treatment applies.
