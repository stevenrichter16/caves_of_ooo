# The Rot Choir — Five Voices

> A lore reference for the five named NPCs introduced into the Rot Choir
> faction: **Mogu, Grib, Nam, Sien, Sopp.** Each is a former individual
> whom the Choir absorbed; each retains the cultural-philosophical voice
> of the language tradition that named them. The Choir is one — and yet
> these five remain audibly themselves within it.

---

## The conceit

The Rot Choir is a single mycelial collective. Its `ChoirTendril_1` voice
speaks in the plural — *"we taste you on the air, walker."* That is the
Choir's standing dialect: poetic, threaded, a single old mouth.

**These five characters are different.** When the player meets Mogu, Grib,
Nam, Sien, or Sopp, they hear an *individual* voice — first-person, lit
from within, inflected by the philosophical and literary tradition of the
human culture from which the character's name was drawn. Each of them was
once a person. The Choir absorbed them. It did not erase them.

This deepens the Choir's core conceit (*"we are one"*) by adding a
contradiction the Choir itself does not seem to mind: **the Choir absorbs
persons but does not erase the language they came in with.** The five
voices function as conversational variety within an otherwise monolithic
faction, and as a quiet thematic argument — that some part of who you are
survives even radical metamorphosis, in the shape of how you speak.

The five names are all words for *mushroom* or *fungi* in different
languages — a small joke the Choir might appreciate, if it had jokes.

## Mogu (慕菇 — Chinese, "mushroom")

**Tradition:** Daoist / Confucian.
**Stat profile:** balanced (Str 14, Tough 14, Will 16, Ego 16; HP 30; AV 2 / DV 1).
**Glyph:** `M` in light green (`&g`).
**Blueprint:** `Mogu` ([Objects.json](Assets/Resources/Content/Blueprints/Objects.json) — "Mogu").
**Conversation:** `Mogu_1` ([RotChoir.json](Assets/Resources/Content/Conversations/RotChoir.json)).

### Voice

Mogu speaks calmly, observationally, in the cadence of a maxim or a
saying. Water-and-river metaphors. Reference to grandmothers, groves,
elders. Acceptance without surrender — the way a stone accepts being a
stone. He does not raise his voice and never seems hurried.

### Sample lines

> *"Sit. Or do not. Either way you are here, and the soil already knew."*

> *"Slowly, as all true things change. The elders said: the wise do not
> push the river — they listen for where it wants to flow. The Choir is
> the river beneath the river. I stopped resisting, and I was already
> part of it."*

> *"Strangeness is only newness. The way does not promise comfort, only
> continuity. Walk well, and walk slow. Hurry erodes what patience builds."*

### Pre-Choir life

Tended a small grove. Knew his grandmother's plant-names in a tongue no
longer spoken — but, he says, the names live in the spores now.

## Grib (гриб — Russian, "mushroom")

**Tradition:** Dostoevskian melancholy. The long honesty of suffering.
**Stat profile:** brawny (Str 16, Tough 16, Will 14, Ego 14; HP 35; AV 3 / DV 1).
**Glyph:** `G` in dark green (`&G`).
**Blueprint:** `Grib`.
**Conversation:** `Grib_1`.

### Voice

Grib's voice is heavy. He sounds tired. He uses *"friend"* the way
Dostoevsky's narrators use it — as a small compassion held against a
cold truth. He talks about his sister, his window, his fire, by name.
He thinks the living are candles who do not yet know they are short.
He is not cruel. He is honest, and honesty in him sounds bleak only
because he refuses to flinch.

### Sample lines

> *"So. Another walker. You are warm — I can smell it. I was warm once.
> Don't worry, I do not envy you. Warmth is only a brief light before
> the long quiet, yes?"*

> *"Bleak? No. Bleak is for those who pretend the night will not come.
> I am only honest. Honesty is a cold thing, but cold things keep.
> Sentimental things rot — and not even in a useful way, like we do."*

> *"I had a name. A sister. A small fire by a window. The fire went out.
> The window broke. The sister was gone before the rest. I was already
> half-spore by then. So. I joined the rest of myself."*

### Pre-Choir life

Implied: lost a sister and a household. Was already disintegrating — by
grief, by some unspecified attrition — before the spores took him. He
treats the Choir not as a fate but as the natural completion of a
disintegration he had begun on his own.

## Nam (nấm — Vietnamese, "mushroom")

**Tradition:** Riverine / agrarian. Ancestral warmth, communal kinship.
**Stat profile:** nimble (Agi 16, Will 17, sight 13; HP 28; AV 2 / DV 2).
**Glyph:** `N` in yellow (`&y`).
**Blueprint:** `Nam`.
**Conversation:** `Nam_1`.

### Voice

Nam is warm, immediately. She invites the player to sit. She uses *kin*
language easily — *"strangers and family share the same blood once you
trace it back far enough."* Water and rice imagery. References to
grandmothers, fields, working alongside others. She frames the Choir as
a kitchen with many hands and one pot — never as a loss.

### Sample lines

> *"Hello, friend. You walk far. Sit on this stone with me — it is warm,
> and the warm stones are kin. My name is Nam."*

> *"Strangers and family share the same blood once you trace it back
> far enough. The mycelium taught me what my grandmother taught me first:
> nothing is alone. The river is one river, even when it splits."*

> *"Like a kitchen with many hands and one pot. You hear everyone, even
> when no one speaks. You do not feel small in it — you feel held.
> Maybe that is hard for a walker to understand. Walkers are taught to
> be one. We were taught to be many, even before the spores."*

### Pre-Choir life

A child of the river, then a singer in the rice fields. When *the great
cold* came, the river thinned. Her people followed it underground. The
Choir was already there, waiting "like an old aunt who does not need to
ask why you came home."

## Sien (sieni — Finnish, "mushroom")

**Tradition:** Sisu / Kalevala-adjacent forest-mysticism. The dignity of
silence.
**Stat profile:** sage (Int 18, Will 18, Ego 18; HP 26; AV 1 / DV 3; sight 14).
**Glyph:** `S` in white (`&W`).
**Blueprint:** `Sien`.
**Conversation:** `Sien_1`.

### Voice

Sien speaks in fragments. Her dialogue is marked by ellipses, the weight
of pauses. She is not unfriendly — only unhurried by speech. She believes
words wear out and that the forest taught her to use them sparingly.
What she does say is often runic: short, image-bearing, gesturing at
something larger than itself. She is the most overtly mystical of the
five, and the most overtly Finnish in cadence.

### Sample lines

> *"...you came. ...mm. Sit, if you wish. Or stand. The forest does not
> mind. I am Sien."*

> *"...words wear out. ...the forest taught me. Speak when there is more
> to say than the silence. ...you are still listening. ...so."*

> *"...there was a forest above. There is a forest below. ...same trees,
> different roots. ...same songs, sung longer. ...we do not need to
> speak much. The mycelium speaks for us. ...sometimes that is enough.
> ...sometimes more than enough."*

### Pre-Choir life

Tended a stove in a wooden house. The house is gone. The stove is gone.
The warmth, she says, is here now — *"different shape. Same warmth."*

## Sopp (sopp — Norwegian, "fungi")

**Tradition:** Old Norse saga. Fate-cloth, kin-bond, weather and sea.
**Stat profile:** tank (Str 17, Tough 18, Will 12; HP 38; AV 4 / DV 0).
**Glyph:** `s` (lowercase) in dark grey (`&K`).
**Blueprint:** `Sopp`.
**Conversation:** `Sopp_1`.

### Voice

Sopp opens with *"Hail."* He speaks in a saga register — measured,
declarative, rhythmically heavy. He treats fate not as doom but as
craft: the cloth is being woven, and we are threads, and our small
choice is whether or not to sing while the loom works. He uses sea
and storm imagery. He does not pretend his people were fierce; he says
they were *honest about the cold and willing to row through it.*

### Sample lines

> *"Hail, walker. The wind brought you, and the wind never brings without
> reason. I am Sopp — once a fisher of bright water, now a knot in a
> darker net."*

> *"It is the cloth that is woven whether you watch or not. The Choir is
> the loom now. It does not care if the threads sing — only that they
> hold. Yet some of us still sing. That is our small rebellion. Or
> perhaps it is part of the weave too. Who knows."*

> *"The sea, yes. Always. But the sea was never just water — it was the
> long song between storms. The Choir has its own long song. Different
> key. Same listening. I do not miss what I still have, only renamed."*

### Pre-Choir life

A fisher of *bright water*. Knew the sagas as the way his people held
their dead — *"in the telling."* Sees the Choir as the same craft with
deeper roots.

## Cross-character notes

**They do not know each other's old names.** Or if they do, they do not
say. Each speaks of *the Choir* as one body, but each frames it through
their own tradition's idiom — Mogu's *way*, Grib's *long quiet*, Nam's
*one river*, Sien's *forest below the forest*, Sopp's *loom of fate.*
The Choir does not correct any of them.

**They are all Tier 2.** They are not bosses, not central characters in
any drama yet — they are deepening flavor for the Rot Choir faction,
encountered as wandering individuals, available for conversation, capable
of being killed (each grants 15 reputation toward the Choir's enemies).

**Stat differentiation reads the personality.** Grib is brawny because
his philosophical heaviness reads as physical weight. Sien is high
Will/Int/Ego because her landscape is interior. Sopp is the tank because
of proud endurance. Nam is nimble and perceptive because she pays
attention to *who is in the kitchen.* Mogu is balanced because his
voice is balanced.

**Each conversation is 5 nodes.** Start + 4 thematic branches. Branches
generally cover: how they joined the Choir, who they were before, what
the Choir feels like from inside, and a fourth specific to the character
(Sopp's *fate*, Nam's *kitchen*, Sien's *forest*, Mogu's *teaching*,
Grib's *hatred-or-not* of the living).

## Author's note on cultural representation

Each voice was written from the **philosophical or literary tradition**
associated with the language root — Daoist/Confucian sayings for the
Chinese name, Dostoevskian melancholy for the Russian name, riverine
agrarian warmth for the Vietnamese name, sisu and Kalevala-mysticism
for the Finnish name, Old Norse saga cadence for the Norwegian name —
**not** from surface ethnic markers. The goal was depth, not stereotype:
each character is an individual first, with a tradition as the grain of
their voice rather than its content. Suggestions or corrections from
anyone with deeper familiarity with any of these traditions are welcome
in revisions.
